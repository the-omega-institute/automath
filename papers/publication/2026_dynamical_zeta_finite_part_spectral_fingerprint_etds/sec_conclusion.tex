\section{Discussion and open problems}\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of the main contributions}

The paper establishes three results. First, the finite part of the
dynamical \(\zeta\)-function at the Perron pole admits a closed
primitive-orbit formula in terms of the reduced determinant
(\Cref{thm:finite-part-primitive-closed-form}). Second, the
cyclic-lift constants and one sufficiently large root-of-unity layer
reconstruct the reduced spectral polynomial, hence the non-Perron
spectrum with multiplicity
(\Cref{thm:finite-part-cyclic-lift-spectrum-identifiability,%
thm:finite-part-cyclic-lift-single-q-torsion-reconstruction}). Third,
finite-group extensions admit a primitive determinant calculus in
Peter--Weyl coordinates via Adams--M\"obius inversion, and quotient
functoriality identifies cyclic data as exactly the abelian shadow of
the full primitive class profile
(\Cref{cor:class-function-adams-mobius,%
thm:abelian-shadow-defect,%
thm:cyclic-detection-boundary}).

The determinant identities and the Adams--M\"obius inversion hold
throughout the open Perron disc without extra hypotheses. The twisted
spectral-gap condition \(\eta<\lambda\) is used precisely when the
primitive Peter--Weyl series are evaluated at the boundary point
\(z=\lambda^{-1}\) and converted into conjugacy-class Mertens
constants.

\subsection{Open problems}

We close with three problems that arise naturally from the results
above but remain outside the present scope.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Dynamical criteria for the twisted spectral gap.} The
  condition
  \(\eta=\max_{\varrho\neq\mathbf 1}\rho(A_\varrho)<\lambda\) is
  assumed in \Cref{thm:class-mertens-explicit}. For the abelian
  (cyclic-lift) case this reduces to the classical condition that the
  Perron root is strictly dominant, which holds by primitivity.  For
  non-abelian extensions the analogous condition was also assumed
  without derivation in \cite{ParryPollicott1986Chebotarev}.
  Identifying computable sufficient conditions on the cocycle
  \(\tau:E_A\to G\) that guarantee \(\eta<\lambda\) for broad classes
  of finite-group extensions remains open. The analysis of Parry and
  Liv\v{s}ic obstructions in
  \cite{BoyleSchmieding2017FiniteGroupExtensions}, together with
  Parry's non-abelian periodic-point theorem
  \cite{Parry1999LivsicNonAbelian}, gives a partial structural answer,
  and the \(S_3\)-model of \Cref{prop:s3-example-channels} (where
  \(\rho(A_\rho)=1<2=\lambda\)) provides one explicit verification.

\item \emph{Non-abelian analogues of the arithmetic appendix.} The
  Dirichlet-character inversion of \Cref{app:arithmetic} and the
  growth criterion for the square-root bound are abelian in nature,
  relying on cyclic quotient data. It would be of interest to know
  which of these arithmetic consequences extend to the non-abelian
  setting, for instance whether boundary multiplicities
  (\Cref{thm:finite-part-boundary-multiplicity-qaxis-energy}) admit
  an analogue formulated in terms of the higher-dimensional twisted
  channels.

\item \emph{Transfer-operator extensions.} The present paper works
  entirely at the level of finite matrices and algebraic determinants.
  For countable-state shifts or for the Ruelle transfer operator acting
  on spaces of H\"older-continuous functions, the finite-part constant
  and the Adams--M\"obius mechanism should have natural analogues
  involving the Fredholm determinant of the transfer operator. Relating
  \(\FP(A)\) to the spectral data of the transfer operator on an
  appropriate Banach space would connect the present results to the
  broader programme of Baladi
  \cite{Baladi2000PositiveTransfer,Baladi2018ZetaDeterminants}, the
  regularity results of J\'ez\'equel
  \cite{Jezequel2019ParameterRegularity}, and the
  Ruelle--Pollicott resonance theory.
\end{enumerate}
