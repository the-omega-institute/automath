\title{Recursive Addressing via Prefix Sites, Local Sections, and Inverse Limits}
\author{}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We isolate the categorical core of recursive addressing.  Starting from a
measurable dynamical system and a finite family of observable concepts, we show
that recursive addressing refines visible information without enlarging the
visible $\sigma$-algebra.  This measurable statement is then rewritten in site
language: admissible addresses form a prefix site with cylinder covers, and the
readout mechanism becomes a presheaf of local readout objects.  Within this
internal formulation, address failure splits into three standard phenomena:
empty local fibers, failure of effective descent, and failure of
separatedness.  We make the finite-level obstruction theory explicit by
introducing a Cech-type class, the minimal visible quotient on which the
obstruction vanishes, and a sharp register lower bound for recovering the full
resolution.  Finally, for a dyadic inverse system of finite address spaces we
prove an inverse-limit theorem for stable addressed objects and identify
single-valued readout with separatedness at an inverse-limit address.  All
external protocol terminology from the larger manuscript is removed; the paper
is self-contained in measurable, site-theoretic, and inverse-limit language.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The slogan behind recursive addressing is simple: addresses precede values.
Used carelessly, however, that slogan invites too much external terminology.
The aim of this paper is to rewrite the same structure internally.  We begin
with a measurable dynamical system and a hierarchy of observable concepts.  The
first theorem states that recursively generated address cylinders refine the
visible information already present in the previous layer, but they do not add
new measurable structure.  This is the bridge from the master manuscript to a
standalone categorical paper.

Once the measurable statement is isolated, the rest of the paper can be stated
in site language.  Admissible addresses form a prefix site whose covers are
finite cylinder refinements.  Over that site we place a presheaf $\Rpre$ of
admissible local readout objects.  What the master manuscript informally
treated as address failure is then rewritten internally:
\begin{itemize}
\item there may be no local object over a given address;
\item compatible local objects may fail to descend to a global one;
\item global objects may fail to be separated by their restrictions.
\end{itemize}
The three cases correspond respectively to empty local fibers, descent
obstructions, and non-separatedness.

Two outputs drive the paper.  The first is a finite-level obstruction theory on
the prefix site: we define a Cech-type obstruction class, quotient the band by
the subgroup it generates, and identify the smallest visible quotient on which
the obstruction disappears pointwise.  A quantitative consequence is a sharp
register lower bound: recovering the full band requires at least as much extra
memory as the size of the obstruction subgroup.  The second output is an
inverse-limit theorem: in a dyadic tower of finite address spaces, compatible
address threads determine unique stable objects once the fibers shrink at the
correct rate, and single-valued readout at a stable address is equivalent to
separatedness of the readout presheaf at that address.

The paper is deliberately self-contained.  We use measurable dynamics only to
set up recursive refinement, then shift entirely to sites, local sections, and
inverse limits.  We do not retain the external interface language, and we do
not import folding, scan-error, arithmetic, or protocol-specific machinery.
Background references for measurable dynamics, sheaves on sites, and Cech
cohomology include
\cite{WaltersErgodicTheory,MacLaneMoerdijk1992,Bredon1997SheafTheory,
Munkres2000Topology}.

\section{Recursive Addressing in Measurable Systems}

Let $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)$ be a measurable dynamical system.  Fix a finite
family of measurable sets
\[
\mathcal{C}^{(L)} = \{C_1^{(L)},\dots,C_{r_L}^{(L)}\}\subset \mathcal{B},
\]
thought of as the observable concepts available at level $L$.  Their binary
readout process is
\[
\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_t(x)
:=
\bigl(
\ind_{C_1^{(L)}}(T^t x),\dots,\ind_{C_{r_L}^{(L)}}(T^t x)
\bigr)\in\{0,1\}^{r_L}.
\]
For a word
\[
a=(a_0,\dots,a_{\ell-1})\in \bigl(\{0,1\}^{r_L}\bigr)^\ell
\]
and an integer $t$, define the derived cylinder event
\[
C_{a,t}^{(L+1)}
:=
\{x\in X:\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_t(x)=a_0,\dots,\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_{t+\ell-1}(x)=a_{\ell-1}\}.
\]
Thus the next level is produced by finite windows in the previous one.

\begin{proposition}[Recursive addressing is measurable refinement]
\label{prop:recursive-addressing-refinement}
Let
\[
\mathcal{G}^{(L)}
:=
\sigma\bigl(T^{-t}C_i^{(L)}:\ 1\le i\le r_L,\ t\in\ZZ\bigr)
\]
be the visible $\sigma$-algebra generated by the level-$L$ concepts and their
time shifts.  Then every derived cylinder event $C_{a,t}^{(L+1)}$ belongs to
$\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For fixed $i$ and $t$, the map
\[
x\longmapsto \ind_{C_i^{(L)}}(T^t x)=\ind_{T^{-t}C_i^{(L)}}(x)
\]
is $\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$-measurable.  Hence the event
\[
\{x:\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_t(x)=a_0,\dots,\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_{t+\ell-1}(x)=a_{\ell-1}\}
\]
is a finite intersection of sets of the form $T^{-s}C_i^{(L)}$ and their
complements, and therefore lies in $\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Visible $\sigma$-algebras do not expand]
\label{cor:recursive-addressing-visible-sigma-nonexpansion}
Suppose $\mathcal{C}^{(L+1)}$ is chosen from the derived cylinder events
$C_{a,t}^{(L+1)}$, and define
\[
\mathcal{G}^{(L+1)}
:=
\sigma\bigl(T^{-t}C:\ C\in \mathcal{C}^{(L+1)},\ t\in\ZZ\bigr).
\]
Then
\[
\mathcal{G}^{(L+1)}\subseteq \mathcal{G}^{(L)}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:recursive-addressing-refinement}, every generator of
$\mathcal{G}^{(L+1)}$ already belongs to $\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$.  Taking the
generated $\sigma$-algebra gives the inclusion.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This is the measurable content of recursive addressing.  New addresses are
produced by reorganizing visible cylinder events; they do not enlarge the
observable $\sigma$-algebra.  The rest of the paper repackages this fact in
site-theoretic language.
\end{remark}

\section{Prefix Sites and Readout Presheaves}

\subsection{The Prefix Site}

Fix a finite alphabet $\Sigma$ and let $\Sigma^{<\infty}$ be the set of all
finite words.  We write $a\preceq b$ when $a$ is a prefix of $b$.  Let
$\Aadm\subseteq \Sigma^{<\infty}$ be the set of admissible addresses.

For each $a\in\Aadm$, let $C_a\subseteq X$ be the cylinder event determined by
$a$.  Only the following two features matter:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item if $b\succeq a$, then $C_b\subseteq C_a$;
\item every finite refinement of a cylinder is again a finite union of deeper
cylinders.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{definition}[Prefix site]
\label{def:prefix-site}
The \emph{prefix site} $\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm)$ is the category whose objects are
addresses $a\in\Aadm$ and where there is a morphism $b\to a$ exactly when
$b\succeq a$.  A finite family $\{b_i\to a\}_{i=1}^n$ is declared to be a
\emph{cover} if
\[
C_a = \bigcup_{i=1}^n C_{b_i}.
\]
Such covers will be called \emph{cylinder covers}.
\end{definition}

This is the site-theoretic version of prefix refinement.  The measurable
non-expansion result from Section~2 says that recursive addressing does not
leave this site: it only chooses deeper covers inside the same visible
structure.

\subsection{The Readout Presheaf}

The paper keeps the readout object deliberately abstract.  A local readout over
$a$ is any object that can be evaluated using only the information visible on
$C_a$ and that remains admissible under further refinement.

\begin{definition}[Readout presheaf]
\label{def:prefix-sheaf-readout}
A \emph{readout presheaf} on $\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm)$ is a contravariant functor
\[
\Rpre:\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm)^{\mathrm{op}}\to \mathbf{Set}.
\]
For $a\in\Aadm$, the set $\Rpre(a)$ is interpreted as the set of admissible
local readout or certificate objects over $C_a$.  For a refinement $b\succeq
a$, the restriction map
\[
\rho_{a\leftarrow b}:\Rpre(a)\to \Rpre(b)
\]
restricts a local readout from $C_a$ to the deeper cylinder $C_b$.
\end{definition}

The presheaf formalism is minimal but sufficient.  Existence of a local object
is encoded by $\Rpre(a)\neq\varnothing$, while compatibility and descent are
expressed by the restriction maps and cylinder covers.

\section{Local Sections and Descent}

\subsection{Local Sections}

\begin{proposition}[Addressability as existence of a local section]
\label{prop:null-as-local-section-obstruction}
For an admissible address $a\in\Aadm$, readout is internally defined at $a$ if
and only if
\[
\Rpre(a)\neq\varnothing.
\]
Moreover, for every cylinder cover $\{b_i\to a\}$, at most one global section
$s\in\Rpre(a)$ can restrict to a given compatible family of local sections
$s_i\in \Rpre(b_i)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first statement is simply the internal reformulation of addressability: a
readout over $a$ exists exactly when there is an admissible local object over
the cylinder $C_a$.

For uniqueness, if $s,t\in\Rpre(a)$ have identical restrictions to each
$b_i$, then they agree on every piece of the cover.  Since the cover exhausts
$C_a$, the two global sections induce the same local behavior on every visible
piece, so the presheaf semantics treats them as the same global readout over
$a$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Finite-level trichotomy of address failure]
\label{cor:null-trichotomy-local-section}
For a finite-level address, failure of readout has exactly three internal
forms:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item the address is not admissible, so there is no object of the site at all;
\item the address is admissible but $\Rpre(a)=\varnothing$, so no local section
exists;
\item local sections exist on a cylinder cover of $a$ but fail to descend to a
global section.
\end{enumerate}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The first two cases are immediate from the definition of the site and
Proposition~\ref{prop:null-as-local-section-obstruction}.  If the address is
admissible and some local sections exist but there is still no global readout,
the remaining possibility is failure of descent along a cover.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Cech-Type Gluing Obstructions}

We now record the standard Cech obstruction that witnesses finite-level
descent failure.

\begin{definition}[Cech gluing obstruction]
\label{def:prefix-site-h2-gluing-obstruction}
Fix an admissible address $a\in\Aadm$, a cylinder cover $\{b_i\to a\}_{i=1}^n$,
and an abelian group $A$.  Suppose one is given local sections
$s_i\in \Rpre(b_i)$ together with transition data
\[
g_{ij}\in A
\]
on nonempty overlaps $C_{b_i}\cap C_{b_j}$, interpreted as the change of local
trivialization from $s_j$ to $s_i$.  The associated \emph{Cech gluing
obstruction} is the $2$-cochain
\[
\alpha_{ijk}:=g_{ij}g_{jk}g_{ik}^{-1}\in A
\]
on triple overlaps $C_{b_i}\cap C_{b_j}\cap C_{b_k}\neq\varnothing$.  Its
cohomology class will be denoted by $[\alpha]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Nontrivial gluing obstruction prevents global readout]
\label{prop:null-as-h2-obstruction}
In the setting of Definition~\ref{def:prefix-site-h2-gluing-obstruction}, if
$[\alpha]\neq 0$, then the local sections $\{s_i\}$ do not descend to a global
section of $\Rpre(a)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume for contradiction that a global section $s\in\Rpre(a)$ exists.  Then
each $s_i$ is obtained by restricting $s$ to $b_i$, so the transition data can
be chosen consistently across overlaps.  In particular, the resulting Cech
$2$-cocycle is cohomologically trivial.  This contradicts $[\alpha]\neq 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The point of the obstruction language is not to introduce a separate gerbe
theory into the paper.  It is only to name the standard situation in which
local sections exist and are pairwise compatible, but effective descent still
fails.
\end{remark}

\section{Minimal Visible Quotients and Quantitative Recovery}

Let $A$ be a finite abelian group, and let $[\alpha]$ be a gluing obstruction
class as above.  The visible content of the obstruction is determined by the
subgroup generated by the actual triple-overlap defects.

\begin{proposition}[Minimal visible quotient]
\label{prop:prefix-site-min-visible-quotient}
Define
\[
H_\alpha := \langle \alpha_{ijk}:\ C_{b_i}\cap C_{b_j}\cap C_{b_k}\neq\varnothing\rangle
\le A,
\qquad
\Avis := A/H_\alpha.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item the image of $\alpha$ in $\Avis$ vanishes pointwise, so the obstruction
becomes trivial after passing to $\Avis$;
\item if $\varphi:A\to B$ is any homomorphism such that $\varphi(\alpha_{ijk})=0$
for every nonempty triple overlap, then $\varphi$ factors uniquely through the
quotient map $A\twoheadrightarrow \Avis$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Every generator $\alpha_{ijk}$ of $H_\alpha$ maps to zero in $\Avis$, so the
obstruction class becomes pointwise trivial after quotienting.

If $\varphi(\alpha_{ijk})=0$ for every triple overlap, then
$H_\alpha\subseteq \ker\varphi$.  By the universal property of the quotient,
$\varphi$ factors uniquely through $A/H_\alpha=\Avis$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Register lower bound for full-resolution recovery]
\label{thm:prefix-site-register-lower-bound}
Assume $A$ is finite.  Suppose one wishes to recover the full $A$-resolution
from the visible quotient $\Avis$ by adjoining a finite register set $R$ in
such a way that there exists an injection
\[
A\hookrightarrow \Avis\times R.
\]
Then
\[
\lvert R\rvert \ge \lvert H_\alpha\rvert.
\]
This bound is sharp: equality can be attained with $R=H_\alpha$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Every fiber of the quotient map $A\twoheadrightarrow \Avis$ is a coset of
$H_\alpha$, hence has cardinality $\lvert H_\alpha\rvert$.  If
$A\hookrightarrow \Avis\times R$, then restricting the injection to any one of
these fibers yields an injection into $R$.  Therefore
\[
\lvert R\rvert\ge \lvert H_\alpha\rvert.
\]

For sharpness, take $R:=H_\alpha$.  Choose a set-theoretic section
$s:\Avis\to A$ of the quotient map.  Then every $a\in A$ can be written
uniquely as
\[
a = s(\bar a)+h
\qquad
(\bar a\in\Avis,\ h\in H_\alpha),
\]
and the map $a\mapsto (\bar a,h)$ is injective.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral definability occurs only on the visible quotient]
\label{thm:prefix-site-spectral-definability-avis}
Let $\widehat A:=\Hom(A,\TT)$ be the Pontryagin dual, and define the
annihilator
\[
H_\alpha^\perp
:=
\{\chi\in \widehat A:\ \chi(h)=1\ \text{for all }h\in H_\alpha\}.
\]
Then $H_\alpha^\perp\cong \widehat{\Avis}$, and a character-based spectral
readout descends to a well-defined global object if and only if the chosen
character lies in $H_\alpha^\perp$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The quotient map $A\twoheadrightarrow \Avis$ dualizes to an embedding
$\widehat{\Avis}\hookrightarrow \widehat A$ with image exactly
$H_\alpha^\perp$.

Now let $\chi\in\widehat A$.  On triple overlaps the obstruction contributes a
multiplier $\chi(\alpha_{ijk})$.  If $\chi\notin H_\alpha^\perp$, then
$\chi(\alpha_{ijk})\neq 1$ for some triple overlap, so the resulting spectral
object depends on the choice of local trivialization and cannot descend to a
global readout.  If $\chi\in H_\alpha^\perp$, all such multipliers are trivial,
so the character factors through $\Avis$ and the spectral object is globally
well defined.
\end{proof}

\section{Dyadic Inverse Limits and Separatedness}

\subsection{Stable Objects in the Dyadic Tower}

\begin{theorem}[Dyadic inverse-limit stable objects]
\label{thm:recursive-addressing-dyadic-inverse-limit-stable-object}
Let $(\mathcal{A}_b,\pi_{b'\to b})_{b'\ge b\ge 0}$ be an inverse system of
finite nonempty sets with surjective bonding maps
\[
\pi_{b'\to b}:\mathcal{A}_{b'}\twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{A}_b
\]
satisfying the usual compatibility relations.  Define the inverse limit
\[
\Ainf
:=
\varprojlim_b \mathcal{A}_b
=
\left\{
(a_b)_{b\ge 0}\in \prod_{b\ge 0}\mathcal{A}_b:\ 
\pi_{b'\to b}(a_{b'})=a_b\ \text{for all }b'\ge b
\right\}.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item $\Ainf\neq\varnothing$;
\item the dyadic metric
\[
d_{\mathrm{dy}}(a,a')
:=
\begin{cases}
0,& a=a',\\[1mm]
2^{-b_\ast(a,a')},& a\neq a',
\end{cases}
\qquad
b_\ast(a,a')
:=
\max\{b\ge 0:\ a_b=a'_b\}
\]
makes $\Ainf$ into a compact, complete, totally disconnected ultrametric
space;
\item if $Y$ is a complete metric space and for each $a_b\in \mathcal{A}_b$ one
is given a nonempty closed set $F_b(a_b)\subseteq Y$ such that
\[
F_{b'}(a_{b'})\subseteq F_b(a_b)
\quad\text{whenever}\quad
\pi_{b'\to b}(a_{b'})=a_b
\]
and
\[
\diam\bigl(F_b(a_b)\bigr)\le C\,2^{-b}
\qquad
(\forall b,\ \forall a_b\in\mathcal{A}_b)
\]
for some constant $C>0$, then every $a=(a_b)_{b\ge 0}\in \Ainf$ determines a
unique stable object
\[
x(a)\in \bigcap_{b\ge 0} F_b(a_b).
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each $b\ge 0$, let
\[
E_b
:=
\left\{
(a_j)_{j\ge 0}\in\prod_{j\ge 0}\mathcal{A}_j:\ 
\pi_{j\to i}(a_j)=a_i\ \text{for all }b\ge j\ge i\ge 0
\right\}.
\]
Because each $\mathcal{A}_j$ is finite and nonempty, the product
$\prod_j \mathcal{A}_j$ is compact in the product topology, and each $E_b$ is
closed.  Surjectivity of the bonding maps guarantees that every finite
intersection $\bigcap_{r=0}^m E_r$ is nonempty, so compactness gives
$\Ainf=\bigcap_{b\ge 0} E_b\neq\varnothing$.

The dyadic metric is the standard prefix ultrametric.  Compactness of $\Ainf$
follows again from the compact product representation.  Completeness is
obtained by coordinatewise stabilization of Cauchy sequences, and total
disconnectedness follows because the basic cylinder neighborhoods
\[
U_b(a):=\{a'\in\Ainf:\ a'_b=a_b\}
\]
are clopen and form a basis.

For the final statement, fix $a=(a_b)\in\Ainf$.  The family
$\{F_b(a_b)\}_{b\ge 0}$ is a nested sequence of nonempty closed subsets of $Y$
with diameters tending to zero.  Choose $x_b\in F_b(a_b)$.  The diameter bound
makes $(x_b)$ a Cauchy sequence, hence it converges to some $x\in Y$ because
$Y$ is complete.  Since each $F_b(a_b)$ is closed and contains all sufficiently
deep $x_{b'}$, it follows that $x\in F_b(a_b)$ for every $b$.  Uniqueness
follows from
\[
d_Y(x,y)\le \diam(F_b(a_b))\le C2^{-b}
\qquad
(\forall b),
\]
which forces $x=y$ as $b\to\infty$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Single-Valued Readout and Separatedness}

Fix $a=(a_b)_{b\ge 0}\in \Ainf$ and write
\[
U_b(a):=\{a'\in\Ainf:\ a'_b=a_b\}.
\]
These sets form the dyadic neighborhood basis of $a$.  We identify each
$U_b(a)$ with the finite address $a_b$, and therefore write
\[
\Rpre(U_b(a)):=\Rpre(a_b).
\]
The compatible readout threads at $a$ are collected in the inverse-limit fiber
\[
\Rpre_\infty(a)
:=
\varprojlim_b \Rpre(U_b(a)).
\]

\begin{theorem}[Separatedness criterion for single-valued readout]
\label{thm:recursive-addressing-readout-separatedness-null}
For a stable address $a\in\Ainf$, the following are equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item Readout at $a$ is single-valued: whenever two compatible threads in
$\Rpre_\infty(a)$ induce the same restrictions on every member of a finite
dyadic cover of some neighborhood $U_b(a)$, the two threads are equal.
\item The presheaf $\Rpre$ is separated at $a$: for every finite dyadic cover
\[
\{U_{b_i}(a)\to U_b(a)\}_{i=1}^r,
\]
the map
\[
\Rpre(U_b(a))\longrightarrow \prod_{i=1}^r \Rpre(U_{b_i}(a))
\]
is injective on compatible families.
\end{enumerate}

Moreover, failure of single-valued readout at $a$ has three internal forms:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{empty local fiber}: $\Rpre(U_b(a))=\varnothing$ for some sufficiently
deep neighborhood;
\item \emph{descent failure}: compatible local sections on a finite dyadic cover
of some $U_b(a)$ do not glue to a section over $U_b(a)$;
\item \emph{non-separatedness}: two distinct sections over some $U_b(a)$ have
identical restrictions to a finite dyadic cover of $U_b(a)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The equivalence of \textup{(1)} and \textup{(2)} is exactly the usual
separatedness condition, specialized to the dyadic neighborhood basis of the
inverse-limit address.

If some sufficiently deep neighborhood has empty fiber, then no readout thread
through $a$ exists at all, so readout fails by absence of local data.  If local
sections exist on a finite cover but do not glue, then the failure is one of
effective descent.  Finally, if two distinct sections over $U_b(a)$ restrict to
the same family on a finite cover, then finite observations do not distinguish
them, so readout is not single-valued.  Conversely, any failure of single-valued
readout must occur through one of these three mechanisms: no local object, no
descent, or no separatedness.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion}

The paper isolates one clean categorical chain:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{recursive refinement of visible events}
&\Longrightarrow \text{prefix site and readout presheaf} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{local-section and descent obstructions} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{minimal visible quotient and register bound} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{inverse-limit stability and separatedness.}
\end{aligned}
\]
This is the correct boundary for the categorical companion paper.  The opening
measurable proposition keeps the paper anchored to observable dynamics, but the
main payoffs are site-theoretic and descent-theoretic.  In particular, the
paper explains how recursive addressing can remain conservative at the level of
visible $\sigma$-algebras while still exhibiting genuinely nontrivial internal
obstruction theory.

Just as importantly, this is the right stopping point.  We do not retain the
external interface terminology of the master manuscript, and we do not enlarge
the paper with protocol semantics, gerbe packages, or unrelated dynamical
machinery.  The contribution here is narrower and cleaner: recursive addressing
as a prefix-site construction with explicit finite-level obstruction theory and
a dyadic separatedness criterion at the inverse-limit level.
