\begin{abstract}
We isolate the categorical core of recursive addressing.  Starting from a
measurable dynamical system and a finite family of observable concepts, we show
that recursive addressing refines visible information without enlarging the
visible $\sigma$-algebra.  This measurable statement is then rewritten in site
language: admissible addresses form a prefix site with cylinder covers, and the
readout mechanism becomes a presheaf of local readout objects.  Within this
internal formulation, address failure splits into three standard phenomena:
empty local fibers, failure of effective descent, and failure of
separatedness.  For finite covers equipped with an abelian banded comparison
datum relating local readouts on overlaps, we define a \v{C}ech-type defect
class, the minimal visible quotient on which the defect vanishes, and a sharp
register lower bound for recovering the full band.  Finally, for a dyadic
inverse system of finite address spaces we record the standard inverse-limit
existence theorem for stable addressed objects and identify single-valued
readout with separatedness at an inverse-limit address.  All external
protocol-specific terminology is removed; the paper is self-contained in
measurable, site-theoretic, and inverse-limit language.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The slogan behind recursive addressing is simple: addresses precede values.
Used carelessly, however, that slogan invites too much external terminology.
The aim of this paper is to rewrite the same structure internally.  We begin
with a measurable dynamical system and a hierarchy of observable concepts.  The
first main result states that recursively generated address cylinders refine the
visible information already present in the previous layer, but they do not add
new measurable structure.  This is the bridge from the measurable formulation
to a standalone categorical paper.

Once the measurable statement is isolated, the rest of the paper can be stated
in site language.  Admissible addresses form a prefix site whose covers are
finite cylinder refinements.  Over that site we place a presheaf $\Rpre$ of
admissible local readout objects.  What informal recursive-addressing language
treated as address failure is then rewritten internally:
\begin{itemize}
\item there may be no local object over a given address;
\item compatible local objects may fail to descend to a global one;
\item global objects may fail to be separated by their restrictions.
\end{itemize}
The three cases correspond respectively to empty local fibers, descent
obstructions, and non-separatedness.

The paper makes four concrete contributions.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item Proposition~\ref{prop:recursive-addressing-refinement} and
Corollary~\ref{cor:recursive-addressing-visible-sigma-nonexpansion} isolate
the measurable non-expansion statement that underlies recursive refinement.
\item Proposition~\ref{prop:prefix-site-grothendieck-topology},
Definition~\ref{def:prefix-sheaf-readout}, and
Corollary~\ref{cor:null-trichotomy-local-section} recast admissible addresses
and readout as a prefix site together with a presheaf-theoretic trichotomy, and
Example~\ref{ex:binary-prefix-site-failure-modes} makes the three failure modes
explicit on the binary prefix site.
\item Definition~\ref{def:prefix-site-h2-gluing-obstruction},
Lemma~\ref{prop:prefix-site-min-visible-quotient},
Lemma~\ref{thm:prefix-site-register-lower-bound}, and
Proposition~\ref{thm:prefix-site-spectral-definability-avis} show that, once an
explicit abelian banded comparison datum has been fixed on a finite cylinder
cover, one obtains a \v{C}ech defect class, its minimal visible quotient, a
sharp register bound, and a character criterion for spectral definability.
\item Proposition~\ref{thm:recursive-addressing-dyadic-inverse-limit-stable-object}
and Theorem~\ref{thm:recursive-addressing-readout-separatedness-null} record
the dyadic inverse-limit background and identify single-valued readout at a
stable address with separatedness along its dyadic neighborhood basis.
\end{enumerate}

Two outputs drive the paper.  The first is a finite-level obstruction theory
for covers equipped with an abelian banded comparison datum: we define a
\v{C}ech-type defect class, quotient the band by the subgroup it generates, and
identify the smallest visible quotient on which the defect disappears
pointwise.  A quantitative consequence is a sharp register lower bound:
recovering the full band requires at least as much extra memory as the size of
the defect subgroup.  The second output is an inverse-limit theorem: in a
dyadic tower of finite address spaces, compatible address threads determine
unique stable objects once the fibers shrink at the correct rate, and
single-valued readout at a stable address is equivalent to separatedness of the
readout presheaf at that address.

The paper is deliberately self-contained.  We use measurable dynamics only to
set up recursive refinement, then shift entirely to sites, local sections, and
inverse limits.  We do not retain the external interface language, and we do
not import folding, scan-error, arithmetic, or protocol-specific machinery.
Background references for measurable dynamics, sites and Grothendieck
topologies, descent, \v{C}ech cohomology, symbolic dynamics, and profinite
inverse limits include
\cite{WaltersErgodicTheory,MacLaneMoerdijk1992,Bredon1997SheafTheory,
Munkres2000Topology,SGA4I,Johnstone2002Elephant,Vistoli2008Grothendieck,
Milne1980EtaleCohomology,Tamme1994IntroductionEtaleCohomology,
LindMarcus1995SymbolicDynamics,
Kitchens1998SymbolicDynamics,RibesZalesskii2010ProfiniteGroups,
Wilson1998ProfiniteGroups}.

\section{Recursive Addressing in Measurable Systems}

Let $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)$ be a measurable dynamical system.  Fix a finite
family of measurable sets
\[
\mathcal{C}^{(L)} = \{C_1^{(L)},\dots,C_{r_L}^{(L)}\}\subset \mathcal{B},
\]
thought of as the observable concepts available at level $L$.  Their binary
readout process is
\[
\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_t(x)
:=
\bigl(
\ind_{C_1^{(L)}}(T^t x),\dots,\ind_{C_{r_L}^{(L)}}(T^t x)
\bigr)\in\{0,1\}^{r_L}.
\]
For a word
\[
a=(a_0,\dots,a_{\ell-1})\in \bigl(\{0,1\}^{r_L}\bigr)^\ell
\]
and an integer $t$, define the derived cylinder event
\[
C_{a,t}^{(L+1)}
:=
\{x\in X:\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_t(x)=a_0,\dots,\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_{t+\ell-1}(x)=a_{\ell-1}\}.
\]
Thus the next level is produced by finite windows in the previous one.

\begin{proposition}[Recursive addressing is measurable refinement]
\label{prop:recursive-addressing-refinement}
Let
\[
\mathcal{G}^{(L)}
:=
\sigma\bigl(T^{-t}C_i^{(L)}:\ 1\le i\le r_L,\ t\in\ZZ\bigr)
\]
be the visible $\sigma$-algebra generated by the level-$L$ concepts and their
time shifts.  Then every derived cylinder event $C_{a,t}^{(L+1)}$ belongs to
$\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For fixed $i$ and $t$, the map
\[
x\longmapsto \ind_{C_i^{(L)}}(T^t x)=\ind_{T^{-t}C_i^{(L)}}(x)
\]
is $\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$-measurable.  Hence the event
\[
\{x:\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_t(x)=a_0,\dots,\mathbf{b}^{(L)}_{t+\ell-1}(x)=a_{\ell-1}\}
\]
is a finite intersection of sets of the form $T^{-s}C_i^{(L)}$ and their
complements, and therefore lies in $\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Visible $\sigma$-algebras do not expand]
\label{cor:recursive-addressing-visible-sigma-nonexpansion}
Suppose $\mathcal{C}^{(L+1)}$ is chosen from the derived cylinder events
$C_{a,t}^{(L+1)}$, and define
\[
\mathcal{G}^{(L+1)}
:=
\sigma\bigl(T^{-t}C:\ C\in \mathcal{C}^{(L+1)},\ t\in\ZZ\bigr).
\]
Then
\[
\mathcal{G}^{(L+1)}\subseteq \mathcal{G}^{(L)}.
\]
\leanverified{paper_recursive_addressing_visible_sigma_nonexpansion_seeds}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Each set $C\in\mathcal{C}^{(L+1)}$ is one of the derived cylinder events
$C_{a,t_0}^{(L+1)}$, so Proposition~\ref{prop:recursive-addressing-refinement}
gives $C\in\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$.  Since $\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$ is generated by all
time shifts $T^{-s}C_i^{(L)}$, it is stable under every shift $T^{-t}$:
\[
T^{-t}\bigl(T^{-s}C_i^{(L)}\bigr)=T^{-(s+t)}C_i^{(L)}\in \mathcal{G}^{(L)}.
\]
Hence $T^{-t}C\in\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$ for every $C\in\mathcal{C}^{(L+1)}$ and
every $t\in\ZZ$.  Therefore every generator of $\mathcal{G}^{(L+1)}$ lies in
$\mathcal{G}^{(L)}$, so
\[
\mathcal{G}^{(L+1)}\subseteq \mathcal{G}^{(L)}.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This is the measurable content of recursive addressing.  New addresses are
produced by reorganizing visible cylinder events; they do not enlarge the
observable $\sigma$-algebra.  The rest of the paper repackages this fact in
site-theoretic language.
\end{remark}

\section{Prefix Sites and Readout Presheaves}

\subsection{The Prefix Site}

Fix a finite alphabet $\Sigma$ and let $\Sigma^{<\infty}$ be the set of all
finite words.  We write $a\preceq b$ when $a$ is a prefix of $b$.  Let
$\Aadm\subseteq \Sigma^{<\infty}$ be the set of admissible addresses.

For each $a\in\Aadm$, let $C_a\subseteq X$ be the cylinder event determined by
$a$.  Only the following three features matter:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item if $b\succeq a$, then $C_b\subseteq C_a$;
\item whenever $b,c\succeq a$ and $C_b\cap C_c\neq\varnothing$, there exists
an admissible address $d$ with $d\succeq b,c$ and
\[
C_d=C_b\cap C_c;
\]
\item every finite refinement of a cylinder is again a finite union of deeper
cylinders.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{definition}[Prefix site]
\label{def:prefix-site}
The \emph{prefix site} $\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm)$ is the category whose objects are
addresses $a\in\Aadm$ and where there is a morphism $b\to a$ exactly when
$b\succeq a$.  A finite family $\{b_i\to a\}_{i=1}^n$ is declared to be a
\emph{cover} if
\[
C_a = \bigcup_{i=1}^n C_{b_i}.
\]
Such covers will be called \emph{cylinder covers}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Cylinder covers generate a Grothendieck topology]
\label{prop:prefix-site-grothendieck-topology}
The cylinder covers satisfy the identity, base-change, and transitivity axioms
of a finitary pretopology on $\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm)$.  Hence they generate a
Grothendieck topology $J_{\mathrm{cyl}}$, and
\[
(\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm),J_{\mathrm{cyl}})
\]
is a well-defined site.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The singleton family $\{a\to a\}$ is a cylinder cover because $C_a=C_a$.

Now let $\{b_i\to a\}_{i=1}^n$ be a cylinder cover and let $c\to a$ be a
refinement.  Then
\[
C_c
=
C_c\cap C_a
=
\bigcup_{i=1}^n (C_c\cap C_{b_i}).
\]
For each index $i$ with $C_c\cap C_{b_i}\neq\varnothing$, property
\textup{(2)} yields an admissible address $d_i$ with $d_i\succeq c,b_i$ and
$C_{d_i}=C_c\cap C_{b_i}$.  Discarding the empty intersections, we obtain a
finite cylinder cover $\{d_i\to c\}$ of $c$, and each composite
$d_i\to c\to a$ factors through $b_i\to a$.

Finally, if $\{b_i\to a\}_{i=1}^n$ is a cylinder cover and each
$\{c_{ij}\to b_i\}_{j=1}^{m_i}$ is a cylinder cover of $b_i$, then
\[
C_a
=
\bigcup_{i=1}^n C_{b_i}
=
\bigcup_{i=1}^n \bigcup_{j=1}^{m_i} C_{c_{ij}},
\]
so the composite family $\{c_{ij}\to a\}_{i,j}$ is again a cylinder cover.
Therefore the cylinder covers form a finitary pretopology and hence generate a
Grothendieck topology.
\end{proof}

This is the site-theoretic version of prefix refinement.  The measurable
non-expansion result from Section~2 says that recursive addressing does not
leave this site: it only chooses deeper covers inside the same visible
structure.  Cylinder covers of this kind are familiar both from symbolic
dynamics and from the general language of sites generated by finitary
coverages; see
\cite{LindMarcus1995SymbolicDynamics,Kitchens1998SymbolicDynamics,
Johnstone2002Elephant,SGA4I}.

\subsection{The Readout Presheaf}

The paper keeps the readout object deliberately abstract.  A local readout over
$a$ is any object that can be evaluated using only the information visible on
$C_a$ and that remains admissible under further refinement.

\begin{definition}[Readout presheaf]
\label{def:prefix-sheaf-readout}
A \emph{readout presheaf} on $\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm)$ is a contravariant functor
\[
\Rpre:\mathsf{Pref}(\Aadm)^{\mathrm{op}}\to \mathbf{Set}.
\]
For $a\in\Aadm$, the set $\Rpre(a)$ is interpreted as the set of admissible
local readout or certificate objects over $C_a$.  For a refinement $b\succeq
a$, the restriction map
\[
\rho_{a\leftarrow b}:\Rpre(a)\to \Rpre(b)
\]
restricts a local readout from $C_a$ to the deeper cylinder $C_b$.
\end{definition}

The presheaf formalism is minimal but sufficient.  Existence of a local object
is encoded by $\Rpre(a)\neq\varnothing$, while compatibility and descent are
expressed by the restriction maps and cylinder covers.

\section{Local Sections and Descent}

\subsection{Local Sections}

\begin{proposition}[Addressability as existence of a local section]
\label{prop:null-as-local-section-obstruction}
For an admissible address $a\in\Aadm$, readout is internally defined at $a$ if
and only if
\[
\Rpre(a)\neq\varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Definition~\ref{def:prefix-sheaf-readout}, the set $\Rpre(a)$ consists
exactly of the admissible local readout objects over the cylinder $C_a$.
Therefore $\Rpre(a)\neq\varnothing$ if and only if at least one such local
readout exists.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Uniqueness of descent along a cylinder cover is not automatic for a presheaf of
sets.  It is exactly the separatedness condition, which is treated explicitly
in Section~6.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Finite-level trichotomy of address failure]
\label{cor:null-trichotomy-local-section}
For an admissible finite-level address $a\in\Aadm$, failure of a well-defined
readout at $a$ has three internal
forms:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item \emph{empty local fiber}: $\Rpre(a)=\varnothing$;
\item \emph{descent failure}: local sections exist on a cylinder cover of $a$
and are pairwise compatible, but no section of $\Rpre(a)$ restricts to them;
\item \emph{non-separatedness}: two distinct sections of $\Rpre(a)$ have the
same restrictions to a cylinder cover of $a$.
\end{enumerate}
\leanverified{paper_recursive_addressing_null_trichotomy_local_section}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The first case is Proposition~\ref{prop:null-as-local-section-obstruction}.  If
$\Rpre(a)\neq\varnothing$ but a compatible family on a cylinder cover does not
come from any section of $\Rpre(a)$, then the failure is one of effective
descent.  If distinct sections of $\Rpre(a)$ induce the same compatible family
on a cover, then the failure is one of separatedness.  These are the three
standard internal failure modes for the existence and uniqueness of readout on
a presheaf over a fixed admissible object; admissibility itself is a standing
input condition for belonging to the site.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Binary prefix site and the three failure modes]
\label{ex:binary-prefix-site-failure-modes}
Let $X=\{0,1\}^{\NN}$, let $\Sigma=\{0,1\}$, let $\Aadm=\Sigma^{<\infty}$, and
let $C_a\subseteq X$ be the usual cylinder of sequences beginning with the word
$a$.  The root address $\varepsilon$ is covered by the two depth-one cylinders
$0\to \varepsilon$ and $1\to \varepsilon$.

Three explicit readout presheaves on this same binary prefix site realize the
three failure modes from Corollary~\ref{cor:null-trichotomy-local-section}.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item \emph{Empty fiber}: let $\Rpre_{\mathrm{emp}}(\varepsilon)=\varnothing$
and $\Rpre_{\mathrm{emp}}(b)=\{*\}$ for every nonempty word $b$, with the
unique possible restriction maps.  Readout fails immediately at the root.
\item \emph{Descent failure}: let $\Rpre_{\mathrm{desc}}(\varepsilon)=\{g\}$,
let $\Rpre_{\mathrm{desc}}(0)=\{x_0,y_0\}$ and
$\Rpre_{\mathrm{desc}}(1)=\{x_1,y_1\}$, and let every deeper cylinder below
$0$ or $1$ inherit the corresponding two-point set with identity restrictions.
The two restriction maps from $\varepsilon$ send $g$ to $x_0$ and $x_1$.
The pair overlap $C_0\cap C_1$ is empty, so every pair of local sections on the
cover is automatically compatible.  In particular, the family $(y_0,y_1)$ is
compatible on the cover
$\{0,1\to\varepsilon\}$ but does not descend to a global section.
\item \emph{Non-separatedness}: let
$\Rpre_{\mathrm{ns}}(\varepsilon)=\{u,v\}$ and
$\Rpre_{\mathrm{ns}}(b)=\{*\}$ for every nonempty word $b$, with both
restrictions from $\varepsilon$ constant.  Then $u\neq v$, but their
restrictions to the cover $\{0,1\to\varepsilon\}$ agree.
\end{enumerate}
This concrete binary site keeps the geometry fixed while isolating the three
internal presheaf-theoretic mechanisms.
\end{example}

\subsection{\v{C}ech-Type Gluing Obstructions}

The set-valued presheaf $\Rpre$ does not by itself produce group-valued
transition functions.  To speak about a \v{C}ech defect, one must add
comparison data for a chosen local readout problem.  The next definition makes
that extra structure explicit in a way that still sits over the same finite
cylinder cover; compare \cite{Bredon1997SheafTheory,Milne1980EtaleCohomology,
Tamme1994IntroductionEtaleCohomology,Vistoli2008Grothendieck}.

\begin{definition}[$A$-banded comparison datum and \v{C}ech defect]
\label{def:prefix-site-h2-gluing-obstruction}
Fix an admissible address $a\in\Aadm$, a cylinder cover $\{b_i\to a\}_{i=1}^n$,
and an abelian group $A$.  Suppose one is given local sections
$s_i\in \Rpre(b_i)$.  An \emph{$A$-banded comparison datum} for
$\{s_i\}_{i=1}^n$ consists of:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item for each nonempty pair overlap, an $A$-torsor
$\Comp_{ij}$ of admissible comparisons between the restrictions of $s_j$ and
$s_i$ to that overlap;
\item chosen comparison elements $\gamma_{ij}\in \Comp_{ij}$;
\item associative composition maps
$\Comp_{ij}\times \Comp_{jk}\to \Comp_{ik}$ on triple overlaps, equivariant for
the $A$-action.
\end{enumerate}
After choosing trivializations $\Comp_{ij}\cong A$ on the nonempty pair
overlaps, write $g_{ij}\in A$ for the coordinate of $\gamma_{ij}$.  The
associated \emph{\v{C}ech defect} is the $2$-cochain
\[
\alpha_{ijk}:=g_{ij}g_{jk}g_{ik}^{-1}\in A
\]
on triple overlaps $C_{b_i}\cap C_{b_j}\cap C_{b_k}\neq\varnothing$.  Its
cohomology class will be denoted by $[\alpha]$.  A different choice of
trivializations modifies the $g_{ij}$ by a $1$-cochain, hence changes
$(\alpha_{ijk})$ by a \v{C}ech coboundary.  Therefore $[\alpha]$ depends only
on the banded comparison datum.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Nontrivial \v{C}ech defect prevents coherent global readout]
\label{prop:null-as-h2-obstruction}
In the setting of Definition~\ref{def:prefix-site-h2-gluing-obstruction}, if
$[\alpha]\neq 0$, then there is no section $s\in \Rpre(a)$ whose restrictions
to the cover are the sections $\{s_i\}$ and whose induced comparison data agree
with the chosen banded comparison datum.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume such a global section $s\in\Rpre(a)$ exists.  On every pair overlap the
restrictions of $s$ induce a distinguished comparison between $s_j$ and $s_i$,
and on every triple overlap these induced comparisons compose coherently:
\[
\gamma_{ij}\circ \gamma_{jk}=\gamma_{ik}.
\]
In the chosen trivializations this says $g_{ij}g_{jk}=g_{ik}$, so every
$\alpha_{ijk}$ is trivial.  Hence $[\alpha]=0$, contrary to the assumption.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The comparison torsors are the bridge between the abstract set-valued readout
presheaf and the abelian-group calculations of Section~5.  Once such a banded
comparison datum has been fixed, the remaining statements of that section are
elementary algebra attached to the defect family $(\alpha_{ijk})$.
\end{remark}

\section{Minimal Visible Quotients and Quantitative Recovery}

Let $A$ be a finite abelian group, and let $[\alpha]$ be a \v{C}ech defect
class as above.  The visible content of the obstruction is determined by the
subgroup generated by the actual triple-overlap defects.  The next two results
are elementary group-theoretic consequences of that datum.

\begin{lemma}[Minimal visible quotient]
\label{prop:prefix-site-min-visible-quotient}
Define
\[
H_\alpha := \langle \alpha_{ijk}:\ C_{b_i}\cap C_{b_j}\cap C_{b_k}\neq\varnothing\rangle
\le A,
\qquad
\Avis := A/H_\alpha.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item the image of $\alpha$ in $\Avis$ vanishes pointwise, so the obstruction
becomes trivial after passing to $\Avis$;
\item if $\varphi:A\to B$ is any homomorphism such that $\varphi(\alpha_{ijk})=0$
for every nonempty triple overlap, then $\varphi$ factors uniquely through the
quotient map $A\twoheadrightarrow \Avis$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every generator $\alpha_{ijk}$ of $H_\alpha$ maps to zero in $\Avis$, so the
obstruction class becomes pointwise trivial after quotienting.

If $\varphi(\alpha_{ijk})=0$ for every triple overlap, then
$H_\alpha\subseteq \ker\varphi$.  By the universal property of the quotient,
$\varphi$ factors uniquely through $A/H_\alpha=\Avis$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Register lower bound for full-resolution recovery]
\label{thm:prefix-site-register-lower-bound}
Assume $A$ is finite.  Suppose one wishes to recover the full $A$-resolution
from the visible quotient $\Avis$ by adjoining a finite register set $R$ in
such a way that there exists an injection
\[
A\hookrightarrow \Avis\times R.
\]
Then
\[
\lvert R\rvert \ge \lvert H_\alpha\rvert.
\]
This bound is sharp: equality can be attained with $R=H_\alpha$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every fiber of the quotient map $A\twoheadrightarrow \Avis$ is a coset of
$H_\alpha$, hence has cardinality $\lvert H_\alpha\rvert$.  If
$A\hookrightarrow \Avis\times R$, then restricting the injection to any one of
these fibers yields an injection into $R$.  Therefore
\[
\lvert R\rvert\ge \lvert H_\alpha\rvert.
\]

For sharpness, take $R:=H_\alpha$.  Choose a set-theoretic section
$s:\Avis\to A$ of the quotient map.  Then every $a\in A$ can be written
uniquely as
\[
a = s(\bar a)h
\qquad
(\bar a\in\Avis,\ h\in H_\alpha),
\]
and the map $a\mapsto (\bar a,h)$ is injective.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Character-based spectral readout]
\label{def:character-based-spectral-readout}
Let $\chi\in \widehat A:=\Hom(A,\TT)$.  Applying $\chi$ to the comparison
coordinates $g_{ij}$ of an $A$-banded comparison datum produces
circle-valued multipliers $\chi(g_{ij})$ on the pair overlaps.  The resulting
$\TT$-valued descent problem is called the \emph{character-based spectral
readout} associated to $\chi$.  It is said to be \emph{spectrally definable} if
the pushed-forward multipliers satisfy the \v{C}ech descent condition on triple
overlaps, equivalently if the induced $\TT$-valued comparison datum descends to
a well-defined global circle-valued object on the cover.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Spectral definability occurs only on the visible quotient]
\label{thm:prefix-site-spectral-definability-avis}
Let $\widehat A:=\Hom(A,\TT)$ be the Pontryagin dual, and define the
annihilator
\[
H_\alpha^\perp
:=
\{\chi\in \widehat A:\ \chi(h)=1\ \text{for all }h\in H_\alpha\}.
\]
Then $H_\alpha^\perp\cong \widehat{\Avis}$, and the character-based spectral
readout of Definition~\ref{def:character-based-spectral-readout} is spectrally
definable if and only if the chosen character lies in $H_\alpha^\perp$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Because $A$ is finite abelian, a character of $A$ factors through the quotient
$A\twoheadrightarrow \Avis=A/H_\alpha$ if and only if it is trivial on
$H_\alpha$.  This identifies $\widehat{\Avis}$ with the subgroup
$H_\alpha^\perp\le \widehat A$.

Now let $\chi\in\widehat A$.  The pushed-forward triple-overlap defect is
$\chi(\alpha_{ijk})$.  The spectral readout is definable exactly when all of
these defects are trivial, or equivalently when $\chi$ kills every generator of
$H_\alpha$.  This is precisely the condition $\chi\in H_\alpha^\perp$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Binary prefix cover with nontrivial defect subgroup]
\label{ex:binary-prefix-site-defect-subgroup}
Return to the binary prefix site of
Example~\ref{ex:binary-prefix-site-failure-modes}, and let
$a=\varepsilon$.  Consider the cylinder cover
\[
b_1=0,\qquad b_2=00,\qquad b_3=000,\qquad b_4=1.
\]
This is indeed a cylinder cover of $\varepsilon$ because
$C_{b_1}\cup C_{b_4}=C_0\cup C_1=C_\varepsilon$.  Equip the site with the
terminal presheaf $\Rpre(b)=\{*\}$ for every address $b$, with identity
restrictions, and let $s_i=*$ be the unique local section on each $b_i$.
The nested chain
$C_{000}\subseteq C_{00}\subseteq C_0$
provides a nonempty triple overlap for $(b_1,b_2,b_3)$.  Let
$A=\ZZ/3\ZZ$, written additively, and fix an $A$-banded comparison datum whose comparison
coordinates on $C_{000}$ satisfy
\[
g_{12}=1,\qquad g_{23}=1,\qquad g_{13}=0.
\]
Take all remaining comparison coordinates on nonempty overlaps to be $0$.  Then
\[
\alpha_{123}=g_{12}+g_{23}-g_{13}=2,
\]
so $H_\alpha=\langle 2\rangle=A$ and therefore $\Avis=0$.  By
Lemma~\ref{thm:prefix-site-register-lower-bound}, every recovery register $R$
must satisfy $\lvert R\rvert\ge 3$, and equality is attained by $R=A$.  This
gives an explicit finite prefix-site example with nontrivial defect subgroup and
sharp register bound.
\end{example}

\section{Dyadic Inverse Limits and Separatedness}

\subsection{Stable Objects in the Dyadic Tower}

The next proposition records standard facts about inverse limits of finite
discrete spaces and the Cantor intersection argument; see
\cite{Johnstone1982StoneSpaces,RibesZalesskii2010ProfiniteGroups,
Wilson1998ProfiniteGroups,Munkres2000Topology,Bourbaki1989GeneralTopology}.

\begin{proposition}[Standard inverse-limit facts for dyadic towers]
\label{thm:recursive-addressing-dyadic-inverse-limit-stable-object}
Let $(\mathcal{A}_b,\pi_{b'\to b})_{b'\ge b\ge 0}$ be an inverse system of
finite nonempty sets with surjective bonding maps
\[
\pi_{b'\to b}:\mathcal{A}_{b'}\twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{A}_b
\]
satisfying the usual compatibility relations.  Define the inverse limit
\[
\Ainf
:=
\varprojlim_b \mathcal{A}_b
=
\left\{
(a_b)_{b\ge 0}\in \prod_{b\ge 0}\mathcal{A}_b:\ 
\pi_{b'\to b}(a_{b'})=a_b\ \text{for all }b'\ge b
\right\}.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item $\Ainf\neq\varnothing$;
\item the dyadic metric
\[
d_{\mathrm{dy}}(a,a')
:=
\begin{cases}
0,& a=a',\\[1mm]
2^{-b_\ast(a,a')},& a\neq a',
\end{cases}
\qquad
b_\ast(a,a')
:=
\max\{b\ge 0:\ a_b=a'_b\}
\]
makes $\Ainf$ into a compact, complete, totally disconnected ultrametric
space;
\item if $Y$ is a complete metric space and for each $a_b\in \mathcal{A}_b$ one
is given a nonempty closed set $F_b(a_b)\subseteq Y$ such that
\[
F_{b'}(a_{b'})\subseteq F_b(a_b)
\quad\text{whenever}\quad
\pi_{b'\to b}(a_{b'})=a_b
\]
and
\[
\diam\bigl(F_b(a_b)\bigr)\le C\,2^{-b}
\qquad
(\forall b,\ \forall a_b\in\mathcal{A}_b)
\]
for some constant $C>0$, then every $a=(a_b)_{b\ge 0}\in \Ainf$ determines a
unique stable object
\[
x(a)\in \bigcap_{b\ge 0} F_b(a_b).
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
For each $b\ge 0$, let
\[
E_b
:=
\left\{
(a_j)_{j\ge 0}\in\prod_{j\ge 0}\mathcal{A}_j:\ 
\pi_{j\to i}(a_j)=a_i\ \text{for all }b\ge j\ge i\ge 0
\right\}.
\]
Because each $\mathcal{A}_j$ is finite and nonempty, the product
$\prod_j \mathcal{A}_j$ is compact in the product topology, and each $E_b$ is
closed.  Surjectivity of the bonding maps guarantees that every finite
intersection $\bigcap_{r=0}^m E_r$ is nonempty: choose a compatible thread
$a_0,\dots,a_m$ recursively through level $m$ using surjectivity, then choose
arbitrary values in the remaining coordinates.  Compactness therefore gives
$\Ainf=\bigcap_{b\ge 0} E_b\neq\varnothing$.

The dyadic metric is the standard prefix ultrametric.  Compactness of $\Ainf$
follows again from the compact product representation.  Completeness is
obtained by coordinatewise stabilization of Cauchy sequences, and total
disconnectedness follows because the basic cylinder neighborhoods
\[
U_b(a):=\{a'\in\Ainf:\ a'_b=a_b\}
\]
are clopen and form a basis.

For the final statement, fix $a=(a_b)\in\Ainf$.  The family
$\{F_b(a_b)\}_{b\ge 0}$ is a nested sequence of nonempty closed subsets of $Y$
with diameters tending to zero.  Choose $x_b\in F_b(a_b)$.  The diameter bound
makes $(x_b)$ a Cauchy sequence, hence it converges to some $x\in Y$ because
$Y$ is complete.  Since each $F_b(a_b)$ is closed and contains all sufficiently
deep $x_{b'}$, it follows that $x\in F_b(a_b)$ for every $b$.  Uniqueness
follows from
\[
d_Y(x,y)\le \diam(F_b(a_b))\le C2^{-b}
\qquad
(\forall b),
\]
which forces $x=y$ as $b\to\infty$.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Dyadic intervals]
\label{ex:dyadic-intervals}
Take $\mathcal{A}_b=\{0,1\}^b$ with the bonding maps that delete the last
digit.  Let $Y=[0,1]$, and for
$a_b=(\varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_b)\in\{0,1\}^b$ define
\[
F_b(a_b):=
\left[
\sum_{j=1}^b \varepsilon_j2^{-j},
\sum_{j=1}^b \varepsilon_j2^{-j}+2^{-b}
\right].
\]
Then $F_{b'}(a_{b'})\subseteq F_b(a_b)$ whenever
$\pi_{b'\to b}(a_{b'})=a_b$, and
$\diam(F_b(a_b))=2^{-b}$.  Theorem~\ref{thm:recursive-addressing-dyadic-inverse-limit-stable-object} therefore
recovers the familiar fact that every compatible binary thread determines a
unique point of $[0,1]$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Single-Valued Readout and Separatedness}

Fix $a=(a_b)_{b\ge 0}\in \Ainf$ and write
\[
U_b(a):=\{a'\in\Ainf:\ a'_b=a_b\}.
\]
These sets form the dyadic neighborhood basis of $a$.  If
$c\in\mathcal{A}_n$ satisfies $\pi_{n\to b}(c)=a_b$, write
\[
U(c):=\{a'\in\Ainf:\ a'_n=c\}\subseteq U_b(a).
\]
A \emph{finite dyadic cylinder cover} of $U_b(a)$ is a finite family
\[
\{U(c_i)\to U_b(a)\}_{i=1}^r
\]
with $c_i\in\mathcal{A}_{n_i}$, $\pi_{n_i\to b}(c_i)=a_b$, and
\[
U_b(a)=\bigcup_{i=1}^r U(c_i).
\]
We identify each $U_b(a)$ with the finite address $a_b$ and each $U(c)$ with
the finite address $c$, and therefore write
\[
\Rpre(U_b(a)):=\Rpre(a_b).
\]
Likewise, for such a cylinder $U(c)$ we write $\Rpre(U(c)):=\Rpre(c)$.

\begin{theorem}[Separatedness criterion for single-valued readout]
\label{thm:recursive-addressing-readout-separatedness-null}
For a stable address $a\in\Ainf$, the following are equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item Readout at $a$ is single-valued: for every $b\ge 0$, every finite dyadic
cylinder cover
\[
\{U(c_i)\to U_b(a)\}_{i=1}^r,
\]
and every pair of sections $s,t\in \Rpre(U_b(a))$, if
\[
\rho_{a_b\leftarrow c_i}(s)=\rho_{a_b\leftarrow c_i}(t)
\qquad
(1\le i\le r),
\]
then $s=t$.
\item The presheaf $\Rpre$ is separated along the dyadic neighborhood basis of
$a$: for every $b\ge 0$ and every finite dyadic cylinder cover
\[
\{U(c_i)\to U_b(a)\}_{i=1}^r,
\]
the canonical map
\[
\Rpre(U_b(a))\longrightarrow \prod_{i=1}^r \Rpre(U(c_i))
\]
is injective.
\end{enumerate}

Moreover, failure of a well-defined readout at $a$ has three internal forms:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \emph{empty local fiber}: $\Rpre(U_b(a))=\varnothing$ for some sufficiently
deep neighborhood;
\item \emph{descent failure}: compatible local sections on a finite dyadic
cylinder cover of some $U_b(a)$ do not glue to a section over $U_b(a)$;
\item \emph{non-separatedness}: two distinct sections over some $U_b(a)$ have
identical restrictions to a finite dyadic cylinder cover of $U_b(a)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For fixed $b$ and a fixed finite dyadic cylinder cover
$\{U(c_i)\to U_b(a)\}_{i=1}^r$, condition \textup{(1)} says exactly that the
map
\[
\Rpre(U_b(a))\longrightarrow \prod_{i=1}^r \Rpre(U(c_i))
\]
sends no two distinct sections to the same tuple of restrictions.  That is the
injectivity asserted in \textup{(2)}.  Thus \textup{(1)} and \textup{(2)} are
identical formulations of separatedness along the dyadic neighborhood basis of
$a$; compare \cite{MacLaneMoerdijk1992,Vistoli2008Grothendieck}.

A well-defined readout at $a$ requires both the local existence condition
$\Rpre(U_b(a))\neq\varnothing$ on sufficiently deep neighborhoods and the
separatedness condition above.  If some sufficiently deep neighborhood has
empty fiber, then readout fails by absence of local data.  If local sections
exist on a finite dyadic cylinder cover but do not glue, then the failure is
one of effective descent.  Finally, if two distinct sections over $U_b(a)$
restrict to the same family on a finite dyadic cylinder cover, then finite
observations do not distinguish them, so uniqueness fails.  Conversely, any
failure of well-defined readout at $a$ must occur through one of these three
mechanisms: no local object, no descent, or no separatedness.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion}

The paper isolates one clean categorical chain:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{recursive refinement of visible events}
&\Longrightarrow \text{prefix site and readout presheaf} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{local-section, descent, and separatedness obstructions} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{minimal visible quotient and register bound} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{inverse-limit stability and separatedness.}
\end{aligned}
\]
This is the correct boundary for a categorical account of recursive addressing.
The opening
measurable proposition keeps the paper anchored to observable dynamics, but the
main payoffs are site-theoretic and descent-theoretic.  In particular, the
paper explains how recursive addressing can remain conservative at the level of
visible $\sigma$-algebras while still exhibiting nontrivial internal failure
modes.

From the categorical side, the prefix site is a finitary coverage in the sense
of Johnstone, and the local-section, descent, and separatedness statements of
Sections~4 and~6 are precisely the usual presheaf and sheaf conditions
specialized to cylinder covers
\cite{SGA4I,Johnstone2002Elephant,Vistoli2008Grothendieck}.  From the dynamical
side, the basic geometry is the familiar symbolic-dynamical cylinder geometry
of prefix codes and shift spaces
\cite{WaltersErgodicTheory,LindMarcus1995SymbolicDynamics,
Kitchens1998SymbolicDynamics}.  The inverse-limit step is likewise standard
profinite compactness
\cite{Johnstone1982StoneSpaces,RibesZalesskii2010ProfiniteGroups,
Wilson1998ProfiniteGroups}.  The point of the paper is therefore not to claim
new general theorems in these areas, but to isolate the recursive-addressing
framework at the place where these standard tools meet.

This also clarifies the present limitation.  The \v{C}ech defect of
Sections~4.2 and~5 is not attached to an arbitrary set-valued presheaf
$\Rpre$; it requires additional $A$-banded comparison data on a chosen finite
cover.  Producing that banded datum functorially from a concrete readout
semantics is the next mathematical step, and it would naturally lead toward
stack-theoretic or gerbe-theoretic refinements.  Likewise, the register lower
bound is elementary once the defect subgroup $H_\alpha$ has been identified, so
the substantive problem is to compute $H_\alpha$ in examples where the prefix
geometry forces nontrivial comparison defects.

The examples in Sections~4--6 suggest two concrete directions.  The first is to
classify recursive-addressing models that carry canonical banded comparison
data.  The second is to determine which prefix-site geometries force empty
fibers, descent failure, or non-separatedness in a way that does not collapse
to generic presheaf pathology.  Those are the directions in which the present
reorganization of standard machinery could develop into genuinely new
categorical results.
