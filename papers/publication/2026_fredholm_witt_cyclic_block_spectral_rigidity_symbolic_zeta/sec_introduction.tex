\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

For a primitive finite matrix \(A\), the dynamical \(\zeta\)-function
\[
  \zeta_A(z)=\det(I-zA)^{-1}
\]
packages periodic-orbit data, transfer-operator traces, and non-Perron
spectral data into one holomorphic object. In symbolic dynamics this
identity is classical and can be viewed as the finite-state shadow of a
broader Fredholm determinant identity valid for general trace-class
operators.

The sole new contribution of this note is
\Cref{thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation} (the cyclic-block
characterization theorem): we give a complete characterization of when
an abstract Euler product admits a normal trace-class realisation by
cyclic blocks, and we show that within the normal class this
cyclic-block geometry is forced and the non-zero support is minimal.
The paper does not address non-normal trace-class realisability, and all
occurrences of ``realisation,'' ``necessary,'' ``optimal,'' and
``forced'' in Sections~2--3 refer to the normal trace-class category
unless explicitly stated otherwise.

The Fredholm--Witt bridge
(\Cref{thm:fredholm-witt-bridge}) and the spectral rigidity statement
(\Cref{thm:fredholm-spectral-rigidity}) are classical results recorded
here to fix notation and to make the cyclic-block theorem usable in
symbolic applications: the former turns periodic data into determinants,
and the latter turns determinant identities back into spectral
information. \Cref{sec:perturbation} collects determinant-level
applications in symbolic dynamics; these repackage standard
perturbative and spectral-gap consequences at the determinant level and
are included for context and illustration, not as additional claims of
structural novelty.

\subsection{Main results}

We begin by recording the classical bridge in the form used throughout
the paper; this is not new and is stated here only to fix notation.
\begin{proposition}[Connecting Fredholm--Witt identity]
  \label{prop:intro-fredholm-witt}
  Let \(T\in \sone(H)\) be a trace-class operator on a complex Hilbert
  space. Define
  \[
    a_n(T):=\Tr(T^n),
    \qquad
    p_n(T):=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)\,a_{n/d}(T)
    \qquad (n\ge 1).
  \]
  Then for every \(\abs{z}<\norm{T}^{-1}\),
  \[
    \det(I-zT)^{-1}
    =\exp\!\left(\sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{a_n(T)}{n}z^n\right)
    =\prod_{n\ge 1}(1-z^n)^{-p_n(T)}.
  \]
\end{proposition}

The following is the only new theorem in this paper. It strengthens the
converse realization statement by adding necessity and optimality in the
normal trace-class category.
\begin{theorem}[Cyclic-block characterization and optimality]
  \label{thm:intro-cyclic-characterization}
  Let \(\{(n_j,\beta_j,m_j)\}_{j\in J}\) be a countable family with
  \(n_j,m_j\in\NN\), \(\beta_j\in\CC\setminus\{0\}\), and
  \[
    \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty.
  \]
  Then the Euler product
  \[
    \prod_{j\in J}(1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{-m_j}
  \]
  is the Fredholm determinant of a trace-class normal operator if and
  only if, after adjoining an arbitrary zero summand, that operator is
  unitarily equivalent to
  \[
    \bigoplus_{j\in J}\bigl(C(n_j,\alpha_j)\otimes I_{m_j}\bigr),
    \qquad \alpha_j^{n_j}=\beta_j.
  \]
  Consequently every trace-class normal realization has non-zero
  spectral multiplicity
  \(
    \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j
  \)
  and trace norm
  \(
    \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}
  \).
  In particular, each factor \((1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{-m_j}\) requires
  exactly \(m_j n_j\) non-zero dimensions. These counts are attached to
  the chosen Euler data \(\mathcal D=\{(n_j,\beta_j,m_j)\}\); distinct
  but equivalent factorizations of the same Euler product produce
  unitarily equivalent normal models.
\end{theorem}

Once the determinant is fixed, the non-zero spectrum is fixed as well.
The next statement is classical (it follows directly from
\cite[Theorem~3.7]{Simon2005TraceIdeals}) but is recorded here because
it is the mechanism that turns determinant identities into rigidity.
\begin{proposition}[Spectral rigidity]
  \label{prop:intro-spectral-rigidity}
  Let \(T_1,T_2\in \sone\). If
  \[
    \det(I-zT_1)=\det(I-zT_2)
  \]
  in a neighbourhood of \(z=0\), then \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) have the same
  non-zero eigenvalues with algebraic multiplicity.
\end{proposition}

\Cref{sec:perturbation} collects applications and examples showing how
the determinant viewpoint organizes standard perturbative and
spectral-gap consequences. These are included for context and
illustration; none of the results in that section are new.
Statements are given with proof sketches; full proofs of the
perturbative propositions appear in \Cref{sec:appendix-proofs}.

\subsection{Previous work}

The rationality of symbolic dynamical \(\zeta\)-functions goes back to
Bowen--Lanford and Manning
\cite{BowenLanford1970Zeta,Manning1971Axiom}; see also
\cite{Ruelle1976ZetaExpanding,Ruelle1978Thermodynamic,ParryPollicott1990Zeta,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}.
The theory of Fredholm determinants for trace-class operators, and more
generally for operators in trace ideals, originates with Grothendieck
\cite{Grothendieck1956}, is extended to the nuclear setting by Ruelle
\cite{Ruelle1990Extension}, and is developed systematically in Simon
\cite{Simon2005TraceIdeals}; see also the comprehensive treatment of
traces and determinants for general linear operators in Gohberg,
Goldberg, and Krupnik \cite{GohbergGoldbergKrupnik2000}. The product
representation for trace-class Fredholm determinants that underlies the
spectral rigidity discussion is
\cite[Theorem~3.7]{Simon2005TraceIdeals}.

The operator-theoretic approach to dynamical zeta-functions via
Fredholm determinants of transfer operators was developed by Pollicott
\cite{Pollicott1986Meromorphic} in the Axiom~A setting, and the
Fredholm theory of dynamical determinants for piecewise invertible
systems in the trace-class sense was carried out by Keller
\cite{Keller1989Fredholm}. For the broader theory of dynamical
determinants beyond the finite-state symbolic setting, including
hyperbolic diffeomorphisms and piecewise expanding maps, see Baladi and
Tsujii \cite{BaladiTsujii2007} and Baladi
\cite{Baladi2018DynZeta}. The perturbative arguments in the symbolic
applications draw on transfer-operator spectral theory in the sense of
Ruelle and Baladi
\cite{Ruelle1969,Ruelle1978Thermodynamic,Baladi2000PositiveTransfer}.
For finite-state spectral-gap CLT, cumulants, and perturbative
regularity of eigenvalues, we also follow the standard finite-dimensional
framework described in
\cite[Chapter~5]{Ruelle1978Thermodynamic},
\cite[Chapter~3]{Baladi2000PositiveTransfer},
\cite[Chapters~I and~IV]{HennionHerve2001},
and \cite[Chapter~II]{Kato1995Perturbation}.
The spectral-gap approach to limit theorems for Markov chains was
initiated by Nagaev \cite{Nagaev1957} and extended to the dynamical
setting by Guivarc'h and Hardy \cite{GuivarchHardy1988}; for broader
stability results for transfer-operator spectra, see also
\cite[Theorem~1 and Corollary~1]{KellerLiverani1999}. For group inverses
and reduced resolvents we use standard matrix-analytic material from
\cite{CampbellMeyer1979}, while the analytic perturbation theory for
isolated eigenvalues is taken from Kato \cite{Kato1995Perturbation}.
The finite-state Liv\v{s}ic-type coboundary criterion used in the
off-origin Fourier estimate is cited in the form of Parry's cocycle
theorem \cite[Theorem~1]{Parry1992Cocycle}. For the parameter
regularity of holomorphic families of dynamical determinants beyond the
finite-dimensional setting, see J\'ez\'equel \cite{Jezequel2017Parameter}.

The present note does not claim a new symbolic model, a new Fredholm
identity, or a new spectral-gap limit theorem. The sole new
contribution is the strengthened cyclic-block theorem of
\Cref{thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation}: sufficiency, necessity in the
normal trace-class class, and support-optimality of the resulting
non-zero block size. The remaining operator statements are classical
connective input or determinant-level applications, included to place
the cyclic-block theorem in the right framework and to illustrate how
the determinant package interfaces with standard symbolic perturbation
theory.

\subsection{Relation to classical determinant and spectral theory}
\label{sec:positioning}

Because the cyclic-block characterization
(\Cref{thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation}) combines ingredients that are
individually classical, it is important to separate what is already
known from what is new.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
  \item \textbf{What Simon gives.}
    For any trace-class operator \(T\), the Fredholm determinant
    \(z\mapsto\det(I-zT)\) is an entire function of order at most~\(1\)
    whose zeros are exactly the reciprocals of the non-zero eigenvalues,
    counted with algebraic multiplicity
    \cite[Theorem~3.7]{Simon2005TraceIdeals}. This canonical zero
    description pins down the non-zero spectrum from the determinant, but
    it says nothing about the operator's internal block structure.

  \item \textbf{What the spectral theorem gives for normal operators.}
    If \(T\) is additionally normal, the spectral theorem decomposes
    \(T\) into scalar multiples of orthogonal projectors. This
    diagonalises \(T\) and determines it up to unitary equivalence from
    its eigenvalues and multiplicities.

  \item \textbf{What is new here.}
    \Cref{thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation} combines the two observations
    above with a constructive packaging step: it shows that when the
    determinant has the specific Euler-product form
    \(\prod_j(1-\beta_jz^{n_j})^{-m_j}\), the spectral-theorem
    decomposition of \emph{every} normal trace-class realisation must
    organize into cyclic blocks \(C(n_j,\alpha_j)\otimes I_{m_j}\) tied
    to the Euler factors, and the non-zero support count
    \(\sum_j m_jn_j\) is forced. Neither Simon's zero description alone
    nor the spectral theorem alone yields this exact cyclic-block
    packaging or the support-optimality statement for a single Euler
    factor \((1-\beta z^n)^{-m}\).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}[Non-normal realisability is not addressed]
  \label{rem:non-normal-not-addressed}
  All characterization and optimality statements in this paper concern
  the normal trace-class category exclusively. Whether a given Euler
  product admits a non-normal trace-class realisation with fewer non-zero
  dimensions, or under a weaker summability condition, remains open. In
  particular, the term ``necessary'' in
  \Cref{thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation} means necessary among normal
  realisations, not among all trace-class operators.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Organisation}

\Cref{sec:preliminaries} recalls the finite-state \(\zeta\)-function,
primitive orbit counts, and the finite-part constants used later.
\Cref{sec:fredholm} contains the Fredholm--Witt bridge, the
cyclic-block characterization theorem (the main new result), its
immediate support-optimality corollary, and the classical determinant
rigidity statement. \Cref{sec:perturbation} collects applications and
examples: finite-part variation and truncation, twisted-determinant
moment and cumulant rigidity, off-origin decay, and explicit
computations for the golden-mean family and the full two-shift; proofs
of the perturbative propositions are deferred to
\Cref{sec:appendix-proofs}. \Cref{sec:conclusion} closes with the
logical status of the main ingredients and the scope of the
operator-theoretic package.
