\section{Further remarks}\label{sec:conclusion}

The three operator-theoretic statements proved here have different
logical status. The Fredholm--Witt bridge is the connective identity
that places traces, Witt coordinates, and determinants in one common
language. The genuinely new input is the sharp characterization theorem:
an Euler product admits a trace-class block-cyclic normal realisation if
and only if the trace-class summability threshold
\(
  \sum_j m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty
\)
holds, and in that case the canonical cyclic-block model is already
trace-norm optimal. Spectral characterization then shows that this extra
operator-theoretic freedom is geometric rather than spectral: once the
determinant is fixed, both the non-zero spectrum and the minimal
trace-class size are fixed.

For symbolic dynamics, the payoff is correspondingly sharper than a
repackaging of standard perturbation theory. The determinant does not
merely encode periodic-point counts; it also determines the finite-part
invariants \(C(A)\) and \(\MM(A)\), remains rigid under
determinant-preserving deformations, and yields a uniform zero-free disc
for twisted determinants away from the central frequency. This zero-free
gap produces exponential cancellation in weighted periodic-orbit sums
and the off-origin characteristic-function decay used in the spectral-gap
CLT. In this sense, the symbolic applications are genuinely consequences
of the Fredholm package, not parallel finite-dimensional calculations.

Several natural extensions merit further investigation. First, the
cyclic-block construction should have an analogue in Banach-space
nuclear settings, but that would require a concrete operator class and a
non-Hilbertian trace framework. Second, one expects analogous
determinant-rigidity statements for Axiom~A flows via symbolic
suspension models, although a full treatment of the flow setting remains
outside the scope of this paper. Third, quantitative refinements of the
CLT, such as Berry--Esseen bounds or moderate deviations, should be
accessible by combining the zero-free determinant gap of
\Cref{prop:twisted-zero-free} with higher-order perturbation expansions
of the dominant eigenvalue.
