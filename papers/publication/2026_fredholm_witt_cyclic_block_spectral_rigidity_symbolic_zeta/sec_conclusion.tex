\section{Further remarks}\label{sec:conclusion}

The three operator-theoretic statements proved here have different
logical status. The Fredholm--Witt bridge is the connective identity
that places traces, Witt coordinates, and determinants in one common
language. The genuinely new input is the sharp characterization theorem:
an Euler product admits a trace-class block-cyclic normal realisation if
and only if the trace-class summability threshold
\(
  \sum_j m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty
\)
holds, and in that case the canonical cyclic-block model is already
trace-norm and support optimal. Spectral rigidity then shows that this
extra operator-theoretic freedom is geometric rather than spectral:
once the determinant is fixed, both the non-zero spectrum and the
minimal non-zero size are fixed.

For symbolic dynamics, the payoff is correspondingly sharper than a mere
repackaging of standard perturbation theory. The determinant does not
merely encode periodic-point counts; it also yields group-inverse and
secondary-Fredholm formulas for \(C(A)\) and \(\MM(A)\), gives an
exponentially convergent truncation scheme for the Abel finite part, and
rigidly controls weighted orbit moments and asymptotic cumulants through
the two-variable twisted determinant. The explicit golden-mean and full
two-shift computations in \Cref{rem:example-golden-mean,rem:example-full-two-shift}
show that these are concrete computational consequences, not only
formal identities. This is the gap with ordinary finite-dimensional
perturbation theory highlighted in \Cref{sec:perturbation}: the
Fredholm package remembers the invariant determinant object across
different symbolic presentations.

Several natural extensions merit further investigation. First, the
cyclic-block construction should have an analogue in Banach-space
nuclear settings, but that would require a concrete operator class and a
non-Hilbertian trace framework. Second, one expects analogous
determinant-rigidity statements for Axiom~A flows via symbolic
suspension models, although a full treatment of the flow setting remains
outside the scope of this paper. Third, quantitative refinements of the
CLT, such as Berry--Esseen bounds or moderate deviations, should be
accessible by combining \Cref{thm:twisted-determinant-rigidity,thm:clt-spectral}
with higher-order perturbation expansions of the dominant eigenvalue.
