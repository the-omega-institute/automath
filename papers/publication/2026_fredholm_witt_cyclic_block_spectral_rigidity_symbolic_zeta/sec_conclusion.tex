\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

The paper is best read as a trace-ideal classification note for
determinant data. Its exact realization statements concern the normal
class: \Cref{thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation,thm:schatten-regularized}
characterize precisely when the prescribed Euler products admit normal
trace-class or \(\mathcal S_p\)-realizations and identify the canonical
block-cyclic model. Once that model has been fixed, the determinant
constrains the full realization class much more strongly than the trace
sequence alone: it forces the non-zero eigenvalue multiset, yields the
sharp trace/Schatten norm floor, and determines the Jordan geometry
away from \(0\) up to the unrestricted quasinilpotent sector described
in \Cref{rem:zero-spectrum-freedom}.

This scope is narrower than the inverse spectral theories surveyed in
\cite{PoschelTrubowitz1987,Marchenko2011}, where one typically prescribes
Weyl data, norming constants, or spectral measures, and it is likewise
narrower than the Banach-space dynamical determinant framework of
\cite{Ruelle1990Extension,Pollicott1986Meromorphic,Baladi2018DynZeta}.
Within that narrower setting, however, the paper isolates exactly what a
single determinant can still recover: complete non-zero spectral data,
the associated extremal geometry, and in the Schatten case the coercive
estimate of \Cref{thm:schatten-quantitative-stability}, which promotes
the norm inequality to quantitative stability of the normal block-cyclic
orbit.

The symbolic-dynamical input is therefore auxiliary rather than
structural. It explains the Euler products appearing at the start, but
all later arguments are carried out in Hilbert trace and Schatten
classes and should be understood as statements about determinant
constraints on compact operators.
