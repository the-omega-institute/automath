\section{Further remarks}\label{sec:conclusion}

The three operator-theoretic statements proved here have different
logical status. The Fredholm--Witt bridge is formal once trace-class
determinants are available. The cyclic-block theorem is an existence
result showing that Euler products admit far more realisations than the
finite-state category would suggest. Spectral rigidity then shows that
this additional freedom is geometric rather than spectral: once the
determinant is fixed, the non-zero spectrum is fixed as well.

For symbolic dynamics, the practical consequence is that several
standard perturbative arguments may be organised entirely at the level
of reduced determinants and resolvents. This is the reason that the
finite-part variation formula and the spectral-gap central limit theorem
fit naturally into the same short note.

Several natural extensions merit investigation. First, the cyclic-block
construction applies verbatim to nuclear operators on Banach spaces,
where the Grothendieck--Fredholm determinant theory
\cite{Grothendieck1956,Ruelle1990Extension} provides the analytic
foundation; the spectral rigidity statement carries over whenever the
Fredholm determinant is entire of finite order. Second, for Axiom~A
flows the symbolic coding produces a suspended shift whose
\(\zeta\)-function involves a continuous roof function
\cite{Pollicott1986Meromorphic}; the existence-versus-rigidity picture
developed here should extend to the associated Ruelle operators, though
the trace-class condition must be replaced by nuclearity in a suitable
sense. Third, quantitative refinements of the CLT---such as Berry--Esseen
rates or moderate-deviation principles---could be obtained by combining
the spectral-gap mechanism of
\Cref{thm:clt-spectral} with higher-order perturbation
expansions of the dominant eigenvalue.
