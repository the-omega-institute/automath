
\section{State diagram, timing convention, and carryover rule}\label{app:state-diagram}

The counter has two hidden states $R$ (ready) and $D$ (refractory/dead). The visible output is produced by the interval click register described in Appendix~\ref{app:gkls}, not by a direct measurement of the hidden state. The state diagram and timing convention for one sampling interval $[0,\Delta\tau]$ are as follows.

To remove any ambiguity in the sampling protocol, we distinguish three moments in each interval:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),itemsep=0.15em]
\item immediately after the interval-end readout at time $n\Delta\tau^+$, which resets the click register and sets the hidden state for interval $n$;
\item during the open interval $(n\Delta\tau,(n+1)\Delta\tau)$, where recovery and excitation can occur;
\item immediately before the next readout at time $(n+1)\Delta\tau^-$, whose hidden state is then carried into interval $n+1$ unless a click occurred.
\end{enumerate}
The carryover rule is the following: a click in interval $n$ forces interval $n+1$ to start in $D$; a no-click does \emph{not} reset the detector to $R$, but instead carries the hidden end-of-interval state into the next interval.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
  state/.style={draw, circle, minimum size=1.2cm, thick},
  arr/.style={-Latex, thick},
  every node/.style={font=\small}
]
\node[state, fill=green!12] (R) at (0,0) {$R$};
\node[state, fill=red!12] (D) at (4,0) {$D$};

\draw[arr] (R) to[bend left=25] node[above] {excite at rate $\Gamma$} node[below] {output $A_n=1$} (D);
\draw[arr] (R) to[loop left, looseness=8] node[left] {no excitation} node[right,xshift=-0.3cm,yshift=-0.5cm] {$A_n=0$} (R);
\draw[arr] (D) to[bend left=25] node[below] {recover at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$} (R);
\draw[arr] (D) to[loop right, looseness=8] node[right] {no recovery} node[left,xshift=0.3cm,yshift=-0.5cm] {$A_n=0$} (D);

\node[anchor=north, text width=7cm, align=left] at (2,-2.2) {
\textbf{Key rule.} Every click ($A_n=1$) leaves the detector in state $D$ at the start of interval $n+1$, regardless of the hidden state at the start of interval $n$. This is the single assumption that makes click times regeneration points.
};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\noindent\textbf{Timing within one interval.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),itemsep=0.15em]
\item \emph{Start in $R$:} The detector may be excited during $[0,\Delta\tau]$ at rate $\Gamma$. If excited (probability $1-p$, with $p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}$), it ends in $D$ and emits $A_n=1$. If not excited (probability $p$), it ends in $R$ and emits $A_n=0$.
\item \emph{Start in $D$:} Recovery to $R$ occurs at an exponential time $s$ with rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. If $s\le\Delta\tau$, the detector is ready for the remaining subinterval $[s,\Delta\tau]$ and may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited after recovery, it ends in $D$ with $A_n=1$ (probability $a$). If not excited after recovery, it ends in $R$ with $A_n=0$ (probability $b$). If $s>\Delta\tau$ (no recovery during the interval), it remains in $D$ with $A_n=0$ (probability $c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}$). Here $a+b=1-c$, so $a+b+c=1$.
\end{enumerate}

The output-labelled kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ in~\eqref{eq:t0-t1-main} encode precisely these transition-and-output probabilities. Note that the transition $(D,D)$ can produce either output: $A_n=0$ with probability $c$ (no recovery) or $A_n=1$ with probability $a$ (recovery followed by re-excitation). This is why the output is \emph{not} a deterministic function of the hidden-state pair $(H_n,H_{n+1})$, a point exploited in the factorization proof of $1$-dependence in \cref{sec:records}.

\section{Interval click-register instrument for the phenomenological kernels}\label{app:gkls}

This appendix fixes the status of the model used in the main text. We do \emph{not} claim a microscopic reduction from a bare UDW detector to the sampled counter throughout the full regime explored in Sections~4--5. Instead, the UDW detector supplies the stationary excitation input rate $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)$, while dead time, recovery, and interval readout are assigned to separate counter electronics. The resulting sampled counter admits an explicit interval instrument, which is all that is required for the record-level analysis of Section~\ref{sec:records}.

Let the hidden counter state be $H\in\{R,D\}$ and the click register be $C\in\{0,1\}$. At the beginning of each interval the register is reset to $C=0$. During the interval, the combined hidden/register process has three relevant states,
\[
(R,0),\qquad (D,0),\qquad (D,1),
\]
where $(D,1)$ means that the register has already fired and is then held until the interval-end readout. In the ordered basis $((R,0),(D,0),(D,1))$, the interval generator is the classical Markov generator
\begin{equation}\label{eq:register-generator}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ctr}}=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\Gamma & \kappa_{\mathrm r} & 0\\
0 & -\kappa_{\mathrm r} & 0\\
\Gamma & 0 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The transition $(R,0)\to(D,1)$ represents a click. The transition $(D,0)\to(R,0)$ represents recovery. Once the register has fired, the state $(D,1)$ is absorbing until the interval-end measurement.

Let
\[
J=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0\\
0 & 1\\
0 & 0
\end{pmatrix}
\]
embed the hidden-state distribution at the beginning of the interval into the combined hidden/register space, and let
\[
M_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0\\
0 & 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
M_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 0 & 0\\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\]
denote the interval-end readout maps: outcome $0$ keeps the hidden state reached with register value $0$, while outcome $1$ resets the next interval to the refractory state $D$. The output-labelled instrument is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:instrument-main}
\mathcal I_0=M_0e^{\Delta\tau\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ctr}}}J,
\qquad
\mathcal I_1=M_1e^{\Delta\tau\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ctr}}}J.
\end{equation}

A direct evaluation of~\eqref{eq:instrument-main} gives
\[
e^{\Delta\tau\mathcal L_{\mathrm{ctr}}}J=
\begin{pmatrix}
p & b\\
0 & c\\
1-p & a
\end{pmatrix},
\]
where
\[
p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau},
\qquad
c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\]
and
\[
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds,
\qquad
b=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\]
Hence
\[
T_0=\mathcal I_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
p & 0\\
b & c
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=\mathcal I_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1-p\\
0 & a
\end{pmatrix},
\]
which are exactly the kernels used in~\eqref{eq:t0-t1-main}. The construction is consistent for all positive $\Gamma\Delta\tau$ and $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$; no assumption such as $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\ll1$ is used. This is the interval instrument implicitly underlying the main text.

\section{Key derivations}\label{app:derivations}

This appendix collects the proofs of the main technical results. Complete details for all intermediate steps appear in the Supplementary Material.

\subsection{Zero-run law and hazard monotonicity}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:recovery-main}: zero-run law]
Every observed click places the detector in the refractory state $D$ for the next interval, independently of the earlier history. Hence the post-click hidden state is deterministic, and the strong Markov property of the hidden two-state chain makes click times regeneration times. The resulting zero-run lengths are therefore i.i.d., and a gap of length $k$ consists of $k$ successive output-$0$ transitions followed by one output-$1$ transition, giving~\eqref{eq:gap-main}.

To evaluate it explicitly, note that $T_0$ is lower triangular with eigenvalues $p$ and $c$. Direct integration of~\eqref{eq:a-b-main} yields, for $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$,
\[
a=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\qquad
b=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\]
Since $T_0$ is lower triangular,
\[
T_0^k=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p^k & 0 \\
 b\,\dfrac{p^k-c^k}{p-c} & c^k
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:gap-main} with $e_D=(0,1)^\top$ and $T_1\mathbf{1}=(1-p,\,a)^\top$ gives
\[
g_k = e_D^\top T_0^k T_1\mathbf{1}
= qb\frac{p^k-c^k}{p-c}+ac^k
= \frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\]
where $q=1-p$ and the simplification uses the explicit forms of $a$ and $b$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:recovery-main}: hazard monotonicity]
The tail of the gap law is
\[
\sum_{j\ge k}g_j=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\]
Therefore
\[
h_k = \frac{g_k}{\sum_{j\ge k}g_j}
= \frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k}.
\]
Direct subtraction yields
\[
h_{k+1}-h_k = \frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(p-c)^2p^kc^k}{(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^{k+1}-\Gamma c^{k+1})} > 0,
\]
since all factors in the numerator and denominator are strictly positive for $p\neq c$ (i.e.\ $\Gamma\neq\kappa_{\mathrm r}$). Strict monotonicity excludes the possibility that the visible process is an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$: if it were, $h_k$ would eventually be constant.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Covariance formula, exact 1-dependence, and edge cases}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{lem:stationary-main}]
The aggregate hidden-state matrix is
\[
P=T_0+T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 1-p\\
 b & 1-b
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Solving $\pi P=\pi$ with $\pi_R+\pi_D=1$ gives
\[
\pi_R=\frac{b}{1-p+b},
\qquad
\pi_D=\frac{1-p}{1-p+b}.
\]
The stationary click rate is therefore
\[
\rho=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a
=
\frac{b(1-p)+(1-p)a}{1-p+b}
=
\frac{(1-p)(a+b)}{1-p+b}
=
\frac{(1-p)(1-c)}{1-p+b}.
\]
Finally,
\[
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds
<
(1-p)\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\,ds
=(1-p)(1-c)
<
1-p.
\]
Since $\rho=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a$ is a strict convex combination of $a$ and $1-p$, one gets $a<\rho<1-p$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:recovery-main}: covariance formula]
The aggregate transition matrix is
\[
P = T_0+T_1 =
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 1-p\\
 b & 1-b
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Its eigenvalues are $1$ (with stationary left eigenvector $\pi$) and $\lambda=p-b$. The column vector $u=T_1\mathbf{1}=(1-p,\,a)^\top$ satisfies $\rho=\pi u$. Whenever $A_0=1$, the next hidden state is deterministically $D$, so
\[
\PP(A_0=1,A_n=1)=\rho\,e_D^\top P^{n-1}u,\qquad n\ge1.
\]
The two-state spectral decomposition gives
\[
P^{n-1}u = \rho\mathbf{1}+\lambda^{n-1}(u-\rho\mathbf{1}),
\]
so
\[
e_D^\top P^{n-1}u = \rho+\lambda^{n-1}(a-\rho),
\]
and therefore
\[
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n) = \PP(A_0=1,A_n=1)-\rho^2 = \rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1}.
\]
The bounds $a<\rho<1-p$ are given by \cref{lem:stationary-main}. Hence $\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_1)=\rho(a-\rho)<0$: the record is negatively correlated at lag~$1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of exact $1$-dependence]
At the critical line~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}, $\lambda=p-b=0$, so $P=\mathbf{1}\pi$ (rank one), and hence $P^\ell=P$ for every $\ell\ge1$. Write $A_i^j=(A_i,\dots,A_j)$ and let $u=u_0\cdots u_r$ and $v=v_0\cdots v_s$ be visible words, with corresponding output-labelled kernel products
\[
T_u=T_{u_0}\cdots T_{u_r},
\qquad
T_v=T_{v_0}\cdots T_{v_s}.
\]
If the two words are separated by at least one sampling step, then for some $\ell\ge1$ their joint cylinder probability is
\[
\pi\,T_u\,P^\ell\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}.
\]
Using $P^\ell=P=\mathbf{1}\pi$ gives
\[
\pi\,T_u\,P^\ell\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}
=
\pi\,T_u\,(\mathbf{1}\pi)\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}
=
(\pi\,T_u\,\mathbf{1})(\pi\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}),
\]
which is the product of the two marginal cylinder probabilities. By linearity, any events $B\in\sigma(A_i:i\le m)$ and $C\in\sigma(A_j:j\ge m+2)$ factorize in the same way, so the process is exact $1$-dependent.

Note that the output is not a deterministic function of the hidden-state pair $(H_n,H_{n+1})$: both $T_0$ and $T_1$ have non-zero $(D,D)$ entries, so the same hidden transition $(D,D)$ can produce either visible outcome. The kernel argument above avoids that issue by working directly with output-labelled cylinders.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Equal-rate limit and Lambert $W$ representation of $\Gamma_c$]\label{rem:edge-cases}
Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery-main} is stated for $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$. In the equal-rate limit $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\to\Gamma$, the expression~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main} is a $0/0$ form. Applying l'H\^opital's rule in $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ gives
\[
g_k\big|_{\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma}
=
p^k\bigl[(1-p)+\Gamma\Delta\tau\bigl(k(1-p)-p\bigr)\bigr],
\]
and the formulas for $a$, $b$, and $\lambda$ extend continuously in the same way. The hazard remains strictly increasing by continuity of the positivity argument in \cref{app:derivations}.

The critical rate $\Gamma_c$ is defined implicitly by~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}. Setting $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $x=\Gamma_c/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, one rewrites that equation as $xe^{-sx}=e^{-s}$. With $u=-sx$, this becomes $ue^u=-se^{-s}$, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambert-Gc}
\frac{\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}}
=
-\frac{W_k(-se^{-s})}{s},
\qquad
k=
\begin{cases}
-1 & \text{if } 0<s<1, \\
 0 & \text{if } s>1,
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
with $\Gamma_c=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ at $s=1$. For $s<1$ the non-trivial root lies on the $W_{-1}$ branch, whereas for $s>1$ it lies on the $W_0$ branch.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Spectral density and Fano factor}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:spectrum-main}]
From the covariance formula, $\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1}$ for $n\ge1$ with $|\lambda|<1$. Hence
\[
f(\theta) = \rho(1-\rho)+2\rho(a-\rho)\sum_{n\ge1}\lambda^{n-1}\cos(n\theta).
\]
The geometric sum evaluates via
\[
\sum_{n\ge1}\lambda^{n-1}e^{in\theta} = \frac{e^{i\theta}}{1-\lambda e^{i\theta}},
\]
taking real parts gives~\eqref{eq:spectrum-main}. Monotonicity: write $x=\cos\theta$ and $R_\lambda(x)=(x-\lambda)/(1-2\lambda x+\lambda^2)$; then $R_\lambda'(x)=(1-\lambda^2)/(1-2\lambda x+\lambda^2)^2>0$. Since $a-\rho<0$, the spectral density is decreasing in $x$, hence increasing in $\theta$ on $[0,\pi]$.

For the counting variance,
\[
\Var(N_N) = N\gamma_0 + 2\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}(N-n)\gamma_n
= N\rho(1-\rho) + 2\rho(a-\rho)\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}(N-n)\lambda^{n-1}.
\]
The geometric sum evaluates to $(N(1-\lambda)-1+\lambda^N)/(1-\lambda)^2$, giving~\eqref{eq:fano-main} in the limit $N\to\infty$. The strict inequality $F<1$ follows from $a-\rho<0$ and $1-\lambda>0$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Two-state interval classification and gap inference}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{thm:two-state-class-main}]
Let
\[
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
u & 0\\
v & w
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1-u\\
0 & a
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
a=1-v-w,
\]
and set $P=T_0+T_1=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}u&1-u\\ v&1-v\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, $e_D=(0,1)^\top$, and $\lambda=u-v$.

Every click resets the next interval to the hidden state $D$. The visible record is therefore renewal, and the zero-run tail is
\[
\tau_k=\PP(G\ge k)=e_D^\top T_0^k\mathbf 1.
\]
Because $u\neq w$,
\[
T_0^k=
\begin{pmatrix}
u^k & 0\\
v\,\dfrac{u^k-w^k}{u-w} & w^k
\end{pmatrix},
\]
and hence
\[
\tau_k=
v\,\frac{u^k-w^k}{u-w}+w^k
=
\frac{v\,u^k+(\lambda-w)w^k}{u-w},
\]
which is~\eqref{eq:general-tail-main}. Since $\PP(G=k)=\PP(G\ge k)-\PP(G\ge k+1)$, the gap law follows.

For the hazard, write
\[
\tau_k=A u^k + B w^k,
\qquad
A=\frac{v}{u-w},
\qquad
B=\frac{\lambda-w}{u-w}.
\]
Then
\[
h_k
=
\frac{\tau_k-\tau_{k+1}}{\tau_k}
=
1-\frac{\tau_{k+1}}{\tau_k}.
\]
A direct calculation gives
\[
\tau_{k+1}^2-\tau_k\tau_{k+2}
=
-AB(u-w)^2u^kw^k
=
v(w-\lambda)u^kw^k.
\]
Therefore
\[
h_{k+1}-h_k
=
\frac{\tau_{k+1}^2-\tau_k\tau_{k+2}}{\tau_k\tau_{k+1}}
=
\frac{v(w-\lambda)u^kw^k}{\tau_k\tau_{k+1}},
\]
which is~\eqref{eq:general-hazard-diff-main}. Since $v>0$, $u^kw^k>0$, and $\tau_k>0$, the sign of $h_{k+1}-h_k$ is exactly the sign of $w-\lambda$.

The stationary distribution of $P$ is
\[
\pi_R=\frac{v}{1-u+v},
\qquad
\pi_D=\frac{1-u}{1-u+v}.
\]
Hence the stationary click probability is
\[
\rho=\pi_R(1-u)+\pi_Da
=
\frac{(1-u)(1-w)}{1-u+v}.
\]
Let $q=(1-u,a)^\top=T_1\mathbf 1$. As in the proof of \cref{prop:recovery-main},
\[
\PP(A_0=1,A_n=1)=\rho\,e_D^\top P^{n-1}q,
\qquad n\ge1.
\]
The eigenvalues of $P$ are $1$ and $\lambda$, so
\[
P^{n-1}q=\rho\mathbf 1+\lambda^{n-1}(q-\rho\mathbf 1).
\]
Evaluating at $e_D$ yields
\[
e_D^\top P^{n-1}q=\rho+\lambda^{n-1}(a-\rho),
\]
and therefore
\[
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},
\]
which is~\eqref{eq:general-cov-main}.

The difference between the post-refractory click probability and the stationary click probability is
\[
\rho-a
=
\frac{(1-u)(1-w)}{1-u+v}-(1-v-w)
=
\frac{v(w-\lambda)}{1-u+v}.
\]
Hence $a=\rho$ if and only if $\lambda=w$. If $\lambda=w$, then $v=u-w$ and the tail formula reduces to $\tau_k=u^k$, so
\[
\PP(G=k)=(1-u)u^k,
\]
which is the geometric zero-run law of a Bernoulli process with click probability $1-u$. Conversely, a Bernoulli process has constant hazard, and the hazard-difference formula forces $\lambda=w$. This proves the Bernoulli characterization.

If $\lambda=0$, then $P=\mathbf 1\pi$ has rank one. The cylinder-factorization argument from the proof of exact $1$-dependence above therefore applies verbatim, so the visible process is exact $1$-dependent. Conversely, if the visible process is exact $1$-dependent and non-Bernoulli, then $\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_2)=0$ while $a-\rho\neq0$, and the covariance formula gives $\lambda=0$. This proves the characterization of the non-Bernoulli exact $1$-dependence locus.

In the exponential-recovery model $(u,v,w)=(p,b,c)$, one has
\[
w-\lambda
=
c-(p-b)
=
-(p-c)+\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
=
\frac{\Gamma(p-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\]
Because $p-c$ has the same sign as $\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma$, the final expression is strictly positive. Accordingly the model lies in the increasing-hazard sector. The identity $\lambda=0$ is exactly $p=b$, which is equivalent to \eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{thm:gap-inference-main}]
Since $Y_1=G+1$ and \cref{prop:recovery-main} gives
\[
\PP_\Gamma(G=k)=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\qquad k\ge0,
\]
the moment generating function of $Y_1$ is the sum of two geometric series:
\[
M_\Gamma(t)
=
\sum_{k\ge0}e^{t(k+1)}\PP_\Gamma(G=k)
=
\frac{e^t}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
\left(
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)}{1-pe^t}
-
\frac{\Gamma(1-c)}{1-ce^t}
\right),
\]
valid whenever $|pe^t|<1$ and $|ce^t|<1$, that is, for $t<-\log\max\{p,c\}$. In particular, $M_\Gamma$ is finite on a neighbourhood of the origin, and $Y_1$ has moments of all orders.

The identity $\mu(\Gamma)=1/\rho(\Gamma)$ follows from the renewal law. To prove strict monotonicity, write
\[
\rho(\Gamma)=\frac{(1-p)(1-c)}{1-p+b},
\qquad
b(\Gamma)=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\]
Differentiation gives
\[
b'(\Gamma)
=
-\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}(\Delta\tau-s)e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds<0,
\]
and therefore
\[
\rho'(\Gamma)
=
\frac{(1-c)\bigl[\Delta\tau\,p\,b-(1-p)b'(\Gamma)\bigr]}{(1-p+b)^2}
>0.
\]
Hence $\mu'(\Gamma)=-\rho'(\Gamma)/\rho(\Gamma)^2<0$. The map $\mu$ is therefore $C^\infty$ and strictly decreasing on $(0,\infty)$, with inverse defined on its image.

The variables $(Y_j)_{j\ge1}$ are i.i.d. The classical Cram\'er theorem and the contraction principle \cite{DemboZeitouni1998} yield the good large-deviation principle for $\bar Y_m$ with rate function~\eqref{eq:gap-rate-main}, and then for $\widehat\Gamma_m=\mu^{-1}(\bar Y_m)$ with rate function~\eqref{eq:gap-rate-gamma-main}. The ordinary i.i.d.\ central limit theorem gives
\[
\sqrt m\,(\bar Y_m-\mu(\Gamma))
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal N\!\bigl(0,\sigma_Y^2(\Gamma)\bigr).
\]
Applying the delta method to the $C^\infty$ inverse map $\mu^{-1}$ \cite{vanDerVaart1998} yields
\[
\sqrt m\,(\widehat\Gamma_m-\Gamma)
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal N\!\left(0,\frac{\sigma_Y^2(\Gamma)}{\mu'(\Gamma)^2}\right),
\]
which is~\eqref{eq:gap-clt-main}.

For the wrong-side error exponent, suppose first that $\Gamma>\Gamma_c$. Since $\mu$ is strictly decreasing, $\mu(\Gamma)<\mu(\Gamma_c)$ and
\[
\{\widehat\Gamma_m\le\Gamma_c\}
=
\{\bar Y_m\ge\mu(\Gamma_c)\}.
\]
The convex rate function $J_\Gamma$ has unique minimum at $\mu(\Gamma)$, so the infimum of $J_\Gamma$ on $[\mu(\Gamma_c),\infty)$ is attained at the boundary point $\mu(\Gamma_c)$. The large-deviation principle therefore gives
\[
\PP_\Gamma(\widehat\Gamma_m\le\Gamma_c)
=
\exp\!\bigl(-mJ_\Gamma(\mu(\Gamma_c))+o(m)\bigr)
=
\exp\!\bigl(-mI_\Gamma(\Gamma_c)+o(m)\bigr).
\]
The case $\Gamma<\Gamma_c$ is identical, with the set $(-\infty,\mu(\Gamma_c)]$ in place of $[\mu(\Gamma_c),\infty)$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Near-horizon expansion and local inverse theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{thm:horizon-main}]
In Gaussian normal coordinates from the bifurcation surface, the lapse expands as $N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)$ with $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$. Therefore
\[
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(\rho)}-1 = 2\pi\Omega\rho\,(1+O(\rho)).
\]
Inserting both shell points into~\eqref{eq:ratio-main} and cancelling the common factor $2\pi\Omega$ gives
\[
\frac{\rho_\varphi}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}} = \frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c}{\log\varphi}\,(1+O(\rho_\varphi+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})).
\]
For the area excess in Schwarzschild, $d\rho=(1-2M/r)^{-1/2}\,dr$ gives $r-r_H=\rho^2/(8M)+O(\rho^4)$ near the horizon. Hence
\[
A(\rho)-A_H = 4\pi(r(\rho)^2-r_H^2) = 4\pi(2r_H)(r-r_H)+O(\rho^4) = 2\pi\rho^2+O(\rho^4),
\]
and the area-excess ratio is the square of the proper-distance ratio.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{thm:two-mode-main}: local inverse]
Each mode $D_m$ contributes one equation $F_m(\theta)=0$ by the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main}. Both $F_m$ are $C^1$ in $\theta$ under the stated regularity of $N$ and $\beta_\infty$; the common amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels from each $F_m$. At a solution $\theta_*$ with $\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0$, the implicit function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002} guarantees that $\theta_*$ is locally unique and the solution map is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism. The Jacobian degenerates when $C_1=C_2$, a codimension-one condition avoided by choosing modes with distinct sampling intervals.
\end{proof}

\section{Simulation methodology for Sections~\ref{subsec:finite-sample} and~\ref{subsec:noisy-inverse}}\label{app:monte}

All Monte Carlo statements in the main text were produced with reproducible scripts that sample directly from the analytic laws of the ideal and finite-recovery models. We use fixed seeds for each experiment; the precise generator is not important for the conclusions because all reported uncertainties are empirical standard deviations across independent replicates.

\paragraph{Finite-recovery diagnostics.} For a chosen $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ we draw successive zero-run lengths from the discrete distribution $g_k$ in~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main} by inverse transform sampling: we precompute the cumulative distribution up to $k=200$ (which already captures probability $>0.999$ for the parameter ranges displayed) and sample from the remaining tail by rejection. Each simulated record contains $N_{\mathrm{samp}}$ sampled intervals after discarding an initial burn-in of $5\times10^3$ intervals. Hazards $\widehat h_k$ use the exact run-length counts, covariances $\widehat\gamma_n$ use the unbiased $(N_{\mathrm{samp}}-n)^{-1}$ estimator, and Fano factors $\widehat F$ use block sizes $B=50$. The figures in Sections~\ref{sec:records} and~\ref{subsec:finite-sample} use $\Delta\tau=0.3$, $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=5$, and $\Gamma$ ranging from $0.8\Gamma_c$ to $1.6\Gamma_c$ in steps of $0.05\Gamma_c$. For each grid point we simulate $5\times10^3$ independent records at $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=5\times10^3$, $5\times10^4$, and $10^5$ and report the empirical frequency with which $\widehat\gamma_2$ matches the sign of $\gamma_2$. The error bars quoted in the main text correspond to $\pm$ one empirical standard deviation divided by $\sqrt{\text{replicates}}$.

\paragraph{End-to-end shell pipeline.} The experiment of Section~\ref{subsec:noisy-inverse} uses the mode-$D_1$ design of \cref{tab:worked}. In the ideal-blanking mode we simulate records on the five-point radial grid
\[
r/r_{\mathrm s}\in\{1.006,\,1.007,\,1.008,\,1.009,\,1.010\},
\]
estimate
\[
\widehat p(r)=\frac{\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}}{\#\{A_n=0\}},
\]
and linearly interpolate the zero of $\widehat p(r)-\varphi^{-1}$. In the finite-recovery mode we simulate records on the five-point grid
\[
r/r_{\mathrm s}\in\{1.041,\,1.043,\,1.045,\,1.047,\,1.049\},
\]
estimate the empirical click fraction $\widehat\rho(r)$, solve the monotone equation $\rho(\Gamma)=\widehat\rho(r)$ with $\rho(\Gamma)$ from~\eqref{eq:rho-main}, and then linearly interpolate the zero of $\widehat\Gamma(r)-\Gamma_c$. Each record again has $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=1.2\times10^5$ sampled intervals after a burn-in of $5\times10^3$ intervals. We use $120$ independent Monte Carlo replicates. Whenever both radial grids bracket the corresponding root, the resulting estimates $(\widehat r_\varphi,\widehat r_{\mathrm{mem}})$ are substituted into~\eqref{eq:beta-infty-main}--\eqref{eq:mass-main}; the reported uncertainties are empirical standard deviations across those end-to-end replicates.
