
\section{State diagram, timing convention, and carryover rule}\label{app:state-diagram}

The detector has two hidden states $R$ (ready) and $D$ (refractory/dead). The state diagram and timing convention for one sampling interval $[0,\Delta\tau]$ are as follows.

To remove any ambiguity in the sampling protocol, we distinguish three moments in each interval:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),itemsep=0.15em]
\item immediately after the readout at time $n\Delta\tau^+$, which sets the hidden state for interval $n$;
\item during the open interval $(n\Delta\tau,(n+1)\Delta\tau)$, where recovery and excitation can occur;
\item immediately before the next readout at time $(n+1)\Delta\tau^-$, whose hidden state is then carried into interval $n+1$ unless a click occurred.
\end{enumerate}
The carryover rule is the following: a click in interval $n$ forces interval $n+1$ to start in $D$; a no-click does \emph{not} reset the detector to $R$, but instead carries the hidden end-of-interval state into the next interval.

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
  state/.style={draw, circle, minimum size=1.2cm, thick},
  arr/.style={-Latex, thick},
  every node/.style={font=\small}
]
\node[state, fill=green!12] (R) at (0,0) {$R$};
\node[state, fill=red!12] (D) at (4,0) {$D$};

\draw[arr] (R) to[bend left=25] node[above] {excite at rate $\Gamma$} node[below] {output $A_n=1$} (D);
\draw[arr] (R) to[loop left, looseness=8] node[left] {no excitation} node[right,xshift=-0.3cm,yshift=-0.5cm] {$A_n=0$} (R);
\draw[arr] (D) to[bend left=25] node[below] {recover at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$} (R);
\draw[arr] (D) to[loop right, looseness=8] node[right] {no recovery} node[left,xshift=0.3cm,yshift=-0.5cm] {$A_n=0$} (D);

\node[anchor=north, text width=7cm, align=left] at (2,-2.2) {
\textbf{Key rule.} Every click ($A_n=1$) leaves the detector in state $D$ at the start of interval $n+1$, regardless of the hidden state at the start of interval $n$. This is the single assumption that makes click times regeneration points.
};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\noindent\textbf{Timing within one interval.}
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*),itemsep=0.15em]
\item \emph{Start in $R$:} The detector may be excited during $[0,\Delta\tau]$ at rate $\Gamma$. If excited (probability $1-p$, with $p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}$), it ends in $D$ and emits $A_n=1$. If not excited (probability $p$), it ends in $R$ and emits $A_n=0$.
\item \emph{Start in $D$:} Recovery to $R$ occurs at an exponential time $s$ with rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. If $s\le\Delta\tau$, the detector is ready for the remaining subinterval $[s,\Delta\tau]$ and may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited after recovery, it ends in $D$ with $A_n=1$ (probability $a$). If not excited after recovery, it ends in $R$ with $A_n=0$ (probability $b$). If $s>\Delta\tau$ (no recovery during the interval), it remains in $D$ with $A_n=0$ (probability $c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}$). Here $a+b=1-c$, so $a+b+c=1$.
\end{enumerate}

The output-labelled kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ in~\eqref{eq:t0-t1-main} encode precisely these transition-and-output probabilities. Note that the transition $(D,D)$ can produce either output: $A_n=0$ with probability $c$ (no recovery) or $A_n=1$ with probability $a$ (recovery followed by re-excitation). This is why the output is \emph{not} a deterministic function of the hidden-state pair $(H_n,H_{n+1})$, a point exploited in the factorization proof of $1$-dependence in \cref{sec:records}.

\section{GKLS derivation of the phenomenological kernels}\label{app:gkls}

The phenomenological counter model of \cref{sec:operational} can be obtained from a standard weak-coupling GKLS description. For clarity we summarize the derivation and the approximations that go into it.

Let $\rho_{\mathrm{det}}$ denote the reduced density matrix of a two-level detector with Hamiltonian $H_{\mathrm{det}}=(\Omega/2)\sigma_z$. Along a stationary worldline in a static KMS state, the usual Born--Markov and rotating-wave approximations lead to the GKLS master equation
\[
\dot\rho_{\mathrm{det}}
=
-i[\tfrac{\Omega}{2}\sigma_z,\rho_{\mathrm{det}}]
+\Gamma_+\,\mathcal D[\sigma_+](\rho_{\mathrm{det}})
+\Gamma_-\,\mathcal D[\sigma_-](\rho_{\mathrm{det}})
\]
with dissipator $\mathcal D[L](\rho)=L\rho L^\dagger-\tfrac12\{L^\dagger L,\rho\}$ and upward/downward transition rates $(\Gamma_+,\Gamma_-)$ given by the usual Wightman-function integrals \cite{LoukoSatz2006,KayWald1991}. Weak coupling means $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ is small enough that $\Gamma_\pm\Delta\tau\ll1$ remains controlled while still allowing $\Gamma_\pm\Delta\tau=O(1)$. The coarse graining assumes that the field correlation time $\tau_{\mathrm{corr}}$ obeys $\tau_{\mathrm{corr}}\ll\Delta\tau\ll 1/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, so that the field re-equilibrates between readouts and the GKLS generator may be treated as constant over each interval.

Within one sampling interval we integrate the master equation exactly to obtain the propagator $e^{\Delta\tau\mathcal L}$, where $\mathcal L$ is the GKLS generator above augmented with a recovery term $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\mathcal D[\sigma_-]$ that enforces exponential relaxation of the refractory state. Conditioning on the readout outcome gives two Kraus maps:
\begin{align*}
K_1\rho_{\mathrm{det}}K_1^\dagger &= (1-e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau})\,|1\rangle\!\langle0|\,\rho_{\mathrm{det}}\,|0\rangle\!\langle1|,\\
K_0\rho_{\mathrm{det}}K_0^\dagger &= e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}\,|0\rangle\!\langle0|\,\rho_{\mathrm{det}}\,|0\rangle\!\langle0| + \text{(terms with }\kappa_{\mathrm r}\text{)}.
\end{align*}
Tracing over the hidden state and using the carryover rule specified in Appendix~\ref{app:state-diagram} yields the classical output-labelled kernels
\[
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau} & 0\\
\dfrac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}-e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau})}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma} & e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1-e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}\\
0 & \dfrac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau})-\Gamma(1-e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau})}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
\end{pmatrix},
\]
which coincide with~\eqref{eq:t0-t1-main}. The remainder terms are of order $O((\lambda_c\Delta\tau)^2)$ and vanish uniformly as the coarse-graining assumptions are enforced. This shows that the phenomenological kernels arise from a GKLS evolution with repeated projective readout and the stipulated carryover rule.

\section{Key derivations}\label{app:derivations}

This appendix collects the proofs of the main technical results. Complete details for all intermediate steps appear in the Supplementary Material.

\subsection{Zero-run law and hazard monotonicity}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:recovery-main}: zero-run law]
Every observed click places the detector in the refractory state $D$ for the next interval, independently of the earlier history. Hence the post-click hidden state is deterministic, and the strong Markov property of the hidden two-state chain makes click times regeneration times. The resulting zero-run lengths are therefore i.i.d., and a gap of length $k$ consists of $k$ successive output-$0$ transitions followed by one output-$1$ transition, giving~\eqref{eq:gap-main}.

To evaluate it explicitly, note that $T_0$ is lower triangular with eigenvalues $p$ and $c$. Direct integration of~\eqref{eq:a-b-main} yields, for $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$,
\[
a=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\qquad
b=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\]
Since $T_0$ is lower triangular,
\[
T_0^k=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p^k & 0 \\
 b\,\dfrac{p^k-c^k}{p-c} & c^k
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:gap-main} with $e_D=(0,1)^\top$ and $T_1\mathbf{1}=(1-p,\,a)^\top$ gives
\[
g_k = e_D^\top T_0^k T_1\mathbf{1}
= qb\frac{p^k-c^k}{p-c}+ac^k
= \frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\]
where $q=1-p$ and the simplification uses the explicit forms of $a$ and $b$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:recovery-main}: hazard monotonicity]
The tail of the gap law is
\[
\sum_{j\ge k}g_j=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\]
Therefore
\[
h_k = \frac{g_k}{\sum_{j\ge k}g_j}
= \frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k}.
\]
Direct subtraction yields
\[
h_{k+1}-h_k = \frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(p-c)^2p^kc^k}{(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^{k+1}-\Gamma c^{k+1})} > 0,
\]
since all factors in the numerator and denominator are strictly positive for $p\neq c$ (i.e.\ $\Gamma\neq\kappa_{\mathrm r}$). Strict monotonicity excludes the possibility that the visible process is an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$: if it were, $h_k$ would eventually be constant.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Covariance formula and 1-dependence}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:recovery-main}: covariance formula]
The aggregate transition matrix is
\[
P = T_0+T_1 =
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 1-p\\
 b & 1-b
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Its eigenvalues are $1$ (with stationary left eigenvector $\pi$) and $\lambda=p-b$. The column vector $u=T_1\mathbf{1}=(1-p,\,a)^\top$ satisfies $\rho=\pi u$. Whenever $A_0=1$, the next hidden state is deterministically $D$, so
\[
\PP(A_0=1,A_n=1)=\rho\,e_D^\top P^{n-1}u,\qquad n\ge1.
\]
The two-state spectral decomposition gives
\[
P^{n-1}u = \rho\mathbf{1}+\lambda^{n-1}(u-\rho\mathbf{1}),
\]
so
\[
e_D^\top P^{n-1}u = \rho+\lambda^{n-1}(a-\rho),
\]
and therefore
\[
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n) = \PP(A_0=1,A_n=1)-\rho^2 = \rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1}.
\]
The inequality $a<\rho<1-p$ follows because $\rho$ is a strict convex combination of $a$ (the click probability from state $D$) and $1-p$ (the click probability from state $R$), with both weights positive. Hence $\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_1)=\rho(a-\rho)<0$: the record is negatively correlated at lag~$1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of exact $1$-dependence]
At the critical line~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}, $\lambda=p-b=0$, so $P=\mathbf{1}\pi$ (rank one), and hence $P^\ell=P$ for every $\ell\ge1$. Let $u$ and $v$ be visible words, with corresponding output-labelled kernel products $T_u$ and $T_v$. If the words are separated by at least one sampling step, then for some $\ell\ge1$ their joint cylinder probability is
\[
\pi\,T_u\,P^\ell\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}.
\]
Using $P^\ell=P=\mathbf{1}\pi$ gives
\[
\pi\,T_u\,P^\ell\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}
=
\pi\,T_u\,(\mathbf{1}\pi)\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}
=
(\pi\,T_u\,\mathbf{1})(\pi\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}),
\]
which is the product of the two marginal cylinder probabilities. By linearity, any events $B\in\sigma(A_i:i\le m)$ and $C\in\sigma(A_j:j\ge m+2)$ factorize in the same way, so the process is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Spectral density and Fano factor}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{prop:spectrum-main}]
From the covariance formula, $\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1}$ for $n\ge1$ with $|\lambda|<1$. Hence
\[
f(\theta) = \rho(1-\rho)+2\rho(a-\rho)\sum_{n\ge1}\lambda^{n-1}\cos(n\theta).
\]
The geometric sum evaluates via
\[
\sum_{n\ge1}\lambda^{n-1}e^{in\theta} = \frac{e^{i\theta}}{1-\lambda e^{i\theta}},
\]
taking real parts gives~\eqref{eq:spectrum-main}. Monotonicity: write $x=\cos\theta$ and $R_\lambda(x)=(x-\lambda)/(1-2\lambda x+\lambda^2)$; then $R_\lambda'(x)=(1-\lambda^2)/(1-2\lambda x+\lambda^2)^2>0$. Since $a-\rho<0$, the spectral density is decreasing in $x$, hence increasing in $\theta$ on $[0,\pi]$.

For the counting variance,
\[
\Var(N_N) = N\gamma_0 + 2\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}(N-n)\gamma_n
= N\rho(1-\rho) + 2\rho(a-\rho)\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}(N-n)\lambda^{n-1}.
\]
The geometric sum evaluates to $(N(1-\lambda)-1+\lambda^N)/(1-\lambda)^2$, giving~\eqref{eq:fano-main} in the limit $N\to\infty$. The strict inequality $F<1$ follows from $a-\rho<0$ and $1-\lambda>0$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Near-horizon expansion and local inverse theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{thm:horizon-main}]
In Gaussian normal coordinates from the bifurcation surface, the lapse expands as $N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)$ with $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$. Therefore
\[
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(\rho)}-1 = 2\pi\Omega\rho\,(1+O(\rho)).
\]
Inserting both shell points into~\eqref{eq:ratio-main} and cancelling the common factor $2\pi\Omega$ gives
\[
\frac{\rho_\varphi}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}} = \frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c}{\log\varphi}\,(1+O(\rho_\varphi+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})).
\]
For the area excess in Schwarzschild, $d\rho=(1-2M/r)^{-1/2}\,dr$ gives $r-r_H=\rho^2/(8M)+O(\rho^4)$ near the horizon. Hence
\[
A(\rho)-A_H = 4\pi(r(\rho)^2-r_H^2) = 4\pi(2r_H)(r-r_H)+O(\rho^4) = 2\pi\rho^2+O(\rho^4),
\]
and the area-excess ratio is the square of the proper-distance ratio.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of \cref{thm:two-mode-main}: local inverse]
Each mode $D_m$ contributes one equation $F_m(\theta)=0$ by the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main}. Both $F_m$ are $C^1$ in $\theta$ under the stated regularity of $N$ and $\beta_\infty$; the common amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels from each $F_m$. At a solution $\theta_*$ with $\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0$, the implicit function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002} guarantees that $\theta_*$ is locally unique and the solution map is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism. The Jacobian degenerates when $C_1=C_2$, a codimension-one condition avoided by choosing modes with distinct sampling intervals.
\end{proof}

\section{Simulation methodology for Sections~\ref{subsec:finite-sample} and~\ref{subsec:noisy-inverse}}\label{app:monte}

All Monte Carlo statements in the main text were produced with reproducible scripts that sample directly from the analytic renewal law. The pseudo-random generator is the PCG64 bit generator as implemented in \texttt{NumPy~1.26}, seeded at $2^{10}$ for the Section~\ref{subsec:finite-sample} sweeps and at $2^{12}$ for the perturbation study of Section~\ref{subsec:noisy-inverse}.

\paragraph{Finite-recovery records.} For a chosen $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ we draw successive zero-run lengths from the discrete distribution $g_k$ in~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main} by inverse transform sampling: we precompute the cumulative distribution up to $k=200$ (which already captures probability~$>0.999$ for the parameter ranges displayed) and sample from the remaining tail via rejection. Each record contains $N$ clicks after discarding an initial burn-in of $5\times10^3$ samples. Hazards $\widehat h_k$ use the exact run-length counts, covariances $\widehat\gamma_n$ use the unbiased $(N-n)^{-1}$ estimator, and Fano factors $\widehat F$ use block sizes $B=50$. The figures in Sections~\ref{sec:records} and~\ref{subsec:finite-sample} use $\Delta\tau=0.3$, $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=5$, and $\Gamma$ ranging from $0.8\Gamma_c$ to $1.6\Gamma_c$ in steps of $0.05\Gamma_c$. For each grid point we simulate $5\times10^3$ independent records at $N=5\times10^3$, $5\times10^4$, and $10^5$ and report the empirical frequency with which $\widehat\gamma_2$ matches the sign of $\gamma_2$. The error bars quoted in the main text correspond to $\pm$ one empirical standard deviation divided by $\sqrt{\text{replicates}}$.

\paragraph{Noisy shell inversion.} The perturbation study of Section~\ref{subsec:noisy-inverse} takes the analytic shell radii produced by mode~$D_1$ in \cref{tab:worked}, draws $\delta r\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma_r^2)$ independently for each shell, and evaluates~\eqref{eq:beta-infty-main}--\eqref{eq:mass-main}. For each $\sigma_r$ we use $10^4$ independent perturbations with seed $2^{12}$; the reported uncertainties are empirical standard deviations across those draws. The same code can be reused with arbitrary radii or detector parameters by supplying the desired $(\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Omega,\lambda_c^2\mathcal S)$.
