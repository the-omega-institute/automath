\begin{abstract}
We consider a sampled-counter model driven by the excitation rate of an Unruh--DeWitt detector on a stationary worldline in a static KMS state. The detector response and the readout are treated separately: the UDW sector fixes the local input rate, while the counter is operated either with deterministic one-step blanking or with exponential recovery. In the ideal mode the sampled record is a Markov chain on the golden-mean shift, and the Parry measure is realized at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. In the recovery mode the record is a full-support renewal process; we derive the zero-run distribution, show that the click hazard is strictly increasing, obtain the covariance profile, and identify the unique rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ for which the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent. The same reset mechanism extends to a broader two-state interval class, where non-Bernoulli exact $1$-dependence remains a rank-one condition. More generally, exact $m$-dependence of any stationary binary renewal record is shown to be equivalent to a rational inter-click law of degree $m+1$, placing the two-state counter at the first non-Bernoulli rung of a finite-dependence hierarchy. Complete inter-click intervals then provide a local-rate estimator with explicit large-deviation and central-limit asymptotics. Substituting the record thresholds into the static-KMS response law yields detector-defined shell equations. For a single detector operated in the two readout modes, one obtains an exact shell-ratio formula in the homogeneous spectral-weight model and quantitative shell-displacement bounds under controlled spectral drift.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Unruh--DeWitt detector, static KMS state, renewal process, symbolic dynamics, counting statistics

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates, detailed balance and Tolman scaling are the standard observables in Unruh--DeWitt (UDW) detector theory \cite{Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,Takagi1986,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. Once the detector output is sampled as a binary click record, however, the readout carries more than the mean rate. Dead time and finite recovery generate forbidden words, waiting-time statistics, lagged correlations and modified counting noise. The question we address is whether such record-level observables single out distinguished rates within a stationary response family.

Our emphasis is operational. A realistic counter has its own temporal dynamics, and those dynamics need not disappear even when the local excitation rate is held fixed. In a stationary KMS setting, the readout protocol therefore enters directly into the map from detector data to shell location in a chosen response model.

Several strands of work meet at this point. On the detector side we use the standard stationary response theory for UDW probes \cite{LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}, together with detector-based measurement schemes in relativistic quantum field theory \cite{Fewster2020,PranziniEtAl2024,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click}, analyses of detector thermalization \cite{FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016}, and proposals that use detector observables for geometric inference \cite{TjoaMann2020,BraunsteinCaves1994,PercheMartinMartinez2022}. On the stochastic side, the closest precedents are waiting-time methods, antibunching diagnostics and renewal formulas \cite{CarmichaelWaiting1989,KimbleAntibunching1977,Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014}, with classical dead-time models providing the counter-physics background \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975}. Abstract one-dependent and $m$-dependent constructions form a separate background \cite{AaronsonGilatKeaneDeValk1989,Janson1984}, while the renewal-sequence formalism used below is classical \cite{Cinlar1975}. What seems to be missing is a closed-form treatment of the stationary binary record produced by interval sampling with an explicit reset rule, together with a converse classification of exact finite dependence inside that reset-renewal class, and of the shell conditions that arise when those record thresholds are combined with a static-KMS response law.

In this paper we adopt a deliberately simple model. The UDW detector supplies a stationary excitation input rate, while the readout is represented by an interval click-register instrument with either deterministic one-step blanking or exponential recovery. In the ideal mode the sampled output is a Markov chain on the golden-mean shift, and the Parry measure is obtained at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. In the recovery mode the sampled output is a renewal process with full symbolic support. We derive the zero-run law, the hazard and covariance formulas, and the unique rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ at which dependence vanishes beyond one lag. We also show that the same reset architecture extends to a wider two-state interval class, where non-Bernoulli exact $1$-dependence remains a rank-one condition.

These selectors are distinct from the usual thermal criteria. Detailed balance constrains the ratio $\Gamma_-/\Gamma_+$, whereas Gibbs thermalization concerns the asymptotic detector state. By contrast, the Parry threshold is a statement about the word statistics of the sampled record, and the recovery threshold is a statement about its dependence structure. In the recovery model the hazard remains strictly increasing for every positive sampling interval, so the Parry law is excluded even though adjacent clicks are quadratically suppressed as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$. The same renewal structure yields explicit antibunching and low-frequency-noise formulas.

Complete inter-click intervals also support inference. Because the gaps are i.i.d.\ in the recovery regime, one obtains a local-rate estimator with large-deviation and central-limit asymptotics. After substitution of the static-KMS response law, the record thresholds become detector-defined shell equations. For a shared detector operated in two readout modes, the homogeneous spectral-weight model gives an exact shell-ratio identity, while controlled spectral drift yields shell-separation and displacement bounds in static spherical geometries.

The renewal sector also contains a converse theorem that does not depend on the static-KMS substitution. Exact $m$-dependence of a stationary binary renewal record is equivalent to the eventual constancy of its renewal sequence and hence to a rational inter-click law of degree $m+1$. The exact $1$-dependence threshold of the finite-recovery detector is therefore the first non-Bernoulli member of a full finite-dependence hierarchy rather than an isolated two-state coincidence.

The analysis is intended as a record-level study of the sampled-counter model, not as a microscopic theory of repeatedly measured UDW dynamics. We work throughout with weak coupling, one stationary worldline, discrete sampling of size $\Delta\tau$, and exponential recovery in the non-ideal mode. The shell loci are detector-defined hypersurfaces rather than detector-independent invariants of the spacetime, and the shell-pair comparison is tied to the shared-hardware two-mode protocol introduced in \cref{subsec:two-mode}. In black-hole settings the neglected spatial dependence of $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ is associated with greybody filtering and mode propagation \cite{Page1976Emission,ArbeyCalzaPerez2025}; we therefore distinguish the present use of Tolman scaling in a fixed detector model from broader notions of local thermal equilibrium in curved spacetime \cite{BuchholzSchlemmer2007,Solveen2012,GranseePinamontiVerch2017}.

The paper is organized as follows. \Cref{sec:operational,sec:records} set up the sampled-counter model and derive the record-level thresholds together with their statistical signatures. \Cref{sec:kms-shells,sec:inverse} combine these thresholds with a static KMS response law, derive the shell equations, and study shell comparison and inversion. Appendices~\ref{app:state-diagram}--\ref{app:monte} collect the interval instrument, the technical derivations and the simulation protocol; additional calculations are recorded in the Supplementary Material.

\section{Model and observables}\label{sec:operational}

We consider a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector sampled at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. The observed output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ denotes a click in the $n$th sampling interval. Compressing the record to the empirical click frequency retains only the one-point observable
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n.
\]
The full record contains more information. In the present setting the natural observables are the support of the stationary law, the distribution of zero-runs between clicks, the click hazard after $k$ zeros, the low-lag covariance profile, and the residual counting noise after coarse graining. If a click is followed by $G$ consecutive zeros before the next click, then $G$ is the \emph{zero-run length}, and its hazard $h_k$ is the conditional probability that the next click occurs after exactly $k$ zeros since the previous one. In the ideal mode the \emph{Parry-law benchmark} means that the stationary record coincides with the Parry measure on the golden-mean grammar. In the recovery mode \emph{exact $1$-dependence} means that blocks of samples separated by one intervening sample are independent. These are the two record-level conditions that later become shell equations.

The two detector regimes studied below are shown schematically in \cref{fig:scheme}. The ideal mode imposes a hard symbolic grammar, whereas finite recovery produces a full-support renewal law. The corresponding thresholds are therefore different in kind: one is grammar based, the other dependence based.

\section{Record-level thresholds}\label{sec:records}

\subsection{Sampled counter}\label{subsec:phenom-model}
The detector model consists of a sampled counter with two hidden states, $R$ (ready) and $D$ (refractory), together with an interval click register. At the beginning of each interval $[n\Delta\tau,(n+1)\Delta\tau]$ the register is reset to $0$. If the counter is ready, an excitation supplied by the stationary UDW rate $\Gamma_+\equiv\Gamma$ flips the register irreversibly to $1$ during that interval. If the counter begins in $D$, it may recover to $R$ at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ and then click later in the same interval. At the end of the interval only the register is read out:
\[
A_n=
\begin{cases}
1,&\text{the interval register fired},\\
0,&\text{the interval register remained silent}.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus $A_n$ is not a projective measurement of the detector's internal two-level system. It is the output of an interval instrument in which the hidden counter state and the visible register are kept separate; Appendix~\ref{app:gkls} records one consistent matrix realization.

The post-readout update is equally important. If $A_n=1$, the next interval starts in the refractory state $D$. If $A_n=0$, the hidden end-of-interval state is carried into the next interval without further reset. This carryover rule generates the serial dependence of the observed record. In particular, it makes click times regeneration points in the recovery mode, which is the source of the renewal structure used below.

Concretely, the detector dynamics within a single interval $[0,\Delta\tau]$ are as follows (see also the state diagram in \cref{app:state-diagram}):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item If the detector begins the interval in state $R$ (ready): during $[0,\Delta\tau]$ it may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited, the interval ends in state $D$ and registers a click ($A_n=1$). If not excited, the interval ends in state $R$ and registers no click ($A_n=0$).
\item If the detector begins the interval in state $D$ (refractory): it first recovers to $R$ at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. If recovery occurs at time $s\in[0,\Delta\tau]$, the detector is then ready for the remaining subinterval $[s,\Delta\tau]$ and may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited after recovery, the interval ends in $D$ with a click. If not excited after recovery, or if recovery does not occur during the interval, the hidden state at the end of the interval is determined by whether recovery happened.
\end{enumerate}
The output-labelled kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ used below are obtained by integrating these continuous-time dynamics over $[0,\Delta\tau]$. The structural point to keep in view is simple: every click forces the next interval to start in $D$, so the observed click times are regeneration points of the hidden process.

The kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ define a \emph{phenomenological counter model} driven by the stationary UDW excitation rate $\Gamma_+$, in the spirit of classical dead-time counting models \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,CarmichaelWaiting1989,KimbleAntibunching1977}. The separation between detector physics and counter electronics is intentional: in \cref{sec:kms-shells,sec:inverse} static-KMS response theory supplies the input rate $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)$, while dead time, recovery and interval readout belong to the counter model. No small-parameter assumption is imposed on $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$; the interval probabilities are integrated exactly for all positive $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau$. Appendix~\ref{app:gkls} records one explicit interval instrument realizing these kernels.

\subsection{Ideal blanking and the Parry benchmark}

The ideal mode is defined by one deterministic blank interval after each click: once the detector fires, it is held in the refractory state $D$ for the whole next sampling step and is returned to the ready state $R$ only at the start of the following interval. In particular, $A_{n+1}=0$ whenever $A_n=1$. This is not the limit $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\to0$ of the exponential-recovery model, which would instead trap the detector in the refractory state after the first click. Writing
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-beta-main}
p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}, \qquad q_\beta=1-p_\beta,
\end{equation}
the sampled output is the stationary Markov chain on the golden-mean shift with transition matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-mean-main}
P_\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p_\beta & 1-p_\beta \\
 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The reference law on that shift is the Parry measure, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy compatible with the grammar $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ideal-main}
For the ideal dead-time detector, the sampled output realizes the Parry-law benchmark if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-threshold-main}
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\end{equation}
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ is the golden ratio. This threshold defines the exact-clock selector.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} gives the unique sampled rate for which the dead-time record realizes the maximum-entropy law compatible with the forbidden word $11$. A short derivation is recorded in the Supplementary Material. After substitution of the KMS rate, this becomes the exact-clock shell condition.

The Parry benchmark is distinct from both detailed balance and detector-state thermalization. Detailed balance constrains the ratio $\Gamma_-/\Gamma_+$ \cite{Unruh1976,BirDav1982}, while weak-coupling Gibbs thermalization concerns the asymptotic detector state \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016}. Here the statement is instead about the full word distribution of the sampled record.

\subsection{Exponential recovery and exact \texorpdfstring{$1$}{1}-dependence}

We now replace deterministic blanking by exponential recovery at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$. Throughout this section, and later when no ambiguity arises, we write $\Gamma\equiv\Gamma_+$ for the excitation rate. Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-c-main}
p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}, \qquad c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-b-main}
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds,
\qquad
b=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\end{equation}
When $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, these integrals evaluate to
$a=[\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)]/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$
and
$b=\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$.
Because every observed click resets the next interval to the refractory state, the visible record is renewal and the zero-run lengths are i.i.d. The same reset structure also makes the exact $1$-dependence threshold transparent: it occurs when the non-trivial eigenvalue of the hidden two-state transition matrix vanishes, so that dependence beyond one step disappears.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:recovery-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the zero-run length $G$ between successive clicks has law
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-main}
\PP(G=k)=g_k=e_D^{\top}T_0^kT_1\mathbf 1,
\qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t0-t1-main}
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 0 \\
 b & c
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
 0 & 1-p \\
 0 & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
If $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-closed-main}
g_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\end{equation}
and the click hazard after $k$ zeros is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard-main}
h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}
     {\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k},
\qquad k\ge0.
\end{equation}
The hazard is strictly increasing, so the visible process is not an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$.

Let $\rho=\PP(A_0=1)$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda-main}
\lambda=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}-\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}}
{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cov-main}
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},\qquad n\ge1.
\end{equation}
Consequently there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>0$ determined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-c-main}
\Delta\tau=
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma_c},
\end{equation}
such that the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$. Below $\Gamma_c$ all covariances are negative; above $\Gamma_c$ they alternate in sign.
\end{proposition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:stationary-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the stationary hidden-state distribution of $P=T_0+T_1$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stationary-main}
\pi_R=\frac{b}{1-p+b},
\qquad
\pi_D=\frac{1-p}{1-p+b}.
\end{equation}
The stationary click rate is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-main}
\rho=\PP(A_0=1)=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a=\frac{(1-p)(1-c)}{1-p+b},
\end{equation}
and it satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-bounds-main}
a<\rho<1-p.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

The derivations of \cref{prop:recovery-main,lem:stationary-main}, including the closed zero-run law, the hazard monotonicity, the covariance formula and the kernel factorization at the critical line, are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}; further intermediate steps are recorded in the Supplementary Material.

The recovery threshold is therefore different in character from the Parry threshold. A Bernoulli benchmark on the full two-shift would require constant hazard, so the Parry law is excluded throughout the recovery regime. \Cref{fig:hazard-cov} shows both the strictly increasing hazard and the covariance sign change across $\Gamma_c$.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:no-exact-main}
No finite-recovery detector with positive parameters $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ realizes the Parry-law benchmark within the full-support renewal class.
\end{corollary}

A short discussion of the equal-rate limit together with an explicit Lambert $W$ representation of $\Gamma_c$ is recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

\subsection{The wider two-state interval class}\label{subsec:two-state-class}

The exponential-recovery counter occupies one point in a broader three-parameter family of sampled instruments with the same ready/refractory architecture and the same post-click reset rule. Fix parameters
\[
0<u,w<1,\qquad 0<v<1-w,\qquad u\neq w,
\]
and define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:general-kernels-main}
T_0(u,v,w)=
\begin{pmatrix}
u & 0\\
v & w
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1(u,v,w)=
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & 1-u\\
0 & 1-v-w
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The exponential model corresponds to $(u,v,w)=(p,b,c)$. The next theorem isolates which record-level thresholds are consequences of the reset architecture itself and which depend on the recovery sector.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:two-state-class-main}
Let $(A_n)$ be the visible record generated by the kernels~\eqref{eq:general-kernels-main}, and let $G$ denote the zero-run length between successive clicks. Set
\[
a=1-v-w,\qquad \lambda=u-v,\qquad \rho=\frac{(1-u)(1-w)}{1-u+v}.
\]
Then the following statements hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item For every $k\ge0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:general-tail-main}
\PP(G\ge k)=\tau_k=\frac{v\,u^k+(\lambda-w)w^k}{u-w},
\qquad
\PP(G=k)=\tau_k-\tau_{k+1}.
\end{equation}
\item The hazard satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:general-hazard-diff-main}
h_{k+1}-h_k=
\frac{v(w-\lambda)u^k w^k}{\tau_k\tau_{k+1}},
\qquad k\ge0.
\end{equation}
Hence the hazard is strictly increasing when $\lambda<w$, constant when $\lambda=w$, and strictly decreasing when $\lambda>w$.
\item The covariance profile is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:general-cov-main}
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},
\qquad n\ge1.
\end{equation}
The visible process is Bernoulli if and only if $\lambda=w$. It is exact $1$-dependent and non-Bernoulli if and only if $\lambda=0$.
\item In the exponential-recovery model $(u,v,w)=(p,b,c)$ one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:general-specialization-main}
w-\lambda=\frac{\Gamma\,(p-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}>0.
\end{equation}
Accordingly the exponential model lies entirely in the increasing-hazard sector, and its unique non-Bernoulli exact $1$-dependence point is the rank-one condition $p=b$, equivalently $\Gamma=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

Theorem~\ref{thm:two-state-class-main} separates the consequences of the reset architecture from those of the recovery sector. Renewal and the rank-one memory transition are architecture driven; Bernoulli reduction and the direction of hazard drift depend on the sign of $w-\lambda$. The proof is recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

\subsection{Finite-dependence hierarchy in the reset-renewal class}\label{subsec:renewal-hierarchy}

For the reset architecture, the visible record is determined by its renewal sequence
\[
u_n=\PP(A_n=1\mid A_0=1),\qquad n\ge0,
\]
and by the inter-click interval law. Classical renewal theory relates these two objects through the generating-function identity $U=1/(1-F)$ \cite{Cinlar1975}. The next theorem shows that exact finite dependence is rigid inside this class: the dependence order is equivalent to the degree of a rational inter-click law.

\begin{theorem}[Finite-dependence classification {\bfseries [New-1 | 2026-04-17]}]\label{thm:renewal-md-main}
Let $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb Z}$ be a stationary binary renewal process with click probability $\rho=\PP(A_0=1)\in(0,1)$. Let $Y\in\{1,2,\dots\}$ denote the inter-click interval, set
\[
f_n=\PP(Y=n),
\qquad
F(z)=\sum_{n\ge1}f_n z^n,
\qquad
u_n=\PP(A_n=1\mid A_0=1),
\qquad
u_0=1,
\]
and fix an integer $m\ge1$. Then the following statements are equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The record $(A_n)$ is exact $m$-dependent.
\item One has $u_n=\rho$ for every $n\ge m+1$.
\item The inter-click generating function satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:renewal-q-main}
F(z)=1-\frac{1-z}{Q_m(z)},
\qquad |z|<1,
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:renewal-poly-main}
Q_m(z)=1+\sum_{j=1}^{m}(u_j-u_{j-1})z^j+(\rho-u_m)z^{m+1}.
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
If the dependence order is exactly $m$, then $\deg Q_m=m+1$. In that case the gap law satisfies the linear recurrence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:renewal-rec-main}
f_n=
(1-u_1)f_{n-1}
+\sum_{j=2}^{m}(u_{j-1}-u_j)f_{n-j}
+(u_m-\rho)f_{n-m-1},
\qquad n\ge m+2.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

Theorem~\ref{thm:renewal-md-main} turns exact $1$-dependence into the first step of a full renewal hierarchy. In particular, the current two-state class is structurally optimal at order one: higher exact dependence orders would require an inter-click law of higher renewal degree than the two-mode form in \cref{eq:general-tail-main}. The proof is recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

\subsection{Soft approach to the ideal grammar}

Corollary~\ref{cor:no-exact-main} is an exact obstruction, but the recovery record still approaches the ideal grammar in a controlled short-sampling regime.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:soft-main}
In the finite-recovery model,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soft-main}
\PP(A_{n+1}=1\mid A_n=1)=a
=\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2+O((\Delta\tau)^3)
\qquad (\Delta\tau\downarrow0).
\end{equation}
For a fixed window length $m\ge2$, let
\[
V_m=\{(a_0,\dots,a_{m-1})\in\{0,1\}^m:\exists\,k\le m-2,\ a_ka_{k+1}=11\}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:window-main}
\PP\!\bigl((A_0,\dots,A_{m-1})\in V_m\bigr)
\le
\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2\,(m-1)
+O\!\bigl(m(\Delta\tau)^3\bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus, for every fixed $m$, the finite-recovery record concentrates on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:soft-main} shows that the ideal grammar remains a meaningful short-sampling limit. At fixed finite window length, inadmissible words become quadratically rare, but the Parry benchmark is still excluded at every positive sampling interval because the hazard retains memory of the zero-run length.

\subsection{Spectral density and antibunching}

The renewal structure also makes the low-frequency counting noise accessible in closed form. Define the spectral density
\[
f(\theta)=\rho(1-\rho)+2\sum_{n\ge1}\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)\cos(n\theta),
\qquad \theta\in[-\pi,\pi].
\]
Since the covariance sequence is explicit, both the spectral density and the Fano factor can be computed exactly. This is useful because finite recovery suppresses immediate reclicks but does not, a priori, rule out enhanced low-frequency noise.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectrum-main}
For the stationary finite-recovery record,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectrum-main}
f(\theta)=
\rho(1-\rho)
+
2\rho(a-\rho)\,
\frac{\cos\theta-\lambda}{1-2\lambda\cos\theta+\lambda^2},
\end{equation}
and $f$ is strictly increasing on $[0,\pi]$. If
\[
N_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n,
\]
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fano-main}
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{\Var(N_N)}{\EE N_N}
=
1-\rho+\frac{2(a-\rho)}{1-\lambda}
<1.
\end{equation}
Hence the finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range.
\end{proposition}

The recovery regime is therefore characterized by more than the sign of a single covariance: the lag-sign pattern changes at exact $1$-dependence, while the whole renewal class remains antibunched and low-frequency suppressed. \Cref{fig:spectrum-fano} displays both quantities across the parameter range.

\subsection{Finite-record inference}\label{subsec:finite-sample}
Finite records suggest practical diagnostics for both thresholds. For a binary record $(A_n)_{n=0}^{N-1}$, set
\[
\widehat{h}_k=
\frac{\#\{\text{runs of exactly }k\text{ zeros}\}}
     {\#\{\text{runs of at least }k\text{ zeros}\}},
\]
\[
\widehat{\gamma}_n=
\frac{1}{N-n}\sum_k A_kA_{k+n}-\widehat\rho^2,
\qquad
\widehat F=
\frac{\widehat{\Var}(N_B)}{\widehat{\mathbb E}N_B},
\]
where $\widehat h_k$ is the empirical hazard, $\widehat\gamma_n$ the empirical lag-$n$ covariance, and $\widehat F$ the block Fano factor for block size $B$. By renewal and ergodic arguments, all three converge almost surely to their population values.

Appendix~\ref{app:monte} records the simulation protocol used for the numerical statements quoted below, including the seeded pseudorandom sampler, the inverse-CDF construction for zero-run lengths, the block sizes, the burn-in, and the number of replicated records at each parameter point.

For the memory shell, the most immediate indicator is the sign of $\widehat\gamma_2$, which changes at $\Gamma_c$. We use this only as a finite-sample heuristic. For $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=5$ and $\Delta\tau=0.3$ (so $\Gamma_c\approx2.09$), Monte Carlo records generated directly from the analytic law show that the sign of $\widehat\gamma_2$ is reliable away from the threshold and degrades close to it. Over $5\times10^3$ synthetic records, the sign agreed with the true one about $81\%$ of the time at $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=5\times10^3$ and $99.7\%$ of the time at $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=5\times10^4$ when $\Gamma=1.5\Gamma_c$. At $\Gamma=1.1\Gamma_c$ the corresponding frequencies were about $57\%$ and $72\%$, rising to roughly $80\%$ only near $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=10^5$. For this reason the end-to-end shell estimation experiment in \cref{subsec:noisy-inverse} uses the benchmark equations for $\widehat p$ and $\widehat\rho$ rather than a bare covariance-sign test.

For the exact-clock shell, the natural record-level diagnostic is
\[
\widehat p=\frac{\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}}{\#\{A_n=0\}},
\]
which targets the benchmark condition $\widehat p\approx\varphi^{-1}$ in the ideal-blanking mode.

The renewal regime also admits a theorem-level rate-identification statement because the complete inter-click intervals are i.i.d. Let
\[
Y_j=G_j+1,\qquad j\ge1,
\]
denote the successive inter-click intervals extracted from the binary record. These variables are directly observable once the record has been segmented into complete zero-runs.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:gap-inference-main}
Fix $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ in the finite-recovery detector. Let
\[
\bar Y_m=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=1}^m Y_j
\]
be the empirical mean of $m$ complete inter-click intervals. Then the following statements hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The moment generating function of $Y_1$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-mgf-main}
M_\Gamma(t)=\EE_\Gamma e^{tY_1}
=
\frac{e^t}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
\left(
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)}{1-pe^t}
-
\frac{\Gamma(1-c)}{1-ce^t}
\right),
\qquad
t<-\log\max\{p,c\}.
\end{equation}
If
\[
\mu(\Gamma)=\EE_\Gamma Y_1=\frac{1}{\rho(\Gamma)},
\qquad
\sigma_Y^2(\Gamma)=\Var_\Gamma(Y_1),
\]
then $\mu$ is $C^\infty$ and strictly decreasing on $(0,\infty)$.
\item The estimator
\[
\widehat\Gamma_m=\mu^{-1}(\bar Y_m)
\]
is well defined with probability tending to one and satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-clt-main}
\sqrt m\,(\widehat\Gamma_m-\Gamma)
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal N\!\left(0,\frac{\sigma_Y^2(\Gamma)}{\mu'(\Gamma)^2}\right).
\end{equation}
\item The empirical means $\bar Y_m$ satisfy a good large-deviation principle with rate function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-rate-main}
J_\Gamma(y)=\sup_{t<-\log\max\{p,c\}}
\bigl\{ty-\log M_\Gamma(t)\bigr\},
\end{equation}
and the estimator $\widehat\Gamma_m$ satisfies the contracted large-deviation principle with rate function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-rate-gamma-main}
I_\Gamma(x)=J_\Gamma(\mu(x)).
\end{equation}
\item If $\Gamma\neq\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, then the shell-side classifier $\widehat\Gamma_m\lessgtr\Gamma_c$ has exponentially small wrong-side probability:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-error-main}
\PP_\Gamma(\widehat\Gamma_m\le\Gamma_c)
=
\exp\!\bigl(-mI_\Gamma(\Gamma_c)+o(m)\bigr)
\qquad (\Gamma>\Gamma_c),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-error-below-main}
\PP_\Gamma(\widehat\Gamma_m\ge\Gamma_c)
=
\exp\!\bigl(-mI_\Gamma(\Gamma_c)+o(m)\bigr)
\qquad (\Gamma<\Gamma_c).
\end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

Theorem~\ref{thm:gap-inference-main} turns the renewal law into a finite-record rate-identification statement for the memory shell. At any fixed spacetime point off $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the wrong-side classification probability decays exponentially in the number of observed complete gaps. The proof is given in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

\section{Shell equations in static KMS spacetimes}\label{sec:kms-shells}

We now combine the record-level thresholds with a static-KMS response model. The constants $\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ are fixed by the sampled-counter analysis; the shell construction follows by inserting the local excitation rate into those benchmark conditions. From this point on, the argument is algebraic once the response law has been chosen.

Let $N(x)$ be the lapse function of a static spacetime and assume the field is in a global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state for Killing time. Along a static worldline at position $x$, Tolman scaling gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-main}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
At weak coupling, the excitation rate of a detector with energy gap $\Omega$ takes the Planck form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-rate-main}
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\end{equation}
where
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\frac{1}{\pi}\,\mathrm{Im}\,\mathcal W_x(\Omega+i0^+)
\]
is the local commutator spectral weight along the static worldline, written in terms of the Fourier transform $\mathcal W_x$ of the restricted Wightman function. We assume $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ for $\Omega>0$ in the KMS settings under consideration \cite{KayWald1991}. Throughout, $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ is treated as part of the response model; the homogeneous regime below is the idealization in which its spatial variation is negligible on the region of interest.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:shells-general-main}
Fix a detector design $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ in a static KMS background with $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ and lapse $N(x)>0$ everywhere in the region of interest.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The ideal dead-time detector realizes the Parry-law benchmark exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-phi-general-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\log\varphi
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the exact-clock locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface wherever the gradient of $x\mapsto\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau/(e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1)$ is nonvanishing; otherwise it may fail to be a manifold and we refer to it as the exact-clock locus.

\item The finite-recovery detector reaches the memory-transition point exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-mem-general-main}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the memory-transition locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface under the analogous nonvanishing-gradient condition. On $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Shell existence, uniqueness, and multiplicity]\label{rem:shell-existence}
The loci $\Sigma_\varphi$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ may be empty or disconnected when $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ varies with position. Regularity holds wherever the corresponding defining function has non-vanishing gradient. In the homogeneous regime $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ the shell equations reduce to monotone functions of the lapse alone, so each shell is a lapse level set under the usual condition $dN\neq0$. Outside that regime, the exact comparison between the two shells is provided by the spectral-transport identity of \cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:homogeneous-main}
Assume that $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ on the region under study. Then both shells are pure lapse level sets. Their associated local inverse temperatures are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}
\beta_{\varphi}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-level-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\varphi}\},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

In the homogeneous regime, the exact-clock shell is selected by a grammar law and the memory shell by a dependence law, even though both are built from the same local response scale. \Cref{fig:shell-radii} illustrates the corresponding lapse values and proper-distance ratio in Schwarzschild.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:order-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define
\[
\Gamma_{\varphi}=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}.
\]
Let $N_{\varphi}$ and $N_{\mathrm{mem}}$ denote the lapse values of $\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:order-main}
N_{\varphi}<N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_{\varphi}>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if $\Gamma_{\varphi}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. Thus the shell ordering is detector controlled.
\end{proposition}

\section{Shell comparison and inversion}\label{sec:inverse}

We next compare the two shells generated by one detector design and examine the inverse information carried by the pair. The stochastic input has already been reduced to the thresholds $\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, so the remaining statements concern shell comparison within the chosen response model.

\subsection{Shared-hardware two-mode protocol}\label{subsec:two-mode}
To make the shell comparison operational, we work with a single detector head whose gap $\Omega$, coupling constant $\lambda_c$, spectral weight $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ and sampling step $\Delta\tau$ are fixed. The hardware is then toggled between two readout modes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item \textbf{Mode A (ideal blanking).} After every click, the readout circuit enforces one deterministic blank interval of duration $\Delta\tau$ before the detector is armed again. This realizes the one-step dead-time model of \cref{sec:operational} with transition matrix~\eqref{eq:golden-mean-main} and selects the Parry-law benchmark at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$; we denote the corresponding target rate by $\Gamma_\varphi=\log\varphi/\Delta\tau$.
\item \textbf{Mode B (finite recovery).} The blanking gate is disabled and the same detector runs with an exponential recovery law characterized by $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, matching the finite-recovery model of \cref{sec:operational}. The record is a renewal process with hazard~\eqref{eq:hazard-main}, and the exact $1$-dependence threshold occurs at $\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\end{itemize}

Operationally, the two modes correspond to ``forced reset'' and ``free recovery'' on the same sensor. Both therefore inherit the same response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$, and this shared-hardware assumption is precisely what allows the shell-pair ratio to eliminate the otherwise unknown amplitude.

The shell pair thus compares two externally imposed readout policies of the same detector head. Its meaning is operational and protocol dependent; it is not an invariant of a single autonomous detector dynamics.

\subsection{Common-amplitude cancellation and spectral transport}

The most useful shell-pair observable arises when both shells are generated by the same hardware operated in the two-mode protocol of \cref{subsec:two-mode}. Dividing the two shell equations removes the common response amplitude $\lambda_c^2$. In the homogeneous idealization, this leaves a pure lapse relation.

See \cite[Theorem~4.28]{MaZhang2026ShellGeometry}.
\end{theorem}

The cancellation in \cref{thm:ratio-main} is exact but not detector free: the remaining ratio still depends on the chosen sampling design and recovery law, and the conclusion uses the homogeneous idealization $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$. What disappears is the unknown common response amplitude.

\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectral-stability}
Assume the two-mode protocol and suppose that on the region of interest $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)(1+\varepsilon(x))$ with $|\varepsilon(x)|\le\varepsilon_0<1$. Let $R$ denote the shell-ratio observable governed by \cref{cor:spectral-transport-main} and let $C=\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)/\log\varphi$. Then
\[
C\,\frac{1-\varepsilon_0}{1+\varepsilon_0}
\le
R
\le
C\,\frac{1+\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}.
\]
\end{proposition}

This is an immediate consequence of \cref{cor:spectral-transport-main} and the bounds $-\varepsilon_0\le \varepsilon(x)\le\varepsilon_0$. A uniform spectral inhomogeneity of size $\varepsilon_0$ therefore perturbs the homogeneous ratio by at most the multiplicative factor $\frac{1+\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}$.

Pointwise ratio control does not by itself locate the displaced shells. In static spherical settings one can nevertheless recover that information when the logarithmic spectral drift is subordinate to the Tolman drift. The next theorem gives a quantitative comparison principle for this regime.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:spectral-drift-main}
Assume a static spherically symmetric KMS background on a radial interval $I=[r_-,r_+]$, with lapse $N\in C^1(I)$ satisfying $N'(r)>0$. Fix detector parameters $(\beta_\infty,\Omega,\lambda_c)$ and define
\[
L(r)=\log\!\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r)}-1\bigr),
\qquad
\Gamma_+(r)=\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S(r)}{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r)}-1},
\]
where $\mathcal S(r)=\mathcal S_{x(r)}(\Omega)$ is positive and belongs to $C^1(I)$. Suppose that for some $\eta\in[0,1)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:log-slope-control-main}
\left|\frac{d}{dr}\log\mathcal S(r)\right|
\le
\eta\,L'(r),
\qquad r\in I.
\end{equation}
Then the following statements hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The map $r\mapsto\Gamma_+(r)$ is strictly decreasing on $I$. Consequently every threshold $T$ in the image $\Gamma_+(I)$ is realized at a unique radius $r(T)\in I$.
\item For any thresholds $T_1,T_2\in\Gamma_+(I)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:threshold-comparison-main}
\frac{1}{1+\eta}\left|\log\frac{T_1}{T_2}\right|
\le
\left|L(r(T_1))-L(r(T_2))\right|
\le
\frac{1}{1-\eta}\left|\log\frac{T_1}{T_2}\right|.
\end{equation}
\item Let $I_{12}$ denote the closed interval with endpoints $r(T_1)$ and $r(T_2)$, and assume $T_1\neq T_2$. The left equality in~\eqref{eq:threshold-comparison-main} holds if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:extremal-plus-main}
\mathcal S(r)=K\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r)}-1\bigr)^{\eta}
\qquad\text{for all }r\in I_{12}
\end{equation}
for some constant $K>0$. The right equality in~\eqref{eq:threshold-comparison-main} holds if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:extremal-minus-main}
\mathcal S(r)=K\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r)}-1\bigr)^{-\eta}
\qquad\text{for all }r\in I_{12}
\end{equation}
for some constant $K>0$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:greybody-control-main}
Assume the hypotheses of \cref{thm:spectral-drift-main}. Suppose that the two record thresholds
\[
\Gamma_\varphi=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau},
\qquad
\Gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\]
belong to $\Gamma_+(I)$, and write
\[
r_\varphi=r(\Gamma_\varphi),
\qquad
r_{\mathrm{mem}}=r(\Gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}),
\qquad
C=\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\log\varphi}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:greybody-ratio-band-main}
\min\!\left\{C^{1/(1-\eta)},\,C^{1/(1+\eta)}\right\}
\le
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
\le
\max\!\left\{C^{1/(1-\eta)},\,C^{1/(1+\eta)}\right\}.
\end{equation}
If, in addition, $\mathcal S(r)=\mathcal S_0e^{\sigma(r)}$ on $I$ with $\sup_I|\sigma|\le\varepsilon$, and if for $T\in\{\Gamma_\varphi,\Gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}\}$ the homogeneous reference shell $r_0(T)\in I$ is defined by
\[
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_0}{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_0(T))}-1}
=
T,
\]
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:greybody-log-displacement-main}
\left|L(r(T))-L(r_0(T))\right|\le\varepsilon.
\end{equation}
Moreover, if
\[
m_T:=\inf_{r\in I_T}L'(r)>0,
\]
where $I_T$ is the closed interval with endpoints $r(T)$ and $r_0(T)$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:greybody-radius-displacement-main}
\left|r(T)-r_0(T)\right|\le\frac{\varepsilon}{m_T}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

The proofs of \cref{thm:spectral-drift-main,cor:greybody-control-main} are recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

\subsection{Near-horizon asymptotics}

The shell-pair ratio is particularly transparent near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:horizon-main}
Assume the shared-hardware two-mode protocol and the homogeneous spectral-weight regime $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$. Suppose, in addition, that the static region ends at a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-main}
N(\ell)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\ell+O(\ell^3)
\end{equation}
as the proper distance $\ell\downarrow0$ from the horizon. Then the shell distances satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ell-ratio-main}
\frac{\ell_{\varphi}}{\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\Bigl(1+O(\ell_{\varphi}+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
In Schwarzschild, if $A_H=16\pi M^2$ is the horizon area and $A_{\varphi}$ and $A_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are the areas of the shell spheres, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:area-ratio-main}
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left[
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\right]^2
\Bigl(1+O(\ell_{\varphi}+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The derivation is a straightforward near-horizon expansion of the lapse combined with the shell-ratio law. We record the details in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}, with additional algebra in the Supplementary Material.

Theorem~\ref{thm:horizon-main} gives a leading-order relation between two detector-defined stretched-horizon distances in the shared-hardware homogeneous model. One shell is defined by the Parry benchmark and the other by exact $1$-dependence, but their near-horizon separation is governed at leading order by a ratio independent of the common response amplitude.

A particularly informative regime is the fast-sampling limit. Let $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\downarrow0$ and $L=\log(1/s)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-main}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-ratio-main}
\frac{\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\ell_{\varphi}}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus the memory-transition shell approaches the horizon parametrically faster than the exact-clock shell. The shell pair therefore exhibits a parametric hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales within the model.

\subsection{Static spherical inversion in the homogeneous model}

The shell pair also carries direct inverse information in static spherical settings, under the homogeneous spectral-weight assumption $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$. In realistic black-hole detector problems greybody factors and mode sums typically make the response weight position dependent, so the formulas in this subsection and in \cref{subsec:worked-example} are exact only within that idealized model. \Cref{subsec:nonhomogeneous} returns to the general case.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:schw-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define the detector constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-constants-main}
\beta_{\varphi}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right).
\end{equation}
If the two shells generated by the same detector design occur at distinct radii $r_{\varphi}\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in Schwarzschild, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-infty-main}
\beta_\infty^2=
\frac{\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_{\varphi}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mass-main}
M=
\frac{r_{\varphi}r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)}
{2\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:schw-main} is an exact algebraic statement within the homogeneous model. It does not remove detector dependence, but it does show that two record-defined shell locations determine the global redshift scale once the detector design is fixed. A related local two-mode identifiability criterion is standard: if $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ parametrizes a $C^1$ two-parameter family of static models and, for two operating modes $D_1,D_2$, the corresponding shell-ratio constraints are written as
\[
F_j(\theta)=0,
\qquad
j=1,2,
\]
with $F_j\in C^1(\Theta)$, then any solution $\theta_0$ satisfying
\[
F_1(\theta_0)=F_2(\theta_0)=0,
\qquad
\det D_\theta(F_1,F_2)\big|_{\theta_0}\neq0,
\]
is locally unique and depends $C^1$ on the shell data by the implicit function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002}. We record this only as a standard identifiability criterion within the fixed response model.

\begin{remark}[Jacobian conditioning and error propagation]\label{rem:jacobian-main}
For a mode $D=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$, write
\[
C(D):=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\]
In this local two-mode criterion, a basic degeneracy occurs when the two operating modes satisfy $C(D_1)=C(D_2)$. Since $C(D)$ depends only on $(\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$, this is a codimension-one condition in the mode parameter space and is generically avoided by choosing modes with distinct sampling intervals.

For the single-mode Schwarzschild inverse of \cref{prop:schw-main}, the system is linear in $(u,v)=(\beta_\infty^2,\,2M\beta_\infty^2)$ with coefficient matrix $A=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}r_\varphi & -1\\ r_{\mathrm{mem}} & -1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ and $\det A=-(r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}})$. The condition number is $\kappa(A)=\|A\|\|A^{-1}\|$, which diverges as the shells approach each other. For the worked example of \cref{tab:worked} with mode~$D_1$, $\kappa(A)\approx111$: this is moderate conditioning. A first-order error propagation gives: a $1\%$ relative error on the shell radii $(r_\varphi,r_{\mathrm{mem}})$ produces a ${\sim}38\%$ relative error on $\beta_\infty^2$ but only a ${\sim}1.2\%$ relative error on $M$. The mass is well conditioned because it involves the ratio $r_\varphi r_{\mathrm{mem}}/(r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}})$, which is less sensitive to correlated perturbations of the two radii. Improving the conditioning requires wider shell separation, which can be achieved by increasing the product $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ (pushing the memory shell farther from the exact-clock shell).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Schwarzschild worked example}\label{subsec:worked-example}

\Cref{tab:worked} gives a simple algebraic check in Schwarzschild with $r_{\mathrm s}=2M=1$ (so $\beta_\infty=4\pi$), $\Omega=1$, and $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S=2$. The two detector modes $D_1=(\Omega,\lambda_c,0.5,\,8.0)$ and $D_2=(\Omega,\lambda_c,1.2,\,3.0)$ have distinct products $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and therefore distinct shell-ratio constants, which is the simplest generic mechanism avoiding the basic Jacobian degeneracy discussed in \cref{rem:jacobian-main}. For each mode, $\Gamma_c$ is obtained from~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main} and cross-checked against the Lambert $W$ expression~\eqref{eq:lambert-Gc}; the shell radii follow from \cref{cor:homogeneous-main}. Applying \cref{prop:schw-main} to the $D_1$ shell pair then recovers $\beta_\infty$ and $M$ exactly within the model.

\subsection{Shell estimation from finite records}\label{subsec:noisy-inverse}

The algebraic inversion of \cref{prop:schw-main} becomes operational only once the shell radii have themselves been estimated from records. We therefore complement the algebraic formulas with an end-to-end pipeline in the homogeneous Schwarzschild model. Using the mode-$D_1$ design of \cref{tab:worked}, we simulated records at five radii around $r_\varphi$ and five radii around $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$, each of length $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=1.2\times10^5$ sampled intervals after a burn-in of $5\times10^3$ intervals; Appendix~\ref{app:monte} gives the algorithm.

For the exact-clock shell we estimated
\[
\widehat p(r)=\frac{\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}}{\#\{A_n=0\}}
\]
in the ideal-blanking mode and linearly interpolated the zero of $\widehat p(r)-\varphi^{-1}$. For the memory shell we used the finite-recovery mode, estimated the empirical click fraction $\widehat\rho(r)$, and then solved the explicit renewal-rate equation $\rho(\Gamma)=\widehat\rho(r)$ with $\rho(\Gamma)$ given by \eqref{eq:rho-main}; this yields a monotone estimator $\widehat\Gamma(r)$ of the local input rate. The shell estimator $\widehat r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ was then obtained by interpolating the zero of $\widehat\Gamma(r)-\Gamma_c$. This rate-matching step uses the full renewal model and is markedly more stable in finite data than a raw sign test on $\widehat\gamma_2$.

Across $120$ independent Monte Carlo replicates, all of which bracketed both shell roots, we obtained
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=0.2em]
\item $\widehat r_\varphi/r_{\mathrm s}=1.008077\pm0.000047$ and $\widehat r_{\mathrm{mem}}/r_{\mathrm s}=1.045097\pm0.000304$, to be compared with the exact shell locations $1.008069$ and $1.045109$.
\item $\widehat M=0.500006\pm0.000044$ and $\widehat\beta_\infty^2=158.00\pm1.27$, to be compared with the exact values $M=0.5$ and $\beta_\infty^2=157.9137$.
\end{itemize}
This experiment validates the record-to-shell-to-inverse pipeline at the Monte Carlo level within the homogeneous model. It does not provide a CLT or optimality theory for the shell estimators, nor should it be read as a detector-independent metrological claim. It does show that, once the response model is fixed, the finite-record benchmark equations recover the shell pair accurately enough for the model-conditional inverse formulas to behave as predicted by the conditioning analysis of \cref{rem:jacobian-main}.

\subsection{Illustrative spectral-weight inhomogeneity}\label{subsec:nonhomogeneous}

The homogeneous regime $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is an idealization. In a realistic Schwarzschild detector problem the response weight is position dependent because of greybody factors, mode sums and detector-profile effects \cite{Page1976Emission,ArbeyCalzaPerez2025}. Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral-drift-main} and Corollary~\ref{cor:greybody-control-main} convert that variation into shell-position bounds when the logarithmic spectral drift is controlled. For the illustrative family
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)\bigl(1+\alpha(r(x)/r_H-1)\bigr),
\]
with $\alpha>0$ parametrizing a linear greybody enhancement away from the horizon, the mode-$D_1$ Schwarzschild example satisfies~\eqref{eq:log-slope-control-main} on the interval between the homogeneous shell radii with
\[
\eta\le1.66\times10^{-2}.
\]
Consequently~\eqref{eq:greybody-ratio-band-main} yields
\[
0.15989
\le
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi^{(\alpha)})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}}^{(\alpha)})}-1}
\le
0.16972,
\]
whereas the exact nonlinear shell pair gives
\[
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi^{(\alpha)})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}}^{(\alpha)})}-1}
\approx0.16177.
\]
The spectral-weight ratio between the exact-clock and memory-transition shells is
\[
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
=
\frac{1+\alpha(r_\varphi/r_H-1)}{1+\alpha(r_{\mathrm{mem}}/r_H-1)}
=
\frac{1+0.5\times0.008069}{1+0.5\times0.045109}
\approx0.982,
\]
so the spectral-transport factor acquires a multiplier $\approx0.982$ relative to the homogeneous shell-ratio constant $0.1648$ for mode~$D_1$. The displacement bound~\eqref{eq:greybody-radius-displacement-main} gives
\[
|r_\varphi^{(\alpha)}-r_\varphi^{(0)}|
\le
3.97\times10^{-5},
\qquad
|r_{\mathrm{mem}}^{(\alpha)}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}^{(0)}|
\le
7.68\times10^{-4},
\]
while direct solution of the deformed shell equations gives
\[
|r_\varphi^{(\alpha)}-r_\varphi^{(0)}|
\approx
3.96\times10^{-5},
\qquad
|r_{\mathrm{mem}}^{(\alpha)}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}^{(0)}|
\approx
7.63\times10^{-4}.
\]
Thus the linear greybody deformation leaves the exact-clock shell nearly fixed while producing an order-of-magnitude larger outward shift of the memory shell.

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec:discussion}

The sampled-counter model considered here shows that a stationary click record can carry operational information beyond the mean excitation rate. Two selectors emerge within the same response family: a grammar-based selector in the ideal blanking mode and a dependence-based selector in the recovery mode. Both are functions of the same local excitation rate, so they are not detector-independent invariants of the spacetime, but they do distinguish genuinely different record-level regimes.

In the recovery model the Parry benchmark is excluded at every positive sampling interval because the hazard remains strictly increasing, even though adjacent clicks are quadratically suppressed as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$. The same renewal structure yields explicit formulas for the zero-run law, covariance profile, spectral density and counting noise, and it keeps the record strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range considered. In the broader two-state interval class, the rank-one memory transition persists as a non-Bernoulli exact $1$-dependence condition, while Bernoulli reduction and hazard monotonicity depend on the sector.

The stochastic core also admits a converse classification: exact dependence order $m$ in any stationary reset-renewal record is equivalent to a rational inter-click law of degree $m+1$. This identifies the present exact $1$-dependence threshold as the first non-Bernoulli member of a finite-dependence hierarchy, rather than as an isolated property of the two-state calculation.

After insertion into a static-KMS response law, these record thresholds become detector-defined shell equations. For a shared detector operated in the two-mode protocol of \cref{subsec:two-mode}, the homogeneous spectral-weight regime yields an exact cancellation of the common response amplitude, while the general case gives the spectral-transport identity of \cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}. In static spherical settings, controlled logarithmic spectral drift leads to quantitative shell-separation and displacement bounds, and near a nonextremal horizon it produces a detector-dependent hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales. The resulting inverse formulas remain conditional on the chosen detector-response model, but within that framework they provide a concrete route from full-record statistics to shell-pair geometry.

Several limitations remain. The analysis assumes weak coupling, a two-level detector, one stationary worldline, discrete readout and, for the cleanest inverse formulas, homogeneous spectral weight. The shell pair compares two externally chosen operating modes of the same detector/counter assembly rather than two outputs of a single autonomous detector dynamics. Adaptive shell localization, paralyzable counters and a microscopic derivation from detector dynamics lie beyond the present model. Within that scope, however, the results identify a compact set of record-level thresholds and show how they propagate into shell geometry in static KMS spacetimes.
