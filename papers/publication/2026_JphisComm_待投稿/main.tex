\begin{abstract}
We consider a sampled counter driven by the stationary excitation rate of an Unruh--DeWitt detector and ask what is gained by retaining the binary click record rather than only its mean rate. The detector input and the readout electronics are kept separate: the UDW detector fixes the stationary excitation rate, while dead time and recovery are modelled by an interval click-register instrument. In the ideal one-step dead-time mode the sampled record is a golden-mean Markov chain, and the Parry measure is realized at the exact threshold $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. In the exponential-recovery mode the observed record is a renewal process; we obtain the zero-run law, show that the click hazard is strictly increasing, derive the covariance formula, and identify a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ at which the record is exactly $1$-dependent. Finite recovery therefore does not reproduce the Parry benchmark at any positive sampling interval, although adjacent clicks are quadratically suppressed as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$, and the record remains antibunched with sub-Poissonian counting noise. In static KMS spacetimes these record-selected rates define two detector-dependent shell equations. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the associated shell pair satisfies an exact amplitude-cancellation law; in the general case it obeys a spectral-transport identity. The inverse statements are correspondingly model-conditional.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Unruh--DeWitt detector, KMS state, static spacetime, symbolic dynamics, renewal process

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates, detailed balance and Tolman scaling are the standard observables in Unruh--DeWitt detector theory \cite{Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,Takagi1986,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. A sampled detector, however, does not output a rate. It outputs a binary click record. Once dead time or recovery is included, that record carries support constraints, waiting-time statistics and lag correlations that are not captured by the mean click frequency alone. The question considered here is whether such record-level statistics pick out operationally distinguished rates within a fixed stationary response family.

This question is relevant because any realistic counting device is temporal. A refractory interval or a finite recovery law introduces serial dependence into the observed record, and that dependence need not disappear when the local excitation rate is held fixed. If those additional observables can be transplanted to stationary KMS settings, detector design enters not merely as a nuisance parameter but as part of the operational map from click statistics to spacetime geometry.

The present work sits between several established strands of the literature. In the UDW setting the primary observables are still the response rate and its dependence on switching and detector profile \cite{LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. Detector-based measurement frameworks clarify how records are defined in relativistic quantum field theory \cite{Fewster2020,PranziniEtAl2024,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click}. Detector thermalization studies address the approach to Gibbs behaviour \cite{FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016}, while metrological proposals extract geometric information from rates or state functionals \cite{TjoaMann2020,BraunsteinCaves1994,PercheMartinMartinez2022}. On the stochastic side, waiting-time methods, antibunching diagnostics and renewal-noise formulas provide the closest precedents \cite{CarmichaelWaiting1989,KimbleAntibunching1977,Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014}. Dead time and recovery are, of course, standard features of real counters \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975}.

We therefore adopt a deliberately narrow model. The stationary UDW detector supplies the excitation input rate, while the readout electronics are represented by an interval click-register instrument with either deterministic one-step blanking or exponential recovery. Within that explicit sampled-counter model the full stationary record singles out two benchmarks. In the ideal mode, the benchmark is realization of the Parry measure on the golden-mean grammar and occurs at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. In the recovery mode, the benchmark is exact $1$-dependence and occurs at a unique rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. To the best of our knowledge, the closed renewal law, strict hazard monotonicity, explicit covariance formula and exact $1$-dependence threshold for this sampled finite-recovery instrument have not previously been isolated in the detector literature.

These benchmarks should be kept distinct from more familiar thermal criteria. Detailed balance concerns the rate ratio, and Gibbs thermalization concerns the asymptotic detector state. By contrast, the Parry-law condition is a statement about the full word distribution of the sampled record. In the finite-recovery mode the relevant selector is different again: the record has full symbolic support, and the distinguished point is the rate at which dependence collapses beyond one step. The two selectors are therefore genuinely inequivalent even though both are built from the same local excitation rate.

Two additional features make the recovery regime operationally useful. First, the approach to the ideal grammar is soft rather than exact: adjacent clicks are quadratically suppressed as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$, but the Parry benchmark is excluded at every positive sampling interval because the hazard remains strictly increasing. Secondly, the stationary record is antibunched throughout the parameter range; its low-frequency spectral density is suppressed and the asymptotic counting Fano factor stays below one.

Once the local response rate is inserted, these record-level thresholds become detector-defined shell equations in a static KMS background. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the two shells reduce to lapse level sets and obey an exact common-amplitude cancellation law. This leads to a detector-dependent shell-pair observable, a near-horizon hierarchy law and model-conditional inverse statements in static spherical settings. When the spectral weight varies with position, the same comparison survives as an exact spectral-transport identity.

The scope is correspondingly limited. We work with a single stationary worldline, weak coupling, discrete readout with step $\Delta\tau$, and exponential recovery in the non-ideal mode. The shells obtained below are detector-defined hypersurfaces, not detector-independent invariants of the spacetime. The inverse consequences are likewise conditional on the response model and are not intended as generic metric reconstruction results. We also distinguish this use of Tolman scaling in a fixed detector model from broader notions of local thermal equilibrium in curved spacetime \cite{BuchholzSchlemmer2007,Solveen2012,GranseePinamontiVerch2017}.

For convenience, the main results may be summarized as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item In the ideal dead-time mode the sampled record is a golden-mean Markov chain, and the Parry-law benchmark occurs at the exact threshold $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$.
\item In the exponential-recovery mode the sampled record is a full-support renewal process with strictly increasing hazard, and exact $1$-dependence occurs at a unique rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\item The recovery mode approaches the ideal grammar only softly and remains strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range.
\item In static KMS backgrounds the two record-selected rates define a shell pair which obeys an exact amplitude-cancellation law in the homogeneous regime and an exact spectral-transport identity in the general case.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organized as follows. \Cref{sec:operational,sec:records} introduce the sampled-counter model and derive the record-level thresholds together with their operational signatures. \Cref{sec:kms-shells,sec:inverse} insert those thresholds into a static KMS response model, derive the shell equations, and examine shell-pair ratios and inverse consequences. Appendices~\ref{app:state-diagram}--\ref{app:monte} collect the interval instrument, technical derivations and simulation protocol; additional calculations are recorded in the Supplementary Material.

\section{Detector model and record observables}\label{sec:operational}

We consider a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector sampled at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. The visible output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ denotes a click in the $n$th sampling interval. Compressing this record to the empirical click frequency recovers only the one-point observable
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n.
\]
The full record carries additional information. Natural observables include:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item what words belong to the support of the stationary law;
\item how the zero-run lengths between clicks are distributed;
\item whether the click hazard after a long run of zeros remembers the run length;
\item how the low-lag covariance profile behaves;
\item what counting noise remains after coarse time averaging.
\end{enumerate}
If a click is followed by $G$ consecutive zeros before the next click, then $G$ is the \emph{zero-run length}. Its hazard $h_k$ is the conditional probability that the next sample clicks after exactly $k$ zeros since the previous click. In the ideal mode, the \emph{Parry-law benchmark} means that the stationary record coincides with the Parry measure on the golden-mean grammar. In the recovery mode, \emph{exact $1$-dependence} means that blocks of samples separated by at least one intervening sample are independent. These are the two record-level conditions that later lift to shell equations.

The two detector regimes studied below are shown schematically in \cref{fig:scheme}. The ideal mode imposes a hard symbolic grammar, whereas finite recovery produces a full-support renewal law. The corresponding thresholds are therefore of different type: one is grammar-based, the other is dependence-based.

\section{Record laws and operational thresholds}\label{sec:records}

\subsection{Sampled counter model}\label{subsec:phenom-model}
The detector model consists of a sampled counter with two hidden states, $R$ (ready) and $D$ (refractory), together with an interval click register. At the beginning of each interval $[n\Delta\tau,(n+1)\Delta\tau]$ the register is reset to $0$. If the counter is ready, an excitation supplied by the stationary UDW rate $\Gamma_+\equiv\Gamma$ flips the register irreversibly to $1$ during that interval. If the counter begins in $D$, it recovers to $R$ at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ and may then click later in the same interval. At the end of the interval only the register is read out:
\[
A_n=
\begin{cases}
1,&\text{the interval register fired},\\
0,&\text{the interval register remained silent}.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus $A_n$ is not a projective measurement of the detector's internal two-level system. It is the output of an explicit interval instrument in which the hidden counter state and the visible register are kept separate; Appendix~\ref{app:gkls} records one consistent matrix realization.

The post-readout update is equally important. If $A_n=1$, the next interval starts in the refractory state $D$. If $A_n=0$, the hidden end-of-interval state is carried into the next interval without further reset. This carryover rule is what generates the serial dependence of the observed record. In particular, it makes click times regeneration times in the recovery mode, which is the source of the renewal structure used below.

Concretely, the detector dynamics within a single interval $[0,\Delta\tau]$ are as follows (see also the state diagram in \cref{app:state-diagram}):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item If the detector begins the interval in state $R$ (ready): during $[0,\Delta\tau]$ it may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited, the interval ends in state $D$ and registers a click ($A_n=1$). If not excited, the interval ends in state $R$ and registers no click ($A_n=0$).
\item If the detector begins the interval in state $D$ (refractory): it first recovers to $R$ at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. If recovery occurs at time $s\in[0,\Delta\tau]$, the detector is then ready for the remaining subinterval $[s,\Delta\tau]$ and may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited after recovery, the interval ends in $D$ with a click. If not excited after recovery, or if recovery does not occur during the interval, the hidden state at the end of the interval is determined by whether recovery happened.
\end{enumerate}
The output-labelled kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ used below are obtained by integrating these continuous-time dynamics over $[0,\Delta\tau]$. The structural point to keep in view is that every click forces the next interval to start in $D$, so observed click times are regeneration points of the hidden process.

\emph{Model status.}
The kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ define a \emph{phenomenological counter model} driven by the stationary UDW excitation rate $\Gamma_+$, in the spirit of classical dead-time counting models \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,CarmichaelWaiting1989,KimbleAntibunching1977}. The separation between detector physics and counter electronics is deliberate. In \cref{sec:kms-shells,sec:inverse} static KMS response theory supplies the input rate $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)$, while dead time, recovery and interval readout belong to the counter. No small-parameter assumption is imposed on $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$: the interval probabilities are integrated exactly for all positive $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau$. Appendix~\ref{app:gkls} should therefore be read as a consistency-level interval instrument for the sampled counter, not as a microscopic reduction from the bare detector dynamics over the full parameter range considered here.

\subsection{Ideal dead time and the Parry threshold}

The ideal mode is defined by one deterministic blank interval after each click: once the detector fires, it is kept in the refractory state $D$ for the whole next sampling step and is then returned to the ready state $R$ at the start of the following interval. In particular, $A_{n+1}=0$ whenever $A_n=1$. This is not the limit $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\to0$ of the exponential-recovery model, which would instead trap the detector in the refractory state after the first click. Writing
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-beta-main}
p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}, \qquad q_\beta=1-p_\beta,
\end{equation}
the sampled output is the stationary Markov chain on the golden-mean shift with transition matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-mean-main}
P_\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p_\beta & 1-p_\beta \\
 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The reference law on that shift is the Parry measure, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy compatible with the grammar $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ideal-main}
For the ideal dead-time detector, the sampled output realizes the Parry-law benchmark if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-threshold-main}
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\end{equation}
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ is the golden ratio. This threshold defines the exact-clock selector.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
After a click the detector enters the refractory state $D$. Under the deterministic-recovery rule, the detector remains refractory for the full next interval and the block $11$ is forbidden: any consecutive-click sequence $(A_n,A_{n+1})=(1,1)$ has zero probability. After a zero, the detector is in the ready state $R$ and clicks next with probability $q_\beta=1-p_\beta$. This yields transition matrix~\eqref{eq:golden-mean-main} with the single free parameter $p_\beta$. The Parry measure on the golden-mean shift is the unique stationary measure matching the Parry transition probabilities $P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1}$, $P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2}$, $P(1,0)=1$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. Matching gives $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$ and hence~\eqref{eq:parry-threshold-main}. Full derivations appear in Supplementary Material, section~1.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} identifies the unique sampled rate for which the dead-time record realizes the maximum-entropy law compatible with the forbidden word $11$. When the local excitation rate is later replaced by its KMS expression, this condition becomes the exact-clock shell.

\subsection{Detailed balance, Gibbs thermalization, and the Parry record benchmark}\label{subsec:thermality-notions}
Three distinct criteria are worth separating in UDW detector settings. \emph{Detailed balance}: the rate ratio satisfies $\Gamma_-/\Gamma_+=e^{-\beta\Omega}$, the standard criterion \cite{Unruh1976,BirDav1982}. \emph{Gibbs thermalization}: in the usual weak-coupling analyses, the reduced detector state approaches a thermal fixed point determined by the ambient KMS temperature \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016}. \emph{Parry-law record benchmark}: the stationary click record realizes the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift, the unique measure of maximal entropy for the dead-time grammar $11\notin\mathrm{supp}\,\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. The third criterion is the one studied here. Neither of the first two implies the third, nor conversely. To avoid confusion with detector-state thermality, we use the term \emph{Parry-law benchmark} throughout: it is a record-level benchmark on the full word distribution, not a statement about Gibbs equilibration.

\subsection{Finite recovery, renewal structure, and exact \texorpdfstring{$1$}{1}-dependence}

We now replace deterministic blanking by exponential recovery at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$. Throughout this section, and later when no ambiguity arises, we write $\Gamma\equiv\Gamma_+$ for the excitation rate. Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-c-main}
p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}, \qquad c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-b-main}
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds,
\qquad
b=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\end{equation}
When $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, these integrals evaluate to
$a=[\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)]/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$
and
$b=\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$.
Because every observed click resets the next interval to the refractory state, the visible record is a renewal process whose zero-run lengths are i.i.d. The same reset property also makes the exact $1$-dependence threshold transparent: it occurs when the non-trivial eigenvalue of the hidden two-state transition matrix vanishes, so that dependence beyond one step disappears.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:recovery-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the zero-run length $G$ between successive clicks has law
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-main}
\PP(G=k)=g_k=e_D^{\top}T_0^kT_1\mathbf 1,
\qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t0-t1-main}
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 0 \\
 b & c
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
 0 & 1-p \\
 0 & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
If $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-closed-main}
g_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\end{equation}
and the click hazard after $k$ zeros is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard-main}
h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}
     {\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k},
\qquad k\ge0.
\end{equation}
The hazard is strictly increasing, so the visible process is not an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$.

Let $\rho=\PP(A_0=1)$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda-main}
\lambda=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}-\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}}
{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cov-main}
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},\qquad n\ge1.
\end{equation}
Consequently there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>0$ determined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-c-main}
\Delta\tau=
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma_c},
\end{equation}
such that the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$. Below $\Gamma_c$ all covariances are negative; above $\Gamma_c$ they alternate in sign.
\end{proposition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:stationary-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the stationary hidden-state distribution of $P=T_0+T_1$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stationary-main}
\pi_R=\frac{b}{1-p+b},
\qquad
\pi_D=\frac{1-p}{1-p+b}.
\end{equation}
The stationary click rate is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-main}
\rho=\PP(A_0=1)=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a=\frac{(1-p)(1-c)}{1-p+b},
\end{equation}
and it satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-bounds-main}
a<\rho<1-p.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Solving $\pi P=\pi$ with $\pi_R+\pi_D=1$ gives~\eqref{eq:stationary-main}. Since the click probabilities from $R$ and $D$ are $1-p$ and $a$, respectively,
\[
\rho=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a
=
\frac{b(1-p)+(1-p)a}{1-p+b}
=
\frac{(1-p)(a+b)}{1-p+b}
=
\frac{(1-p)(1-c)}{1-p+b},
\]
which is~\eqref{eq:rho-main}. Also
\[
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds
<
(1-p)\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\,ds
=(1-p)(1-c)
<
1-p.
\]
Because $\rho=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a$ is a strict convex combination of $a$ and $1-p$, with $\pi_R,\pi_D>0$, the bounds~\eqref{eq:rho-bounds-main} follow.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Every click resets the hidden state to $D$ (refractory) deterministically, so click times are regeneration times and the zero-run lengths are i.i.d.; see Appendix~\ref{app:derivations} and Supplementary Material, section~2. Evaluating $e_D^\top T_0^k T_1\mathbf{1}$ using the lower-triangular structure of $T_0$ gives the closed form~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main}. The tail sum $\sum_{j\ge k}g_j=(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$, and computing $h_{k+1}-h_k$ yields the positive quantity $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(p-c)^2p^kc^k/[(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^{k+1}-\Gamma c^{k+1})]$, confirming strict monotonicity. For the covariance: the nontrivial eigenvalue of the aggregate transition matrix $P=T_0+T_1$ is $\lambda=p-b$; two-state spectral decomposition gives~\eqref{eq:cov-main}. Setting $\lambda=0$ and solving for $\Gamma$ yields~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}.
\end{proof}

At the critical line, a direct kernel-level factorization shows that cylinder events separated by one intermediate sample are independent; the full argument is given in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

The memory-transition threshold is therefore genuinely different from the Parry threshold. Since a Bernoulli benchmark on the full two-shift would require constant hazard, the Parry law is excluded throughout the recovery regime. \Cref{fig:hazard-cov} shows both the strictly increasing hazard and the covariance sign change across $\Gamma_c$.

\subsection{Soft approach to the ideal grammar}

Corollary~\ref{cor:no-exact-main} is an exact obstruction, but the recovery record still approaches the ideal grammar in a controlled short-sampling regime.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:soft-main}
In the finite-recovery model,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soft-main}
\PP(A_{n+1}=1\mid A_n=1)=a
=\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2+O((\Delta\tau)^3)
\qquad (\Delta\tau\downarrow0).
\end{equation}
For a fixed window length $m\ge2$, let
\[
V_m=\{(a_0,\dots,a_{m-1})\in\{0,1\}^m:\exists\,k\le m-2,\ a_ka_{k+1}=11\}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:window-main}
\PP\!\bigl((A_0,\dots,A_{m-1})\in V_m\bigr)
\le
\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2\,(m-1)
+O\!\bigl(m(\Delta\tau)^3\bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus, for every fixed $m$, the finite-recovery record concentrates on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:soft-main} explains why the ideal grammar is a singular but physically meaningful limit. At fixed finite window length, inadmissible words become quadratically rare. Yet the Parry-law benchmark is still excluded at any positive sampling interval because the hazard remains strictly increasing and hence genuinely remembers the zero-run length.

\subsection{Spectral density and universal antibunching}

The renewal structure also makes the low-frequency counting noise accessible in closed form. Define the spectral density
\[
f(\theta)=\rho(1-\rho)+2\sum_{n\ge1}\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)\cos(n\theta),
\qquad \theta\in[-\pi,\pi].
\]
Since the covariance sequence is explicit, both the spectral density and the Fano factor can be computed exactly. This is useful because finite recovery suppresses immediate reclicks but does not, a priori, rule out enhanced low-frequency noise.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectrum-main}
For the stationary finite-recovery record,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectrum-main}
f(\theta)=
\rho(1-\rho)
+
2\rho(a-\rho)\,
\frac{\cos\theta-\lambda}{1-2\lambda\cos\theta+\lambda^2},
\end{equation}
and $f$ is strictly increasing on $[0,\pi]$. If
\[
N_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n,
\]
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fano-main}
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{\Var(N_N)}{\EE N_N}
=
1-\rho+\frac{2(a-\rho)}{1-\lambda}
<1.
\end{equation}
Hence the finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range.
\end{proposition}

Operationally, the recovery regime may therefore be characterized by more than the sign of a single covariance: the lag-sign pattern changes at exact $1$-dependence, while the whole renewal class remains antibunched and low-frequency suppressed. \Cref{fig:spectrum-fano} displays both quantities across the parameter range.

\subsection{Finite-record diagnostics}\label{subsec:finite-sample}
Finite records suggest practical diagnostics for both thresholds, although we do not attempt a central-limit theorem, optimality theory or confidence-interval analysis here. Given a binary record $(A_0,\dots,A_{N-1})$, define
\[
\widehat{h}_k=
\frac{\#\{\text{runs of exactly }k\text{ zeros}\}}
     {\#\{\text{runs of at least }k\text{ zeros}\}},
\]
\[
\widehat{\gamma}_n=
\frac{1}{N-n}\sum_k A_kA_{k+n}-\widehat\rho^2,
\qquad
\widehat F=
\frac{\widehat{\Var}(N_B)}{\widehat{\mathbb E}N_B},
\]
where $\widehat h_k$ is the empirical hazard, $\widehat\gamma_n$ the empirical lag-$n$ covariance, and $\widehat F$ the block Fano factor for block size $B$. By renewal and ergodic arguments, all three converge almost surely to their population values.

Appendix~\ref{app:monte} records the exact simulation protocol behind the numerical statements quoted below: the seeded pseudorandom sampling scheme, the inverse-CDF sampler for zero-run lengths, the block sizes, the burn-in discarded before estimation, and the number of replicated records for each parameter point.

For the memory shell, the simplest indicator is the sign of $\widehat\gamma_2$, which changes at $\Gamma_c$. We use this only as empirical guidance. For $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=5$ and $\Delta\tau=0.3$ (so $\Gamma_c\approx2.09$), Monte Carlo records generated directly from the analytic law show that the sign of $\widehat\gamma_2$ is reliable away from the threshold and deteriorates in its immediate neighbourhood. Over $5\times10^3$ synthetic records, the sign agreed with the true one about $81\%$ of the time at $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=5\times10^3$ and $99.7\%$ of the time at $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=5\times10^4$ when $\Gamma=1.5\Gamma_c$. At $\Gamma=1.1\Gamma_c$ the same frequencies were about $57\%$ and $72\%$, rising to roughly $80\%$ only near $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=10^5$. For this reason the end-to-end shell estimation experiment in \cref{subsec:noisy-inverse} uses the benchmark equations for $\widehat p$ and $\widehat\rho$ rather than a bare covariance-sign rule.

For the exact-clock shell, the natural record-level diagnostic is
\[
\widehat p=\frac{\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}}{\#\{A_n=0\}},
\]
which targets the benchmark condition $\widehat p\approx\varphi^{-1}$ in the ideal-blanking mode.

\section{Static KMS shell equations}\label{sec:kms-shells}

We now insert the record-level thresholds into a static KMS response model. The constants $\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ are fixed before geometry enters; the shell construction follows by substituting the local excitation rate into those benchmark conditions.

Let $N(x)$ be the lapse function of a static spacetime and assume the field is in a global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state for Killing time. Along a static worldline at position $x$, Tolman scaling gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-main}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
At weak coupling, the excitation rate of a detector with energy gap $\Omega$ takes the Planck form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-rate-main}
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\end{equation}
where
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\frac{1}{\pi}\,\mathrm{Im}\,\mathcal W_x(\Omega+i0^+)
\]
is the local commutator spectral weight along the static worldline, written in terms of the Fourier transform $\mathcal W_x$ of the restricted Wightman function. We assume $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ for $\Omega>0$ in the KMS settings under consideration \cite{KayWald1991}. Throughout, $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ is treated as part of the response model; the homogeneous regime below is the idealization in which its spatial variation is negligible on the region of interest.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:shells-general-main}
Fix a detector design $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ in a static KMS background with $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ and lapse $N(x)>0$ everywhere in the region of interest.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The ideal dead-time detector realizes the Parry-law benchmark exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-phi-general-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\log\varphi
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the exact-clock locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface wherever the gradient of $x\mapsto\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau/(e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1)$ is nonvanishing; otherwise it may fail to be a manifold and we refer to it as the exact-clock locus.

\item The finite-recovery detector reaches the memory-transition point exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-mem-general-main}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the memory-transition locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface under the analogous nonvanishing-gradient condition. On $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Shell existence, uniqueness, and multiplicity]\label{rem:shell-existence}
The loci $\Sigma_\varphi$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ may be empty or disconnected when $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ varies with position. Regularity holds wherever the corresponding defining function has non-vanishing gradient. In the homogeneous regime $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ the shell equations reduce to monotone functions of the lapse alone, so each shell is a lapse level set under the usual condition $dN\neq0$. Outside that regime, the exact comparison between the two shells is provided by the spectral-transport identity of \cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:homogeneous-main}
Assume that $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ on the region under study. Then both shells are pure lapse level sets. Their associated local inverse temperatures are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}
\beta_{\varphi}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-level-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\varphi}\},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

In this regime, the exact-clock shell is selected by a grammar law and the memory shell by a dependence law, even though both are built from the same local response scale. \Cref{fig:shell-radii} illustrates the resulting lapse values and proper-distance ratio in Schwarzschild.

\section{Shell-pair ratios and model-conditional inverses}\label{sec:inverse}

We next compare the two shells generated by one detector design and examine the inverse information carried by the pair. The stochastic input has already been distilled into the thresholds $\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$; what remains in this section is geometry within the response model. All inverse statements should therefore be read in that conditional sense.

\subsection{Two-mode protocol with shared hardware}\label{subsec:two-mode}
To make the shell comparison operational, we work with a single detector head whose gap $\Omega$, coupling constant $\lambda_c$, spectral weight $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ and sampling step $\Delta\tau$ are fixed. The hardware is then toggled between two readout modes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item \textbf{Mode A (ideal blanking).} After every click, the readout circuit enforces one deterministic blank interval of duration $\Delta\tau$ before the detector is armed again. This realizes the one-step dead-time model of \cref{sec:operational} with transition matrix~\eqref{eq:golden-mean-main} and selects the Parry-law benchmark at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$; we denote the corresponding target rate by $\Gamma_\varphi=\log\varphi/\Delta\tau$.
\item \textbf{Mode B (finite recovery).} The blanking gate is disabled and the same detector runs with an exponential recovery law characterized by $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, matching the finite-recovery model of \cref{sec:operational}. The record is a renewal process with hazard~\eqref{eq:hazard-main}, and the exact $1$-dependence threshold occurs at $\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\end{itemize}

Operationally, the two modes correspond to ``forced reset'' and ``free recovery'' on the same sensor. Both therefore inherit the same response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$. This shared-hardware assumption is the reason the shell-pair ratio can eliminate the otherwise unknown amplitude.

\subsection{Common-amplitude cancellation and spectral transport}

The most useful shell-pair observable arises when both shells are generated by the same hardware operated in the two-mode protocol of \cref{subsec:two-mode}. Dividing the two shell equations then removes the common response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:ratio-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and operate the shared hardware in the two-mode protocol of \cref{subsec:two-mode}. Let the resulting detector design generate an exact-clock shell at $r_{\varphi}$ and a memory-transition shell at $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in a static spherically symmetric background with lapse $N(r)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
The common multiplicative response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels exactly.
\end{theorem}

The cancellation in Theorem~\ref{thm:ratio-main} is exact but not detector-free: the right-hand side still depends on the chosen sampling design and recovery law. What disappears is the unknown common response amplitude. The homogeneous ratio law is the simplest specialization of the more general identity below.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:spectral-transport-main}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:shells-general-main}, let $x_{\varphi}$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$ be points on the exact-clock and memory-transition shells for the same detector design. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-transport-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_{\varphi}}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}
Hence deviations from the homogeneous ratio law directly measure spectral transport between the two shell points.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectral-stability}
Assume the two-mode protocol and suppose that on the region of interest $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)(1+\varepsilon(x))$ with $|\varepsilon(x)|\le\varepsilon_0<1$. Let $R$ denote the shell-ratio observable on the left-hand side of~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} and let $C=\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)/\log\varphi$. Then
\[
C\,\frac{1-\varepsilon_0}{1+\varepsilon_0}
\le
R
\le
C\,\frac{1+\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} gives
\[
R=C\,\frac{1+\varepsilon(x_\varphi)}{1+\varepsilon(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}.
\]
The bounds follow by maximizing and minimizing the fraction over admissible $\varepsilon$. The denominator is positive because $\varepsilon_0<1$.
\end{proof}

Thus a uniform spectral inhomogeneity of size $\varepsilon_0$ perturbs the homogeneous ratio by at most the multiplicative factor $\frac{1+\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}$.

\subsection{Near-horizon universal hierarchy}

The shell-pair ratio is particularly transparent near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:horizon-main}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:ratio-main}. Suppose, in addition, that the static region ends at a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-main}
N(\ell)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\ell+O(\ell^3)
\end{equation}
as the proper distance $\ell\downarrow0$ from the horizon. Then the shell distances satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ell-ratio-main}
\frac{\ell_{\varphi}}{\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\Bigl(1+O(\ell_{\varphi}+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
In Schwarzschild, if $A_H=16\pi M^2$ is the horizon area and $A_{\varphi}$ and $A_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are the areas of the shell spheres, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:area-ratio-main}
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left[
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\right]^2
\Bigl(1+O(\ell_{\varphi}+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Work in Gaussian normal (proper-distance) coordinates from the horizon: let $\ell$ denote proper distance from the bifurcation surface, so the lapse expands as $N(\ell)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\ell+O(\ell^3)$ with $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$. Then $e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(\ell)}-1=2\pi\Omega\ell(1+O(\ell))$. Inserting both shell points into the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main} and cancelling the common $2\pi\Omega$ factor gives~\eqref{eq:ell-ratio-main} to leading order. For the area excess in Schwarzschild, note that the areal radius is related to proper distance by $r-r_H=\ell^2/(8M)+O(\ell^4)$ near the horizon (since $d\ell=(1-2M/r)^{-1/2}\,dr$). Therefore the area excess satisfies
\[
A(\ell)-A_H=4\pi\bigl(r(\ell)^2-r_H^2\bigr)=4\pi(2r_H)(r-r_H)+O(\ell^4)=2\pi\ell^2+O(\ell^4),
\]
where we used $r_H=2M$. It follows that
$
(A_\varphi-A_H)/(A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H)=\ell_\varphi^2/\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}^2\,(1+O(\ell_\varphi+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})),
$
which is the square of the proper-distance ratio, yielding~\eqref{eq:area-ratio-main}. The details are given in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}, with additional intermediate algebra recorded in Supplementary Material, section~5.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:horizon-main} shows that the shell pair acts as a detector-dependent stretched-horizon ruler. One shell is defined by the Parry benchmark and the other by exact $1$-dependence, but their near-horizon separation is governed by a universal leading-order ratio independent of the common response amplitude.

A particularly informative regime is the fast-sampling limit. Let $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\downarrow0$ and $L=\log(1/s)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-main}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-ratio-main}
\frac{\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\ell_{\varphi}}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus the memory-transition shell approaches the horizon parametrically faster than the exact-clock shell. The shell pair therefore resolves a genuine hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales.

\subsection{Model-conditional static spherical inversion}

The shell pair also carries direct inverse content in static spherical settings, under the homogeneous spectral-weight assumption $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$. In realistic black-hole detector problems greybody factors and mode sums typically make the response weight position-dependent, so the formulas in this subsection and in \cref{subsec:worked-example} should be read as exact only within that idealized model. \Cref{subsec:nonhomogeneous} returns to the general case.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:schw-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define the detector constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-constants-main}
\beta_{\varphi}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right).
\end{equation}
If the two shells generated by the same detector design occur at distinct radii $r_{\varphi}\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in Schwarzschild, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-infty-main}
\beta_\infty^2=
\frac{\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_{\varphi}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mass-main}
M=
\frac{r_{\varphi}r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)}
{2\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:schw-main} is exact algebra within the homogeneous model. Its point is not to remove detector dependence, but to show that two record-defined shell locations already determine the global redshift scale once the design is fixed. The same idea extends beyond Schwarzschild in a local form that does not require separate calibration of the common response amplitude. The Jacobian condition in the theorem below is simply the statement that the two shell-ratio equations are locally independent.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:two-mode-main}
Let $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ parametrize a $C^1$ family of static spherically symmetric backgrounds, with parameter $\theta\in\Theta$ determining both the lapse $N(r;\theta)$ and the global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty(\theta)$. Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime at fixed gap $\Omega$ and fixed hardware prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.

Fix one detector hardware design $(\Omega,\lambda_c)$ and operate it in two modes
\[
D_m=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m}),
\qquad m=1,2,
\]
with observed shell radii $r_{\varphi,m}$ and $r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}$. Define the known constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-constants-main}
C_m=\frac{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}{\log\varphi},
\qquad m=1,2,
\end{equation}
and the shell-ratio functions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-functions-main}
F_m(\theta)=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1}
-C_m.
\end{equation}
If $\theta_*\in\Theta$ satisfies $F_1(\theta_*)=F_2(\theta_*)=0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main}
\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0,
\end{equation}
then the two shell pairs determine $\theta$ locally uniquely near $\theta_*$. In particular, the reconstruction is self-calibrating: no prior knowledge of $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is required.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Each mode $D_m$ contributes one equation $F_m(\theta)=0$ by the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main}. Both functions are $C^1$ in $\theta$ under the stated regularity of $N$ and $\beta_\infty$, and the common amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels from each $F_m$ separately. The two equations therefore form a $C^1$ system in two unknowns. By the implicit function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002}, any solution $\theta_*$ at which $\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0$ is locally unique, and the solution map is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism near $\theta_*$. The amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ does not appear in the $F_m$, confirming self-calibration. The proof is recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}, with additional details in Supplementary Material, section~5.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Jacobian conditioning and error propagation]\label{rem:jacobian-main}
The Jacobian condition~\eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main} fails in particular when the two operating modes generate shell pairs with the same ratio $C_1=C_2$. Since $C_m$ depends on $(\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m})$, this is a codimension-one condition in the mode parameter space and is generically avoided by choosing modes with distinct sampling intervals.

For the single-mode Schwarzschild inverse of \cref{prop:schw-main}, the system is linear in $(u,v)=(\beta_\infty^2,\,2M\beta_\infty^2)$ with coefficient matrix $A=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}r_\varphi & -1\\ r_{\mathrm{mem}} & -1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ and $\det A=-(r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}})$. The condition number is $\kappa(A)=\|A\|\|A^{-1}\|$, which diverges as the shells approach each other. For the worked example of \cref{tab:worked} with mode~$D_1$, $\kappa(A)\approx111$: this is moderate conditioning. A first-order error propagation gives: a $1\%$ relative error on the shell radii $(r_\varphi,r_{\mathrm{mem}})$ produces a ${\sim}38\%$ relative error on $\beta_\infty^2$ but only a ${\sim}1.2\%$ relative error on $M$. The mass is well conditioned because it involves the ratio $r_\varphi r_{\mathrm{mem}}/(r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}})$, which is less sensitive to correlated perturbations of the two radii. Improving the conditioning requires wider shell separation, which can be achieved by increasing the product $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ (pushing the memory shell farther from the exact-clock shell).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Schwarzschild worked example}\label{subsec:worked-example}

\Cref{tab:worked} gives a simple algebraic check in Schwarzschild with $r_{\mathrm s}=2M=1$ (so $\beta_\infty=4\pi$), $\Omega=1$, and $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S=2$. The two detector modes $D_1=(\Omega,\lambda_c,0.5,\,8.0)$ and $D_2=(\Omega,\lambda_c,1.2,\,3.0)$ have distinct products $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and therefore distinct ratio constants, as required for the local two-mode inversion of \cref{thm:two-mode-main}. For each mode, $\Gamma_c$ is obtained from~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main} and cross-checked against the Lambert $W$ expression~\eqref{eq:lambert-Gc}; the shell radii follow from \cref{cor:homogeneous-main}. Applying \cref{prop:schw-main} to the $D_1$ shell pair then recovers $\beta_\infty$ and $M$ exactly within the model.

\subsection{End-to-end shell estimation from finite records}\label{subsec:noisy-inverse}

The algebraic inversion of \cref{prop:schw-main} becomes operational only after the shell radii themselves have been estimated from records. We therefore complement the algebraic formulas with an end-to-end pipeline inside the homogeneous Schwarzschild model. Using the mode-$D_1$ design of \cref{tab:worked}, we simulated records at five radii around $r_\varphi$ and five radii around $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$, each of length $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=1.2\times10^5$ sampled intervals after a burn-in of $5\times10^3$ intervals; Appendix~\ref{app:monte} gives the algorithm.

For the exact-clock shell we estimated
\[
\widehat p(r)=\frac{\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}}{\#\{A_n=0\}}
\]
in the ideal-blanking mode and linearly interpolated the zero of $\widehat p(r)-\varphi^{-1}$. For the memory shell we used the finite-recovery mode, estimated the empirical click fraction $\widehat\rho(r)$, and then solved the explicit renewal-rate equation $\rho(\Gamma)=\widehat\rho(r)$ with $\rho(\Gamma)$ given by \eqref{eq:rho-main}; this yields a monotone estimator $\widehat\Gamma(r)$ of the local input rate. The shell estimator $\widehat r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ was then obtained by interpolating the zero of $\widehat\Gamma(r)-\Gamma_c$. This rate-matching step uses the full renewal model and is markedly more stable in finite data than a raw sign test on $\widehat\gamma_2$.

Across $120$ independent Monte Carlo replicates, all of which bracketed both shell roots, we obtained
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=0.2em]
\item $\widehat r_\varphi/r_{\mathrm s}=1.008077\pm0.000047$ and $\widehat r_{\mathrm{mem}}/r_{\mathrm s}=1.045097\pm0.000304$, to be compared with the exact shell locations $1.008069$ and $1.045109$.
\item $\widehat M=0.500006\pm0.000044$ and $\widehat\beta_\infty^2=158.00\pm1.27$, to be compared with the exact values $M=0.5$ and $\beta_\infty^2=157.9137$.
\end{itemize}
This experiment validates the full record-to-shell-to-inverse pipeline at the Monte Carlo level inside the homogeneous model. It does not provide a CLT or optimality theory for the shell estimators, and it should not be read as a detector-independent metrological claim. It does show that, once the response model is fixed, the finite-record benchmark equations recover the shell pair accurately enough for the model-conditional inverse formulas to behave as predicted by the conditioning analysis of \cref{rem:jacobian-main}.

\subsection{Illustrative non-homogeneous correction}\label{subsec:nonhomogeneous}

The homogeneous regime $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is an idealization. In a realistic Schwarzschild detector problem the response weight is position-dependent because of greybody factors, mode sums and detector profile effects. The spectral-transport identity~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} governs the resulting correction. Rather than proposing a microscopic model, we record only a simple illustrative variation:
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)\bigl(1+\alpha(r(x)/r_H-1)\bigr),
\]
with $\alpha>0$ parametrizing a linear greybody enhancement away from the horizon. In the worked example of \cref{tab:worked} with $\alpha=0.5$, the spectral-weight ratio between the exact-clock and memory-transition shells of mode $D_1$ is
\[
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
=
\frac{1+\alpha(r_\varphi/r_H-1)}{1+\alpha(r_{\mathrm{mem}}/r_H-1)}
=
\frac{1+0.5\times0.008069}{1+0.5\times0.045109}
\approx0.982,
\]
so the right-hand side of~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} acquires a factor $\approx0.982$ relative to the homogeneous ratio $C_1=0.1648$, shifting the effective ratio to $\approx0.1618$. The qualitative picture persists, but the shell-pair observable is no longer pure lapse information: it mixes lapse and spectral transport. We do not convert this ratio shift into a shell-position bound here, because that would require additional implicit-function hypotheses.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The main conclusion is that, within the sampled-counter model studied here, the full click record carries operational information that is absent from a rate-only summary. Both record benchmarks considered in this paper are ultimately functions of the same local excitation rate, so they do not produce detector-independent geometric invariants. What they do provide are two distinguished selectors inside a single stationary response family: a grammar-based selector in the ideal dead-time mode and a dependence-based selector in the recovery mode.

The recovery mode is not merely a blurred version of the ideal one. At every positive sampling interval the Parry benchmark is excluded, because the hazard remains strictly increasing, yet adjacent clicks are quadratically suppressed as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$. The same renewal structure also yields universal antibunching: low-frequency noise is suppressed and the asymptotic Fano factor remains below one throughout the parameter range.

From the geometric side, the shell pair is more informative than either shell taken separately. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime it satisfies an exact common-amplitude cancellation law, giving a detector-dependent but amplitude-light observable. Near a nonextremal horizon this leads to a hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales, while in static spherical settings it yields model-conditional inverse statements whose stability is controlled by the shell separation. Outside the homogeneous regime, the comparison survives as the exact spectral-transport identity of \cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}.

Several features are expected to survive beyond exponential recovery. Any model in which a click resets the hidden state should retain a renewal structure, and the corresponding covariance profile should again be governed by the hidden-state spectrum. It is plausible that the monotone-hazard behaviour extends to broader recovery kernels with full support on $\mathbb R_+$, but that question requires a separate analysis.

The limitations are straightforward. We have fixed a weak-coupling response model, a two-level detector, a single stationary worldline, discrete readout and exponential recovery. Switching effects, non-exponential recovery, paralyzable counters, multilevel detectors, greybody corrections, backreaction and a full finite-sample inference theory all lie outside the present scope. The shell equations should therefore be read as exact statements within the specified detector-response class and as a template for more elaborate models, rather than as universal properties of the background geometry.

Within that scope, the sampled-counter analysis is complete at the level proved here. The record statistics single out two inequivalent thresholds, exhibit a soft but singular route from finite recovery to the ideal grammar, and remain strictly antibunched in the renewal regime. The resulting shell equations then provide a concrete route from full-record detector statistics to shell-pair geometry in static KMS spacetimes.
