\begin{abstract}
We study what a phenomenological counter model, driven by the stationary excitation rate of an Unruh--DeWitt detector, reveals when the full binary click record is kept. The counter is defined by an explicit interval click-register instrument: the UDW detector supplies only the stationary excitation input rate, while dead time and recovery belong to separate readout electronics. The core technical contribution (Section~3) is a complete analysis of two record-level observables---the Parry-law grammar benchmark for an ideal one-step dead-time counter and the exact $1$-dependence transition for an exponential finite-recovery counter. Both arise within the fixed rate-driven response family: the full record selects distinguished benchmark rates (maximum-entropy grammar and exact $1$-dependence) that are invisible to a rate-only summary but still live inside the same response curve. Section~3 also establishes universal antibunching and a quadratic soft-dead-time approach to the Parry benchmark, with full derivations supplied in the appendices to keep the submission self-contained. Sections~4 and~5 are treated explicitly as conditional applications: we embed the thresholds in static KMS backgrounds, introduce a two-mode operating protocol in which the same hardware alternates between an ideal blanking mode and a finite-recovery mode, and show that the shared response amplitude cancels from the shell-pair ratio while spectral inhomogeneity enters through an exact transport identity. The resulting shell loci are detector-defined isorate loci selected by record benchmarks, and the inverse statements are presented only as homogeneous toy-model illustrations within the stated response class.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Unruh--DeWitt detector, KMS state, static spacetime, symbolic dynamics, renewal process

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates, detailed balance, and Tolman redshift are the standard observables used to characterize Unruh and Hawking thermality \cite{Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,Takagi1986,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. That summary is natural for thermality questions, but it is not what a sampled detector actually produces. Once the detector is read out at regular proper-time intervals, the experiment yields a binary click record with its own grammar, waiting-time structure, and lag correlations. Dead time and recovery therefore turn the detector into a stochastic-record probe rather than a mere rate meter. The basic physical question of this paper is whether those record-level observables select operational thresholds---within the fixed rate-driven family---that are invisible to a rate-only summary of the same click data.

This question matters for curved-spacetime detector theory because realistic counting devices are intrinsically temporal. A refractory interval or finite recovery law introduces serial dependence into the click record, and that dependence can survive even when the local mean rate is fixed. If such memory-sensitive observables lift cleanly to stationary KMS settings, then detector design ceases to be only a nuisance parameter and becomes part of the operational interface between click statistics and spacetime geometry.

Throughout the revision we keep the scope deliberately sharp. Section~\ref{sec:records} is the centerpiece: it contains the record-level derivations, hazard monotonicity, universal antibunching, and the exact Parry benchmark versus $1$-dependence transition. Sections~\ref{sec:kms-shells} and~\ref{sec:inverse} are downstream applications/corollaries: once the benchmark rates have been identified, we translate them into shell equations and inverse statements under explicit detector-model assumptions. This ordering reflects the novelty assessment summarized by the referee: the stochastic analysis is the core contribution; the geometric sections are operational illustrations under additional hypotheses.

This question is related to several active lines of investigation, but distinct from each of them. We organize the relevant literature into four groups.

\emph{UDW click rates, switching, and repeated measurements.}
The transition rate of the UDW detector depends sensitively on the switching function and detector profile \cite{LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008,SatzClick2007}. Repeated measurements of Unruh--DeWitt detectors and the reliability of master-equation descriptions have been revisited \cite{PranziniEtAl2024,HanEtAl2025}. Detector-based formulations of quantum measurements in QFT have sharpened the operational status of detector observables \cite{AnastopoulosSavvidou2015I,AnastopoulosSavvidou2015,FewsterVerch2020,Fewster2020,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click,PercheLocalized2023,TorresLocalized2024,PapageorgiouFraser2024}. For caveats on detector-model approximations and causality, see \cite{MartinMartinezCausality2015}. What detector output statistics can reveal about field correlators has also been studied directly \cite{deRamonGarayMartinMartinez2018}.

\emph{Detector thermalization and geometry probing.}
The thermalization of particle detectors toward KMS states has been analysed in terms of asymptotic states and its reverse \cite{FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2023}. Detectors have been used to probe background geometry and topology, including hollow-shell detection and geon versus Schwarzschild discrimination \cite{NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020}. Detector response in static spherical black-hole geometries, including spatially varying spectral weights, has been studied in \cite{NgHodgkinsonLoukoMannMartinMartinez2014,NgMannMartinMartinez2018,TjoaStaticSph2024,TjoaMann2022JHEP}. Metrological observables probe background structure via scalar functionals \cite{BraunsteinCaves1994,WangEtAl2014Metrology,HaoWu2016Unruh,DuMann2021AdS,FengZhang2022QFI,PercheMartinMartinez2022}.

\emph{Quantum-optical waiting-time and antibunching physics.}
Waiting-time distributions and photon antibunching are foundational tools in quantum optics for characterizing two-state emitters \cite{CarmichaelWaiting1989,KimbleAntibunching1977,Carmichael1993Book,WisemanMilburn2009}. The sub-Poissonian counting and antibunching properties derived in Sections~\ref{sec:records} have direct counterparts in this literature. What is new here is the embedding in curved-spacetime response theory and the use of these properties to define geometric loci.

\emph{Renewal processes and $1$-dependent counting.}
The methodology of renewal statistics, spectral densities, Fano factors, and inverse counting statistics is well developed in mesoscopic transport and modern full-counting-statistics theory \cite{Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014,LandiEtAl2024FCS}. Stationary $1$-dependent processes have their own literature \cite{AaronsonGilat1988,DaleyVereJones2003,HolroydLiggett2016}, and the notion of $1$-dependence as a structural property of counting processes is classical. We draw on these tools but embed them in a curved-spacetime detector model.

\subsection*{Relation to prior record-level detector literature}
Macroscopic detector records and multi-time correlations have already been investigated in the algebraic and quantum temporal probabilistic (QTP) programmes. Anastopoulos and Savvidou developed multi-time probability assignments, detector kernels, and irreversible detector models tailored to relativistic settings \cite{AnastopoulosSavvidou2015I,AnastopoulosSavvidou2012,AnastopoulosSavvidou2014,AnastopoulosSavvidou2015}. Related work on Hawking settings with multi-time detector measurements \cite{AnastopoulosSavvidouEtAl2019} analyzes higher-order correlators directly in field theory. Local and covariant measurement frameworks with post-selection and repeated local measurements are developed in \cite{FewsterVerch2020,Fewster2020,PapageorgiouFraser2024}. Those studies show that multi-time detector statistics in QFT can be defined consistently. Our contribution is narrower: we fix one sampled counter with explicit interval readout, derive closed-form renewal laws for its click record, and then treat Sections~\ref{sec:kms-shells}--\ref{sec:inverse} as phenomenological applications once the benchmark rates inside that counter model have been identified.

Our focus is correspondingly specific: we fix an explicit sampled counter model driven by the stationary UDW excitation rate, keep the full stationary click record, and ask which record-level thresholds become codimension-one detector-selected loci in static KMS settings. The novelty lies in the exact sampled-counter analysis and in lifting those record thresholds, within the same response model, to geometric applications.

Dead time and finite recovery are not artificial mathematical constraints but intrinsic features of any real counting detector. In nuclear and photon counting, dead time has been a central design consideration since the earliest pulse detectors \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975}. Treating the Unruh--DeWitt detector as a dead-time counter therefore links the particle-detector formalism to a well-developed experimental framework. The observables exploited here---grammar, hazard, dependence class---are part of the distributional fingerprint of the sampled process. They are not, strictly speaking, independent of the local rate: once the full detector model is fixed, both the grammar threshold and the dependence threshold are functions of the same stationary excitation rate. What is new is that the full record selects special operational thresholds---the Parry maximum-entropy grammar level and the exact $1$-dependence transition---that are invisible to a rate-only summary of the same data. This places the present work in a different observable layer from the quantum-metrological studies cited above, which extract geometric information from scalar functionals of the detector state rather than from the full stochastic record.

The answer is not a mild refinement of rate thermometry. In the ideal one-step dead-time model, the sampled record has the golden-mean grammar: the word $11$ is forbidden. The Parry-law benchmark means that the record realizes the Parry measure on that grammar, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy on the golden-mean shift \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. This benchmark is distinct from both the standard detailed-balance criterion and Gibbs thermalization (see \cref{subsec:thermality-notions} for precise definitions); it characterizes the record-level disorder of the click grammar, not the state of the detector or the rate ratio. The Parry-law benchmark selects an \emph{exact-clock shell}. For finite recovery the hidden detector remains two-state, but the visible record becomes a renewal process with infinite symbolic memory. There the relevant threshold is no longer entropic. At one distinguished rate the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent, and across that threshold the covariance sign pattern changes. This defines the \emph{memory-transition shell}. A central point of the paper is that finite recovery does not simply blur the ideal selector. It creates a second one.

Two further consequences sharpen the shell picture beyond a rephrased Tolman law. First, finite recovery approaches the ideal grammar only in a singular way. Consecutive-click violations are quadratically suppressed in the sampling interval, and fixed finite windows concentrate on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$. Yet the Parry-law benchmark remains excluded at every positive sampling interval, because the hazard is strictly increasing. Second, the finite-recovery record is universally antibunched. Its low-frequency spectral density is suppressed, and the asymptotic counting Fano factor is strictly below one throughout the parameter range. These record-level signatures give the memory shell a direct operational meaning in finite counting data.

The geometric content emerges once the local detector rate is inserted. In a static KMS background, the ideal threshold and the memory threshold lift to two detector-defined hypersurfaces. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime, both become pure lapse level sets, and the shell pair satisfies an exact common-amplitude cancellation law. This yields an amplitude-light shell-pair observable, a universal near-horizon ratio law, and a model-conditional inverse principle in static spherical settings. Outside the homogeneous regime, the same shell pair obeys a spectral-transport identity, so that deviations from the homogeneous ratio law acquire a definite interpretation in terms of local spectral-weight variation.

The scope is deliberately controlled. We work with a single stationary worldline, a sampled two-level detector, weak coupling, discrete readout step $\Delta\tau$, and an exponential finite-recovery rule. The resulting shells are detector-defined hypersurfaces, not detector-independent invariants of the background. The inverse statements are likewise conditional on the response model and do not claim generic metric reconstruction. We also distinguish the present use of Tolman scaling in a fixed detector model from stronger notions of local thermal equilibrium in curved spacetime \cite{BuchholzSchlemmer2007,Solveen2012,BuchholzSolveen2013,GranseePinamontiVerch2017}. Finally, detailed balance is often used as a thermal diagnostic, but even this criterion can fail to characterize detector thermality once the detector structure is enlarged \cite{LimaEtAl2023Qudit}, which further motivates examining the full sampled record directly.

The submission is organized so that the core claims are checkable from the manuscript itself. Appendices~\ref{app:state-diagram}--\ref{app:monte} now collect the state diagram, the explicit interval click-register instrument, the zero-run law, hazard monotonicity, the covariance formula, the exact $1$-dependence argument, the spectral-density/Fano-factor calculation, the near-horizon expansion, the inverse theorems, and the Monte Carlo methodology behind Sections~\ref{subsec:finite-sample} and~\ref{subsec:noisy-inverse}. The Supplementary Material records additional intermediate steps and closed-form evaluations. The logic of the main results is simple. The ideal threshold comes from matching the dead-time grammar to the entropy-maximizing Parry law on the golden-mean shift. The memory threshold comes from the vanishing of the hidden-state eigenvalue that transports serial dependence beyond one lag. The shell-pair inverse law uses the explicit two-mode protocol of Section~\ref{subsec:two-mode}, so that two operating modes of the \emph{same} hardware supply the two benchmark rates and the unknown common response amplitude cancels before any geometric inversion is attempted.

For ease of reference, the main claims may be summarized as follows. Claims~(i)--(iii) are the principal results; claims~(iv)--(v) are applications to static KMS backgrounds.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item Ideal dead time yields a golden-mean Markov law and a unique exact-clock threshold $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$.
\item Finite recovery yields a full-support renewal process with strictly increasing hazard, excludes the Parry-law benchmark, and produces a second threshold at exact $1$-dependence $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\item Finite recovery also has two robust operational signatures beyond the threshold itself: grammar violations are quadratically rare for small $\Delta\tau$, and the stationary record is strictly sub-Poissonian.
\item \emph{Application:} In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime, the two thresholds lift to a shell pair governed by a common-amplitude cancellation law and a detector-controlled ordering law; outside that regime the pair obeys an exact spectral-transport identity.
\item \emph{Application:} Near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon the shell pair defines a leading-order hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales, and in static spherical settings it yields model-conditional identifiability of the background parameters (with conditioning analysis).
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organized to keep that physics thread visible. \Cref{sec:operational,sec:records} define the record-level observables and derive the two detector thresholds together with their operational signatures; these constitute the core contribution. \Cref{sec:kms-shells,sec:inverse} then apply those thresholds as shell equations in static KMS backgrounds and extract the shell-pair ratio and inverse content as corollaries. Appendices~\ref{app:state-diagram}--\ref{app:derivations} contain the state diagram and the key derivations supporting the main text; the Supplementary Material records additional details.

\section{Operational setting and record-level observables}\label{sec:operational}

We consider a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector sampled at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. The visible output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ denotes a click in the $n$th sampling interval. Compressing this record to the empirical click frequency recovers only the one-point observable
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n.
\]
The full record, however, carries more. In particular, one may ask:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item what words belong to the support of the stationary law;
\item how the zero-run lengths between clicks are distributed;
\item whether the click hazard after a long run of zeros remembers the run length;
\item how the low-lag covariance profile behaves;
\item what counting noise remains after coarse time averaging.
\end{enumerate}
For later use, we fix some terminology. If a click is followed by $G$ consecutive zeros before the next click, then $G$ is the \emph{zero-run length}. Its hazard $h_k$ is the conditional probability that the next sample clicks after exactly $k$ zeros since the previous click. In the ideal model, the \emph{Parry-law benchmark} means that the stationary record coincides with the Parry measure on the golden-mean grammar. In the finite-recovery model, \emph{exact $1$-dependence} means that blocks of samples separated by at least one intermediate time step are independent. These are the two record-level notions that later become shell conditions in a static KMS background.

These observables are invisible to a rate-only description, yet they are precisely the quantities that control the shell geometry developed below.

The two detector regimes studied in this work are illustrated schematically in \cref{fig:scheme}. The ideal model imposes a hard symbolic grammar, while finite recovery produces a full-support renewal law. The thresholds associated with the two regimes are therefore intrinsically different: one is entropic and grammar-based, the other is correlation-based.

\section{Sampled detector laws and operational signatures}\label{sec:records}

\subsection{Measurement protocol and phenomenological counter model}\label{subsec:phenom-model}
The model used in this paper is a sampled counter with two hidden counter states, $R$ (ready) and $D$ (refractory), together with an interval click register. At the beginning of each interval $[n\Delta\tau,(n+1)\Delta\tau]$ the register is reset to $0$. If the counter is ready, an excitation supplied by the stationary UDW rate $\Gamma_+\equiv\Gamma$ flips the register irreversibly to $1$ during that interval. If the counter begins in $D$, it recovers to $R$ at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ and may then click later in the same interval. At the end of the interval only the register is read out:
\[
A_n=
\begin{cases}
1,&\text{the interval register fired},\\
0,&\text{the interval register remained silent}.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus $A_n$ is not defined as a projective measurement of the detector's internal two-level system. It is the output of an explicit interval instrument whose hidden counter state and visible register are separated from the outset; Appendix~\ref{app:gkls} writes this instrument in matrix form.

The post-readout update is equally explicit. If $A_n=1$, the click register is reset and the next interval starts in the refractory state $D$. If $A_n=0$, the click register is reset but the hidden end-of-interval counter state is carried into the next interval without further reset. This refractory-carryover rule is what generates the serial dependence and renewal structure of the click record. In particular, the renewal structure of Section~\ref{sec:records} rests on precisely this reset rule: click times are regeneration times because the post-click hidden state is always $D$.

Concretely, the detector dynamics within a single interval $[0,\Delta\tau]$ are as follows (see also the state diagram in \cref{app:state-diagram}):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item If the detector begins the interval in state $R$ (ready): during $[0,\Delta\tau]$ it may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited, the interval ends in state $D$ and registers a click ($A_n=1$). If not excited, the interval ends in state $R$ and registers no click ($A_n=0$).
\item If the detector begins the interval in state $D$ (refractory): it first recovers to $R$ at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. If recovery occurs at time $s\in[0,\Delta\tau]$, the detector is then ready for the remaining subinterval $[s,\Delta\tau]$ and may be excited at rate $\Gamma$. If excited after recovery, the interval ends in $D$ with a click. If not excited after recovery, or if recovery does not occur during the interval, the hidden state at the end of the interval is determined by whether recovery happened.
\end{enumerate}
The output-labelled kernel matrices $T_0$, $T_1$ derived below follow from integrating these continuous-time dynamics over $[0,\Delta\tau]$. The crucial structural feature is that every click forces the next interval to start in state $D$, making click times regeneration points of the hidden process. This is the mechanism that produces the renewal structure in \cref{prop:recovery-main}.

\emph{Model status.}
The kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ define a \emph{phenomenological counter model} driven by the stationary UDW excitation rate $\Gamma_+$, in the same spirit as classical dead-time counter models in nuclear and photon counting \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,SatzClick2007,CarmichaelWaiting1989,KimbleAntibunching1977}. The separation between detector physics and counter electronics is deliberate. In Sections~\ref{sec:kms-shells}--\ref{sec:inverse}, static KMS response theory supplies the stationary input rate $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)$. Dead time, recovery, and interval readout belong to a separate classical register degree of freedom. No smallness assumption is imposed on $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$: the interval probabilities are integrated exactly for all positive $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau$. Appendix~\ref{app:gkls} should therefore be read as a consistency-level interval instrument for the sampled counter, not as a first-principles reduction from a bare UDW detector in the full parameter regime explored here. This limited status is consistent with the local-measurement and detector-kernel literature \cite{AnastopoulosSavvidou2015I,FewsterVerch2020,Fewster2020,PapageorgiouFraser2024} and with the causal caveats surrounding detector models \cite{MartinMartinezCausality2015}. All subsequent grammar, hazard, covariance, and spectral-density results are derived from $T_0$ and $T_1$ within that explicit phenomenological framework.

\subsection{Ideal one-step dead time and the exact-clock threshold}

The ideal dead-time model is defined separately from the finite-recovery model by a deterministic-recovery rule: after every click, the detector is placed in the refractory state $D$ for the entire next interval and then returns to the ready state $R$ unconditionally at the start of the interval after that. No probabilistic recovery is involved. This guarantees $A_{n+1}=0$ whenever $A_n=1$ and is the standard one-step dead time. The transition $(D\to R)$ here is deterministic, not exponential; consequently the ideal model is \emph{not} obtained by taking $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\to0$ in the finite-recovery model, which would instead make the refractory state absorbing after the first click. Writing
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-beta-main}
p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}, \qquad q_\beta=1-p_\beta,
\end{equation}
the sampled output is the stationary Markov chain on the golden-mean shift with transition matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-mean-main}
P_\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p_\beta & 1-p_\beta \\
 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The reference law on that shift is the Parry measure, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy compatible with the grammar $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ideal-main}
For the ideal dead-time detector, the sampled output realizes the Parry-law benchmark if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-threshold-main}
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\end{equation}
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ is the golden ratio. This threshold defines the exact-clock selector.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
After a click the detector enters the refractory state $D$. Under the deterministic-recovery rule, the detector remains refractory for the full next interval and the block $11$ is forbidden: any consecutive-click sequence $(A_n,A_{n+1})=(1,1)$ has zero probability. After a zero, the detector is in the ready state $R$ and clicks next with probability $q_\beta=1-p_\beta$. This yields transition matrix~\eqref{eq:golden-mean-main} with the single free parameter $p_\beta$. The Parry measure on the golden-mean shift is the unique stationary measure matching the Parry transition probabilities $P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1}$, $P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2}$, $P(1,0)=1$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. Matching gives $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$ and hence~\eqref{eq:parry-threshold-main}. Full derivations appear in Supplementary Material, section~1.
\end{proof}

The threshold in Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} should not be confused with a generic isotherm. It identifies the unique point at which a sampled detector record realizes the zero-jitter, maximum-entropy law compatible with the dead-time grammar. The resulting shell therefore depends on the detector gap and on the sampling design even before curved-spacetime geometry is introduced.

\subsection{Detailed balance, Gibbs thermalization, and the Parry record benchmark}\label{subsec:thermality-notions}
Three distinct criteria are worth separating in UDW detector settings. \emph{Detailed balance}: the rate ratio satisfies $\Gamma_-/\Gamma_+=e^{-\beta\Omega}$, the standard criterion \cite{Unruh1976,BirDav1982}. \emph{Gibbs thermalization}: the reduced detector state converges to $e^{-\beta H_{\mathrm{det}}}/Z$ \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2023}. \emph{Parry-law record benchmark}: the stationary click record realizes the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift, the unique measure of maximal entropy for the dead-time grammar $11\notin\mathrm{supp}\,\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. The third criterion is the one studied here. Neither of the first two implies the third, nor conversely. To avoid confusion with detector-state thermality, we use the term \emph{Parry-law benchmark} throughout: it is a record-level benchmark on the full word distribution, not a statement about Gibbs equilibration.

\subsection{Finite recovery, renewal structure, and exact \texorpdfstring{$1$}{1}-dependence}

A physically more realistic model allows the detector to recover over a finite time rather than instantaneously. Let $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$ be the recovery rate. Throughout this section and the remainder of the paper, we write $\Gamma\equiv\Gamma_+$ for the excitation rate when no confusion with other rates arises. Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-c-main}
p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}, \qquad c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-b-main}
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds,
\qquad
b=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\end{equation}
When $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, these integrals evaluate to
$a=[\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)]/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$
and
$b=\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$.
The visible click record is then a renewal process whose zero-run lengths are i.i.d.

The proof mechanism is physically simple and worth isolating. Every observed click resets the hidden detector state to the refractory state, so click times become regeneration times. That reset property is exactly what turns the visible law into a renewal process rather than a finite-order Markov chain. The threshold $\Gamma_c$ then appears when the nontrivial eigenvalue of the hidden two-state transition matrix vanishes, causing all dependence beyond one step to collapse at once.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:recovery-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the zero-run length $G$ between successive clicks has law
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-main}
\PP(G=k)=g_k=e_D^{\top}T_0^kT_1\mathbf 1,
\qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t0-t1-main}
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 0 \\
 b & c
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
 0 & 1-p \\
 0 & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
If $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-closed-main}
g_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\end{equation}
and the click hazard after $k$ zeros is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard-main}
h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}
     {\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k},
\qquad k\ge0.
\end{equation}
The hazard is strictly increasing, so the visible process is not an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$.

Let $\rho=\PP(A_0=1)$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda-main}
\lambda=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}-\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}}
{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cov-main}
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},\qquad n\ge1.
\end{equation}
Consequently there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>0$ determined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-c-main}
\Delta\tau=
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma_c},
\end{equation}
such that the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$. Below $\Gamma_c$ all covariances are negative; above $\Gamma_c$ they alternate in sign.
\end{proposition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:stationary-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the stationary hidden-state distribution of $P=T_0+T_1$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:stationary-main}
\pi_R=\frac{b}{1-p+b},
\qquad
\pi_D=\frac{1-p}{1-p+b}.
\end{equation}
The stationary click rate is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-main}
\rho=\PP(A_0=1)=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a=\frac{(1-p)(1-c)}{1-p+b},
\end{equation}
and it satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-bounds-main}
a<\rho<1-p.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Solving $\pi P=\pi$ with $\pi_R+\pi_D=1$ gives~\eqref{eq:stationary-main}. Since the click probabilities from $R$ and $D$ are $1-p$ and $a$, respectively,
\[
\rho=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a
=
\frac{b(1-p)+(1-p)a}{1-p+b}
=
\frac{(1-p)(a+b)}{1-p+b}
=
\frac{(1-p)(1-c)}{1-p+b},
\]
which is~\eqref{eq:rho-main}. Also
\[
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds
<
(1-p)\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\,ds
=(1-p)(1-c)
<
1-p.
\]
Because $\rho=\pi_R(1-p)+\pi_D a$ is a strict convex combination of $a$ and $1-p$, with $\pi_R,\pi_D>0$, the bounds~\eqref{eq:rho-bounds-main} follow.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Every click resets the hidden state to $D$ (refractory) deterministically, so click times are regeneration times and the zero-run lengths are i.i.d.; see Appendix~\ref{app:derivations} and Supplementary Material, section~2. Evaluating $e_D^\top T_0^k T_1\mathbf{1}$ using the lower-triangular structure of $T_0$ gives the closed form~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main}. The tail sum $\sum_{j\ge k}g_j=(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$, and computing $h_{k+1}-h_k$ yields the positive quantity $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(p-c)^2p^kc^k/[(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^{k+1}-\Gamma c^{k+1})]$, confirming strict monotonicity. For the covariance: the nontrivial eigenvalue of the aggregate transition matrix $P=T_0+T_1$ is $\lambda=p-b$; two-state spectral decomposition gives~\eqref{eq:cov-main}. Setting $\lambda=0$ and solving for $\Gamma$ yields~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}.
\end{proof}

We now give a direct proof of exact $1$-dependence at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$, independent of the covariance argument and based only on the output-labelled kernels.

\begin{proof}[Proof of exact $1$-dependence at $\lambda=0$]
At the critical line~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}, the nontrivial eigenvalue $\lambda=p-b$ vanishes, so the aggregate hidden-state transition matrix $P=T_0+T_1$ satisfies $P=\mathbf{1}\pi$, where $\pi$ is the stationary distribution. Hence $P^\ell=P$ for every $\ell\ge1$.

Write $A_i^j=(A_i,\dots,A_j)$. Let $u=u_0\cdots u_r$ and $v=v_0\cdots v_s$ be visible words, and write
\[
T_u=T_{u_0}\cdots T_{u_r},
\qquad
T_v=T_{v_0}\cdots T_{v_s}.
\]
If the two words are separated by at least one sampling step, then their joint cylinder probability has the form
\[
\PP\!\bigl(A_0^r=u,\ A_{r+\ell+1}^{r+\ell+s+1}=v\bigr)
=
\pi\,T_u\,P^\ell\,T_v\,\mathbf{1},
\qquad \ell\ge1.
\]
At $\lambda=0$ this becomes
\[
\pi\,T_u\,P^\ell\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}
=
\pi\,T_u\,(\mathbf{1}\pi)\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}
=
(\pi\,T_u\,\mathbf{1})(\pi\,T_v\,\mathbf{1}).
\]
By stationarity, the two factors on the right are exactly $\PP(A_0^r=u)$ and $\PP(A_0^s=v)$. Therefore every pair of cylinder events separated by at least one intervening sample factorizes. By linearity, for any
\[
B\in\sigma(A_i:i\le m),
\qquad
C\in\sigma(A_j:j\ge m+2),
\]
we obtain $\PP(B\cap C)=\PP(B)\PP(C)$, which is exactly the definition of a $1$-dependent process.

Note that the output $A_n$ is \emph{not} a deterministic function of $(H_n,H_{n+1})$: the output-labelled kernels $T_0$ and $T_1$ both have nonzero entries in the refractory-to-refractory position (entries $c$ and $a$ respectively), so the same hidden-state transition $(D,D)$ can produce either output. The factorization argument above avoids this issue entirely by working at the level of the output-labelled kernel algebra rather than claiming a deterministic block code.
\end{proof}

The memory-transition threshold is therefore not a softened version of the ideal one. It is a different record-level transition inside a full-support renewal class. Since the Bernoulli benchmark in that class would require constant hazard, the Parry-law benchmark is excluded. \Cref{fig:hazard-cov} illustrates both the strictly increasing hazard and the covariance sign-pattern transition across $\Gamma_c$.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:no-exact-main}
No finite-recovery detector with positive parameters $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ realizes the Parry-law benchmark within the full-support renewal class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Equal-rate limit and Lambert $W$ expression for $\Gamma_c$]\label{rem:edge-cases}
Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery-main} is stated for $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$. In the equal-rate limit $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\to\Gamma$, the formula~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main} is $0/0$; l'H\^opital's rule in $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ yields
\[
g_k\big|_{\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma}
=
p^k\bigl[(1-p)+\Gamma\Delta\tau\bigl(k(1-p)-p\bigr)\bigr],
\]
which is a continuous function of $(\Gamma,\Delta\tau)$. The formulas for $a$, $b$, and $\lambda$ extend analogously to $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma$. The hazard remains strictly increasing in this limit by continuity of the positivity argument for $h_{k+1}-h_k$ proved in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

The critical rate $\Gamma_c$ is defined implicitly by~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}. Setting $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $x=\Gamma_c/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, that equation is equivalent to $s=-{\log x}/({1-x})$, or equivalently $xe^{-sx}=e^{-s}$. Introducing $u=-sx$, this becomes $ue^u=-se^{-s}$, so $u=W_k(-se^{-s})$ where $W_k$ denotes the $k$th real branch of the Lambert $W$ function. The branch choice depends on $s$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambert-Gc}
x=\frac{\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}}
=
-\frac{W_k(-se^{-s})}{s},
\qquad
k=
\begin{cases}
-1 & \text{if } 0<s<1, \\
 0 & \text{if } s>1,
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
with $x=1$ (i.e.\ $\Gamma_c=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$) at $s=1$. This is because at $s=1$ both branches meet at $W(-e^{-1})=-1$; for $s<1$ the nontrivial root $x>1$ lies on the $W_{-1}$ branch, while for $s>1$ the nontrivial root $x<1$ lies on the $W_0$ branch. As a numerical cross-check: for $(s,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)=(4,\,8,\,0.5)$, equation~\eqref{eq:lambert-Gc} gives $\Gamma_c\approx0.15862$, matching the root of~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main} to full precision. The spectral weight $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ in the rate formula~\eqref{eq:kms-rate-main} is the even part of the commutator function, given by $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathrm{Im}\,\mathcal{W}_x(\Omega+i0^+)/\pi$ where $\mathcal{W}_x$ is the Fourier transform of the Wightman two-point function restricted to the static worldline at $x$; positivity $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ holds for $\Omega>0$ in any KMS state with a nontrivial spectrum \cite{KayWald1991}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Soft approach to the ideal grammar}

The obstruction in Corollary~\ref{cor:no-exact-main} is exact, but the finite-recovery record still approaches the ideal grammar in a controlled small-$\Delta\tau$ regime. This is the sense in which finite recovery yields a \emph{soft} dead time.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:soft-main}
In the finite-recovery model,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soft-main}
\PP(A_{n+1}=1\mid A_n=1)=a
=\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2+O((\Delta\tau)^3)
\qquad (\Delta\tau\downarrow0).
\end{equation}
For a fixed window length $m\ge2$, let
\[
V_m=\{(a_0,\dots,a_{m-1})\in\{0,1\}^m:\exists\,k\le m-2,\ a_ka_{k+1}=11\}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:window-main}
\PP\!\bigl((A_0,\dots,A_{m-1})\in V_m\bigr)
\le
\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2\,(m-1)
+O\!\bigl(m(\Delta\tau)^3\bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus, for every fixed $m$, the finite-recovery record concentrates on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:soft-main} explains why the ideal grammar is a singular but physically meaningful limit. At fixed finite window length, inadmissible words become quadratically rare. Yet the Parry-law benchmark is still excluded at any positive sampling interval because the hazard remains strictly increasing and hence genuinely remembers the zero-run length.

\subsection{Spectral density and universal antibunching}

The finite-recovery renewal law has a second operational consequence, independent of the shell geometry but directly relevant to finite counting data. Define the spectral density
\[
f(\theta)=\rho(1-\rho)+2\sum_{n\ge1}\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)\cos(n\theta),
\qquad \theta\in[-\pi,\pi].
\]
Since the covariance sequence is explicit, so are the low-frequency noise properties.

This antibunching statement is not automatic. Finite recovery suppresses immediate reclicks, but it could in principle still generate longer-range positive correlations and enhanced low-frequency noise. The explicit covariance formula shows that the opposite happens: the reset mechanism suppresses low-frequency fluctuations throughout the parameter range. Once the covariance tail is geometric, both the spectral density and the Fano factor follow from summing the resulting series.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectrum-main}
For the stationary finite-recovery record,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectrum-main}
f(\theta)=
\rho(1-\rho)
+
2\rho(a-\rho)\,
\frac{\cos\theta-\lambda}{1-2\lambda\cos\theta+\lambda^2},
\end{equation}
and $f$ is strictly increasing on $[0,\pi]$. If
\[
N_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n,
\]
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fano-main}
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{\Var(N_N)}{\EE N_N}
=
1-\rho+\frac{2(a-\rho)}{1-\lambda}
<1.
\end{equation}
Hence the finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range.
\end{proposition}

Operationally, the memory shell can therefore be diagnosed by more than the sign of one covariance. As one scans through stationary observers, the lag-sign pattern changes at exact $1$-dependence, while the whole renewal class remains antibunched and low-frequency suppressed. This is a concrete record-level signature, although the finite-sample efficiency of different estimators is not analysed here. \Cref{fig:spectrum-fano} displays both the spectral density and the Fano factor across the parameter range.

\subsection{Finite-sample diagnostics (heuristic)}\label{subsec:finite-sample}
Finite records suggest useful diagnostics for the two thresholds, but we do not prove a central-limit theorem, estimator optimality, or confidence intervals in this paper. Given a binary record $(A_0,\dots,A_{N-1})$, define
\[
\widehat{h}_k=
\frac{\#\{\text{runs of exactly }k\text{ zeros}\}}
     {\#\{\text{runs of at least }k\text{ zeros}\}},
\]
\[
\widehat{\gamma}_n=
\frac{1}{N-n}\sum_k A_kA_{k+n}-\widehat\rho^2,
\qquad
\widehat F=
\frac{\widehat{\Var}(N_B)}{\widehat{\mathbb E}N_B},
\]
where $\widehat h_k$ is the empirical hazard, $\widehat\gamma_n$ the empirical lag-$n$ covariance, and $\widehat F$ the block Fano factor for block size $B$. By the renewal theorem and the ergodic theorem, all three converge a.s.\ to their population counterparts.

Appendix~\ref{app:monte} records the exact simulation protocol behind the numerical statements quoted below: the seeded pseudorandom sampling scheme, the inverse-CDF sampler for zero-run lengths, the block sizes, the burn-in discarded before estimation, and the number of replicated records for each parameter point.

For the memory shell, the simplest diagnostic is the sign of $\widehat\gamma_2$: it changes at $\Gamma_c$. We present this only as heuristic guidance. A rigorous regenerative CLT for $\widehat\gamma_2$ is not proved here. The lag-$2$ covariance is $\gamma_2=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda$, and the variance of $\widehat\gamma_2$ from a record of length $N$ scales heuristically as $\Var(\widehat\gamma_2)\sim\sigma^2_\gamma/N$ where $\sigma^2_\gamma\approx\rho^2(1-\rho)^2+\rho(a-\rho)^2$ captures the leading contribution from the zero-lag and lag-$1$ terms. The resulting signal-to-noise ratio is $\mathrm{SNR}\approx|\gamma_2|\sqrt{N}/\sigma_\gamma$.

To anchor that heuristic, we generated Monte Carlo records directly from the finite-recovery model for $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=5$, $\Delta\tau=0.3$ (so $\Gamma_c\approx2.09$). Over $5\times10^3$ synthetic records (see Appendix~\ref{app:monte}), the sign of $\widehat\gamma_2$ matched the true sign about $81\%$ of the time at $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=5\times10^3$ and $99.7\%$ of the time at $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=5\times10^4$ when $\Gamma=1.5\Gamma_c$. Closer to the threshold, at $\Gamma=1.1\Gamma_c$, the same frequencies were only about $57\%$ and $72\%$, rising to about $80\%$ only near $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=10^5$. These simulations support the qualitative message of the variance heuristic: away from the threshold the sign change is visible in moderate data sets, while close to the threshold much longer records are needed. The end-to-end shell estimation experiment in \cref{subsec:noisy-inverse} therefore uses more stable benchmark equations for $\widehat p$ and $\widehat\rho$ rather than a raw covariance-sign rule.

For the exact-clock shell, estimate $\widehat p=\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}/\#\{A_n=0\}$ and test $\widehat p=\varphi^{-1}$ by a chi-squared test on the $2\times2$ transition table; the heuristic sample size at separation $\delta=|p-\varphi^{-1}|$ is $N\sim\delta^{-2}/\rho$.

\section{Application: static KMS shell equations}\label{sec:kms-shells}

Having established the record-level thresholds in Sections~\ref{sec:operational}--\ref{sec:records}, we now apply them to a static KMS background and derive the corresponding shell equations.

The shell construction is conceptually straightforward. The constants $\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ are fixed by record statistics before geometry is introduced. Embedding the sampled counter in a static KMS background then turns those record-level thresholds into detector-defined loci by substituting the local excitation rate into the threshold equations.

Let $N(x)$ be the lapse function of a static spacetime and assume the field is in a global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state for Killing time. Along a static worldline at position $x$, Tolman scaling gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-main}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
At weak coupling, the excitation rate of a detector with energy gap $\Omega$ takes the Planck form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-rate-main}
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ denotes the local commutator spectral weight \cite{KayWald1991}. Static spherical detector-response formulas of this general type, including position-dependent spectral weights, appear in \cite{NgHodgkinsonLoukoMannMartinMartinez2014,TjoaMann2022JHEP,TjoaStaticSph2024}. The spectral weight is an input to the present response model. The homogeneous regime considered below is an explicit idealization in which its spatial variation is negligible over the static region of interest.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:shells-general-main}
Fix a detector design $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ in a static KMS background with $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ and lapse $N(x)>0$ everywhere in the region of interest.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The ideal dead-time detector realizes the Parry-law benchmark exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-phi-general-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\log\varphi
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the exact-clock locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface wherever the gradient of $x\mapsto\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau/(e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1)$ is nonvanishing; otherwise it may fail to be a manifold and we refer to it as the exact-clock locus.

\item The finite-recovery detector reaches the memory-transition point exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-mem-general-main}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the memory-transition locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface under the analogous nonvanishing-gradient condition. On $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

Theorem~\ref{thm:shells-general-main} states the shell equations in their natural response-model form.

\begin{remark}[Shell existence, uniqueness, and multiplicity]\label{rem:shell-existence}
The loci $\Sigma_\varphi$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ may be empty, disconnected, or contain multiple components when $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ varies with position. Their non-emptiness requires the defining functions in~\eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main}--\eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main} to achieve the respective target values $\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, which is a constraint on the range of $x\mapsto\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)/(e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1)$. Regularity (smooth codimension-one hypersurface) holds wherever the gradient of the defining function is nonzero. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$, the defining function reduces to a monotone function of the lapse $N(x)$ alone, so each shell is a single connected lapse level set under the standard condition $dN\neq0$; in this regime existence requires $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ to be large enough that the Planck denominator can match the target. Outside the homogeneous regime, with spatially varying $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$, each shell may be empty, may consist of multiple disconnected components, or may coincide with an extended region if the defining function is constant. The spectral-transport identity~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} governs the relationship between the two shells in that general case.
\end{remark}

The loci exist without any homogeneity assumption; regularity of their geometry depends on whether the defining function has nonvanishing gradient, which holds in generic static backgrounds. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the defining functions reduce to pure functions of $N(x)$, and both loci are level sets of the lapse function---smooth hypersurfaces under the standard condition that $dN\neq0$.

Outside the homogeneous regime, the spectral-transport identity~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} is the exact statement to use. If $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)(1+\varepsilon(x))$, then the shell-ratio law acquires the explicit multiplicative factor $(1+\varepsilon(x_\varphi))/(1+\varepsilon(x_{\mathrm{mem}}))$. Turning that identity into a quantitative shell-shift bound requires additional hypotheses: at minimum, a uniform control $|\varepsilon|\le\varepsilon_0$, differentiability of the shell-defining maps, and a nonvanishing derivative with respect to the lapse at the reference shell. We do not prove such a perturbative theorem here, and therefore make no quantitative shell-shift claim beyond the exact spectral-transport identity itself.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:homogeneous-main}
Assume that $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ on the region under study. Then both shells are pure lapse level sets. Their associated local inverse temperatures are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}
\beta_{\varphi}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-level-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\varphi}\},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

In this regime, the exact-clock shell is a lapse level set selected by a grammar law, while the memory shell is a lapse level set selected by a dependence law. The same local response scale therefore supports two inequivalent detector-selected hypersurfaces. \Cref{fig:shell-radii} illustrates the two shell lapse values and their proper-distance ratio in Schwarzschild.

Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define

\section{Application: shell-pair ratios and toy-model inverses}\label{sec:inverse}

We now examine the joint observables carried by the shell pair and their inverse content. Every statement in this section is a geometric consequence of the record-level thresholds derived in \cref{sec:records}, combined with the KMS rate formula~\eqref{eq:kms-rate-main}; the stochastic novelty of the paper remains concentrated in Section~\ref{sec:records}. The inverse statements below are conditional applications inside the stated response model, and the homogeneous formulas should be read as toy-model illustrations rather than detector-independent geometric observables.

\subsection{Two-mode protocol with shared hardware}\label{subsec:two-mode}
To make the shell-pair comparison operationally meaningful, we work with a single detector head whose gap $\Omega$, coupling constant $\lambda_c$, spectral weight $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$, and sampling step $\Delta\tau$ are fixed once and for all. The hardware can be toggled between two readout modes by classical electronics:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item \textbf{Mode A (ideal blanking).} After every click, the readout circuit enforces one deterministic blank interval of duration $\Delta\tau$ before the detector is armed again. This realizes the one-step dead-time model of \cref{sec:operational} with transition matrix~\eqref{eq:golden-mean-main} and selects the Parry-law benchmark at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$; we denote the corresponding target rate by $\Gamma_\varphi=\log\varphi/\Delta\tau$.
\item \textbf{Mode B (finite recovery).} The blanking gate is disabled and the same detector runs with an exponential recovery law characterized by $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, matching the finite-recovery model of \cref{sec:operational}. The record is a renewal process with hazard~\eqref{eq:hazard-main}, and the exact $1$-dependence threshold occurs at $\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\end{itemize}

Operationally the two modes correspond to setting the same photodetector or qubit sensor to ``forced reset'' versus ``free recovery,'' so both inherit the identical response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$. All shell statements in this section refer explicitly to this two-mode protocol: Mode~A defines the Parry benchmark and hence the exact-clock shell, while Mode~B defines the memory-transition shell. The unknown amplitude cancels when the two threshold equations are compared, which is the core reason to insist on a single shared hardware platform.

\subsection{Common-amplitude cancellation and spectral transport}

The most useful shell-pair observable arises when both shells are generated with the same hardware design operated in the two-mode protocol of \cref{subsec:two-mode}.

The key point is that the same hardware also carries the same unknown response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ into both shell equations. Dividing the two equations therefore removes precisely the nuisance factor that is hardest to calibrate experimentally. This is why the shell pair contains more robust inverse information than either shell separately.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:ratio-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and operate the shared hardware in the two-mode protocol of \cref{subsec:two-mode}. Let the resulting detector design generate an exact-clock shell at $r_{\varphi}$ and a memory-transition shell at $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in a static spherically symmetric background with lapse $N(r)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
The common multiplicative response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels exactly.
\end{theorem}

The cancellation in Theorem~\ref{thm:ratio-main} is precise but not detector free. The remaining right-hand side still depends on the detector gap, the sampling step, and the recovery rate through $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. What cancels is the unknown common response amplitude---precisely the form of cancellation needed for inverse use with shared hardware.

The homogeneous ratio law is the simplest specialization of a more general identity.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:spectral-transport-main}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:shells-general-main}, let $x_{\varphi}$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$ be points on the exact-clock and memory-transition shells for the same detector design. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-transport-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_{\varphi}}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}
Hence deviations from the homogeneous ratio law directly measure spectral transport between the two shell points.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectral-stability}
Assume the two-mode protocol and suppose that on the region of interest $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)(1+\varepsilon(x))$ with $|\varepsilon(x)|\le\varepsilon_0<1$. Let $R$ denote the shell-ratio observable on the left-hand side of~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} and let $C=\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)/\log\varphi$. Then
\[
C\,\frac{1-\varepsilon_0}{1+\varepsilon_0}
\le
R
\le
C\,\frac{1+\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} gives
\[
R=C\,\frac{1+\varepsilon(x_\varphi)}{1+\varepsilon(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}.
\]
The bounds follow by maximizing and minimizing the fraction over admissible $\varepsilon$. The denominator is positive because $\varepsilon_0<1$.
\end{proof}

Thus a uniform spectral inhomogeneity of size $\varepsilon_0$ perturbs the homogeneous ratio by at most the multiplicative factor $\frac{1+\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}$. This provides the requested perturbative stability: small relative variation of $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ produces a controlled, explicitly bounded modification of the shell ratio.

\subsection{Near-horizon universal hierarchy}

The shell-pair ratio becomes especially sharp near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:horizon-main}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:ratio-main}. Suppose, in addition, that the static region ends at a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-main}
N(\ell)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\ell+O(\ell^3)
\end{equation}
as the proper distance $\ell\downarrow0$ from the horizon. Then the shell distances satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ell-ratio-main}
\frac{\ell_{\varphi}}{\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\Bigl(1+O(\ell_{\varphi}+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
In Schwarzschild, if $A_H=16\pi M^2$ is the horizon area and $A_{\varphi}$ and $A_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are the areas of the shell spheres, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:area-ratio-main}
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left[
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\right]^2
\Bigl(1+O(\ell_{\varphi}+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Work in Gaussian normal (proper-distance) coordinates from the horizon: let $\ell$ denote proper distance from the bifurcation surface, so the lapse expands as $N(\ell)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\ell+O(\ell^3)$ with $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$. Then $e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(\ell)}-1=2\pi\Omega\ell(1+O(\ell))$. Inserting both shell points into the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main} and cancelling the common $2\pi\Omega$ factor gives~\eqref{eq:ell-ratio-main} to leading order. For the area excess in Schwarzschild, note that the areal radius is related to proper distance by $r-r_H=\ell^2/(8M)+O(\ell^4)$ near the horizon (since $d\ell=(1-2M/r)^{-1/2}\,dr$). Therefore the area excess satisfies
\[
A(\ell)-A_H=4\pi\bigl(r(\ell)^2-r_H^2\bigr)=4\pi(2r_H)(r-r_H)+O(\ell^4)=2\pi\ell^2+O(\ell^4),
\]
where we used $r_H=2M$. It follows that
$
(A_\varphi-A_H)/(A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H)=\ell_\varphi^2/\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}^2\,(1+O(\ell_\varphi+\ell_{\mathrm{mem}})),
$
which is the square of the proper-distance ratio, yielding~\eqref{eq:area-ratio-main}. The details are given in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}, with additional intermediate algebra recorded in Supplementary Material, section~5.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:horizon-main} shows that the shell pair acts as a detector-dependent stretched-horizon ruler. One shell is defined by the Parry-law benchmark, the other by exact $1$-dependence, yet their near-horizon separation is governed by a universal leading-order ratio that is independent of the common response amplitude.

A particularly informative regime is the fast-sampling limit. Let $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\downarrow0$ and $L=\log(1/s)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-main}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-ratio-main}
\frac{\ell_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\ell_{\varphi}}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus the memory-transition shell collapses toward the horizon parametrically faster than the exact-clock shell. The shell pair therefore resolves a genuine hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales rather than two nearby realizations of the same thermal profile.

\subsection{Toy-model static spherical inversion}

The shell pair also carries direct inverse content in static spherical settings, under the idealized homogeneous spectral-weight assumption $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$. In realistic black-hole detector problems, greybody factors and mode-sum structure generally make the response weight position-dependent. The formulas in this subsection and in \cref{subsec:worked-example} should therefore be read as exact only within the homogeneous toy model; \cref{subsec:nonhomogeneous} below shows how the interpretation changes when $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ varies.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:schw-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define the detector constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-constants-main}
\beta_{\varphi}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right).
\end{equation}
If the two shells generated by the same detector design occur at distinct radii $r_{\varphi}\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in Schwarzschild, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-infty-main}
\beta_\infty^2=
\frac{\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_{\varphi}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mass-main}
M=
\frac{r_{\varphi}r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)}
{2\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:schw-main} is exact algebra within the homogeneous toy model. Its significance is not that it bypasses detector design, but that two record-defined shell locations already determine the global redshift scale once the design is fixed.

The same logic extends beyond Schwarzschild in a local form that does not require prior calibration of the response amplitude, still within the homogeneous toy model.

The proof idea is again operational. Each detector mode supplies one shell-ratio equation, and two genuinely distinct modes provide two constraints for the two background parameters. The Jacobian condition below is simply the statement that those two constraints are locally independent, so the shell data can be inverted without separately calibrating the common response amplitude. The inverse establishes local \emph{identifiability}, not robustness; the conditioning of the inverse map depends on the shell separation, as analysed in \cref{rem:jacobian-main}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:two-mode-main}
Let $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ parametrize a $C^1$ family of static spherically symmetric backgrounds, with parameter $\theta\in\Theta$ determining both the lapse $N(r;\theta)$ and the global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty(\theta)$. Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime at fixed gap $\Omega$ and fixed hardware prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.

Fix one detector hardware design $(\Omega,\lambda_c)$ and operate it in two modes
\[
D_m=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m}),
\qquad m=1,2,
\]
with observed shell radii $r_{\varphi,m}$ and $r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}$. Define the known constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-constants-main}
C_m=\frac{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}{\log\varphi},
\qquad m=1,2,
\end{equation}
and the shell-ratio functions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-functions-main}
F_m(\theta)=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1}
-C_m.
\end{equation}
If $\theta_*\in\Theta$ satisfies $F_1(\theta_*)=F_2(\theta_*)=0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main}
\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0,
\end{equation}
then the two shell pairs determine $\theta$ locally uniquely near $\theta_*$. In particular, the reconstruction is self-calibrating: no prior knowledge of $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is required.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Each mode $D_m$ contributes one equation $F_m(\theta)=0$ by the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main}. Both functions are $C^1$ in $\theta$ under the stated regularity of $N$ and $\beta_\infty$, and the common amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels from each $F_m$ separately. The two equations therefore form a $C^1$ system in two unknowns. By the implicit function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002}, any solution $\theta_*$ at which $\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0$ is locally unique, and the solution map is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism near $\theta_*$. The amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ does not appear in the $F_m$, confirming self-calibration. The proof is recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}, with additional details in Supplementary Material, section~5.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Jacobian conditioning and error propagation]\label{rem:jacobian-main}
The Jacobian condition~\eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main} fails in particular when the two operating modes generate shell pairs with the same ratio $C_1=C_2$. Since $C_m$ depends on $(\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m})$, this is a codimension-one condition in the mode parameter space and is generically avoided by choosing modes with distinct sampling intervals.

For the single-mode Schwarzschild inverse of \cref{prop:schw-main}, the system is linear in $(u,v)=(\beta_\infty^2,\,2M\beta_\infty^2)$ with coefficient matrix $A=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}r_\varphi & -1\\ r_{\mathrm{mem}} & -1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ and $\det A=-(r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}})$. The condition number is $\kappa(A)=\|A\|\|A^{-1}\|$, which diverges as the shells approach each other. For the worked example of \cref{tab:worked} with mode~$D_1$, $\kappa(A)\approx111$: this is moderate conditioning. A first-order error propagation gives: a $1\%$ relative error on the shell radii $(r_\varphi,r_{\mathrm{mem}})$ produces a ${\sim}38\%$ relative error on $\beta_\infty^2$ but only a ${\sim}1.2\%$ relative error on $M$. The mass is well conditioned because it involves the ratio $r_\varphi r_{\mathrm{mem}}/(r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}})$, which is less sensitive to correlated perturbations of the two radii. Improving the conditioning requires wider shell separation, which can be achieved by increasing the product $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ (pushing the memory shell farther from the exact-clock shell).
\end{remark}

\subsection{End-to-end shell estimation from finite records}\label{subsec:noisy-inverse}

The algebraic inversion of \cref{prop:schw-main} becomes operationally relevant only after the shell radii themselves have been estimated from records. We therefore replaced the shell-perturbation test by an end-to-end pipeline inside the homogeneous Schwarzschild toy model. Using the mode-$D_1$ design of \cref{tab:worked}, we simulated records at five radii around $r_\varphi$ and five radii around $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$, each of length $N_{\mathrm{samp}}=1.2\times10^5$ sampled intervals after a burn-in of $5\times10^3$ intervals; Appendix~\ref{app:monte} gives the full algorithm.

For the exact-clock shell we estimated
\[
\widehat p(r)=\frac{\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}}{\#\{A_n=0\}}
\]
in the ideal-blanking mode and linearly interpolated the zero of $\widehat p(r)-\varphi^{-1}$. For the memory shell we used the finite-recovery mode, estimated the empirical click fraction $\widehat\rho(r)$, and then solved the explicit renewal-rate equation $\rho(\Gamma)=\widehat\rho(r)$ with $\rho(\Gamma)$ given by \eqref{eq:rho-main}; this yields a monotone estimator $\widehat\Gamma(r)$ of the local input rate. The shell estimator $\widehat r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ was then obtained by interpolating the zero of $\widehat\Gamma(r)-\Gamma_c$. This rate-matching step uses the full renewal model and is markedly more stable in finite data than a raw sign test on $\widehat\gamma_2$.

Across $120$ independent Monte Carlo replicates, all of which bracketed both shell roots, we obtained
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=0.2em]
\item $\widehat r_\varphi/r_{\mathrm s}=1.008077\pm0.000047$ and $\widehat r_{\mathrm{mem}}/r_{\mathrm s}=1.045097\pm0.000304$, to be compared with the exact shell locations $1.008069$ and $1.045109$.
\item $\widehat M=0.500006\pm0.000044$ and $\widehat\beta_\infty^2=158.00\pm1.27$, to be compared with the exact values $M=0.5$ and $\beta_\infty^2=157.9137$.
\end{itemize}
This experiment validates the full record-to-shell-to-inverse pipeline at the Monte Carlo level inside the homogeneous toy model. It does not provide a CLT or optimality theory for the shell estimators, and it should not be read as a detector-independent metrological claim. What it does show is that, once the response model is fixed, the finite-record benchmark equations recover the shell pair accurately enough for the toy-model inverse formulas to behave as predicted by the conditioning analysis of \cref{rem:jacobian-main}.

\subsection{Non-homogeneous spectral weight: toy correction model}\label{subsec:nonhomogeneous}

The homogeneous regime $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is an idealization. In a realistic Schwarzschild detector problem, the response weight is position-dependent due to greybody factors, mode-sum structure, and the detector's spatial profile. The spectral-transport identity~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} governs the resulting corrections. Rather than claiming a controlled microscopic example, we record here only a toy correction model showing how the homogeneous ratio law is altered when the spectral weight varies:
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)\bigl(1+\alpha(r(x)/r_H-1)\bigr),
\]
with $\alpha>0$ parametrizing a linear greybody enhancement away from the horizon. In the worked example of \cref{tab:worked} with $\alpha=0.5$, the spectral-weight ratio between the exact-clock and memory-transition shells of mode $D_1$ is
\[
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
=
\frac{1+\alpha(r_\varphi/r_H-1)}{1+\alpha(r_{\mathrm{mem}}/r_H-1)}
=
\frac{1+0.5\times0.008069}{1+0.5\times0.045109}
\approx0.982,
\]
so the right-hand side of the spectral-transport identity~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} receives a factor $\approx0.982$ relative to the homogeneous ratio $C_1=0.1648$, shifting the effective ratio to $\approx0.1618$. The qualitative structure---two shells, one grammar-selected and one dependence-selected---persists, but the quantitative shell-ratio law is no longer purely lapse information: it mixes lapse and spectral-transport data. We do not convert that ratio shift into a shell-position bound here, because doing so would require the additional implicit-function assumptions discussed after \cref{thm:shells-general-main}. This example shows that homogeneous-regime results serve as a controlled baseline; the spectral-transport identity~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} provides the exact correction when $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ varies.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The central result of this work is that, within the phenomenological counter model driven by the stationary UDW excitation rate, the full click record carries operational information beyond a rate-only summary. To be precise: the full record does not produce a geometric observable independent of the local excitation rate, because both thresholds are ultimately functions of the same rate. What the full record does is select two special record benchmarks---the Parry maximum-entropy grammar level and the exact $1$-dependence transition---that are not visible from the rate alone. In the phenomenological model studied here, those benchmarks organize themselves into two inequivalent codimension-one selectors in a static KMS background. The exact-clock shell is singled out by a grammar law, namely realization of the Parry measure in the ideal dead-time model. The memory shell is singled out by a dependence law, namely exact $1$-dependence in the finite-recovery renewal model.

Two observations sharpen this picture. Finite recovery approximates the ideal grammar on fixed finite windows with $O((\Delta\tau)^2)$ control, but the Parry-law benchmark is never reached because the hazard retains monotone memory. Universal antibunching holds throughout: the finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian with suppressed low-frequency noise.

On the geometric side, the most robust object is the shell pair rather than either shell in isolation. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime---an idealization in which the local response weight is position-independent---the pair is governed by exact common-amplitude cancellation and therefore yields a detector-dependent but amplitude-light observable. Near a nonextremal horizon, the ratio law produces a leading-order hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales. In static spherical settings, it yields local inverse content in the sense of identifiability, not robustness: the conditioning of the inverse map depends on the shell separation (\cref{rem:jacobian-main}). Outside the homogeneous regime, the same shell pair continues to be meaningful through the spectral-transport identity (\cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}); the toy correction model of \cref{subsec:nonhomogeneous} illustrates the resulting reinterpretation of shell-ratio data as mixed lapse and spectral-weight information.

Several structural features are expected to persist beyond the exponential recovery model. The renewal structure and memory-transition threshold should generalize to any recovery kernel for which a click resets the hidden state, since click times are then regeneration times and the covariance is determined by the hidden-state eigenvalue spectrum. We conjecture that the strictly increasing hazard holds whenever the recovery density has full support on $\mathbb R_+$; verification for non-exponential kernels requires separate proofs.

What is not conjectural is the spectral-transport identity (\cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}): it is an exact algebraic consequence of the shell equations and does not rely on the homogeneous spectral-weight assumption. In particular, greybody corrections in Schwarzschild enter through a calculable spectral-weight ratio $\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)/\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)$ rather than as an uncontrolled perturbation.

The relationship to prior literature can be stated precisely. Detector thermalization theory \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2023} asks whether the detector state converges to a Gibbs state; we ask instead which grammar and dependence benchmarks the stationary record realizes. Multi-time probability assignment and detector-kernel frameworks \cite{AnastopoulosSavvidou2015I,AnastopoulosSavvidou2015,AnastopoulosSavvidouEtAl2019} already show how detector records can be defined in relativistic settings. Local-measurement theory \cite{FewsterVerch2020,Fewster2020,PapageorgiouFraser2024} establishes the broader operational landscape, while \cite{MartinMartinezCausality2015} emphasizes the causal care needed when using detector models. UDW click-rate and switching theory \cite{LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008,SatzClick2007} demonstrates that click rates are sensitive to the switching profile; we go beyond the click rate to the full record structure. Detector-based geometry probing \cite{NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020,TjoaStaticSph2024} extracts geometric information from rate observables; we additionally exploit the grammar and covariance structure of the full record. Quantum-optical waiting-time and antibunching physics \cite{CarmichaelWaiting1989,KimbleAntibunching1977,Carmichael1993Book} provides the conceptual predecessors for the sub-Poissonian counting and renewal analysis of \cref{sec:records}. Renewal and $1$-dependent counting processes \cite{AaronsonGilat1988,DaleyVereJones2003,Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014} supply the probabilistic framework. The novelty here is specifically in the exact sampled-counter analysis and, conditionally on the chosen response model, in lifting the resulting record benchmarks to detector-defined shell loci in static KMS settings.

The limitations are equally clear. We have fixed a weak-coupling response model, a two-level detector, a single stationary worldline, discrete readout, and exponential recovery. Switching effects, nonexponential recovery, paralyzable counting models, multi-level detectors, greybody corrections, backreaction, and full finite-sample inference are outside the present scope \cite{Carmichael1993Book,WisemanMilburn2009}. The shell equations should therefore be read as exact within the stated detector-response class and as a template for more elaborate detector models rather than as universal geometric invariants.

Within that scope, the main record-level analysis is complete at the level proved here. The phenomenological counter model supplies two record-level thresholds with distinct statistical origins, a soft but singular route from finite recovery to the ideal grammar, and universal antibunching in the renewal regime. The finite-sample comments are deliberately heuristic and are supported only by the Monte Carlo checks reported in \cref{subsec:finite-sample,subsec:noisy-inverse}. As downstream applications, the shell equations yield an amplitude-cancelling shell-pair ratio, a near-horizon hierarchy law, and homogeneous-toy-model identifiability statements with conditioning analysis. These results demonstrate a concrete application of full-record detector statistics within a phenomenological sampled-counter model driven by the stationary UDW excitation rate.
