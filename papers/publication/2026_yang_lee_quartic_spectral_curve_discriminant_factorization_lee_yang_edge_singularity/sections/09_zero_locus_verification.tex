\section{Numerical checks and reproducibility}\label{sec:verification}

The preceding sections establish the branch structure and endpoint asymptotics of the quartic family by algebraic and analytic methods.
The computations in this section serve only as consistency checks for the real-axis modulus ordering and the endpoint zero formula.

\subsection{Validation targets from the exact theory}

Set
\[
\Omega:=\CC\setminus\bigl(\Branch\cup\{-1,0,1\}\bigr).
\]
For each fixed $y \in \Omega$, the spectral polynomial $\Pi(\cdot, y)$ has four simple roots $\lambda_1(y), \dots, \lambda_4(y)$, and Appendix~\ref{app:vacuum-edge} gives the spectral decomposition
\[
Z_m(y)=\sum_{j=1}^4 C_j(y)\lambda_j(y)^m,
\qquad
C_j(y)=\frac{\lambda_j(y)^3\,N(1/\lambda_j(y),y)}{\partial_\lambda\Pi(\lambda_j(y),y)}.
\]
The Beraha--Kahane--Weiss theorem, in the form used here \cite{BerahaKahaneWeiss1975,BerahaKahaneWeiss1978,Sokal2004}, gives two alternatives for a limit point of zeros of \(\{Z_m(y)\}_{m\ge0}\): either two or more eigenvalues of maximal modulus tie, or a unique dominant eigenvalue exists but its coefficient vanishes.
On \(\Omega\), the second alternative does not occur.
Indeed, for a simple root \(\lambda_j(y)\) one has \(\partial_\lambda\Pi(\lambda_j(y),y)\neq 0\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:minimal-order} shows that \(N(t,y)\) and \(D(t,y)\) are coprime for \(y\notin\{-1,0,1\}\).
Since \(D(1/\lambda_j(y),y)=0\), it follows that \(N(1/\lambda_j(y),y)\neq 0\), hence \(C_j(y)\neq 0\) on \(\Omega\).
Therefore, on the generic set \(\Omega\), the limiting accumulation set is governed solely by dominant equimodularity:
\[
\bigl\{y \in \Omega : \exists\, i \neq j \text{ with }
\abs{\lambda_i(y)} = \abs{\lambda_j(y)} = \max_k \abs{\lambda_k(y)}\bigr\}.
\]
Branch points in $\Branch$ arise as natural endpoints or singularities of such equimodular curves.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Reproducibility.}
Appendix~\ref{app:pseudocode} records the executable script and the plotting conventions explicitly, together with the exact commands needed to rerun the calculations.
All code, Sage worksheets, and notebooks have been bundled into the supplementary archive \texttt{quartic\_case\_study\_code.zip}, which mirrors the \texttt{scripts/} directory in the paper repository and is deposited alongside the manuscript.
The archive also contains a plain-text \texttt{environment.txt} that pins the tested configuration (Python~3.11.2, NumPy~1.26.4, Matplotlib~3.8.3, SymPy~1.12, mpmath~1.3.0, and SageMath~10.2).
Running
\[
\texttt{python scripts/generate\_validation\_figure.py}
\]
reproduces Figure~\ref{fig:validation}; the script writes \texttt{figures/zero\_locus\_validation.pdf} and logs the maximum modulus gap at the sample points.
For the figure shown, the left panel samples \(1500\) equally spaced real points on \([-1.6,1.2]\), the center panel plots the zeros of \(Z_{80}\) and \(Z_{120}\), and the right panel uses \(m=80,120,200\) together with the first four negative real zeros nearest \(0\).
Every call to high-precision arithmetic (mpmath with \(80\) digits) and every exact factorization used in the elimination steps leaves a short log file in \texttt{scripts/logs/}; these logs certify the modulus comparisons near \(y=0\), \(y=1\), and \(y=\ySub\).
For the center panel, Proposition~\ref{prop:double-zero} gives the exact factorization $(y+1)^2\mid Z_m(y)$ for all $m\ge2$; the script divides out $(y+1)^2$ symbolically before numerical rootfinding, so the double zero at $y=-1$ is obtained as a proven algebraic fact rather than inserted manually.
