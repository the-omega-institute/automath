\begin{abstract}
We study the polynomial family $\{Z_m(y)\}_{m\ge 0}$ defined by the rational generating function $Z(t,y)=N(t,y)/D(t,y)$ and the spectral polynomial
\[
\Pi(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4-\lambda^3-(2y+1)\lambda^2+\lambda+y(y+1).
\]
This paper treats \(\Pi(\lambda,y)\) as an explicit quartic spectral-curve case study.
The balanced-family reduction
\[
P_{a,c,d,g}(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4+a\lambda^3+(c-2y)\lambda^2+d\lambda+y(y-c)+g
\]
converts the affine curve by $\xi=y-\lambda^2$ into a Weierstrass model.
Within this family we prove the normalized statement used later in the paper: imposing double roots at $(\lambda,y)=(1,0)$ and $(\lambda,y)=(-1,1)$ forces the specialization $(a,c,d,g)=(-1,-1,1,0)$.
Its projective quartic curve is normalized by the elliptic curve
\[
Y^2=4\lambda^3-4\lambda+1,
\]
so the four eigenvalue branches form a genus-one degree-$4$ cover of the $y$-line.
The reduction determines all finite branch values and yields the discriminant factorization
\[
\Disc_\lambda \Pi=-y(y-1)(256y^3+411y^2+165y+32).
\]
Among the three real branch values, $y=0$ is the only dominant square-root point, whereas $y=1$ and the negative partner $y_{\mathrm{sub}}$ remain subdominant; the real locus projects to $[y_{\mathrm{sub}},1]\cup[0,\infty)$ with an overlap on $[0,1]$, so the total real image is $[y_{\mathrm{sub}},\infty)$, determining the real-root count of $\Pi(\cdot,y)$ for every real $y$.
We give the complete local expansions needed for a generic analytic double-root branching lemma, the amplitude symmetry, and the complex-uniform remainder bounds that drive the endpoint cosine law.
Consequently the negative zeros nearest $y=0$ satisfy
\[
y_{m,k}
=
-\frac{\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{2m^2}
-\frac{3\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{4m^3}
+O_k(m^{-4}),
\]
and the positive branch exhibits a quarter-power splitting governed by a double tangency at projective infinity.
Finally, we prove the degree law $\deg_y Z_m=\lfloor(m+3)/2\rfloor$ with explicit top-degree coefficients and an algebraically enforced double zero at $y=-1$.
The contribution is therefore a precise worked-out quartic example: an explicit elliptic normalization, an exact real-branch phase diagram, a dominant-branch analysis on \(y>0\), and the resulting endpoint zero asymptotics.
\end{abstract}
