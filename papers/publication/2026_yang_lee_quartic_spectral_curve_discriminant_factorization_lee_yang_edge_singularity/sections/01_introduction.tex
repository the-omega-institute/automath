\section{Introduction}

We study the polynomial family $\{Z_m(y)\}_{m\ge 0}$ defined by the rational generating function
\[
Z(t,y)=\sum_{m\ge 0} Z_m(y)t^m
=
\frac{1+yt+(y^2-y-1)t^2+(y^3-2y)t^3}
{1-t-(2y+1)t^2+t^3+y(y+1)t^4}.
\]
Equivalently, $\{Z_m(y)\}$ is generated by an order-$4$ companion transfer matrix with characteristic polynomial
\[
\Pi(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4-\lambda^3-(2y+1)\lambda^2+\lambda+y(y+1).
\]
The paper analyzes this induced quartic transfer family itself.
Its central questions are spectral: how the normalization of the quartic curve controls the real branch values, how the dominant-modulus envelope differs from the full ramification locus, and how a dominant square-root branch point governs the first finite-volume zeros.

For generic quartic spectral curves, these questions are difficult to answer globally.
In the present case the decisive simplification is not accidental: Section~\ref{sec:genus-one} shows that \(\Pi(\lambda,y)\) belongs to the balanced family
\[
P_{a,c,d,g}(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4+a\lambda^3+(c-2y)\lambda^2+d\lambda+y(y-c)+g,
\]
for which the substitution \(\xi=y-\lambda^2\) always removes the quartic obstruction.
The current transfer polynomial is the smooth genus-one specialization \((a,c,d,g)=(-1,-1,1,0)\), so its spectral curve admits the explicit elliptic normalization
\[
Y^2=4\lambda^3-4\lambda+1,
\]
and this turns the problem into a genus-one covering of the $y$-plane.
As a result, one can compute the finite branch values, classify the real branch points, and write the first edge zeros in closed form.

The paper should also be distinguished from three standard frameworks.
First, the positive-real branch point at $y=0$ considered here is not the classical Yang--Lee edge associated with closure of a zero-free gap in complex fugacity \cite{YangLee1952I,LeeYang1952II}.
Second, the Beraha--Kahane--Weiss theorem describes the general limiting zero set of recursively defined polynomial families \cite{BerahaKahaneWeiss1975}; our contribution is to determine explicitly both the ramification locus and the dominant-modulus geometry for a nontrivial quartic family.
Third, exceptional points are common in non-Hermitian spectral theory \cite{Heiss2012}; the novelty here is that all real branch collisions of the companion family can be classified explicitly as dominant or subdominant, and that the dominant square-root EP2 yields a closed finite-volume edge law.

The scope of the present article is therefore precise.
We do not derive the quartic transfer family from a separate microscopic model within this paper.
Rather, we treat the induced companion family as the primary mathematical-physics object and ask what can be proved exactly about its spectral curve, its real branch points, and the zeros of the associated polynomial sequence.

The main results are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Balanced-family reduction and elliptic specialization} (Section~\ref{sec:genus-one}).
We identify the four-parameter balanced family \(P_{a,c,d,g}\) for which the substitution \(\xi=y-\lambda^2\) converts the quartic curve to a Weierstrass model.
The present transfer polynomial is the specialization \((a,c,d,g)=(-1,-1,1,0)\), with Weierstrass invariants \(g_2=4\), \(g_3=-1\), and \(\Delta=37\).
Its branch values are \(y=0\), \(y=1\), and the three roots of \(256y^3+411y^2+165y+32\).

\item \textbf{Real branch-point classification and real-root counts} (Section~\ref{sec:ep-classification}).
Among the three real branch values, $y=0$ is the only dominant real EP2; $y=1$ and the negative branch value $y_{\mathrm{sub}}$ are subdominant.
The real locus of the elliptic curve projects exactly to $[y_{\mathrm{sub}},1]\cup[0,\infty)$, which yields the full count of real roots of $\Pi(\cdot,y)$ for every real $y$.

\item \textbf{A local edge law for dominant square-root EP2s} (Section~\ref{sec:physical-sheet}).
We prove a general normal form for a dominant pair of branches of the form
\(
\lambda_\pm(s)=\lambda_c\exp(\pm as+bs^2+O(s^3))
\),
and specialize it to the present family to obtain
\[
y_{m,k}
=
-\frac{\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{2m^2}
-\frac{3\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{4m^3}
+O_k(m^{-4})
\]
for the first negative zeros near $y=0$.

\item \textbf{Large-$y$ splitting and degree growth} (Sections~\ref{sec:physical-sheet} and \ref{sec:support-law}).
On the positive large-$y$ branch, the dominant root has a quarter-power splitting governed by a double tangency at projective infinity.
On the polynomial side, $\deg_y Z_m=\lfloor(m+3)/2\rfloor$, and the top-degree coefficients are explicit and subexponential in $m$.

\item \textbf{A rigid boundary value at $y=-1$} (Section~\ref{sec:boundary-zeros}).
For every $m\ge 2$, the polynomial $Z_m(y)$ has a double zero at $y=-1$.
This gives a uniform algebraic boundary point for the entire sequence.
\end{enumerate}

Sections~\ref{sec:model}--\ref{sec:hankel} define the family and establish the generic order-$4$ minimality of the recurrence.
Sections~\ref{sec:genus-one}--\ref{sec:boundary-zeros} contain the exact spectral and algebraic results.
Section~\ref{sec:verification} provides numerical verification of the real-axis modulus ordering and the first edge zeros, and Section~\ref{sec:discussion-new} concludes with the scope and limitations of the quartic analysis.
