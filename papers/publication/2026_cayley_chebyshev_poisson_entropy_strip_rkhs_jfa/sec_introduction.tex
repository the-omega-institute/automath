\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

The Cayley transform
\[
\iota(x)=\frac{1+\ii x}{1-\ii x}
\]
identifies the one-point compactification $\overline\RR$ of the line with
the circle~$\TT$.  Under this identification the normalized Haar measure
on~$\TT$ pulls back to the Cauchy law
\[
\frac{1}{\pi(1+x^{2})}\dd x=P_1(x)\dd x.
\]
This elementary identity does two things at once.  First, it rigidifies
the Cayley chart among normalized orientation-preserving
$C^1$-homeomorphisms $\overline\RR\to\TT$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:haar-pullback-uniqueness}).  Second, it places the
compactification problem directly inside the Poisson semigroup on~$\RR$,
with the logarithmic Jacobian producing an entropy identity in Cayley
coordinates (Theorem~\ref{thm:cayley-entropy-identity}).

The same visible phase factor also supplies the finite recovery interface.
Section~\ref{sec:finite-phase-sampling} records only the root-of-unity
averaging needed for Cayley Fourier coefficients.  The formal
phase-coordinate interface, Proposition~\ref{prop:phase-coordinate-interface},
identifies the finite grids and the analytic Fourier modes with the same
circle characters.  Proposition~\ref{prop:finite-grid-coefficient-recovery}
then gives coefficient recovery: below the explicit $K<N$ aliasing barrier
the grid coefficient is exact, and in general the error is the Fourier
aliasing tail.  This finite phase calculus is used later in
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-grid-poisson-moment-recovery}, which reads the first
nontrivial centred moment coordinates from exact samples on a fixed finite
phase grid with explicit large-time errors, and in
Proposition~\ref{prop:eighth-order-finite-phase-verification}, which shows
that a phase grid $\mu_N$ with $N\ge9$ exactly computes the finite mode
integrals appearing in the entropy expansion through order~$t^{-8}$ once
the moment coordinates are fixed.

The paper develops the analytic consequences of this normalization.  The
central object is the comparison kernel
\[
R_\varepsilon(y):=\frac{1+y^2}{1+(y-\varepsilon)^2},
\]
which measures the ratio between a translated Poisson profile and the
reference Cauchy density after the large-time rescaling
$x=\bar\gamma+ty$.  Passing to the Cayley angle variable
$y=\tan\theta$ reveals a finite Fourier structure governed by Chebyshev
polynomials of the second kind.  This yields the
Cayley--Chebyshev mode calculus of
Theorem~\ref{thm:cayley-chebyshev-mode}: every Taylor mode of
$R_\varepsilon$ admits an explicit trigonometric formula, and the mode
parity forces the relative entropy
$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P_t*\nu\|P_t(\cdot-\bar\gamma))$ to have only even
orders in each finite expansion range covered by the moment hypothesis
(Theorem~\ref{thm:poisson-kl-even-orders}).  At finite order, the same
phase-Fourier coefficients recover the first nontrivial centred moments
$(\sigma^2,\mu_3,\mu_4)$
(Proposition~\ref{prop:poisson-cauchy-moment-inversion-by-phase-modes});
the finite-grid version requires the aliasing statement and the uniform
large-time error estimate recorded in
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-grid-poisson-moment-recovery}.  Once those moment
coordinates are fixed, the same finite phase calculus provides an exact
finite-grid verification for the eighth-order coefficient
computation
(Proposition~\ref{prop:eighth-order-finite-phase-verification}).

The first main consequence is an explicit eighth-order asymptotic
formula for the entropy of a Poisson average relative to the translated
Poisson kernel with matching mean
(Theorem~\ref{thm:poisson-kl-eighth}).  Writing the coefficients of
orders $t^{-6}$ and $t^{-8}$ as $C_6$ and $C_8$, we then recenter them
at their symmetric two-point extremal values.  The resulting defect
variables form an ordered ladder.  Theorem~\ref{thm:poisson-defect-ladder}
identifies the two coefficients through explicit non-negative defect
coordinates.  Theorem~\ref{thm:poisson-defect-amplification} sharpens
this by showing that the eighth-order defect strictly amplifies the
sixth-order one, with equality only for the symmetric two-point law.  The
fourth-moment polynomial extracted from the sixth-order formula is first
separated from the asymptotic coefficient notation.  Under the same
finite centred seventh absolute moment hypothesis as the coefficient
expansion, Theorem~\ref{thm:poisson-defect-stability} gives a
coefficient-level $W_2$ control statement for the sixth-order asymptotic
defect relative to the symmetric two-point extremizer.
Corollary~\ref{cor:poisson-finite-time-defect-stability} converts this into
a quantitative large-time statement for the renormalized sixth-order
entropy deficit, with the explicit $C_8t^{-2}$ correction and an
order~$t^{-3}$ residual in the renormalized scale.  Thus the theorem and
corollary assume the finite centred seventh absolute moment; the
finite-fourth-moment consequence is kept outside the theorem as an
algebraic coupling bound and is not used to assert an entropy coefficient.

The same secant mode family also has a Hilbert-space realization for the
Poisson observation channel.  The functions
\[
\Psi_\varepsilon(y):=\frac{R_\varepsilon(y)-1}{\varepsilon}
\]
have exact Gram kernel
\[
K(a,b)=\frac{2}{4+(a-b)^2}
\]
(Theorem~\ref{thm:mode-gram-kernel}).  Theorem~\ref{thm:mode-space-rkhs}
shows that the closed span of the secant family is exactly the zero-mean
space $L^2_0(\omega)$ and is unitarily equivalent to the
reproducing-kernel Hilbert space generated by~$K$.  Through
Proposition~\ref{prop:gram-space-poisson-image}, this RKHS becomes the
Hardy-type Poisson image space $\sqrt{\pi}\,e^{-|D|}L^2(\RR)$, hence a
translation-invariant strip space with explicit Fourier-side norm and
holomorphic extension to $\{z\in\CC:\ |\Im z|<1\}$.  The strip model
splits orthogonally into two Hardy summands
(Proposition~\ref{prop:poisson-hardy-splitting}).  This identifies the
ambient RKHS in which the fluctuation profiles from the entropy comparison
become evaluation functionals (Theorem~\ref{thm:poisson-channel-rkhs}).

The discrete part comes from the integer secant orbit, with the sampling
mechanism kept to the standard principal shift-invariant-space framework.
What is specific to the Poisson strip is
Proposition~\ref{prop:poisson-lattice-symbol}: the lattice symbol has a
closed hyperbolic formula and sharp constants.  With that symbol in hand,
Proposition~\ref{prop:poisson-lattice-sampling} records the resulting
Riesz and stable sampling bounds for
$\{K(\cdot,n)\}_{n\in\ZZ}$ and $\{\Psi_n\}_{n\in\ZZ}$, and
Corollary~\ref{cor:poisson-cardinal-reconstruction} records the
corresponding cardinal reconstruction: there is a unique cardinal kernel
$L\in\mathcal H_K(\ZZ)$ with $L(n)=\delta_{0n}$, every lattice datum in
$\ell^2(\ZZ)$ admits a unique interpolant
\[
F_\alpha(z)=\sum_{n\in\ZZ}\alpha_nL(z-n),
\]
and the interpolant norm is given exactly by the reciprocal lattice
symbol.  In Fourier realization, $\mathcal H_K(\ZZ)$ is therefore a
principal shift-invariant space generated by the Poisson strip kernel.
Theorem~\ref{thm:poisson-observation-lattice-sampling} then connects this
lattice chain to the Poisson observation profiles: integer samples recover
the lattice projection of the RKHS observation channel, and they recover
the full channel exactly when that channel belongs to
$\mathcal H_K(\ZZ)$.
Thus the lattice material is included as the integer-orbit reconstruction
theory of the same Poisson observation channel, not as an independent
sampling theorem.

The centered Poisson trace proposition
(Proposition~\ref{prop:poisson-central-two-channel}) provides the scalar
reconstruction input for this RKHS interpretation.  It shows that
the central Poisson trace and its central derivative recover the
Laplace transform of the centred characteristic function; by Laplace
uniqueness and characteristic-function inversion they determine the
centred law, and together with the mean they determine the original
measure.  This proposition fits naturally after the mode-space/RKHS package,
where the two observation channels become evaluation functionals.

The standard background for Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} and
Theorem~\ref{thm:cayley-entropy-identity} is classical: the Poisson semigroup
and Fourier representation \cite{Folland1995,Grafakos2014,Stein1971}, the
Laplace transform \cite{Widder1941}, characteristic functions and inversion
\cite{Lukacs1970,Feller1971}, reproducing kernels \cite{Aronszajn1950}, and
Hardy and Poisson boundary theory \cite{Duren1970,Rudin1987}.  These are
used as analytic tools rather than as novelties.  Likewise, the
Haar-pullback rigidity and entropy change-of-variables identity below are
normalizing devices for the Cayley chart.

For entropy asymptotics, we view the entropic CLT, stable-law, and
entropy-production literature as methodological context, citing
\cite{Barron1986,JohnsonBarron2004,ArtsteinBallBartheNaor2004,CarlenCarvalho1992}
and
\cite{BobkovChistyakovGotzeEdgeworth2013,BobkovChistyakovGotzeBerryEsseen2014,BobkovChistyakovGotze2013,Toscani2015,Toscani2016}.
Stable de Bruijn identities and fractional Fisher-information methods
for stable laws, including the Cauchy endpoint, are represented by
\cite{JohnsonStableDeBruijn2013,Toscani2015}.
Recent stability results for entropy-type functional inequalities provide
the closest functional-inequality context for the defect coordinates and
the extremizer stability viewpoint.  The closest recent JFA comparison is
Carlen's stability theorem for the
logarithmic Hardy--Littlewood--Sobolev inequality and its Keller--Segel
application \cite{Carlen2025LogHLS}.  The logarithmic Sobolev stability
result of Brigati--Dolbeault--Simonov
\cite{BrigatiDolbeaultSimonov2024LogSobolev} gives a second recent JFA
reference point for quantitative stability near entropy extremizers.  Both
papers quantify how entropy deficits control distance from extremizers.
The result here is narrower and coefficient-level: it gives finite-moment
asymptotic defects for
Poisson shifts rather than a stability theorem for log-HLS or log-Sobolev
deficits.  The specific contribution is the Poisson-shift coefficient
bookkeeping:
even-order rigidity is parity-controlled, the explicit \(t^{-8}\) term is
computed via the mode family, and Theorems~\ref{thm:poisson-defect-ladder}
and~\ref{thm:poisson-defect-amplification} refine the entropy story via
the defect decomposition.  These results are not de Bruijn identities:
they are asymptotic relative-entropy expansions for arbitrary
finite-moment inputs under comparison with the translated Poisson kernel.

Recent Cauchy information theory is also directly relevant.  Verd{\'u}'s
account of Cauchy information quantities and the divergence geometry of
Cauchy laws studied by Nielsen--Okamura
\cite{Verdu2023Cauchy,NielsenOkamura2023Cauchy} concern exact
information measures within Cauchy families.  Here the Cauchy law enters
through the Cayley pullback and as the Poisson kernel; the main entropy
coefficients measure how a general finite-moment perturbing law separates
from its matched translated Poisson profile at large time.

The lattice results in Section~\ref{sec:strip-lattice} are positioned
against established shift-invariant-space theory:
\begin{itemize}
\item the structural core (Riesz systems, stable translates, principal generators)
is classical by \cite{deBoorDeVoreRon1994,RonShen1995,Bownik2000};
\item nonuniform and stable sampling in finitely generated shift-invariant spaces
is treated in \cite{AldroubiGrochenig2001}; and
\item general RKHS sampling, localized reconstruction, and Galerkin
sampling frameworks are developed in
\cite{NashedWalter1991,NashedSun2010,ChengJiangSun2016,GrochenigRomeroStockler2020,FuhrGrochenigHaimiKlotzRomero2018}.
\end{itemize}
This is the mathematical context for
Proposition~\ref{prop:poisson-lattice-sampling}
and Corollary~\ref{cor:poisson-cardinal-reconstruction}, which by
themselves are standard consequences of that framework once the Poisson
symbol is known.

For explicit Cauchy/Lorentz shifts on integer lattices and nod-function
constructions, we also acknowledge the Gaussian/Lorentz line of work
\cite{KiselevMininNovikov2016,KiselevMininNovikov2019} as prior art.
For the broader radial-basis and cardinal-interpolation viewpoint we also
refer to \cite{Buhmann2003}.
The additional contribution in Section~\ref{sec:strip-lattice} is the
Poisson-strip realization of the secant mode space together with the
closed formulas for the lattice symbol, the cardinal kernel, and the RKHS
norm; these formulas are what make the integer-orbit reconstruction theorem
available for the observation profiles above.

The paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation and conventions.
Section~\ref{sec:cayley-gate} records the Cayley formulas, and
Section~\ref{sec:haar-pullback} proves the rigidity of the Haar
pullback.  Section~\ref{sec:finite-phase-sampling} records the finite
root-of-unity sampling facts used by the Cayley Fourier coefficients.
Section~\ref{sec:precision-potential} develops the
entropy identity, the Cayley--Chebyshev mode calculus, the entropy
asymptotics, the defect amplification, and the coefficient-level and
quantitative large-time $W_2$ control theorems.
Section~\ref{sec:gram-space} constructs the mode Gram kernel and its
reproducing-kernel Hilbert space, and proves the centered
reconstruction theorem.  Section~\ref{sec:strip-lattice} starts by fixing
the complex-Hilbert-space convention and the closed-span notation for the
lattice subspaces, identifies the strip RKHS with a Poisson image space,
establishes the Hardy splitting, and develops lattice sampling and
cardinal reconstruction with explicit constants, including the
integer-orbit reconstruction theorem for Poisson observation channels.
The appendix records the weighted trigonometric integrals used in the
eighth-order expansion.
