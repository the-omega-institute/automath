\section{Concluding remarks}
\label{sec:discussion}

The finite-window model singles out a concrete obstruction to restricting Fibonacci expansions before normalization. The structural point is not only that the relevant graph can be written down, but that its size and its decoding threshold are forced by the local span of the normalization rule. The span-\(3\) rewrite \(011\to100\) yields follower memory \(2\) for the synchronized pair shift and core length bound \(4\) for local invertibility, so the four-state presentation and the threshold \(m\ge 3\) are consequences of the same local mechanism.

In that form the Fibonacci calculation becomes the first explicit example of a broader local principle: zero-synchronized span-\(r\) normalizers produce synchronized pair shifts with memory \(r-1\), and their window codes can be tested for conjugacy by finitely many cores of length at most \(r+1\). The later discrepancy laws, decoder statements, and weighted-fiber asymptotics are therefore organized as consequences of a structural theorem rather than as independent finite-state computations.
