\section{Folding and compatible restriction}
\label{sec:multiscale}

\subsection{A rewriting description of \texorpdfstring{\(\Fold_m\)}{Fold\_m}}

The fold can be computed by a local rewriting procedure for Zeckendorf normalization. Since the literature and the appendices use both LH and MSD-first orderings, we first record the translation between them.

\begin{lemma}[Convention translation]\label{lem:convention}
Let \(\omega=(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m)\in\Om_m\) be written in LH order, so that position \(k\) carries weight \(\Fib_{k+1}\), and let \(\omega^\sharp=(\omega_m,\dots,\omega_1)\) be the same word written in MSD-first order. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The local MSD-first rule \(011\to 100\) applied at positions \((k,k+1,k+2)\) corresponds, after reversal, to the LH rule \(110\to 001\) applied at positions \((m-k-1,m-k,m-k+1)\).
\item[(ii)] After adjoining a leading zero in MSD-first order, scanning left to right and repeatedly applying \(011\to 100\) is equivalent to scanning the LH word from high weights to low weights and repeatedly applying \(110\to 001\).
\item[(iii)] In either convention, each rewrite preserves the Fibonacci value \(N(\omega)\) and strictly decreases the number of \(1\)s. The unique normal form is the Zeckendorf representation of \(N(\omega)\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Part (i) is immediate from reversal: \((a,b,c)\mapsto(c,b,a)\), so \(011\to 100\) becomes \(110\to 001\). Part (ii) follows because left-to-right scanning in MSD-first order is the same as scanning the LH word from the highest-weight position toward the lowest. For part (iii), the identity \(\Fib_k+\Fib_{k+1}=\Fib_{k+2}\) shows that each rewrite preserves the represented value, while the number of \(1\)s drops by one. Termination and uniqueness of the normal form are proved in Appendix~\ref{app:rewriting}.
\end{proof}

Thus one may work either with the MSD-first rule
\[
011\to 100
\]
or, in the window convention of \eqref{eq:Nomega}, with the LH rule
\[
110\to 001.
\]
Both realize the same normalization procedure; see \cite{Frougny1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998,Frougny1999,ShallitShan2023}.

\begin{proposition}[Well-definedness, idempotence, surjectivity]\label{prop:idempotent}
For each \(m\ge 1\), the fold \(\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m\) is well defined, idempotent, and surjective:
\[
\Fold_m\circ \Fold_m=\Fold_m,
\qquad
\Fold_m(\Om_m)=X_m.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Appendix~\ref{app:rewriting} shows that the rewriting system terminates and is confluent. Hence every \(\omega\in\Om_m\) has a unique normal form with the same Fibonacci value, and by definition this normal form is \(\Fold_m(\omega)\). This proves well-definedness.

If \(x\in X_m\), then \(x\) already contains no adjacent \(1\)s and therefore is its own Zeckendorf normal form. Hence \(\Fold_m(x)=x\). Applying this to \(x=\Fold_m(\omega)\in X_m\) gives idempotence, while the same fixed-point property shows that every \(x\in X_m\) lies in the image.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Restriction on the stable side}

For \(m_2\ge m_1\), define
\[
\proj_{m_2\to m_1}:X_{m_2}\to X_{m_1},
\qquad
\proj_{m_2\to m_1}(x_1\cdots x_{m_2}):=x_1\cdots x_{m_1}.
\]
Then \((X_m,\proj_{m_2\to m_1})\) is an inverse system, and the inverse limit
\[
X_\infty:=\varprojlim_m X_m
\]
is naturally the one-sided golden-mean shift; see \cite[Chapter~1]{LindMarcus1995} or \cite[Chapter~1]{Kitchens1998}.

\subsection{Compatible restriction on microstates}

Let
\[
\naive_{m+1\to m}:\Om_{m+1}\to\Om_m,\qquad
\naive_{m+1\to m}(\omega_1\cdots\omega_{m+1}):=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m,
\]
be the naive truncation. The stable inverse system suggests looking for a map on microstates for which folding commutes with restriction, namely
\begin{equation}\label{eq:commuting}
\Fold_m\circ \aware_{m+1\to m}
\;=\;
\proj_{m+1\to m}\circ \Fold_{m+1}.
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Fold-aware restriction]\label{def:fold-aware}
For \(\omega\in\Om_{m+1}\), define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:re-def}
\aware_{m+1\to m}(\omega):=\proj_{m+1\to m}\!\bigl(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega)\bigr)\in X_m\subset\Om_m.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Strict commutation]\label{lem:strict-commute}
The map \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) satisfies \eqref{eq:commuting}. Moreover, it is the unique map \(\Om_{m+1}\to X_m\) with this property.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since \(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega)\in X_{m+1}\), its truncation belongs to \(X_m\) and is therefore fixed by \(\Fold_m\). It follows that
\[
\Fold_m(\aware_{m+1\to m}(\omega))
=
\Fold_m\!\left(\proj_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega))\right)
=
\proj_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega)),
\]
which is exactly \eqref{eq:commuting}. If \(T:\Om_{m+1}\to X_m\) also satisfies \eqref{eq:commuting}, then \(\Fold_m\) acts as the identity on \(T(\omega)\), so \eqref{eq:commuting} forces
\[
T(\omega)=\proj_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega))=\aware_{m+1\to m}(\omega).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[A compatible restriction at \(m=4\)]\label{ex:aware4}
Take \(\omega=11011\in\Om_5\), the word from Example~\ref{ex:worst4}. Since \(N(\omega)=16=\Fib_7+\Fib_4\), one has
\[
\Fold_5(\omega)=00100.
\]
Hence
\[
\aware_{5\to 4}(\omega)=0010.
\]
By contrast,
\[
\naive_{5\to 4}(\omega)=1101.
\]
This is the first nontrivial instance in which compatibility with folding forces a complete change in the visible \(4\)-window.
\end{example}
