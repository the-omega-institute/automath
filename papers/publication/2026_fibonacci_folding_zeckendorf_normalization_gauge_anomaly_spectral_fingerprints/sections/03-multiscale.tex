\section{Compatible restriction across scales}
\label{sec:multiscale}

The finite-window fold admits a simple local description. In most-significant-digit-first order the Zeckendorf rewriting rule is
\[
011\to 100,
\]
whereas in the low-to-high convention of Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} it becomes
\[
110\to 001.
\]
We record the translation once, since the main text and the appendices use both conventions.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:convention}
Let \(\omega=(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m)\in\Om_m\) be written in low-to-high order, and let \(\omega^\sharp=(\omega_m,\dots,\omega_1)\) be the same word in most-significant-digit-first order. Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(i)] The rule \(011\to 100\) in most-significant-digit-first order corresponds, after reversal, to the rule \(110\to 001\) in low-to-high order.
\item[(ii)] Prefixing a zero and scanning left to right in most-significant-digit-first order is equivalent to scanning the low-to-high word from high weights to low weights.
\item[(iii)] Each rewrite preserves the Fibonacci value and strictly decreases the number of \(1\)s. The unique normal form is the Zeckendorf representation of the same integer.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Part (i) is immediate from reversal. Part (ii) is just the corresponding statement about scan direction. For part (iii), the identity \(\Fib_k+\Fib_{k+1}=\Fib_{k+2}\) shows that each rewrite preserves the represented value, while the number of \(1\)s drops by one. Termination and uniqueness of the normal form are proved in Appendix~\ref{app:rewriting}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:idempotent}
For each \(m\ge 1\), the map \(\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m\) is well defined, idempotent, and surjective:
\[
\Fold_m\circ \Fold_m=\Fold_m,
\qquad
\Fold_m(\Om_m)=X_m.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Appendix~\ref{app:rewriting} shows that the rewriting system terminates and is confluent. Hence every \(\omega\in\Om_m\) has a unique normal form with the same Fibonacci value, and by definition this normal form is \(\Fold_m(\omega)\). This proves that \(\Fold_m\) is well defined.

If \(x\in X_m\), then \(x\) already contains no adjacent \(1\)s and is therefore its own Zeckendorf normal form. Hence \(\Fold_m(x)=x\). Applying this to \(x=\Fold_m(\omega)\in X_m\) gives idempotence, and the same fixed-point property shows that every \(x\in X_m\) lies in the image.
\end{proof}

For \(m_2\ge m_1\), define
\[
\proj_{m_2\to m_1}:X_{m_2}\to X_{m_1},
\qquad
\proj_{m_2\to m_1}(x_1\cdots x_{m_2}):=x_1\cdots x_{m_1}.
\]
Then \((X_m,\proj_{m_2\to m_1})\) is an inverse system, and its inverse limit is naturally the one-sided golden-mean shift; see \cite[Chapter~1]{LindMarcus1995} or \cite[Chapter~1]{Kitchens1998}.

On the microstate side there is also the naive truncation
\[
\naive_{m+1\to m}:\Om_{m+1}\to\Om_m,\qquad
\naive_{m+1\to m}(\omega_1\cdots\omega_{m+1})=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m.
\]
The problem is to find a restriction map on microstates for which folding commutes with restriction:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:commuting}
\Fold_m\circ \aware_{m+1\to m}
\;=\;
\proj_{m+1\to m}\circ \Fold_{m+1}.
\end{equation}
The natural candidate is obtained by restricting on the stable side after folding.

\begin{definition}\label{def:fold-aware}
For \(\omega\in\Om_{m+1}\), define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:re-def}
\aware_{m+1\to m}(\omega):=\proj_{m+1\to m}\!\bigl(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega)\bigr)\in X_m\subset\Om_m.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:strict-commute}
The map \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) satisfies \eqref{eq:commuting}. Moreover, it is the unique map \(\Om_{m+1}\to X_m\) with this property.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since \(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega)\in X_{m+1}\), its truncation belongs to \(X_m\) and is fixed by \(\Fold_m\). Therefore
\[
\Fold_m(\aware_{m+1\to m}(\omega))
=
\Fold_m\!\left(\proj_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega))\right)
=
\proj_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega)),
\]
which is exactly \eqref{eq:commuting}. If \(T:\Om_{m+1}\to X_m\) also satisfies \eqref{eq:commuting}, then \(\Fold_m\) acts as the identity on \(T(\omega)\), so
\[
T(\omega)=\proj_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\omega))=\aware_{m+1\to m}(\omega).
\]
\end{proof}
