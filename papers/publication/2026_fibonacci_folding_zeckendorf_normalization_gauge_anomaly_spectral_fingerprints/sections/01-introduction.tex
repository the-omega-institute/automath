\section{Introduction}

Zeckendorf normalization replaces a Fibonacci expansion by the unique expansion with no adjacent \(1\)s. In its classical form one starts with an integer, or with an infinite digit string, and studies the normalized output. The point of view adopted here is different. We keep the observation window finite and regard a binary word \(\omega\in\Om_m\) as a microstate whose visible output is obtained by three operations: Fibonacci-weighted evaluation, Zeckendorf normalization, and restriction back to the original window. The resulting map \(\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m\) is a projection onto the length-\(m\) language \(X_m\) of the golden-mean shift.

Once one asks how these finite windows behave across scales, a new phenomenon appears. Passing from length \(m+1\) to length \(m\) by naive truncation need not agree with first normalizing at scale \(m+1\) and then restricting the normalized word. There is, however, a unique restriction operator \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) compatible with folding, and the discrepancy between \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) and naive truncation is the central object of the paper. We refer to it as the \emph{restriction discrepancy}; the term \emph{gauge anomaly} from earlier drafts will appear only occasionally as shorthand.

\subsection{Main results}

The first part of the paper establishes the finite-window framework. Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} introduces the fold \(\Fold_m\), while Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} records a rewriting realization of Zeckendorf normalization and the compatible restriction map \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\). In particular, Proposition~\ref{prop:idempotent} shows that \(\Fold_m\) is well defined, idempotent, and surjective onto \(X_m\), and Lemma~\ref{lem:strict-commute} shows that \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) is the unique map into \(X_m\) for which restriction and folding commute exactly.

The first substantive phenomenon is that the discrepancy is not a boundary defect. Proposition~\ref{prop:worst} gives, for every \(m\), an explicit word \(\omega^\star\in\Om_{m+1}\) with \(G_m(\omega^\star)=m\), so the two restrictions may disagree at every visible coordinate. Proposition~\ref{prop:tail-patch} strengthens this by showing that no correction rule which modifies only a uniformly bounded tail of the naive restriction can restore commutation.

The structural theorem of the paper is Theorem~\ref{thm:pair-language}. Under the uniform measure on microstates and after passage to the bulk limit, one obtains a stationary pair process \((X_t,Y_t)\) whose first coordinate is the raw digit and whose second coordinate is the normalized digit. Theorem~\ref{thm:pair-language} identifies the interior language of this process with the label language of an explicit four-state synchronized right-resolving graph. Once that presentation is known, Theorem~\ref{thm:joint}, Corollary~\ref{thm:llnclt}, and Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum} are explicit finite-state consequences; we record them because they quantify the restriction discrepancy exactly in the stationary bulk regime.

The sequence-level part concerns the induced folding code. For each window size \(m\), the fold defines a sliding block code \(\Phi_m\) on the full two-sided shift. Proposition~\ref{prop:sofic} shows that the image is always sofic, which is the expected symbolic-dynamical starting point. The nontrivial issue is invertibility. Theorem~\ref{thm:core-resolution} reduces every ambiguity to finitely many local cores of lengths \(3\) and \(4\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:conjugacy} shows that these ambiguities are resolved by a three-label decoder as soon as \(m\ge 3\). Proposition~\ref{thm:m2} identifies the unique exceptional fiber when \(m=2\).

The final part of the paper concerns weighted fold fibers. For
\[
Z_m(y)=\sum_{\omega\in\Om_m} y^{|\Fold_m(\omega)|_1},
\]
the same four-state presentation yields an explicit transfer-matrix description. Proposition~\ref{prop:recurrence}, Corollary~\ref{thm:hankel}, Proposition~\ref{prop:discriminant}, and Corollary~\ref{thm:weight-stats} record the resulting recurrence, exceptional locus, discriminant factorization, and limit laws for the output Hamming weight. The conceptual point of this section is Theorem~\ref{thm:microcanonical-output}, which identifies the same Perron root as the Legendre transform of a constrained entropy profile on the pair shift.

\subsection{Relation to earlier work}

The numeration-theoretic background goes back to the classical papers of Lekkerkerker and Zeckendorf \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}; for broader context on Fibonacci numeration, automata-based normalization, and weighted numeration systems we use \cite{Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998,AlloucheShallit2003,GrabnerKirschenhoferTichy2002,DumontSidorovThomas1999,ShallitShan2023}. The symbolic-dynamical language used later belongs to the standard theory of sofic shifts and sliding block codes \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}, and the transfer-matrix arguments are standard applications of Perron--Frobenius and thermodynamic-formalism methods \cite{Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}.

The closest antecedents are the works on the normalized Fibonacci digit process, or equivalently on the golden-ratio Erd\H{o}s measure. Alexander and Zagier \cite{AlexanderZagier1991}, Sidorov and Vershik \cite{SidorovVershik1998}, Bezhaeva and Oseledets \cite{BezhaevaOseledets2007,BezhaevaOseledets2010,BezhaevaOseledets2Step}, and Kempton \cite{KemptonDigits2014} analyze the normalized digits themselves and, in particular, the classical density \(5/18\) for the digit \(1\). By contrast, the present paper is organized around the finite-window comparison between a raw microstate and its normalized restriction. To the best of our knowledge, the pair observable \((X_t,Y_t)\), the discrepancy process \(X_t\oplus Y_t\), and the compatible-restriction problem created by finite windows have not been isolated previously in the Zeckendorf literature.

The contribution is therefore not a new abstract theorem about sofic shifts or transfer matrices; those tools are classical. Rather, a canonical finite-window normalization problem produces a concrete four-state object, and the paper shows that this object governs three different aspects of the model at once: the bulk statistics of the restriction discrepancy, the invertibility threshold for the induced sliding block code, and the thermodynamics of weighted fold fibers. The explicit constants and rational functions obtained later should be read in that light, as exact consequences of one structural identification.

\subsection{Organization of the paper}

Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation for finite windows, Fibonacci evaluation, and Zeckendorf normalization. Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} introduces folding and compatible restriction. Section~\ref{sec:gauge} studies the restriction discrepancy, beginning with worst-case constructions and then identifying the bulk pair language and its consequences. Section~\ref{sec:sequence} turns to the induced sliding block code and proves the sharp conjugacy threshold. Section~\ref{sec:partition} treats weighted fibers, first through transfer-matrix consequences and then through the variational interpretation. The appendices collect the normalization argument, the detailed construction of the four-state model, the core tables for the decoder, and the algebra behind the recurrence and discriminant formulas.
