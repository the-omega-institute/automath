\section{Introduction}

Zeckendorf normalization replaces a Fibonacci expansion by the unique expansion with no adjacent \(1\)s. In its classical form one starts with an integer, or with an infinite digit string, and studies the normalized output. The point of view adopted here is different. We keep the observation window finite and regard a binary word \(\omega\in\Om_m\) as a microstate whose visible output is obtained by three operations: Fibonacci-weighted evaluation, Zeckendorf normalization, and restriction back to the original window. The resulting map \(\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m\) is a projection onto the length-\(m\) language \(X_m\) of the golden-mean shift.

Once one asks how these finite windows behave across scales, a new phenomenon appears. Passing from length \(m+1\) to length \(m\) by naive truncation need not agree with first normalizing at scale \(m+1\) and then restricting the normalized word. There is, however, a unique restriction operator \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) compatible with folding, and the discrepancy between \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) and naive truncation is the central object of the paper. Following the terminology used in earlier drafts, we call this discrepancy the \emph{gauge anomaly}; in the body of the paper we usually use the less evocative phrase \emph{restriction discrepancy}.

\subsection{Main results}

The first part of the paper establishes the finite-window framework. Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} introduces the fold \(\Fold_m\), while Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} records a rewriting realization of Zeckendorf normalization and the compatible restriction map \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\). In particular, Proposition~\ref{prop:idempotent} shows that \(\Fold_m\) is well defined, idempotent, and surjective onto \(X_m\), and Lemma~\ref{lem:strict-commute} shows that \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) is the unique map into \(X_m\) for which restriction and folding commute exactly.

The first genuinely finite-window result is that the discrepancy can be extensive. Proposition~\ref{prop:worst} gives, for every \(m\), an explicit word \(\omega^\star\in\Om_{m+1}\) with \(G_m(\omega^\star)=m\), so the two restrictions may disagree at every visible coordinate. Proposition~\ref{prop:tail-patch} shows that this is not a boundary artifact: no correction rule that modifies only a uniformly bounded tail of the naive restriction can restore commutation.

The second theme is probabilistic. Under the uniform measure on microstates and after passage to the bulk limit, one obtains a stationary pair process \((X_t,Y_t)\) whose first coordinate is the raw digit and whose second coordinate is the normalized digit. Theorem~\ref{thm:pair-language} identifies the interior language of this process with the label language of an explicit four-state synchronized right-resolving graph. From this presentation we derive the exact one-site law in Theorem~\ref{thm:joint}, the law of large numbers and central limit theorem for the discrepancy process in Corollary~\ref{thm:llnclt}, and the closed covariance and power-spectrum formulas in Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum}.

The third theme concerns the sequence-level folding code. For each window size \(m\), the fold defines a sliding block code \(\Phi_m\) on the full two-sided shift. Proposition~\ref{prop:sofic} shows that the image is always sofic. The more delicate question is whether the code is invertible. Theorem~\ref{thm:core-resolution} reduces every ambiguity to finitely many local cores of lengths \(3\) and \(4\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:conjugacy} shows that these ambiguities are resolved by a three-label decoder as soon as \(m\ge 3\). Proposition~\ref{thm:m2} describes the unique exceptional fiber when \(m=2\).

The final part of the paper concerns weighted fold fibers. For
\[
Z_m(y)=\sum_{\omega\in\Om_m} y^{|\Fold_m(\omega)|_1},
\]
the four-state presentation again leads to an explicit transfer-matrix description. Proposition~\ref{prop:recurrence} gives a quartic recurrence and rational generating function, Corollary~\ref{thm:hankel} identifies the exceptional parameters at which the effective order drops, Proposition~\ref{prop:discriminant} factors the discriminant completely, and Corollary~\ref{thm:weight-stats} derives the corresponding limit laws for the output Hamming weight.

\subsection{Relation to earlier work}

The numeration-theoretic background goes back to the classical papers of Lekkerkerker and Zeckendorf \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}; for broader context on Fibonacci numeration and automata-based normalization we use \cite{Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998,AlloucheShallit2003,ShallitShan2023}. The symbolic-dynamical language used later belongs to the standard theory of sofic shifts and sliding block codes \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}, and the transfer-matrix arguments are routine consequences of Perron--Frobenius and thermodynamic-formalism methods \cite{Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}.

What is closer in spirit to the present work is the literature on the normalized Fibonacci digit process, or equivalently on the golden-ratio Erd\H{o}s measure. Alexander and Zagier \cite{AlexanderZagier1991}, Sidorov and Vershik \cite{SidorovVershik1998}, Bezhaeva and Oseledets \cite{BezhaevaOseledets2007,BezhaevaOseledets2010,BezhaevaOseledets2Step}, and Kempton \cite{KemptonDigits2014} study the hidden-Markov structure of the normalized digits themselves and, in particular, the classical density \(5/18\) for the digit \(1\). We use that literature as background, but our object is different: the paper is organized around the finite-window comparison between a raw microstate and its normalized restriction. The pair observable \((X_t,Y_t)\), the discrepancy process \(X_t\oplus Y_t\), and the associated multiscale restriction problem do not seem to have been isolated in this form.

Two further remarks may help situate the results. First, the fold \(\Fold_m\) should be viewed as a finite-window version of a familiar normalization procedure, whereas the compatible restriction \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) is forced by the demand that folding commute with change of scale. Second, once the four-state presentation is identified, the probabilistic consequences are standard; the substance lies in the exact identification of the pair process and in the fact that the same presentation also governs the weighted fiber counts.

\subsection{Organization of the paper}

Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation for finite windows, Fibonacci evaluation, and Zeckendorf normalization. Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} introduces folding and compatible restriction. Section~\ref{sec:gauge} studies the restriction discrepancy, beginning with worst-case constructions and ending with the bulk limit laws. Section~\ref{sec:sequence} turns to the induced sliding block code and proves the sharp conjugacy threshold. Section~\ref{sec:partition} treats the weighted partition function and its transfer-matrix consequences. The appendices collect the normalization argument, the detailed construction of the four-state model, the core tables for the decoder, and the algebra behind the recurrence and discriminant formulas.
