\section{Introduction}

Finite-window observations of binary words depend on the readout convention used to pass from microscopic configurations to stable observables. When that readout is not \emph{multiscale consistent}, structures visible at one resolution need not agree with those seen after refinement. Such failures appear combinatorially, as an incompatibility of normal forms under restriction, and statistically, as non-vanishing anomaly densities.

This paper studies a readout scheme obtained by combining Fibonacci-weighted evaluation of a microstate word with Zeckendorf normalization, followed by truncation back to the window. We call the resulting projection the \emph{Fibonacci fold}. On the stable side it lands in the golden-mean language, the basic symbolic model for Fibonacci numeration and Zeckendorf representations \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972,Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,AlloucheShallit2003,ShallitShan2023}. On the microstate side we show that the requirement of commutation with stable restriction uniquely determines a \emph{fold-aware restriction} operator; comparing this with naive truncation yields a gauge anomaly. The term is introduced here: it is not borrowed from the existing numeration or symbolic-dynamics literature.
Throughout the paper we use the phrase solely as shorthand for the restriction discrepancy created by the non-commutation of naive truncation with Zeckendorf normalization---a symbolic-dynamics obstruction rather than a new physical effect.

Our viewpoint places a finite-window numeration problem inside the standard symbolic-dynamics and transfer-matrix framework of sofic shifts, finite-state codes, and thermodynamic formalism \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,ParryPollicott1990,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}. What appears to be a local truncation issue is therefore encoded by a finite sofic model, and the exact constants in the anomaly law become accessible through Perron--Frobenius and Markov-chain methods \cite{Billingsley1961,Gordin1969}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Our main contributions can be summarized as follows.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item We define a family of window-level folding maps \(\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m\), prove idempotence and surjectivity, and give a terminating and confluent rewriting realization.
\item We build a commuting multiscale diagram by introducing the fold-aware restriction \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\), characterized by \(\Fold_m\circ \aware_{m+1\to m}=\proj_{m+1\to m}\circ \Fold_{m+1}\), and show that it yields strict consistency between ``restrict-then-fold'' and ``fold-then-restrict''.
\item We define the gauge anomaly \(G_m\) as the Hamming distance between naive truncation and fold-aware restriction. We exhibit extensive worst-case deviations and prove that no bounded tail patch can uniformly correct them.
\item Under the uniform microstate baseline, we derive exact bulk statistics: a joint law for a naive bit and its fold-aware counterpart, a law of large numbers for anomaly density with limit \(4/9\), a central limit theorem with variance constant \(118/243\), and closed-form autocovariances and a rational power spectrum. The normalized digit density \(5/18\) itself is classical in the two-sided normalization literature \cite{SidorovVershik1998,Kempton2014,AlexanderZagier1991}, but the joint law with its fold/discrepancy interpretation and the subsequent anomaly observables appear to be new.
\item We lift folding to a sliding block code \(\Phi_m\) on the two-sided full shift, show that its image is sofic with an explicit labeled graph model, and prove a conjugacy threshold at \(m\ge 3\).
\item We study a weighted partition function over fold fibers, obtain a fourth-order minimal recurrence, and factor the discriminant completely to identify the algebraic locus of spectral degeneracies of the transfer matrix.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Related work and novelty positioning.}
Classical Zeckendorf theory goes back to Lekkerkerker and Zeckendorf \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}; broader numeration-theoretic context may be found in Brown's early note, Fraenkel's survey, and in automata-oriented treatments by Frougny, Allouche, Shallit, and Berstel \cite{Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998,AlloucheShallit2003,ShallitShan2023,Berstel1985}. The conversion of arbitrary Fibonacci representations to Zeckendorf normal form by finite-state transducers is part of the broader literature on confluent and Pisot numeration systems; see in particular \cite{FrougnyConfluent1992,BerendFrougny1994,Frougny1999,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998}. For Fibonacci-specific greedy/minimal-expansion and transducer viewpoints, see \cite{Heuberger2004,Hollander1998,Shallit1994}. On Zeckendorf arithmetic, see Ahlbach et al.\ \cite{AhlbachUsatine2013}, Fenwick \cite{Fenwick2003}, Freitag and Phillips \cite{FreitagPhillips1998}, and Idziaszek \cite{Idziaszek2021}. On base-\(\varphi\) representations, which are closely related to the Zeckendorf encoding used here, see Bergman \cite{Bergman1957} and Dekking \cite{Dekking2020BasePhi,Dekking2021AddBasePhi}. On the dynamical side, our sofic and conjugacy arguments use standard notions from symbolic dynamics and finite-state coding \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,Blanchard1989}. The transfer-matrix and pressure arguments in Sections~\ref{sec:gauge} and \ref{sec:partition} are modeled on the usual thermodynamic-formalism framework, including the intrinsic Markov chain and Parry measure \cite{Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}, and the central-limit inputs are standard Markov-chain results \cite{Billingsley1961,Gordin1969}.

There is also a substantial probabilistic literature on Zeckendorf decompositions and digit statistics. Lekkerkerker proved that the expected number of summands in the Zeckendorf representation of a random integer in \([1,\Fib_n]\) grows as \(n/(\varphi^2+1)\); subsequent work by Kologlu, Kopp, Miller, and Wang \cite{KologluKoppMillerWang2011} and Miller and Wang \cite{MillerWang2012} established Gaussian fluctuations for summand counts; Bower, Insoft, Li, Miller, and Tosteson \cite{BowerInsoftLiMillerTosteson2015} extended these to gap distributions. For digit-location and block-structure questions, see \cite{GrabnerTichy1990,GrabnerTichyNemesPetho1996,Griffiths2010,Dekking2021Structure,Dekking2021SumDigits,Chang2025}. The measure studied in those works averages uniformly over integers in an interval, whereas our fold-pushforward measure \(w_m\) averages uniformly over all \(2^m\) microstate words. The two measures are distinct, and the constants we obtain (e.g., output-weight density \(5/18\) and variance \(67/972\)) are not expected to match the interval-based Zeckendorf constants from the older literature; we discuss this explicitly in Section~\ref{sec:partition}.

\paragraph{Relation to Erd\H{o}s measure and goldenshift literature.}
The two-sided normalization viewpoint we exploit is squarely in the tradition of the Erd\H{o}s measure and goldenshift analyses of Alexander--Zagier \cite{AlexanderZagier1991}, Sidorov--Vershik \cite{SidorovVershik1998}, and the numeration/automata works of Grabner, Kirschenhofer, and Tichy \cite{GrabnerKirschenhoferTichy2002} and Dumont, Sidorov, and Thomas \cite{DumontSidorovThomas1999}. Kempton's thesis \cite{Kempton2014} further isolates digit densities, including the normalized digit-1 frequency \(5/18\), for Bernoulli convolutions mapped under the standard normalization transducer. Our analysis differs in that we keep the finite window, insist on fold-aware restriction, and extract explicit finite-state anomaly processes, but the ambient normalization machinery, the two-sided goldenshift, and the base statistics we recover are part of that prior literature.

To separate what is standard from what is new, we offer the following comparison.

\emph{Standard background.} The Zeckendorf representation, its uniqueness, and its connection to Fibonacci numbers are classical \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}. Finite-state normalization in confluent linear numeration systems, including the Fibonacci/Pisot setting, is part of the standard literature \cite{Frougny1988,FrougnyConfluent1992,BerendFrougny1994,Frougny1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998}. Sofic shifts, right-resolving presentations, and sliding block codes are standard in symbolic dynamics \cite{LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,Blanchard1989}. The Parry measure on a primitive right-resolving presentation, and the resulting Perron--Frobenius machinery for computing stationary distributions and cumulant generating functions, are well established \cite{Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982}. The Markov-chain central limit theorem in Gordin's martingale-coboundary form is standard \cite{Gordin1969}. The G\"{a}rtner--Ellis theorem for large deviations of transfer-matrix observables follows from standard references \cite{Ruelle2004}. The LLN/CLT/LDP conclusions themselves are routine consequences of these tools once the relevant finite-state model has been established.

\emph{What this paper adds.} The numeration-theoretic folding mechanism studied here --- a window-level projection that evaluates Fibonacci weights and Zeckendorf-normalizes --- has not, to our knowledge, been analyzed as a sofic sliding block code or equipped with a multiscale commuting diagram. The specific objects that are new include: the fold-aware restriction operator and its characterization by the exact commuting square \eqref{eq:commuting}; the gauge anomaly \(G_m\) and the proofs of its extensiveness and non-patchability; the four-state sofic model for the bulk discrepancy process; the exact anomaly density \(4/9\), the CLT variance \(118/243\), and the closed-form autocovariance formula and rational spectrum of Theorems~\ref{thm:joint}--\ref{thm:spectrum}; the conjugacy threshold at \(m\ge 3\) established by an explicit inverse sliding block decoder; and the explicit discriminant factorization for the weighted partition function. The model construction, the explicit sofic presentation, and the specific constants are the primary contributions; the general machinery of Perron--Frobenius, Parry measures, transfer-matrix asymptotics, and symbolic-dynamics coding theory is invoked from the standard literature cited above.

\emph{What this paper does not claim.} The general machinery of Perron--Frobenius, Parry measures, transfer-matrix asymptotics, and finite-state coding belongs to the standard literature cited above. The novelty is not in those general tools, but in verifying that this particular Fibonacci fold admits an explicit local inverse for \(m\ge3\), together with the model-specific anomaly constants and spectral formulas.

\paragraph{Theorem-by-theorem novelty map.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item \textbf{Theorem~4.5 (bulk joint law).} The two-sided normalization transducer, the induced goldenshift, and the output-weight density \(5/18\) are known in the Bernoulli-convolution literature \cite{AlexanderZagier1991,SidorovVershik1998,Kempton2014}. Our addition is the exact joint law linking the naive and fold-aware digits together with the anomaly interpretation and the subsequent closed-form covariance/spectral data.
\item \textbf{Theorem~4.9 (anomaly autocovariance and spectrum).} The four-state discrepancy process, its explicit autocovariance, and the rational power spectrum are model-specific to the fold-aware vs.\ naive restriction comparison and are, to our knowledge, new.
\item \textbf{Theorem~5.2 (conjugacy threshold).} The sliding block code is standard. The new contribution is the explicit bounded-memory inverse for \(m\ge3\), which we now justify via a fiber-product argument (Appendix~\ref{app:sofic}).
\item \textbf{Theorem~6.1 (partition-function recurrence).} Transfer-matrix recurrences for fold fibers are routine once the automaton is fixed. The states and weights here come from the explicit four-state synchronized model, so the fourth-order recurrence and discriminant factorization follow from standard Perron--Frobenius theory but are recorded explicitly for this model. Related weighted-counting results for linear recurrences appear in \cite{GrabnerKirschenhoferTichy2002,DumontSidorovThomas1999}.
\item \textbf{Theorem~6.3 (discriminant factorization).} The factorization is an explicit computation for the fourth-order polynomial and is not claimed as conceptually new; it is included to highlight the exact spectral degeneracies of the transfer matrix.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Organization.}
Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} introduces the window model, Fibonacci evaluation, and Zeckendorf normalization. Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} develops the folding projection, multiscale restriction, the fold-aware restriction operator, and the convention-translation lemma. Section~\ref{sec:gauge} defines the gauge anomaly and proves its worst-case behavior and limiting laws, including closed-form second-order statistics. Section~\ref{sec:sequence} upgrades folding to a sliding block code and establishes soficity and the conjugacy threshold. Section~\ref{sec:partition} studies weighted partition functions, the discriminant factorization, and output-weight statistics. Proof details are collected in the appendices.
