\section{Introduction}

Zeckendorf normalization replaces a Fibonacci expansion by the unique expansion with no adjacent \(1\)s. Most of the literature studies either the normalized expansion of an integer or normalization on infinite digit strings. Here we keep the observation window finite. Given a binary word \(\omega\in\Om_m\), we evaluate its Fibonacci sum, replace that sum by its Zeckendorf digits, and retain only the original \(m\) low-weight coordinates. This defines a map
\[
\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m,
\]
where \(X_m\) is the length-\(m\) language of the golden-mean shift.

The finite-window problem has an additional feature that does not appear in the one-shot normalization of a single integer. If one passes from length \(m+1\) to length \(m\) by naive truncation before folding, the result need not coincide with truncation after folding. Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} shows that there is nevertheless a unique restriction map \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) for which folding and restriction commute. The discrepancy between \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) and naive truncation is the basic observable of the paper.

\subsection{Main results}

Our first observation is that the discrepancy is genuinely multiscale. Proposition~\ref{prop:worst} constructs, for every \(m\), a word \(\omega^\star\in\Om_{m+1}\) for which the naive and compatible restrictions disagree at all \(m\) visible sites. Proposition~\ref{prop:tail-patch} shows that this phenomenon cannot be repaired by any rule that modifies only a uniformly bounded tail of the naive restriction.

The structural input is Theorem~\ref{thm:pair-language}. In the bulk limit of uniformly chosen microstates one obtains a stationary pair process \((X_t,Y_t)\), with \(X_t\) the raw digit and \(Y_t\) the normalized digit at the same site. We show that the interior language of this process is presented by an explicit four-state synchronized right-resolving graph. Once this presentation is identified, the discrepancy laws become standard finite-state consequences. We record them because they resolve the compatible-restriction defect completely for the present model: Theorem~\ref{thm:joint}, Corollary~\ref{thm:llnclt}, and Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum} give the exact one-site law and the complete two-point discrepancy statistics, including density \(4/9\), asymptotic variance \(118/243\), closed autocovariance formulas, and a rational power spectral density.

The same graph also governs the sequence-level folding code. For each window size \(m\), the map \(\Fold_m\) induces a sliding block code \(\Phi_m\) on the full two-sided shift. Proposition~\ref{prop:sofic} identifies its image as a sofic shift. The substantive issue is invertibility, not the existence of another graph presentation. Theorem~\ref{thm:core-resolution} shows that every ambiguity reduces to finitely many local cores of lengths \(3\) and \(4\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:conjugacy} proves that a three-label decoder resolves all of them as soon as \(m\ge 3\). Proposition~\ref{thm:m2} describes the unique exceptional fiber when \(m=2\).

The final part of the paper concerns weighted fibers. The same four-state presentation yields a transfer-matrix description of
\[
Z_m(y)=\sum_{\omega\in\Om_m} y^{|\Fold_m(\omega)|_1}.
\]
The explicit recurrence formulas are auxiliary consequences of that transfer matrix. The conceptual statement is Theorem~\ref{thm:microcanonical-output}, which identifies the free energy with the Legendre transform of a strictly concave entropy profile on the pair shift, and Corollary~\ref{thm:weight-stats}, which gives the resulting limit laws for the output Hamming weight.

\subsection{Relation to earlier work}

The numeration-theoretic background goes back to the classical work of Lekkerkerker and Zeckendorf \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}; for Fibonacci numeration, normalization by automata, and related weighted numeration systems we use \cite{Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998,AlloucheShallit2003,GrabnerKirschenhoferTichy2002,DumontSidorovThomas1999,ShallitShan2023}. On the symbolic side we use standard facts about sofic shifts and sliding block codes \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}, together with standard transfer-matrix and thermodynamic-formalism arguments \cite{Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}.

Closest to our setting are works on the normalized Fibonacci digit process, or equivalently on the golden-ratio Erd\H{o}s measure. Alexander and Zagier \cite{AlexanderZagier1991}, Sidorov and Vershik \cite{SidorovVershik1998}, Bezhaeva and Oseledets \cite{BezhaevaOseledets2007,BezhaevaOseledets2010,BezhaevaOseledets2Step}, and Kempton \cite{KemptonDigits2014} study the normalized digits themselves and, in particular, recover the classical density \(5/18\) for a normalized digit to equal \(1\). Our viewpoint is different: raw and normalized digits are kept in the same finite window, and the basic question is how restriction interacts with normalization across scales. To the best of our knowledge, the pair observable \((X_t,Y_t)\) and the associated compatible-restriction problem have not been isolated in this form in the Zeckendorf literature.

The existence of finite automata for normalization in Fibonacci or Pisot numeration is classical \cite{Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998}. What is specific here is not the mere appearance of a finite automaton, but the finite-window compatibility problem: raw and normalized digits are observed on the same window, and the obstruction to passing between window sizes is encoded by a pair shift rather than by the normalized digits alone.

The paper therefore does not claim new general theorems about sofic shifts, sliding block codes, or thermodynamic formalism. Its contribution is to isolate a canonical finite-window normalization problem and to show that, after identifying the relevant four-state pair shift, three questions that initially look unrelated are governed by the same explicit object: the bulk statistics of the restriction discrepancy, the sharp invertibility threshold for the induced sliding block code, and the thermodynamics of weighted fibers. The later exact formulas are included because they complete the analysis of this specific model.

\subsection{Organization}

Sections~\ref{sec:preliminaries} and \ref{sec:multiscale} set up the finite-window fold and the compatible restriction. Section~\ref{sec:gauge} studies the restriction discrepancy and the bulk pair shift. Section~\ref{sec:sequence} treats the induced sliding block code and proves the conjugacy threshold. Section~\ref{sec:partition} develops the weighted theory. The appendices contain the rewriting argument, the construction of the four-state presentation, the fiber-product tables used for local decoding, and the linear-algebraic calculations behind the recurrence and discriminant formulas.
