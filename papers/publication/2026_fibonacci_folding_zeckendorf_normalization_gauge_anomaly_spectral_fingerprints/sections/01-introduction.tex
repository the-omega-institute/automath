\section{Introduction}

Zeckendorf's theorem furnishes a canonical representative among Fibonacci expansions: every nonnegative integer admits a unique expression
\[
\sum_{k\ge 1} z_k\Fib_{k+1},
\qquad z_k\in\{0,1\},\qquad z_kz_{k+1}=0,
\]
with no adjacent \(1\)s. Classical work concerns either the normalized expansion of a single integer or normalization on one-sided or two-sided digit sequences. In this paper we isolate a finite-window version of the problem. Given a binary word \(\omega\in\Om_m\), we evaluate its Fibonacci value, replace it by its Zeckendorf expansion, and retain only the original \(m\) low-weight coordinates. This defines a map
\[
\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m,
\]
where \(X_m\) is the length-\(m\) language of the golden-mean shift.

The point of the finite-window problem is that normalization does not commute with the obvious restriction when the window size changes. If one first truncates a word of length \(m+1\) and then folds, the result need not agree with the truncation of its \((m+1)\)-window fold. We show that there is nevertheless a canonical restriction on microstates, namely the unique map \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) for which folding and restriction commute. The discrepancy between \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) and naive truncation is the basic observable studied here.

At a structural level, the paper shows that this discrepancy is governed by a small sofic system. When raw and normalized digits are observed simultaneously in the bulk, one obtains a stationary pair process \((X_t,Y_t)\). The explicit four-state graph is not treated as an isolated automaton computation: a general structural theorem identifies the mechanism by which any zero-synchronized local normalizer of rewrite span \(r\) produces a synchronized pair shift with follower memory at most \(r-1\), and the Fibonacci rule \(011\to100\) appears as the span-\(3\) instance. A second structural theorem reduces the local invertibility problem for window codes to finitely many ambiguous cores of length at most \(r+1\). Once these two statements are in place, the probabilistic, symbolic, and thermodynamic aspects of the finite-window model become consequences of the same object.

\subsection{Main results}

The basic finite-window properties are established in Sections~\ref{sec:preliminaries} and \ref{sec:multiscale}. In particular, \(\Fold_m\) is an idempotent projection onto \(X_m\), and the compatible restriction \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\) is characterized by the commutative relation
\[
\Fold_m\circ \aware_{m+1\to m}
\;=\;
\proj_{m+1\to m}\circ \Fold_{m+1}.
\]
The first substantive point is that the resulting discrepancy is genuinely multiscale. Proposition~\ref{prop:worst} constructs, for every \(m\), a word \(\omega^\star\in\Om_{m+1}\) for which naive truncation and compatible restriction disagree at every visible coordinate. Proposition~\ref{prop:tail-patch} shows that no rule depending only on a uniformly bounded tail can repair this failure of commutation.

The structural input begins with Theorem~\ref{thm:span-r-pair}, which shows that every zero-synchronized span-\(r\) local normalizer has a synchronized pair presentation whose follower memory is bounded by \(r-1\). Corollary~\ref{cor:fib-pair-criterion} identifies the Fibonacci specialization: the interior pair language of the bulk process \((X_t,Y_t)\) is presented by an explicit four-state synchronized right-resolving graph. Once this graph has been identified, the discrepancy laws are obtained from standard finite-state calculations. Theorem~\ref{thm:joint}, Corollary~\ref{thm:llnclt}, and Theorem~\ref{thm:spectrum} give the exact one-site law and the complete two-point statistics of the discrepancy process, including density \(4/9\), asymptotic variance \(118/243\), closed autocovariance formulas, and a rational power spectrum.

A second part of the paper concerns the sequence-level map induced by the window fold. For each \(m\), the map \(\Fold_m\) defines a sliding block code \(\Phi_m\) on the full two-sided shift. Proposition~\ref{prop:sofic} identifies its image as a sofic shift. The issue is then injectivity. Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-core-criterion} proves, in the general span-\(r\) setting, that every two-label ambiguity is supported on a core of length at most \(r+1\). The Fibonacci specialization \(r=3\) yields Theorem~\ref{thm:core-resolution}, which reduces all ambiguities to the explicit core tables of lengths \(3\) and \(4\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:conjugacy}, which proves that a three-label decoder resolves all such ambiguities as soon as \(m\ge 3\). Proposition~\ref{thm:m2} describes the unique exceptional fiber in the case \(m=2\).

The final part of the paper concerns weighted fibers. The same four-state presentation yields a transfer-matrix description of
\[
Z_m(y)=\sum_{\omega\in\Om_m} y^{|\Fold_m(\omega)|_1}.
\]
The recurrence and generating-function formulas are convenient consequences of the transfer matrix, but the conceptual statement is Theorem~\ref{thm:microcanonical-output}, which identifies the free energy with the Legendre transform of a strictly concave entropy profile on the pair shift. Corollary~\ref{thm:weight-stats} then gives the corresponding limit laws for the output Hamming weight.

\subsection{Relation to earlier work}

The numeration-theoretic background goes back to the classical work of Lekkerkerker and Zeckendorf \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}. For Fibonacci numeration, normalization by automata, and related weighted numeration systems we use \cite{Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998,AlloucheShallit2003,GrabnerKirschenhoferTichy2002,DumontSidorovThomas1999,ShallitShan2023}. On the symbolic side we appeal only to standard material on sofic shifts, sliding block codes, transfer matrices, and thermodynamic formalism \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}.

Closest in spirit are works on the normalized Fibonacci digit process, or equivalently on the golden-ratio Erd\H{o}s measure. Alexander and Zagier \cite{AlexanderZagier1991}, Sidorov and Vershik \cite{SidorovVershik1998}, Bezhaeva and Oseledets \cite{BezhaevaOseledets2007,BezhaevaOseledets2010,BezhaevaOseledets2Step}, and Kempton \cite{KemptonDigits2014} study the normalized digits themselves and recover, in particular, the classical density \(5/18\) for a normalized digit to equal \(1\). Our viewpoint is different. We keep raw and normalized digits in the same finite window, and the central question is how normalization interacts with restriction across scales. To the best of our knowledge, the pair observable \((X_t,Y_t)\) and the corresponding compatible-restriction problem have not been isolated in this form in the Zeckendorf literature.

The existence of finite automata for normalization in Fibonacci or Pisot numeration is classical \cite{Frougny1988,Frougny1992,FrougnyConfluent1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998}. What is specific here is not the appearance of a finite automaton as such, but the finite-window compatibility problem: raw and normalized digits are observed on the same window, and the obstruction to changing the window size is encoded by a pair shift rather than by the normalized digits alone. The new structural statements isolate the local mechanism behind this phenomenon. The pair graph is derived from the rewrite span of the normalization rule, rather than introduced as a stand-alone finite table, and the conjugacy threshold is reduced to a finite-core criterion that applies to every zero-synchronized span-\(r\) local normalizer.

We do not attempt to formulate a general theory of numeration automata or sofic models. The point is rather that, in this finite-window setting, three questions that at first sight are unrelated are governed by the same explicit pair shift: the bulk statistics of the restriction discrepancy, the invertibility threshold for the induced sliding block code, and the thermodynamics of weighted fibers.

\subsection{Organization}

Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} introduces the finite-window fold, and Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} identifies the compatible restriction across scales. Section~\ref{sec:gauge} proves the structural pair theorem and derives the four-state Fibonacci specialization together with the discrepancy statistics. Section~\ref{sec:sequence} develops the finite-core criterion and proves the conjugacy threshold. Section~\ref{sec:partition} treats weighted fibers and the associated variational formalism. The appendices contain the rewriting argument, the synchronized follower computation for the four-state presentation, the explicit core tables used in the decoder theorem, and the linear-algebraic calculations behind the recurrence formulas.
