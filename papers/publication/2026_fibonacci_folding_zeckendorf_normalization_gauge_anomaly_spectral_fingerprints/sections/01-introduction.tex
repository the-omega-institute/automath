\section{Introduction}

Finite-window observations of binary words depend on the readout convention used to pass from microscopic configurations to stable observables. When that readout is not \emph{multiscale consistent}, structures visible at one resolution need not agree with those seen after refinement. Such failures appear combinatorially, as an incompatibility of normal forms under restriction, and statistically, as non-vanishing anomaly densities.

This paper studies a readout scheme obtained by combining Fibonacci-weighted evaluation of a microstate word with Zeckendorf normalization, followed by truncation back to the window. We call the resulting projection the \emph{Fibonacci fold}. On the stable side it lands in the golden-mean language, the basic symbolic model for Fibonacci numeration and Zeckendorf representations \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972,Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,AlloucheShallit2003,ShallitShan2023}. On the microstate side we show that the requirement of commutation with stable restriction uniquely determines a \emph{fold-aware restriction} operator; comparing this with naive truncation yields a gauge anomaly. The term is introduced here: it is not borrowed from the existing numeration or symbolic-dynamics literature.

Our viewpoint places a finite-window numeration problem inside the standard symbolic-dynamics and transfer-matrix framework of sofic shifts, finite-state codes, and thermodynamic formalism \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,ParryPollicott1990,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}. What appears to be a local truncation issue is therefore encoded by a finite sofic model, and the exact constants in the anomaly law become accessible through Perron--Frobenius and Markov-chain methods \cite{Billingsley1961,Gordin1969}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Our main contributions can be summarized as follows.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2.2em]
\item We define a family of window-level folding maps \(\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m\), prove idempotence and surjectivity, and give a terminating and confluent rewriting realization.
\item We build a commuting multiscale diagram by introducing the fold-aware restriction \(\aware_{m+1\to m}\), characterized by \(\Fold_m\circ \aware_{m+1\to m}=\proj_{m+1\to m}\circ \Fold_{m+1}\), and show that it yields strict consistency between ``restrict-then-fold'' and ``fold-then-restrict''.
\item We define the gauge anomaly \(G_m\) as the Hamming distance between naive truncation and fold-aware restriction. We exhibit extensive worst-case deviations and prove that no bounded tail patch can uniformly correct them.
\item Under the uniform microstate baseline, we derive exact bulk statistics: a joint law for a naive bit and its fold-aware counterpart, a law of large numbers for anomaly density with limit \(4/9\), a central limit theorem with variance constant \(118/243\), and closed-form autocovariances and a rational power spectrum. These specific constants are, to our knowledge, new.
\item We lift folding to a sliding block code \(\Phi_m\) on the two-sided full shift, show that its image is sofic with an explicit labeled graph model, and prove a conjugacy threshold at \(m\ge 3\).
\item We study a weighted partition function over fold fibers, obtain a fourth-order minimal recurrence, and factor the discriminant completely to identify the algebraic locus of spectral degeneracies of the transfer matrix.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Related work and novelty positioning.}
Classical Zeckendorf theory goes back to Lekkerkerker and Zeckendorf \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}; broader numeration-theoretic context may be found in Brown's early note, Fraenkel's survey, and in automata-oriented treatments by Frougny, Allouche, Shallit, and Berstel \cite{Brown1964,Fraenkel1985,Frougny1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998,AlloucheShallit2003,ShallitShan2023,Berstel1985}. The conversion of arbitrary Fibonacci representations to Zeckendorf normal form by finite-state transducers is developed in \cite{Frougny1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998}; see also Hollander \cite{Hollander1998} and Shallit \cite{Shallit1994} on regular sets and linear recurrence numeration systems. On Zeckendorf arithmetic, see Ahlbach et al.\ \cite{AhlbachUsatine2013} and Pippenger \cite{Pippenger2015}. On the dynamical side, our sofic and conjugacy arguments use standard notions from symbolic dynamics and finite-state coding \cite{Weiss1973,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}. The transfer-matrix and pressure arguments in Sections~\ref{sec:gauge} and \ref{sec:partition} are modeled on the usual thermodynamic-formalism framework, including the intrinsic Markov chain and Parry measure \cite{Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982,Ruelle2004,Bowen2008}, and the central-limit inputs are standard Markov-chain results \cite{Billingsley1961,Gordin1969}.

There is also a substantial probabilistic literature on Zeckendorf decompositions. Lekkerkerker proved that the expected number of summands in the Zeckendorf representation of a random integer in \([1,\Fib_n]\) grows as \(n/(\varphi^2+1)\); subsequent work by Kologlu, Kopp, Miller, and Wang \cite{KologluKoppMillerWang2011} and Miller and Wang \cite{MillerWang2012} established Gaussian fluctuations for summand counts; Bower, Insoft, Li, Miller, and Tosteson \cite{BowerInsoftLiMillerTosteson2015} extended these to gap distributions. The measure studied in those works averages uniformly over integers in an interval, whereas our fold-pushforward measure \(w_m\) averages uniformly over all \(2^m\) microstate words. The two measures are distinct, and the constants we obtain (e.g., output-weight density \(5/18\) and variance \(67/972\)) are not expected to match the interval-based Zeckendorf constants from the older literature; we discuss this explicitly in Section~\ref{sec:partition}.

To separate what is standard from what is new, we offer the following comparison.

\emph{Standard background.} The Zeckendorf representation, its uniqueness, and its connection to Fibonacci numbers are classical \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}. Finite-state automata for normalization in numeration systems, including Fibonacci normalization and addition, are developed in Frougny's work \cite{Frougny1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1998} and synthesized in Allouche--Shallit \cite{AlloucheShallit2003}. Sofic shifts, right-resolving presentations, and sliding block codes are standard in symbolic dynamics \cite{LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}. The Parry measure on a primitive right-resolving presentation, and the resulting Perron--Frobenius machinery for computing stationary distributions and cumulant generating functions, are well established \cite{Parry1964,ParryPollicott1990,Walters1982}. The Markov-chain central limit theorem in Gordin's martingale-coboundary form is standard \cite{Gordin1969}. The G\"{a}rtner--Ellis theorem for large deviations of transfer-matrix observables follows from standard references \cite{Ruelle2004}. The LLN/CLT/LDP conclusions themselves are routine consequences of these standard tools once the finite-state model is in hand.

\emph{What this paper adds.} The numeration-theoretic folding mechanism studied here --- a window-level projection that evaluates Fibonacci weights and Zeckendorf-normalizes --- has not, to our knowledge, been analyzed as a sofic sliding block code or equipped with a multiscale commuting diagram. The specific objects that are new include: the fold-aware restriction operator and its characterization by the exact commuting square \eqref{eq:commuting}; the gauge anomaly \(G_m\) and the proofs of its extensiveness and non-patchability; the four-state sofic model for the bulk discrepancy process; the exact anomaly density \(4/9\), the CLT variance \(118/243\), and the closed-form autocovariance formula and rational spectrum of Theorems~\ref{thm:joint}--\ref{thm:spectrum}; the conjugacy threshold at \(m\ge 3\) established through the no-off-diagonal-cycle argument for the fiber product; and the explicit discriminant factorization for the weighted partition function. The model construction, the explicit sofic presentation, and the specific constants are the primary contributions; the general machinery of Perron--Frobenius, Parry measures, transfer-matrix asymptotics, and fiber-product injectivity criteria is invoked from the standard literature cited above.

\emph{What this paper does not claim.} The general machinery of Perron--Frobenius, Parry measures, and transfer-matrix asymptotics is invoked as a black box and belongs to the standard literature cited above. The sliding block code conjugacy theorem is proved using the standard fiber-product criterion from \cite{LindMarcus1995}; the novelty is in verifying that the specific Fibonacci fold presentation satisfies the no-off-diagonal-cycle condition, not in the general criterion itself.

\paragraph{Organization.}
Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} introduces the window model, Fibonacci evaluation, and Zeckendorf normalization. Section~\ref{sec:multiscale} develops the folding projection, multiscale restriction, the fold-aware restriction operator, and the convention-translation lemma. Section~\ref{sec:gauge} defines the gauge anomaly and proves its worst-case behavior and limiting laws, including closed-form second-order statistics. Section~\ref{sec:sequence} upgrades folding to a sliding block code and establishes soficity and the conjugacy threshold. Section~\ref{sec:partition} studies weighted partition functions, the discriminant factorization, and output-weight statistics. Proof details are collected in the appendices.
