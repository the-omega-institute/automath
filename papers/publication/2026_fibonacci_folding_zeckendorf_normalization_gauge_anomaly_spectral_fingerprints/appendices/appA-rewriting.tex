\section{Termination and confluence of the normalization procedure}
\label{app:rewriting}

For completeness we record the short rewriting argument used implicitly in the main text. This appendix works in most-significant-digit-first notation. Starting from a binary Fibonacci digit string, prefix a leading zero and repeatedly apply the local rule
\[
011\to 100
\]
while scanning from left to right. In the LH convention of the main text, this is the same as the rule \(110\to 001\) acting from high weights to low weights.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:termination}
Every rewrite sequence terminates after finitely many steps.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each application of \(011\to 100\) lowers the number of \(1\)s by one. Hence no infinite rewrite sequence exists.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:confluence}
The rewriting system is confluent: every input word rewrites to a unique normal form. This normal form contains no block \(11\) and coincides with the Zeckendorf representation of the same Fibonacci value.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each rewrite preserves Fibonacci value. Indeed, if the block \(011\) occupies positions \(k,k+1,k+2\) in MSD-first order, then
\[
\Fib_{k+2}+\Fib_{k+1}=\Fib_{k+3},
\]
so replacing \(011\) by \(100\) does not change the represented integer.

Since termination is already known, it is enough to prove local confluence. The rule \(011\to 100\) has no nontrivial critical overlap: two occurrences of \(011\) are either disjoint or identical. Distinct rewrites therefore commute, and identical rewrites clearly lead to the same result. By Newman's lemma for terminating rewrite systems \cite[Section~2.4]{BaaderNipkow1998}, the system is globally confluent.

Let \(w^\sharp\) be the unique irreducible word obtained from the input. The prefixed word \(0w^\sharp\) contains no occurrence of \(011\). If \(w^\sharp\) contained a block \(11\), then the symbol immediately to its left in \(0w^\sharp\) would be \(0\), and \(0w^\sharp\) would contain an occurrence of \(011\), contrary to irreducibility. Thus \(w^\sharp\) has no adjacent \(1\)s.

Finally, \(w^\sharp\) has the same Fibonacci value as the initial word and no adjacent \(1\)s. By the uniqueness statement in Zeckendorf's theorem \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}, it must be the Zeckendorf representation of that value.
\end{proof}

Together, Propositions~\ref{prop:termination} and \ref{prop:confluence} justify the use of \(\Fold_m\) in the main text: the normalization procedure terminates, produces a unique output, and fixes every already stable word.
