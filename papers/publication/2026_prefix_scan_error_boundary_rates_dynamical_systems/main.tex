\begin{abstract}
We study a basic observable question for binary readout systems: how much can
the decision error for an event be reduced by refining the available prefix
information?  Given a measurable dynamical system with a binary observable, the
first $m$ symbols define a finite prefix partition and a coarse sigma-algebra
$\mathcal{F}_m$.  For an event $\mathsf P$, we define the scan error profile
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)$ as the optimal $\mathcal{F}_m$-measurable
classification error.  We prove an exact cylinder decomposition of this
profile, identify it with the Bayes-optimal threshold rule on the conditional
probability martingale $p_m=\mu(\mathsf P\mid\mathcal F_m)$, and derive a
discrete Tanaka representation
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf P;\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]
\]
in terms of a nonnegative discrete local-time functional at the threshold
$1/2$.  We then show that convergence rates are controlled by the growth of
boundary cylinders: an upper rate follows from a cylinder-mass bound and a
boundary-dimension estimate, while a matching two-sided law follows from a
uniform thickness hypothesis on ambiguous cylinders.  A short symbolic example
for the golden-mean shift illustrates the regime $\varepsilon_m\asymp
\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m}$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The purpose of this paper is narrow.  We fix a binary readout process and ask
how refinement of prefix information improves the best possible decision error
for a target event.  The setup is standard in measurable and symbolic dynamics:
a point $x$ generates a binary sequence, the first $m$ symbols define a finite
prefix partition, and the corresponding sigma-algebra $\mathcal F_m$ contains
exactly the information observable at resolution $m$.  What we study is the
profile
\[
m\longmapsto \varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu),
\]
where $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)$ is the smallest error of any
$\mathcal F_m$-measurable decision rule for the event $\mathsf P$.

The cylinder decomposition of Bayes risk on finite partitions and the
optimality of the threshold classifier $p_m \ge 1/2$ are well-established
results in nonparametric classification
\cite{Tsybakov2009Nonparametric,DevroyeGyorfiLugosi1996}.  We recall them in
Section~3 to make the paper self-contained, but they are not claimed as new.
The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item a \emph{discrete Tanaka representation} of the scan error profile in
  terms of a nonnegative local-time functional built from the
  conditional-probability martingale $(p_m)_{m\ge 0}$
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka}), which connects error decay to the
  accumulation of threshold crossings via the classical discrete Tanaka formula
  \cite{Tanaka1963,RevuzYor1999}; and
\item \emph{boundary-cylinder rate laws}
  (Theorems~\ref{thm:boundary-dimension}
  and~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}), giving explicit upper and two-sided
  bounds on $\varepsilon_m$ in terms of the growth rate and mass of
  boundary cylinders.
\end{enumerate}

The main theorem chain reads
\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{prefix filtration}
&\longrightarrow
\text{cylinder decomposition (known)} \\
&\longrightarrow
\text{Bayes-optimal threshold rule (known)} \\
&\longrightarrow
\text{discrete Tanaka representation (new)} \\
&\longrightarrow
\text{boundary-controlled rate laws (new)}.
\end{aligned}
\]

\paragraph{Related work.}
The Bayes risk decomposition on partitions and the optimality of threshold
classifiers are classical; see Devroye, Gy\"orfi, and Lugosi
\cite{DevroyeGyorfiLugosi1996} for a systematic treatment, and Audibert and
Tsybakov \cite{AudibertTsybakov2007} for refined convergence rates of plug-in
classifiers.  The martingale convergence that underlies the asymptotic
vanishing of $\varepsilon_m$ is Doob's upward theorem
\cite{Doob1953,Williams1991,Durrett2019}.  The discrete Tanaka formula we
employ is a lattice analogue of Tanaka's formula for Brownian local time
\cite{Tanaka1963}; for the continuous-time theory, see Revuz and Yor
\cite{RevuzYor1999}.

On the symbolic dynamics side, the cylinder partition structure and sofic
constraints are treated in Lind and Marcus \cite{LindMarcus1995}.  The
combinatorics of boundary sets in symbolic systems connects to work of Fischer
\cite{Fischer1975}, Krieger \cite{Krieger1984}, and Boyle \cite{Boyle1983} on
sofic systems and their presentations.  The Shannon--McMillan--Breiman theorem
\cite{ShannonMcMillanBreiman}, which controls the exponential decay of cylinder
measures for ergodic sources, provides the measure-theoretic substrate for the
cylinder-mass hypotheses in our rate theorems; see also Walters
\cite{WaltersErgodicTheory} and Barron, Gy\"orfi, and van der Meulen
\cite{BarronGyorfivanderMeulen1992} for distribution estimation in the
information-theoretic setting.

Standard background on measurable dynamics and symbolic coding may be found in
\cite{WaltersErgodicTheory,LindMarcus1995,CoverThomas2006}.  For a classical
symbolic coding example related to binary readouts, see
\cite{MorseHedlund1940}.

\section{Prefix Partitions and Observable Information}

\subsection{Binary Readout and Prefix Sigma-Algebras}

Let $(X,\mathcal B,\mu,T)$ be a measurable dynamical system, where $\mu$ is a
$T$-invariant probability measure.  Fix a measurable set $W\subseteq X$ and
define the binary readout
\[
s_t(x):=\ind_W(T^t x)\in\{0,1\},
\qquad t\in\NN.
\]
The infinite readout map is
\[
\omega_\infty(x):=s_0(x)s_1(x)s_2(x)\cdots \in \Omega_\infty:=\{0,1\}^{\NN}.
\]

For a finite word $a=a_0a_1\cdots a_{m-1}\in\{0,1\}^m$, define the cylinder
\[
[a]:=\{\xi\in\Omega_\infty:\ \xi_0\xi_1\cdots\xi_{m-1}=a\}.
\]
Let
\[
\mathcal A_m:=\sigma([a]:a\in\{0,1\}^m),
\qquad
\mathcal A_\infty:=\sigma\!\Bigl(\bigcup_{m\ge 1}\mathcal A_m\Bigr),
\]
and pull these sigma-algebras back to $X$ by
\[
\mathcal F_m:=\omega_\infty^{-1}(\mathcal A_m),
\qquad
\mathcal F_\infty:=\omega_\infty^{-1}(\mathcal A_\infty).
\]
Thus $\mathcal F_m$ is the observable sigma-algebra generated by the first $m$
symbols of the readout.

\begin{proposition}[Prefix-decidable events are clopen cylinder unions]
\label{prop:decidable-clopen}
Let $P\subseteq\Omega_\infty$ be Borel.  The following are equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item $P\in\mathcal A_m$;
\item there exists a set $A\subseteq\{0,1\}^m$ such that
\[
P=\bigcup_{a\in A}[a];
\]
\item $P$ is clopen in the prefix topology and is a union of balls of radius
$2^{-m}$ in the standard prefix ultrametric.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, if $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_m$, then the truth of $\mathsf P$ is
completely determined by the first $m$ symbols of the observable readout.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The atoms of $\mathcal A_m$ are exactly the length-$m$ cylinders $[a]$.
Therefore a set belongs to $\mathcal A_m$ if and only if it is a union of such
atoms, proving the equivalence of (1) and (2).

In the prefix ultrametric on $\Omega_\infty$, each cylinder $[a]$ is a clopen
ball of radius $2^{-m}$, and every ball of radius $2^{-m}$ is of this form.
Hence (2) and (3) are equivalent.  Pulling back along $\omega_\infty$ gives the
final statement on $X$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Observable Filtration}

Set $\mathcal F_0:=\{\varnothing,X\}$, the trivial sigma-algebra carrying no
prefix information.  The family $(\mathcal F_m)_{m\ge 0}$ is increasing,
because longer prefixes contain shorter ones, and we regard it as the
\emph{observable filtration}.  The event $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_\infty$ may
not be decidable at finite resolution, but every $\mathcal F_m$ yields a
finite partition on which the best classification rule can be computed exactly.

\section{The Scan Error Profile}

\subsection{Definition and Exact Cylinder Decomposition}

\begin{definition}[Scan error profile]
\label{def:scan-error-profile}
Let $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_\infty$.  The scan error profile of $\mathsf P$
with respect to $\mu$ is
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
:=
\inf\{\mu(\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf Q):\ \mathsf Q\in\mathcal F_m\},
\qquad m\ge 1.
\]
Equivalently, it is the smallest classification error achievable using only the
first $m$ symbols of the readout.
\end{definition}

For each $m$, let $\mathcal C_m$ be the collection of atoms of $\mathcal F_m$.
Explicitly, $\mathcal C_m$ is the family of pullbacks of nonempty length-$m$
cylinders under $\omega_\infty$.

\begin{proposition}[Exact cylinder decomposition]
\label{prop:scan-error-cylinder}
For every $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_\infty$ and every $m\ge 1$,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
=
\sum_{C\in\mathcal C_m}
\min\{\mu(\mathsf P\cap C),\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)\}.
\]
If
\[
p_C:=\mu(\mathsf P\mid C)
\quad\text{for }\mu(C)>0,
\]
and $p_C:=0$ when $\mu(C)=0$, then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
=
\sum_{C\in\mathcal C_m}\mu(C)\min\{p_C,1-p_C\}
=
\EE_\mu[\min\{p_m,1-p_m\}],
\]
where
\[
p_m:=\mu(\mathsf P\mid \mathcal F_m).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Any $\mathcal F_m$-measurable event $\mathsf Q$ must be a union of atoms
$C\in\mathcal C_m$.  On a fixed atom $C$, there are only two choices: either
$C\subseteq \mathsf Q$ or $C\cap\mathsf Q=\varnothing$.  In the first case the
local contribution to $\mu(\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf Q)$ is
$\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)$; in the second it is $\mu(\mathsf P\cap C)$.  The
minimum over these two possibilities is therefore
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf P\cap C),\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)\}.
\]
Summing over all atoms gives the first formula.

For the second formula, note that on each atom $C$ one has
\[
\mu(\mathsf P\cap C)=\mu(C)p_C
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)=\mu(C)(1-p_C).
\]
Hence
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf P\cap C),\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)\}
=
\mu(C)\min\{p_C,1-p_C\}.
\]
Since $p_m$ is constant on each atom $C$ with value $p_C$, the final
expectation formula follows.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Bayes-Optimal Interpretation}

\begin{proposition}[Bayes-optimality on the prefix filtration]
\label{prop:bayes-optimality}
Let
\[
p_m:=\mu(\mathsf P\mid\mathcal F_m),
\]
and define the threshold classifier
\[
\mathsf P_m^\star:=\{x\in X:\ p_m(x)\ge 1/2\}\in\mathcal F_m.
\]
(The tie-breaking direction at $p_m(x)=1/2$ is irrelevant: on any atom $C$
where $p_C=1/2$, both inclusion and exclusion yield the same local error
$\mu(C)\min\{p_C,1-p_C\}=\mu(C)/2$.)
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item for every $\mathsf Q\in\mathcal F_m$,
\[
\mu(\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf Q)\ge \varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu),
\qquad
\mu(\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf P_m^\star)=\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu);
\]
\item for every bounded $\mathcal F_\infty$-measurable function $\varphi$,
\[
\left|
\int \varphi\,\ind_{\mathsf P}\,d\mu
-
\int \varphi\,\ind_{\mathsf P_m^\star}\,d\mu
\right|
\le
\lVert\varphi\rVert_\infty\,\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu).
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On each atom $C\in\mathcal C_m$, the quantity $p_m$ is constant and equal to
$p_C:=\mu(\mathsf P\mid C)$.  If one includes $C$ in the classifier, the local
error is $\mu(C)(1-p_C)$; if one excludes it, the local error is
$\mu(C)p_C$.  The smaller of these two numbers is achieved exactly by the
threshold rule $p_C\ge 1/2$.  Summing over all atoms and using
Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder} gives (1).

For (2), the difference
$\ind_{\mathsf P}-\ind_{\mathsf P_m^\star}$ is supported on
$\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf P_m^\star$.  Therefore
\[
\left|
\int \varphi(\ind_{\mathsf P}-\ind_{\mathsf P_m^\star})\,d\mu
\right|
\le
\lVert\varphi\rVert_\infty\,\mu(\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf P_m^\star)
=
\lVert\varphi\rVert_\infty\,\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu).
\qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Basic properties of the scan error profile]
\label{cor:basic-properties}
The map $m\mapsto\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)$ is nonincreasing.  Moreover,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=0
\]
if and only if $\mathsf P$ coincides $\mu$-almost surely with an event in
$\mathcal F_m$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Since $\mathcal F_m\subseteq \mathcal F_{m+1}$, the infimum defining
$\varepsilon_{m+1}$ is taken over a larger class than the infimum defining
$\varepsilon_m$, so the profile is nonincreasing.

If $\mathsf P$ agrees almost surely with some $\mathsf Q\in\mathcal F_m$, then
$\mu(\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf Q)=0$, so $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=0$.
Conversely, if $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=0$, choose a minimizing sequence
$\mathsf Q_j\in\mathcal F_m$ with $\mu(\mathsf P\triangle \mathsf Q_j)\to 0$.
Since $\mathcal F_m$ has only finitely many atoms, an exact minimizer exists by
Proposition~\ref{prop:bayes-optimality}, and it agrees with
$\mathsf P$ almost surely.
\end{proof}

\section{Discrete Tanaka Representation}

Let
\[
p_m:=\mu(\mathsf P\mid \mathcal F_m)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
p_0:=\mu(\mathsf P).
\]
Then $(p_m)_{m\ge 0}$ is a bounded martingale taking values in $[0,1]$.

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Tanaka representation of the scan error]
\label{thm:scan-tanaka}
For $m\ge 1$, define
\[
L_m^{1/2}
:=
\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}
\left(
\left|p_{k+1}-\frac12\right|
-
\left|p_k-\frac12\right|
-
\sgn\!\left(p_k-\frac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)
\right),
\]
with $\sgn(0):=0$.  Then $L_m^{1/2}\ge 0$ and $m\mapsto L_m^{1/2}$ is
nondecreasing.  Moreover,
\[
\boxed{
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
=
\varepsilon_0(\mathsf P;\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}],
}
\]
where
\[
\varepsilon_0(\mathsf P;\mu)=\min\{\mu(\mathsf P),1-\mu(\mathsf P)\}.
\]
If $\ind_{\mathsf P}$ is measurable with respect to
$\bigvee_{m\ge 1}\mathcal F_m$, then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)\to 0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf P;\mu),
\]
where $L_\infty^{1/2}:=\lim_{m\to\infty}L_m^{1/2}$ exists almost surely.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder},
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=\EE[\min\{p_m,1-p_m\}].
\]
Since $\min\{p,1-p\}=\frac12-\lvert p-\frac12\rvert$ for $p\in[0,1]$, this
becomes
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=\frac12-\EE\left|p_m-\frac12\right|.
\]

For any real sequence $(x_k)$ and any threshold $a$, the discrete Tanaka
identity holds:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\lvert x_m-a\rvert
&=
\lvert x_0-a\rvert
+
\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\sgn(x_k-a)(x_{k+1}-x_k) \\
&\quad+
\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}
\left(
\lvert x_{k+1}-a\rvert
-\lvert x_k-a\rvert
-\sgn(x_k-a)(x_{k+1}-x_k)
\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
This is verified by noting that each summand in the second sum is the
nonnegative remainder
$\lvert x_{k+1}-a\rvert - \lvert x_k-a\rvert - \sgn(x_k-a)(x_{k+1}-x_k)$,
and that summing both sums telescopes to
$\lvert x_m-a\rvert - \lvert x_0-a\rvert$; see \cite{Tanaka1963} for the
continuous-time origin and \cite[Chapter~12]{Williams1991} for the
discrete-time formulation.
Apply this with $x_k=p_k$ and $a=\frac12$.  The last sum is exactly
$L_m^{1/2}$.  Taking expectation and using the martingale property,
\[
\EE\!\left[\sgn\!\left(p_k-\frac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)\right]=0
\]
because $\sgn(p_k-\frac12)$ is $\mathcal F_k$-measurable.  Hence
\[
\EE\left|p_m-\frac12\right|
=
\left|p_0-\frac12\right|+\EE[L_m^{1/2}].
\]
Substituting into the formula for $\varepsilon_m$ gives
\[
\varepsilon_m
=
\frac12-\left|p_0-\frac12\right|-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]
=
\varepsilon_0-\EE[L_m^{1/2}],
\]
which is the claimed identity.

Each summand in $L_m^{1/2}$ is nonnegative by convexity of the absolute-value
function, so $L_m^{1/2}\ge 0$ and is nondecreasing in $m$.

If $\ind_{\mathsf P}$ is measurable with respect to $\bigvee_m \mathcal F_m$,
then Doob's martingale convergence theorem (L\'evy's upward theorem) gives
$p_m\to \ind_{\mathsf P}$ almost surely and in $L^1$; see
\cite[Theorem~14.2]{Williams1991} or \cite[Theorem~5.5.8]{Durrett2019}.
Therefore
\[
\varepsilon_m=\EE[\min\{p_m,1-p_m\}]\to 0.
\]
By monotone convergence, $\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]=\lim_m \EE[L_m^{1/2}]$, and the
identity above yields $\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]=\varepsilon_0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka} expresses the reduction in scan error between
resolution $0$ and $m$ as the accumulated local-time mass at the decision
threshold $1/2$.  In the continuous-time setting, the analogous identity is
Tanaka's formula for the local time of Brownian motion \cite{Tanaka1963}; the
present result is its discrete, filtration-based counterpart applied to the
bounded martingale $(p_m)$.
\end{remark}

\section{Boundary-Controlled Rates}

\begin{definition}[Boundary cylinders]
\label{def:boundary-cylinder-dimension}
For $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_\infty$, define the boundary-cylinder family at
resolution $m$ by
\[
\partial_m \mathsf P
:=
\{C\in\mathcal C_m:\ \mu(\mathsf P\cap C)>0\text{ and }\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)>0\}.
\]
Its cardinality is
\[
N_m(\partial\mathsf P):=\#(\partial_m \mathsf P).
\]
If there exists $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}>1$ such that the relevant cylinder
complexity grows like $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^m$, define the upper boundary
dimension by
\[
\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf P)
:=
\limsup_{m\to\infty}
\frac{\log N_m(\partial\mathsf P)}{m\log \lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Boundary-dimension upper bound]
\label{thm:boundary-dimension}
Assume there exist constants $K<\infty$ and $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}>1$ such
that for every $m\ge 1$ and every $C\in\mathcal C_m$,
\[
\mu(C)\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}.
\]
Then for every $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_\infty$ and every $m\ge 1$,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\le
K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf P).
\]
In particular, if $\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf P)=d<1$, then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\le
\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m+o(m)}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
In the cylinder decomposition of Proposition
~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}, only boundary cylinders contribute, because
the term vanishes whenever either $\mu(\mathsf P\cap C)=0$ or
$\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)=0$.  Moreover,
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf P\cap C),\mu(C\setminus \mathsf P)\}\le \mu(C).
\]
Hence
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\le
\sum_{C\in\partial_m \mathsf P}\mu(C)
\le
K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf P).
\]
If $N_m(\partial\mathsf P)=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{dm+o(m)}$ along the limsup
scale, the second claim follows immediately.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Exact rate law from boundary thickness]
\label{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}
Assume there exist constants
\[
0<c_-\le c_+<\infty
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}>1
\]
such that for every $m\ge 1$ and every $C\in\mathcal C_m$,
\[
c_-\,\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}\le \mu(C)\le c_+\,\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}.
\]
Assume further that there exist $\theta\in(0,\frac12]$ and $m_0$ such that for
every $m\ge m_0$ and every boundary cylinder $C\in\partial_m\mathsf P$,
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf P\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf P\mid C)\}\ge \theta.
\]
Then for every $m\ge m_0$,
\[
\boxed{
\theta c_-\,\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf P)
\le
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\le
\frac{c_+}{2}\,\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf P).
}
\]
If in addition
\[
N_m(\partial\mathsf P)=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{dm+o(m)}
\]
for some exponent $d$, then
\[
\lim_{m\to\infty}
\left(
-\frac1m\log_{\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\right)
=
1-d.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder},
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
=
\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf P}
\mu(C)\min\{\mu(\mathsf P\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf P\mid C)\}.
\]
The thickness assumption yields
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\ge
\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf P}\mu(C)\theta
\ge
\theta c_-\,\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf P).
\]
The upper bound follows from $\min\{p,1-p\}\le \frac12$:
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\le
\frac12\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf P}\mu(C)
\le
\frac{c_+}{2}\,\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf P).
\]
The asymptotic exponent is immediate from the two-sided estimate.
\end{proof}

\section{Examples and Specializations}

\begin{example}[Finite-horizon events]
\label{ex:finite-horizon-event}
If $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_r$ for some $r$, then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=0
\qquad\text{for all }m\ge r.
\]
Indeed, for $m\ge r$ one has $\mathsf P\in\mathcal F_m$, so the zero
classifier error is admissible in Definition~\ref{def:scan-error-profile}.
Thus finite-horizon events have exact finite scan depth.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Golden-mean specialization]
\label{ex:golden-mean-specialization}
Let $X$ be the one-sided golden-mean shift with Parry measure, and let
$\omega_\infty$ be the identity readout.  Then the number of admissible
length-$m$ cylinders grows like $\varphi^m$, where
$\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$, and cylinder measures are comparable to $\varphi^{-m}$;
see \cite[Chapters~3--4]{LindMarcus1995}.  Hence
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}=\varphi$ in the preceding theorems.

Consequently, if an event $\mathsf P$ has boundary-cylinder growth
\[
N_m(\partial\mathsf P)=\varphi^{dm+o(m)}
\]
with a uniform thickness parameter $\theta>0$ on its boundary cylinders, then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)=\varphi^{-(1-d)m+o(m)}.
\]
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
The golden-mean example illustrates the general rate law for systems with
exponential cylinder complexity.  Systems with subexponential complexity
(e.g., Sturmian codings) fall outside this regime; see the Discussion.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}

We have shown that the scan error profile of a binary observable decomposes
exactly over prefix cylinders, that the Bayes-optimal classifier is the
threshold rule on the conditional-probability martingale, and that the discrete
Tanaka representation converts error decay into accumulation of local time at
the threshold $1/2$.  The boundary-cylinder rate laws then give explicit
convergence estimates controlled by a single combinatorial invariant: the
growth of ambiguous cylinders.

\paragraph{Connection to entropy theory.}
The Shannon--McMillan--Breiman theorem \cite{ShannonMcMillanBreiman} guarantees
that cylinder measures decay at the exponential rate $h_\mu$ for an ergodic
source, which is the standing hypothesis behind our cylinder-mass bounds.
Conversely, the boundary-dimension exponent $d$ in
Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} may be viewed as a measure of how
much of the entropy-driven cylinder refinement goes into resolving the boundary
of $\mathsf P$.  Making this connection precise---expressing $d$ in terms of
conditional entropies or mutual information---is an open problem.

\paragraph{Extensions.}
Several natural generalizations remain.  First, the binary alphabet
$\{0,1\}$ can be replaced by a finite alphabet of size $q$, and the
threshold $1/2$ by a $q$-class Bayes boundary; the discrete Tanaka
structure extends directly, though the local-time functional acquires
$q-1$ threshold parameters.  Second, in the continuous-time setting
(e.g., diffusion processes observed through a quantizer), the discrete
Tanaka representation should converge to the classical Tanaka--Meyer
formula \cite{RevuzYor1999}; establishing this limit rigorously would
connect the present work to the theory of continuous-time local times.
Third, the uniform thickness hypothesis in
Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} is strong; relaxing it to a
measure-weighted average thickness, or to a condition on the
$L^p$-norm of the conditional probabilities on boundary cylinders,
would extend the two-sided rate law to a broader class of events.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The rate laws in this paper are restricted to the exponential
cylinder-complexity regime.  Systems with subexponential complexity---for
instance, Sturmian codings or substitution systems---require separate
low-complexity analysis, and the boundary-dimension framework does not
directly apply.  We also do not address computational aspects: while the
Bayes classifier is explicit, computing $p_C = \mu(\mathsf P \mid C)$ for
a given invariant measure $\mu$ may itself be intractable.
