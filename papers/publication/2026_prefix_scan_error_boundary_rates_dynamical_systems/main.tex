\begin{abstract}
We study how much the decision error for a measurable event can be reduced by
refining the available prefix information.  Given an increasing filtration
$(\mathcal{F}_m)_{m\ge 0}$ generated by a binary-valued process, the scan
error profile $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$ is the smallest classification
error achievable using only $\mathcal{F}_m$-measurable rules.  Our first
contribution is a discrete Tanaka representation
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]$
in terms of a nonnegative discrete local-time functional.  Our second
contribution is a pair of boundary-controlled rate laws controlled by the
growth of ambiguous boundary cylinders.  The thickness hypothesis underlying
the two-sided law is a combinatorial analogue of the Tsybakov margin condition.
A third theorem extends both laws to log-regular cylinder measures.  As a
fully worked example, we compute $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{E};\mu)\asymp\varphi^{-m/2}$
for an explicit event $\mathsf{E}$ on the golden-mean shift with its Parry
measure, where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt{5})/2$ and the boundary dimension is $d=1/2$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The purpose of this paper is narrow.  We fix a binary readout process and ask
how refinement of prefix information improves the best possible decision error
for a target event.  Let $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu)$ be a probability space with an
increasing filtration $(\mathcal{F}_m)_{m\ge 0}$ generated by $\{0,1\}$-valued
functions $s_0,s_1,\ldots$  The scan error profile
\[
m\longmapsto\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
:=\inf\bigl\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{Q}):\mathsf{Q}\in\mathcal{F}_m\bigr\}
\]
records the smallest classification error at each prefix depth.

The cylinder decomposition of Bayes risk on finite partitions and the
optimality of the threshold classifier $p_m\ge 1/2$ are classical
\cite{Tsybakov2009Nonparametric,DevroyeGyorfiLugosi1996}.  We recall them in
Section~\ref{sec:scan} to make the paper self-contained.  The new contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item A \emph{discrete Tanaka representation}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka}) connecting error decay to the accumulation
  of threshold crossings of the martingale $(p_m)$.
\item \emph{Boundary-cylinder rate laws}
  (Theorems~\ref{thm:boundary-dimension} and~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling})
  giving upper and two-sided bounds in terms of boundary-cylinder count and mass.
\item A \emph{nonuniform-profile extension}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:nonuniform-cylinder}) replacing the uniform cylinder-mass
  assumption by a log-regular condition.
\item A \emph{fully worked example} on the golden-mean shift
  (Example~\ref{ex:golden-mean-specialization}) with $d=1/2$ computed explicitly.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Relation to margin assumptions in classification}
\label{sub:margin}

The thickness hypothesis in Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} is
closely related to margin and noise conditions in nonparametric classification.
Mammen and Tsybakov \cite{MammenTsybakov1999} introduce a margin condition
with exponent $\alpha\ge 0$: for the regression function
$p_\infty(x)=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_\infty)(x)$,
\[
\mu\!\left(\left|p_\infty(x)-\tfrac12\right|\le t\right)\le C_0\,t^\alpha
\qquad\text{for all }t>0.
\]
Tsybakov \cite{Tsybakov2004} extends this to a general noise-exponent
framework, showing that margin conditions control the rate of optimal
plug-in classifiers.

Our uniform thickness condition
$\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}\ge\theta>0$ for all
boundary cylinders $C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}$ is a combinatorial analogue.
The two conditions are related but incomparable:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item Uniform thickness implies that all boundary-cylinder mass is bounded
  away from both $0$ and $1$, preventing one-sided concentration; the
  Mammen--Tsybakov condition is an integrated condition on the regression
  function and does not directly control individual cylinder probabilities.
\item Conversely, a measure satisfying the margin condition with large $\alpha$
  can still have individual boundary cylinders with $\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\to 0$
  as $m\to\infty$, violating uniform thickness.
\item Both conditions control classification rates: the Mammen--Tsybakov
  margin exponent $\alpha$ governs plug-in estimation rates from data, while
  our thickness hypothesis controls the oracle-error decay rate
  $\varepsilon_m\asymp\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m}$.
\end{enumerate}
The precise relationship between the boundary dimension $d$ and the margin
exponent $\alpha$ depends on the filtration geometry and remains open.

\subsection{Related work}
\label{sub:related}

Bayes risk on partitions and optimality of threshold classifiers are classical;
see \cite{DevroyeGyorfiLugosi1996} and \cite{AudibertTsybakov2007} for refined
plug-in rates.  The underlying martingale convergence is Doob's upward theorem
\cite{Doob1953,Williams1991,Durrett2019}.  The discrete Tanaka formula is a
lattice analogue of Tanaka's formula for Brownian local time
\cite{Tanaka1963,RevuzYor1999}.

Cylinder partition structure in symbolic dynamics is treated in
\cite{LindMarcus1995}; boundary combinatorics connect to
\cite{Fischer1975,Krieger1984,Boyle1983}.  Exponential cylinder estimates for
Parry and Gibbs measures are in \cite{Parry1964,Bowen1975,WaltersErgodicTheory}.
The Shannon--McMillan--Breiman theorem \cite{Shannon1948,McMillan1953,Breiman1957}
gives almost-sure asymptotics for the cylinder \emph{containing} a given
sample point; this is an almost-sure pointwise statement, weaker than the
uniform all-cylinder bounds assumed in our rate theorems (see
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).  Background on dynamics and information theory
is in \cite{WaltersErgodicTheory,LindMarcus1995,CoverThomas2006,MorseHedlund1940}.

\section{The Prefix Filtration Setting}
\label{sec:prefix}

The results hold in the following general setting; $T$-invariance and dynamics
are not used in any proof.

\begin{definition}[Prefix filtration]
\label{def:prefix-filtration}
Let $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu)$ be a probability space and $(s_t)_{t\ge 0}$ a
sequence of $\{0,1\}$-valued measurable functions.  Set $\mathcal{F}_0:=\{\varnothing,X\}$
and $\mathcal{F}_m:=\sigma(s_0,\ldots,s_{m-1})$ for $m\ge 1$.  The family
$(\mathcal{F}_m)_{m\ge 0}$ is the \emph{prefix filtration}; set
$\mathcal{F}_\infty:=\bigvee_m\mathcal{F}_m$.
\end{definition}

The dynamical specialization is $s_t(x):=\ind_W(T^tx)$ for a measurable set
$W$; the symbolic specialization is $X=\Omega_\infty:=\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$
and $s_t(\xi):=\xi_t$.  All results below hold for any prefix filtration.

For $a=a_0\cdots a_{m-1}\in\{0,1\}^m$, the length-$m$ cylinder in
$\Omega_\infty$ is $[a]:=\{\xi:\xi_0\cdots\xi_{m-1}=a\}$.  The readout map
$\omega_\infty(x):=s_0(x)s_1(x)\cdots$ pulls back sigma-algebras:
$\mathcal{F}_m=\omega_\infty^{-1}(\sigma([a]:a\in\{0,1\}^m))$.

\begin{proposition}[Prefix-decidable events are clopen cylinder unions]
\label{prop:decidable-clopen}
A set $P\subseteq\Omega_\infty$ is in $\mathcal{A}_m:=\sigma([a]:a\in\{0,1\}^m)$
if and only if it is a union of length-$m$ cylinders $[a]$, equivalently, a
clopen ball of radius $2^{-m}$ in the prefix ultrametric.  Pulling back along
$\omega_\infty$, if $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_m$ then the truth of
$\mathsf{P}$ is determined by the first $m$ symbols of the readout.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The atoms of $\mathcal{A}_m$ are exactly the length-$m$ cylinders, and a set
is measurable with respect to them iff it is a union of atoms.  In the prefix
ultrametric, length-$m$ cylinders are clopen balls of radius $2^{-m}$.
Pulling back along $\omega_\infty$ transfers this to $X$.
\end{proof}

\section{The Scan Error Profile}
\label{sec:scan}

\begin{proposition}[Exact cylinder decomposition]
\label{prop:scan-error-cylinder}
Let $\mathcal{C}_m$ be the atoms of $\mathcal{F}_m$ and $p_m:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_m)$.
For every $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$ and $m\ge 0$,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
=\sum_{C\in\mathcal{C}_m}\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}
=\EE[\min\{p_m,1-p_m\}].
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Any $\mathcal{F}_m$-measurable event $\mathsf{Q}$ is a union of atoms.  On
each atom $C$, inclusion contributes local error $\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})$
and exclusion contributes $\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C)$; the optimum is their
minimum.  Since $\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}=\mu(C)\min\{p_C,1-p_C\}$
where $p_C:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)$, summing over atoms gives the expectation form.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Bayes-optimality]
\label{prop:bayes-optimality}
The threshold classifier $\mathsf{P}_m^\star:=\{x:p_m(x)\ge 1/2\}$ achieves
$\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{P}_m^\star)=\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$, and
$|\int\varphi\ind_{\mathsf{P}}\,d\mu-\int\varphi\ind_{\mathsf{P}_m^\star}\,d\mu|\le\|\varphi\|_\infty\varepsilon_m$
for every bounded $\mathcal{F}_\infty$-measurable $\varphi$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On each atom the threshold rule minimizes the local error; summing gives the
global minimum.  The bound for $\varphi$ follows because
$\ind_{\mathsf{P}}-\ind_{\mathsf{P}_m^\star}$ is supported on
$\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{P}_m^\star$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Monotonicity and decidability]
\label{cor:basic-properties}
The profile $m\mapsto\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$ is nonincreasing, and
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=0$ iff $\mathsf{P}$ coincides $\mu$-a.s.\ with
an event in $\mathcal{F}_m$.
\end{corollary}

\section{Discrete Tanaka Representation}
\label{sec:tanaka}

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Tanaka representation of the scan error]
\label{thm:scan-tanaka}
Let $p_m:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_m)$.  For $m\ge 1$ define
\[
L_m^{1/2}
:=\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}
\left(
\left|p_{k+1}-\tfrac12\right|
-\left|p_k-\tfrac12\right|
-\sgn\!\left(p_k-\tfrac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)
\right),
\]
with $\sgn(0):=0$.  Then $L_m^{1/2}\ge 0$ and $m\mapsto L_m^{1/2}$ is
nondecreasing.  Moreover,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}],
\qquad
\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}),1-\mu(\mathsf{P})\}.
\]
Since $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$, we have $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\to 0$
and $\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder} gives
$\varepsilon_m=\frac12-\EE|p_m-\frac12|$.  Convexity of $y\mapsto|y-a|$
yields the pointwise identity
\[
|x_{k+1}-a|=|x_k-a|+\sgn(x_k-a)(x_{k+1}-x_k)+r_k(a),
\qquad r_k(a)\ge 0,
\]
which is the discrete analogue of Tanaka's formula \cite{Tanaka1963,RevuzYor1999}.
Applying this with $x_k=p_k$, $a=\frac12$, and summing $k=0$ to $m-1$, then
taking expectations,
\[
\EE\!\left[\sgn\!\left(p_k-\tfrac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)\right]
=\EE\!\left[\sgn\!\left(p_k-\tfrac12\right)\EE[p_{k+1}-p_k\mid\mathcal{F}_k]\right]=0
\]
because $(p_m)$ is a martingale.  Hence
$\EE|p_m-\frac12|=|p_0-\frac12|+\EE[L_m^{1/2}]$, and
$\varepsilon_m=\frac12-|p_0-\frac12|-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]=\varepsilon_0-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]$.
L\'{e}vy's upward theorem \cite{Doob1953,Williams1991,Durrett2019} gives
$p_m\to\ind_{\mathsf{P}}$ a.s.\ and in $L^1$, so $\varepsilon_m\to 0$ and
$\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]=\varepsilon_0$ by monotone convergence.
\end{proof}

\section{Boundary-Controlled Rates}
\label{sec:rates}

\subsection{Boundary cylinders and the growth scale}

\begin{definition}[Boundary cylinders and cylinder growth]
\label{def:boundary-cylinder}
For $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$, the boundary-cylinder family at
resolution $m$ is
\[
\partial_m\mathsf{P}
:=\{C\in\mathcal{C}_m:\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C)>0\text{ and }\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})>0\},
\]
with cardinality $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}):=\#(\partial_m\mathsf{P})$.

Let $M_m:=\#\{C\in\mathcal{C}_m:\mu(C)>0\}$.  When the limit
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}:=\lim_{m\to\infty}M_m^{1/m}$ exists and exceeds $1$,
it is the \emph{cylinder growth base}.  For a mixing SFT, $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}$
is the spectral radius of the transition matrix \cite{LindMarcus1995}.  The
\emph{upper boundary dimension} is
\[
\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{P})
:=\limsup_{m\to\infty}
\frac{\log N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})}{m\log\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}.
\]
\end{definition}

The dimension $d=\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{P})\in[0,1]$ is an
exponential growth exponent and is invariant under rescaling of $m$ by a
constant.  The absolute counts $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$ depend on the
particular symbolic representation, but the exponent $d$ is what appears in
all rate theorems.

\subsection{Rate theorems}

\begin{theorem}[Boundary-dimension upper bound]
\label{thm:boundary-dimension}
Suppose $\mu(C)\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ for all $m\ge 1$ and all
$C\in\mathcal{C}_m$.  Then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}).
\]
If $\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{P})=d<1$, then
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\le\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m+o(m)}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Non-boundary cylinders contribute zero to the decomposition
(Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}), since
$\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}=0$ off
$\partial_m\mathsf{P}$.  On boundary cylinders,
$\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}\le\mu(C)\le
K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$.  Summing gives
$\varepsilon_m\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
For $d<1$: fix $\eta>0$; for large $m$,
$N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})\le\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{(d+\eta)m}$, giving
$\varepsilon_m\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d-\eta)m}$.  Since $\eta$ and
the constant $K$ contribute $o(m)$ after taking logs, the bound follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Exact rate law from boundary thickness]
\label{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}
Suppose $c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}\le\mu(C)\le c_+\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$
for all $C\in\mathcal{C}_m$ and all $m\ge 1$, and suppose there exist
$\theta\in(0,\frac12]$ and $m_0$ such that for all $m\ge m_0$ and all
$C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}$,
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}\ge\theta.
\]
Then for all $m\ge m_0$,
\[
\theta c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
\le\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
\le\frac{c_+}{2}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}).
\]
If $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{dm+o(m)}$, then
$\lim_{m\to\infty}(-\frac{1}{m}\log_{\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}\varepsilon_m)=1-d$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder},
$\varepsilon_m=\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}}\mu(C)\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}$.
Lower: thickness and the lower cylinder bound give
$\varepsilon_m\ge\theta c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
Upper: $\min\{p,1-p\}\le\frac12$ and the upper cylinder bound give
$\varepsilon_m\le\frac{c_+}{2}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
Inserting $N_m=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{dm+o(m)}$ and taking
$-\log_{\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}/m$ yields $1-d$ in the limit.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Extension to log-regular cylinder measures}
\label{sub:nonuniform}

The uniform bounds $\mu(C)\asymp\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ hold for Gibbs
measures on mixing SFTs \cite{Bowen1975} but need not hold in general.

\begin{definition}[Log-regular measure]
\label{def:log-regular}
A measure $\mu$ is \emph{log-regular with exponent $\alpha>0$} if there exist
$0<c_1\le c_2<\infty$ such that for all $m\ge 1$ and all $C\in\mathcal{C}_m$
with $\mu(C)>0$,
\[
c_1 m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}\le\mu(C)\le c_2 m^{\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Rate law for log-regular measures]
\label{thm:nonuniform-cylinder}
Let $\mu$ be log-regular with exponent $\alpha>0$.  Assume the thickness
condition of Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} holds with some
$\theta>0$ and $m_0$.  If $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{dm+o(m)}$,
then there exist $C_1,C_2>0$ such that for all large $m$,
\[
C_1 m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m}
\le\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
\le C_2 m^{\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m}.
\]
In particular, the exponential rate $1-d$ is unchanged from the uniform case.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The same decomposition argument as in Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}
applies with $c_1 m^{-\alpha}$ and $c_2 m^\alpha$ in place of $c_-$ and
$c_+/2$.  Lower bound:
$\varepsilon_m\ge\theta c_1 m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
=\theta c_1 m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m+o(m)}$.
Upper bound:
$\varepsilon_m\le\frac{c_2}{2}m^{\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
=\frac{c_2}{2}m^{\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-d)m+o(m)}$.
The polynomial factors $m^{\pm\alpha}$ do not affect the exponential rate.
\end{proof}

\section{Examples}
\label{sec:examples}

\begin{example}[Finite-horizon events]
\label{ex:finite-horizon-event}
If $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_r$ for some finite $r$, then
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=0$ for all $m\ge r$.  Indeed, $\partial_m\mathsf{P}=\varnothing$
for $m\ge r$ since membership in $\mathsf{P}$ is determined by the first $r$
symbols.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Golden-mean shift: explicit computation with $d=1/2$]
\label{ex:golden-mean-specialization}
Let $(X,\mu)$ be the one-sided golden-mean shift with its Parry measure
\cite{Parry1964}.  The alphabet is $\{0,1\}$; the admissible words are those
with no two consecutive $1$s; the transition matrix is
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
\]
with spectral radius $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}=\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$
\cite[Chapter~4]{LindMarcus1995}.  The number of admissible length-$m$ words is
the Fibonacci number $M_m=F_{m+2}\sim C_F\varphi^m$.  The Parry measure
satisfies the two-sided cylinder bounds
$c_-\varphi^{-m}\le\mu([a])\le c_+\varphi^{-m}$ for explicit constants
$c_-,c_+>0$ determined by the left eigenvector of $A$ \cite{Parry1964}.

\paragraph{The event $\mathsf{E}$.}
Define
\[
\mathsf{E}:=\{x\in X:s_{2k}(x)=0\text{ for all }k\ge 0\},
\]
the event that every even-position symbol is $0$.  This event is in
$\mathcal{F}_\infty$ but is not in $\mathcal{F}_m$ for any finite $m$, since
it constrains symbols at positions $0,2,4,\ldots$ without bound.  By
the golden-mean transition structure, admissible words of even length
$m=2k$ satisfying $a_0=a_2=\cdots=a_{2k-2}=0$ always have $a_1,a_3,\ldots,a_{2k-1}$
free subject to admissibility constraints between consecutive pairs.

\paragraph{Boundary count.}
A cylinder $[a]\in\mathcal{C}_m$ for $m=2k$ lies in $\partial_m\mathsf{E}$
if and only if $[a]$ is consistent with $\mathsf{E}$ (i.e., $a_0=a_2=\cdots=a_{2k-2}=0$)
and also has extensions not in $\mathsf{E}$ (i.e., extensions with $a_{2k}=1$,
which is admissible whenever $a_{2k-1}=0$).  Precisely:
\[
\partial_{2k}\mathsf{E}
=\{[a]:a_0=a_2=\cdots=a_{2k-2}=0,\quad a_{2k-1}=0,\quad
\text{and }a\text{ is admissible}\}.
\]
The condition $a_{2k-1}=0$ is needed because if $a_{2k-1}=1$ then the only
admissible extension is $a_{2k}=0$, which is already in $\mathsf{E}$, and
there is no extension $a_{2k}=1$ (as $11$ is forbidden); such cylinders
therefore lie entirely inside $\mathsf{E}$ at the next level and contribute no
boundary.  The number of such words is the number of admissible length-$2k$
words with even positions fixed to $0$ and last symbol $a_{2k-1}=0$.

Counting by induction: let $b_k$ denote the number of admissible length-$k$
words $(a_1,a_3,\ldots,a_{2k-1})$ of odd-position letters such that the full
interleaved word $(0,a_1,0,a_3,\ldots,0,a_{2k-1})$ is admissible and
$a_{2k-1}=0$.  Since $0a_j0a_{j+1}$ is admissible for any $a_j,a_{j+1}$ with
$a_j a_{j+1}$ admissible in the golden-mean shift (because $0$ followed by
anything is admissible), the count $b_k$ satisfies the same Fibonacci
recurrence as the golden-mean shift itself:
\[
b_k=b_{k-1}+b_{k-2},\qquad b_1=1,\quad b_2=2,
\]
giving $b_k=F_{k+1}$.  Thus
\[
N_{2k}(\partial\mathsf{E})=F_{k+1}\sim\frac{\varphi^k}{\sqrt5}=\frac{\varphi^{m/2}}{\sqrt5}.
\]
For odd $m=2k+1$, every admissible word $a$ of length $2k+1$ with
$a_0=a_2=\cdots=a_{2k}=0$ lies entirely in $\mathsf{E}$ at the next level
(since the next even-position constraint is already satisfied), so
$N_{2k+1}(\partial\mathsf{E})=0$.  Therefore
\[
\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{E})
=\limsup_{m\to\infty}\frac{\log N_m(\partial\mathsf{E})}{m\log\varphi}
=\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac{\log(\varphi^k/\sqrt5)}{2k\log\varphi}
=\frac{1}{2}.
\]

\paragraph{Thickness verification.}
For any $[a]\in\partial_{2k}\mathsf{E}$, the Parry measure has the Gibbs
property: the cylinder $[a]$ splits over the next symbol $a_{2k}\in\{0,1\}$
with transition probabilities bounded away from $0$ uniformly over all
admissible $[a]$.  Concretely, since $a_{2k-1}=0$, both extensions
$[a0]$ and $[a1]$ are admissible, and the Parry-measure transition
probabilities from state $0$ are $\PP_\mu(a_{2k}=0\mid a_{2k-1}=0)=\varphi^{-1}$
and $\PP_\mu(a_{2k}=1\mid a_{2k-1}=0)=\varphi^{-2}$.  In particular,
$\mu([a0]),\mu([a1])>0$.  Now $\mathsf{E}$ constrains $a_{2k}=0$; the
extension $[a1]$ is outside $\mathsf{E}$.  Therefore for any
$[a]\in\partial_{2k}\mathsf{E}$,
\[
\mu(\mathsf{E}\mid[a])\ge\mu(\text{next even position is }0\mid[a])
\ge\varphi^{-1}>0
\]
and $\mu(\mathsf{E}^c\mid[a])\ge\varphi^{-2}>0$ similarly.  Hence the thickness
condition holds with $\theta=\varphi^{-2}\approx 0.382$.

\paragraph{Rate conclusion.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} with $d=1/2$ and
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}=\varphi$ gives, for all large even $m=2k$,
\[
\theta c_-\,\varphi^{-m/2+O(1)}
\le\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{E};\mu)
\le\frac{c_+}{2}\,\varphi^{-m/2+O(1)},
\]
so $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{E};\mu)\asymp\varphi^{-m/2}$.  For odd $m$, the
boundary is empty and $\varepsilon_m$ is controlled by the previous even level.
More precisely, there exist explicit constants $C_1=\theta c_->0$ and
$C_2=c_+/2<\infty$ such that
\[
C_1\varphi^{-m/2}\le\varepsilon_{2\lfloor m/2\rfloor}(\mathsf{E};\mu)\le C_2\varphi^{-m/2}.
\]
\end{example}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We have shown that the scan error profile decomposes exactly over prefix
cylinders (Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}), that the Bayes-optimal
rule is the threshold classifier (Proposition~\ref{prop:bayes-optimality}),
and that error decay is encoded by discrete local time at the threshold
(Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka}).  The rate laws quantify the decay in terms of
boundary-cylinder count and mass.  Example~\ref{ex:golden-mean-specialization}
confirms the rate $\varepsilon_m\asymp\varphi^{-m/2}$ for an explicit event
with $d=1/2$ on the golden-mean shift.

\paragraph{Shannon--McMillan--Breiman and uniform bounds.}
The SMB theorem \cite{Shannon1948,McMillan1953,Breiman1957} states that for
$\mu$-a.e.\ $x$,
\[
-\frac{1}{m}\log\mu([x]_m)\to h(\mu)
\quad\text{as }m\to\infty,
\]
where $[x]_m$ is the length-$m$ cylinder containing $x$.  This is a pointwise
almost-sure statement about a single cylinder along a single orbit; it does
\emph{not} give a uniform bound over all admissible cylinders simultaneously.
The uniform bounds $\mu(C)\asymp\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ in
Theorems~\ref{thm:boundary-dimension} and~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}
are strictly stronger.  For mixing SFTs with Parry or Gibbs measures, these
uniform bounds are established by Parry \cite{Parry1964} and Bowen
\cite{Bowen1975}; in that class the theorems apply directly.

\paragraph{Relation to margin conditions.}
The thickness parameter $\theta$ plays the role of the margin exponent
$\alpha$ from \cite{MammenTsybakov1999,Tsybakov2004}: both prevent the
classification problem from becoming degenerate near the decision boundary.
The Mammen--Tsybakov condition governs estimation error (rate of learning from
data), while boundary dimension $d$ governs approximation error (oracle decay
with resolution).  Connecting $d$ to $\alpha$ through the filtration geometry
is an open problem.

\paragraph{Extensions.}
Natural generalizations include: finite alphabets of size $q$ (the discrete
Tanaka structure extends with $q-1$ threshold parameters); the continuous-time
limit to Tanaka--Meyer formula \cite{RevuzYor1999}; weakening the thickness
hypothesis to an $L^p$ condition on boundary-cylinder probabilities; and
expressing the boundary dimension $d$ in terms of conditional entropies or
mutual information.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The rate laws assume exponential cylinder complexity; subexponential systems
(Sturmian codings, substitution shifts) require separate analysis.  The proofs
do not use $T$-invariance: all results are purely filtration-theoretic, and the
dynamical framing motivates the cylinder structure but is not invoked in any
argument.  Adding results that essentially use invariance---for example,
equivariance of boundary growth under $T$, or Rohlin tower constructions---is
an open direction.
