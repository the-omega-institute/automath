\section{KMS thermality, shell geometry, and inverse reconstruction}\label{sec:bridge:kms}

Section~\ref{sec:bridge:deadtime} shows that finite recovery produces a renewal process with infinite memory and an explicit correlation threshold. The point of those renewal and sign-transition formulas is not merely that they define an interesting stochastic channel: once the local excitation rate becomes position dependent through Tolman redshift, their thresholds lift from detector-channel parameters to codimension-one geometric loci. We begin with the ideal dead-time model, where the symbolic output is Markovian and the exact-clock formulas can be analyzed explicitly. The same KMS bridge will then be used to lift the finite-recovery threshold to a second codimension-one shell. The additional input is the weak-coupling response theory of a linearly coupled detector in a stationary KMS state.

\begin{assumption}[Stationary weak-coupling detector setup]\label{ass:kms-bridge}
The detector is a two-level system with energy gap $\Omega>0$, linearly coupled to a free bosonic field along a stationary worldline $x(\tau)$. Let $\chi_T$ be a standard adiabatic switching family with plateau length $T$, and let
\[
W_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(s)
:=
\omega_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}\!\bigl(\Phi(x(\tau))\Phi(x(\tau-s))\bigr)
\]
denote the proper-time pullback of the Wightman function. Assume that the stationary pullback is regular enough for the usual long-time weak-coupling transition rates to exist.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Weak-coupling detector response]\label{prop:weak-coupling-rate}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:kms-bridge}, the leading-order excitation and de-excitation rates are determined by the proper-time pullback of the two-point function:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-weak-coupling-rates}
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
\lambda_c^2\,\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\Omega),
\qquad
\Gamma_-(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
\lambda_c^2\,\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(-\Omega),
\end{equation}
where
\[
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\omega)
:=
\int_{\RR} e^{-i\omega s}W_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(s)\,ds
\]
is the proper-time Fourier transform of the pullback Wightman function. For the ideal dead-time model of Definition~\ref{def:ideal-dead-time}, a click corresponds to an excitation, so
\[
p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)\Delta\tau},
\qquad
q_\beta=1-p_\beta.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a detector initially in the ground state, leading-order perturbation theory gives
\[
P_{g\to e}(T)
=
\lambda_c^2
\int_{\RR^2}
\chi_T(\tau)\chi_T(\tau')
e^{-i\Omega(\tau-\tau')}
W_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\tau-\tau')\,d\tau\,d\tau'
+
O(\lambda_c^4),
\]
and the analogous expression for de-excitation is
\[
P_{e\to g}(T)
=
\lambda_c^2
\int_{\RR^2}
\chi_T(\tau)\chi_T(\tau')
e^{+i\Omega(\tau-\tau')}
W_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\tau-\tau')\,d\tau\,d\tau'
+
O(\lambda_c^4),
\]
see \cite{BirDav1982,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. By stationarity, the pullback depends only on $u=\tau-\tau'$. Writing the double integral as
\[
\int_{\RR}
e^{-i\Omega u}
W_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(u)\,
K_T(u)\,du,
\qquad
K_T(u):=
\int_{\RR}
\chi_T\!\left(v+\frac{u}{2}\right)
\chi_T\!\left(v-\frac{u}{2}\right)\,dv,
\]
and dividing by $T$, the standard adiabatic long-time limit gives $K_T(u)/T\to 1$ on compact $u$-sets. The regularity hypothesis in Assumption~\ref{ass:kms-bridge} then allows dominated convergence, yielding
\[
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{P_{g\to e}(T)}{T}
=
\lambda_c^2\,\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\Omega),
\]
and similarly
\[
\Gamma_-(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
\lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{P_{e\to g}(T)}{T}
=
\lambda_c^2\,\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(-\Omega).
\]
This is \eqref{eq:kms-weak-coupling-rates}. The click probabilities follow from the one-step dead-time model.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[From the continuous response to the discrete click model]\label{rem:kms-bridge-limitations}
Assumption~\ref{ass:kms-bridge} fixes the detector model, while Proposition~\ref{prop:weak-coupling-rate} is the standard long-time weak-coupling bridge that turns the symbolic construction into a temperature-dependent detector model \cite{BirDav1982,BreuerPetruccione2002}. In spacetimes with a bifurcate Killing horizon, the relevant KMS states are provided by the Kay--Wald framework \cite{KayWald1991}. The passage from the continuous detector response to the discrete click models used below therefore has two layers: first one extracts the leading excitation and de-excitation rates from the pulled-back Wightman function; then one samples over intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ and replaces the continuous response by the corresponding coarse-grained click probabilities.

For pointlike Unruh--DeWitt detectors, the singular short-distance structure of the Wightman function requires smooth switching or spatial smearing in order for the response theory to be well defined \cite{LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. Our use of an adiabatic switching family is exactly the standard device that isolates the stationary rate from switching transients, and the same leading weak-coupling rate persists for smoothly smeared detectors of fixed proper size. Recent near-horizon analyses likewise stress that finite-time detector readouts can be dominated by switching before thermalization is reached \cite{ShallueCarroll2025}. This is one reason the present shell construction is explicitly restricted to the stationary long-time regime.

The bridge is therefore controlled only when the weak-coupling rates exist, the adiabatic plateau is long compared with the detector correlation time and any smearing scale, the chosen gap lies in a regime where the geometric-optics or homogeneous-spectral-weight approximation is reliable, and multi-click effects inside a single sampling interval remain higher order in $\lambda_c^2$. No claim is made here beyond that regime. In particular, the discrete-time ideal dead-time and finite-recovery models should be read as effective sampled detector channels, not as exact microscopic detector dynamics. Continuous-time hazard models, paralyzable counters, or nonexponential recovery laws would lead to different threshold equations and must be analyzed separately.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[KMS spectral form of the detector response]\label{prop:kms-response}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:kms-bridge}, suppose the pullback state along the detector worldline is a $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}$-KMS state for the proper-time evolution. Define the commutator spectral weight
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-spectral-weight}
\mathcal S(\Omega)
:=
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(-\Omega)
-
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\Omega).
\end{equation}
For a free bosonic field, $\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is independent of $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}$. For every $\Omega>0$ with $\mathcal S(\Omega)>0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-detailed-balance}
\frac{\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)}{\Gamma_-(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)}
=
e^{-\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-planck-form}
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}-1},
\qquad
\Gamma_-(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}\mathcal S(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}-1}.
\end{equation}
In particular, $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\mapsto \Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)$ is smooth and strictly decreasing.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a proper-time $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}$-KMS state, the pullback Wightman function satisfies the Fourier-space detailed-balance relation
\[
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(-\Omega)
=
e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\Omega)
\qquad
(\Omega>0),
\]
see \cite{KayWald1991,BirDav1982}. Combining this with \eqref{eq:kms-weak-coupling-rates} gives \eqref{eq:kms-detailed-balance}.

Because the detector couples linearly to a free bosonic field, the commutator is a $c$-number distribution, hence state-independent. Its proper-time Fourier transform is exactly the difference
\[
\mathcal S(\Omega)
=
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(-\Omega)
-
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\Omega),
\]
so $\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is independent of $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}$. Using the KMS relation,
\[
\mathcal S(\Omega)
=
\bigl(e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}-1\bigr)\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\Omega),
\]
whence
\[
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(\Omega)
=
\frac{\mathcal S(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}-1},
\qquad
\widehat{W}_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}(-\Omega)
=
\frac{e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}\mathcal S(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}-1}.
\]
Substituting into \eqref{eq:kms-weak-coupling-rates} proves \eqref{eq:kms-planck-form}. Finally,
\[
\partial_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
-\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\,
\frac{\Omega e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}}{(e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}-1)^2}
<0
\]
whenever $\mathcal S(\Omega)>0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Homogeneous spectral weight and the status of Tolman scaling]\label{rem:homogeneous-spectral-weight}
The local spectral weight $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ carries the dependence of the response on field content, local geometry, and the detailed pullback of the commutator to the detector worldline. The ``homogeneous spectral-weight regime'' used later is the special case in which this factor is constant on the static region of interest, so that Tolman redshift supplies the only spatial dependence in the leading-order excitation rate. This is an idealization, not a general statement about local thermal equilibrium in curved spacetime.

The role of this idealization in the present paper is explicit. Theorems such as \ref{thm:tolman-parry}, \ref{thm:shell-order}, and \ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion} use it to convert detector-rate thresholds into pure lapse conditions. Theorem~\ref{thm:parry-surface-stability} then quantifies how small spectral inhomogeneities deform the exact-clock shell away from that ideal limit. Accordingly, the shell formulas below should be interpreted as exact statements within the fixed response model and as leading-order approximations when $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ varies slowly.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Cylinder information process]\label{def:bridge-cylinder-information}
Let $\mu_\Theta$ be the stationary output law from Theorem~\ref{thm:dead-time-golden}. For a sampled trajectory $(A_n)_{n\ge 0}$, define
\[
\tau(t):=-\log \mu_\Theta([A_0\cdots A_{t-1}]).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Exact symbolic thermality and exact-clock shell]\label{def:exact-symbolic-thermality}
For a fixed detector model and sampling rule, \emph{exact symbolic thermality} means that the stationary output law realizes the distinguished zero-jitter maximum-entropy law compatible with the allowed symbolic grammar. In the ideal dead-time model on the golden-mean shift, this distinguished law is the Parry measure. In the full-support renewal class on the full binary shift, it is the Bernoulli law.

The \emph{exact-clock shell} is the locus in a static KMS background at which the ideal dead-time output law realizes the Parry measure, equivalently the locus on which the asymptotic information-clock jitter vanishes.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Exact symbolic thermality versus KMS thermality]\label{rem:exact-symbolic-vs-kms}
The distinction is operational. KMS thermality and detailed balance constrain the underlying two-point function and the ratio of excitation to de-excitation rates. Exact symbolic thermality is instead a statement about the \emph{sampled output process}: it depends on the detector rule, the recovery dynamics, and the sampling step $\Delta\tau$. The exact-clock shell is therefore detector- and sampling-dependent even when the background state is fixed.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[KMS click rate and the information clock]\label{thm:kms-clock}
Under the ideal dead-time detector model with Assumption~\ref{ass:kms-bridge}, the stationary distribution of the output chain is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bridge-stationary-law}
\pi_0=\frac{1}{2-p_\beta},
\qquad
\pi_1=\frac{1-p_\beta}{2-p_\beta}.
\end{equation}
Its entropy rate is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bridge-entropy-rate}
h_\beta
=
\frac{-p_\beta\log p_\beta-(1-p_\beta)\log(1-p_\beta)}{2-p_\beta}.
\end{equation}
Moreover, the asymptotic clock jitter of Definition~\ref{def:bridge-cylinder-information} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bridge-jitter}
\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)
=
\frac{p_\beta(1-p_\beta)}{(2-p_\beta)^3}
\left[
\log\frac{1-p_\beta}{p_\beta^2}
\right]^2.
\end{equation}
In particular,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bridge-parry-point}
\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)=0
\iff
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}
\iff
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The stationary distribution $\pi=(\pi_0,\pi_1)$ solves
\[
\pi=\pi P_\beta,
\qquad
\pi_0+\pi_1=1.
\]
From
\[
\pi_1=\pi_0(1-p_\beta),
\]
one gets
\[
\pi_0(2-p_\beta)=1,
\]
hence \eqref{eq:bridge-stationary-law}.

The entropy rate of a stationary Markov chain is
\[
h_\beta
=
-\sum_i \pi_i\sum_j P_{ij}\log P_{ij}.
\]
Since the only nontrivial branching occurs from state $0$,
\[
h_\beta
=
\pi_0\bigl(-p_\beta\log p_\beta-(1-p_\beta)\log(1-p_\beta)\bigr),
\]
which is exactly \eqref{eq:bridge-entropy-rate}.

For the variance formula, define the edge-information observable
\[
g(i,j):=-\log P_{ij}.
\]
Thus
\[
g(0,0)=-\log p_\beta,\qquad
g(0,1)=-\log(1-p_\beta),\qquad
g(1,0)=0.
\]
Write
\[
\mathfrak a:=-\log p_\beta,
\qquad
\mathfrak b:=-\log(1-p_\beta).
\]
Then
\[
h_\beta=\frac{p_\beta \mathfrak a+(1-p_\beta)\mathfrak b}{2-p_\beta}
=\pi_0\bigl(p_\beta \mathfrak a+(1-p_\beta)\mathfrak b\bigr).
\]
The process $\tau(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{t-2}g(A_k,A_{k+1})+\tau_0$ is a Birkhoff sum of the edge observable $g$ along the Markov chain. By the Gordin martingale approximation \cite{Gordin1969}, the asymptotic variance is $\EE_\pi[m^2]$, where $m(i,j)=g(i,j)-h_\beta+u(j)-u(i)$ is the martingale increment and $u$ solves the Poisson equation $(I-P_\beta)u=F-h_\beta$ with $F(i)=\sum_j P_{ij}g(i,j)$. Since
\[
F(0)=p_\beta\mathfrak a+(1-p_\beta)\mathfrak b,
\qquad
F(1)=0,
\]
the equations
\[
u(0)-p_\beta u(0)-(1-p_\beta)u(1)=F(0)-h_\beta,
\qquad
u(1)-u(0)=-h_\beta
\]
are solved by $u(1)=0$ and $u(0)=h_\beta$. The martingale increments are therefore
\[
m(0,0)=\mathfrak a-h_\beta,
\qquad
m(0,1)=\mathfrak b-2h_\beta,
\qquad
m(1,0)=0,
\]
so
\[
\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)
=
\pi_0\Bigl[
p_\beta(\mathfrak a-h_\beta)^2
+
(1-p_\beta)(\mathfrak b-2h_\beta)^2
\Bigr].
\]

Direct algebra gives
\[
\mathfrak a-h_\beta
=
\frac{1-p_\beta}{2-p_\beta}(2\mathfrak a-\mathfrak b),
\qquad
\mathfrak b-2h_\beta
=
-\frac{p_\beta}{2-p_\beta}(2\mathfrak a-\mathfrak b),
\]
hence
\[
\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)
=
\frac{p_\beta(1-p_\beta)}{(2-p_\beta)^3}(2\mathfrak a-\mathfrak b)^2.
\]
Since
\[
2\mathfrak a-\mathfrak b
=
\log\frac{1-p_\beta}{p_\beta^2},
\]
this is exactly \eqref{eq:bridge-jitter}.

Finally, $\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)=0$ if and only if
\[
\log\frac{1-p_\beta}{p_\beta^2}=0,
\]
that is,
\[
1-p_\beta=p_\beta^2.
\]
The unique positive solution is $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$, and since $p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)\Delta\tau}$ this is equivalent to
\[
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Central limit theorem for the information clock]\label{thm:clt-clock}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:kms-clock}, and assuming $p_\beta\neq\varphi^{-1}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bridge-clt}
\frac{\tau(t)-t\,h_\beta}{\sqrt{t\,\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)}}
\xrightarrow{\;\mathcal D\;}
\mathcal N(0,1)
\qquad(t\to\infty).
\end{equation}
Moreover, the almost-sure convergence $\tau(t)/t\to h_\beta$ holds by the Shannon--McMillan--Breiman theorem for the ergodic Markov chain \cite{CoverThomas2006}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The process $\tau(t)=\sum_{k=0}^{t-2}g(A_k,A_{k+1})-\log\pi_{A_0}$ is a Birkhoff sum of the bounded observable $g$ along the ergodic Markov chain $(A_n)$. Since the chain has a finite state space $\{0,1\}$ and $\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)>0$, the Gordin martingale approximation \cite{Gordin1969} yields the CLT; see also \cite{KipnisVaradhan1986} for the reversible case and \cite{Billingsley1995} for the general finite-state setting. The a.s.\ convergence is standard \cite{CoverThomas2006}.
\end{proof}

The ideal dead-time chain also admits a renewal description, because every click is followed by a positive zero-run before the next click. This yields a cycle-based rigidity theorem that is exactly parallel to the Bernoulli selector on the full binary shift.

\begin{theorem}[Parry rigidity for renewal clocks on the golden-mean shift]\label{thm:golden-renewal-rigidity}
Let $(G_i)_{i\ge 1}$ be i.i.d.\ with law $g=(g_k)_{k\ge 1}$ satisfying
\[
g_k>0\quad(k\ge 1),
\qquad
H(g)<\infty,
\qquad
\EE G<\infty.
\]
Regard the associated binary process as the concatenation of cycles
\[
10^{G_1}\,10^{G_2}\cdots,
\]
so that it is supported on the golden-mean shift $X_\varphi$. Define
\[
T_n:=\sum_{i=1}^n(1+G_i),
\qquad
\Theta_n:=-\sum_{i=1}^n\log g_{G_i},
\]
and let
\[
u^\varphi_k:=\varphi^{-(k+1)},
\qquad k\ge 1.
\]
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
\item \textup{(Exact clock rate.)} Writing
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-renewal-rate}
h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}
:=
\frac{H(g)}{1+\EE G},
\end{equation}
one has
\[
\frac{\Theta_n}{T_n}\xrightarrow{\textup{a.s.}} h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}.
\]
\item \textup{(Exact cycle-normalized jitter.)}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-renewal-jitter}
\Sigma_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}^2
:=
\frac{1}{1+\EE G}
\sum_{k\ge 1}g_k\bigl(-\log g_k-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}(k+1)\bigr)^2.
\end{equation}
\item \textup{(Variational identity against the Parry gap law.)}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-renewal-kl}
\log\varphi-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}
=
\frac{1}{1+\EE G}\,
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(g\Vert u^\varphi).
\end{equation}
\item \textup{(Parry rigidity.)} $\Sigma_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}^2=0$ if and only if $g=u^\varphi$. Equivalently, among full-support renewal laws on the golden-mean shift, the Parry measure is the unique zero-jitter renewal clock.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
Y_i:=-\log g_{G_i},
\qquad
L_i:=1+G_i,
\qquad
Z_i:=Y_i-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}L_i.
\]
Because the $G_i$ are i.i.d.\ with law $g$, so are the triples $(Y_i,L_i,Z_i)$.

\emph{(i)} By the strong law of large numbers,
\[
\frac{1}{n}\Theta_n
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i
\xrightarrow{\textup{a.s.}}
\EE Y_1
=
H(g),
\]
and
\[
\frac{1}{n}T_n
=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n L_i
\xrightarrow{\textup{a.s.}}
\EE L_1
=
1+\EE G.
\]
Taking the ratio gives $\Theta_n/T_n\to h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}$.

\emph{(ii)} Since
\[
\EE Z_1
=
\EE Y_1-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}\EE L_1
=
H(g)-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}(1+\EE G)
=
0,
\]
independence yields
\[
\Var\bigl(\Theta_n-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}T_n\bigr)
=
\Var\Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^n Z_i\Bigr)
=
n\,\Var(Z_1).
\]
Dividing by $n(1+\EE G)$ gives \eqref{eq:golden-renewal-jitter}.

\emph{(iii)} Because $u^\varphi_k=\varphi^{-(k+1)}$,
\begin{align*}
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(g\Vert u^\varphi)
&=
\sum_{k\ge 1}g_k\log\frac{g_k}{\varphi^{-(k+1)}}\\
&=
\sum_{k\ge 1}g_k\log g_k
+
(\log\varphi)\sum_{k\ge 1}g_k(k+1)\\
&=
-H(g)+(1+\EE G)\log\varphi,
\end{align*}
which is exactly \eqref{eq:golden-renewal-kl}.

\emph{(iv)} If $\Sigma_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}^2=0$, then every summand in \eqref{eq:golden-renewal-jitter} vanishes because $g_k>0$ for all $k\ge 1$. Hence
\[
-\log g_k=h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}(k+1)
\qquad(k\ge 1),
\]
so
\[
g_k=e^{-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}(k+1)}.
\]
Summing over $k\ge 1$ gives
\[
1=\sum_{k\ge 1}e^{-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}(k+1)}
=
\frac{e^{-2h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}}}{1-e^{-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}}}.
\]
Writing $z=e^{-h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}}$, one obtains $z^2+z-1=0$, hence $z=\varphi^{-1}$ and $h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}=\log\varphi$. Therefore $g_k=\varphi^{-(k+1)}=u^\varphi$ for all $k\ge 1$. Conversely, if $g=u^\varphi$, then \eqref{eq:golden-renewal-kl} gives $h_{\varphi,\mathrm{ren}}=\log\varphi$, and every summand in \eqref{eq:golden-renewal-jitter} vanishes.

Finally,
\[
u^\varphi_k
=
\varphi^{-2}\,(\varphi^{-1})^{k-1},
\qquad k\ge 1,
\]
is the geometric gap law induced by the transition probabilities
\[
P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1},
\qquad
P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2},
\qquad
P(1,0)=1,
\]
which define the Parry measure on $X_\varphi$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}.
\end{proof}

In the ideal dead-time detector, the renewal gap law is
\[
g_{\beta,k}
=
(1-p_\beta)p_\beta^{k-1},
\qquad k\ge 1.
\]
Therefore Theorem~\ref{thm:golden-renewal-rigidity} yields the exact identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ideal-renewal-parry-gap}
\log\varphi-h_\beta
=
\frac{1}{2-p_\beta}\,
D_{\mathrm{KL}}(g_\beta\Vert u^\varphi),
\qquad
g_\beta=(g_{\beta,k})_{k\ge 1},
\end{equation}
so the ideal dead-time clock selects the same parameter $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$ in both its cylinder and renewal realizations.

The agreement between the cylinder-clock selector and the renewal selector is therefore not accidental; it is the visible signature of a deeper rigidity.

\begin{theorem}[Exact-clock rigidity in the ideal dead-time KMS model]\label{thm:parry-kms}
For the ideal dead-time detector in a KMS state, the following conditions are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
\item The clock jitter vanishes: $\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)=0$.
\item The output law is the Parry measure \cite{Parry1964} on the golden-mean shift $X_\varphi$, i.e., the unique measure of maximal entropy.
\item The entropy rate equals the topological entropy of $X_\varphi$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-entropy}
h_\beta=\log\varphi.
\end{equation}
\item The information observable $g(i,j)=-\log P_{ij}$ is a coboundary modulo $h_\beta$: there exists $u:\{0,1\}\to\RR$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:coboundary}
g(i,j)=\log\varphi+u(j)-u(i)
\end{equation}
for all admissible pairs $(i,j)$ with $P_{ij}>0$, namely $(0,0)$, $(0,1)$, $(1,0)$.
\item The KMS excitation rate satisfies $\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$.
\end{enumerate}
Moreover, the entropy rate is strictly concave at the Parry point: writing $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}+\epsilon$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:entropy-deficit}
\log\varphi-h_\beta
=
\frac{15+7\sqrt 5}{20}\,\epsilon^2
+
O(\epsilon^3).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The equivalence (i)$\Leftrightarrow$(v) was established in Theorem~\ref{thm:kms-clock}.

(v)$\Rightarrow$(iii):\enspace When $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$, the golden-ratio identity $1-\varphi^{-1}=\varphi^{-2}$ gives $q_\beta=\varphi^{-2}$. Substituting into \eqref{eq:bridge-entropy-rate}:
\[
h_\beta
=
\frac{\varphi^{-1}\log\varphi+2\varphi^{-2}\log\varphi}{2-\varphi^{-1}}
=
\frac{(\varphi^{-1}+2\varphi^{-2})\log\varphi}{2-\varphi^{-1}}.
\]
Since $\varphi^{-1}+2\varphi^{-2}=\varphi^{-1}+2(1-\varphi^{-1})=2-\varphi^{-1}$, this equals $\log\varphi$.

(iii)$\Rightarrow$(ii):\enspace The Markov chain with transition matrix $P_\beta$ on $X_\varphi$ has entropy rate $h_\beta$, which by assumption equals $\log\varphi$, the topological entropy of $X_\varphi$ \cite{LindMarcus1995}. Since the Parry measure is the unique measure achieving the topological entropy on an irreducible sofic shift, and the output chain is a Markov measure supported on $X_\varphi$, this Markov measure must be the Parry measure.

(ii)$\Rightarrow$(iv):\enspace The Parry measure on the golden-mean shift has transition probabilities $P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1}$, $P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2}$, $P(1,0)=1$ \cite{LindMarcus1995}. Thus $g(0,0)=\log\varphi$, $g(0,1)=2\log\varphi$, $g(1,0)=0$. Setting $u(0)=0$ and $u(1)=\log\varphi$ verifies \eqref{eq:coboundary}:
\begin{alignat*}{2}
g(0,0)&=\log\varphi+0-0&&=\log\varphi,\\
g(0,1)&=\log\varphi+\log\varphi-0&&=2\log\varphi,\\
g(1,0)&=\log\varphi+0-\log\varphi&&=0.
\end{alignat*}

(iv)$\Rightarrow$(i):\enspace If \eqref{eq:coboundary} holds, then $g(A_k,A_{k+1})=\log\varphi+u(A_{k+1})-u(A_k)$. Summing over $k=0,\dots,t-2$:
\[
\tau(t)=(t-1)\log\varphi+u(A_{t-1})-u(A_0)-\log\pi_{A_0}.
\]
The fluctuation $\tau(t)-t\log\varphi$ is bounded by $2\|u\|_\infty+\log\varphi+\|\log\pi\|_\infty$, so $\operatorname{Var}(\tau(t))=O(1)$ and $\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)=\lim_{t\to\infty}\operatorname{Var}(\tau(t))/t=0$.

For the quadratic deficit \eqref{eq:entropy-deficit}: write $f(p)=H_2(p)/(2-p)$ where $H_2(p)=-p\log p-(1-p)\log(1-p)$. Then $h_\beta=f(p_\beta)$ and $f(\varphi^{-1})=\log\varphi$. We have $f'(\varphi^{-1})=0$ (the Parry measure maximizes entropy among all Markov measures on $X_\varphi$). Computing $f''(\varphi^{-1})$: the quotient rule gives $f''=(H_2''v^2+2H_2'v+2H_2)/v^3$ with $v=2-p$. At $p=\varphi^{-1}$ one has $H_2''=-1/[\varphi^{-1}\varphi^{-2}]=-\varphi^3$, $H_2'=\log(\varphi^{-2}/\varphi^{-1})=-\log\varphi$, $H_2=v\log\varphi$; the terms linear in $\log\varphi$ cancel ($-2v\log\varphi+2v\log\varphi=0$) and
\[
f''(\varphi^{-1})
=
-\frac{\varphi^3}{2-\varphi^{-1}}
=
-\frac{2(2+\sqrt 5)}{5-\sqrt 5}
=
-\frac{15+7\sqrt 5}{10}.
\]
Here we used $\varphi^3=2\varphi+1=2+\sqrt 5$ and $2-\varphi^{-1}=(5-\sqrt 5)/2$, and rationalized via $(5-\sqrt 5)(5+\sqrt 5)=20$. Taylor expanding: $\log\varphi-h_\beta=-\frac12 f''(\varphi^{-1})\epsilon^2+O(\epsilon^3)=\frac{15+7\sqrt 5}{20}\,\epsilon^2+O(\epsilon^3)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:parry-kms-significance}
Theorem~\ref{thm:parry-kms} identifies the distinguished zero-jitter law on the golden-mean shift with a concrete detector condition. At that point the information observable is a coboundary modulo the mean rate, so the accumulated information differs from $t\log\varphi$ only by a bounded boundary term.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Parry parameter values and Tolman matching]\label{cor:parry-parameter-values}
For the ideal dead-time detector, the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift is realized exactly when the detector transition matrix equals
\[
P_*=
\begin{pmatrix}
\varphi^{-1}&\varphi^{-2}\\
1&0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-parameter-values}
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1},
\qquad
q_\beta=\varphi^{-2},
\qquad
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi.
\end{equation}
In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime this is the Tolman condition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-tolman-matching}
\beta_\infty N(x)=\beta_\varphi(D)
:=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right).
\end{equation}
Thus the Parry point is exactly the Tolman-redshifted temperature at which the sampled ideal detector reproduces the maximal-entropy law compatible with the no-double-click grammar.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The Parry measure on the golden-mean shift has transition probabilities $P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1}$, $P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2}$, and $P(1,0)=1$. Identifying this with the ideal dead-time matrix $P_\beta$ gives $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$ and $q_\beta=\varphi^{-2}$, which is equivalent to $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ because $p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}$. Substituting \eqref{eq:kms-planck-form} under homogeneous spectral weight gives \eqref{eq:parry-tolman-matching}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Operational status of the exact-clock shell]\label{rem:exact-clock-attainability}
The exact-clock shell is an ideal benchmark, not a claim that realistic detectors literally implement a mathematically sharp one-step refractory rule. Once the singular dead-time limit is relaxed, the exact-clock shell ceases to be exact and is replaced by nearby detector-dependent thresholds, most notably the memory-transition shell.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Variational structure at the exact-clock point]\label{thm:variational}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:kms-clock}, let $\mu_*$ denote the Parry measure on $X_\varphi$, with transition matrix $P_*:=P_\beta\big|_{p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}}$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
\item \textup{(Relative entropy rate equals entropy deficit.)} For every $p_\beta\in(0,1)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kl-parry}
d_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mu_\beta\Vert\mu_*)
:=
\sum_{i,j}\pi_i\,P_{\beta,ij}\log\frac{P_{\beta,ij}}{P_{*,ij}}
=
\log\varphi-h_\beta.
\end{equation}
\item \textup{(Affine pressure.)} Define the Ruelle transfer matrix
\[
L_t
:=
\begin{pmatrix}
p_\beta^{\,t}&(1-p_\beta)^t\\[2pt]
1&0
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad t\in\RR,
\]
and the topological pressure $\mathcal P(t):=\log\lambda_{\max}(L_t)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pressure-general}
\mathcal P(t)
=
\log\frac{p_\beta^{\,t}+\sqrt{p_\beta^{\,2t}+4(1-p_\beta)^t}}{2}.
\end{equation}
At the exact-clock point,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pressure-affine}
\mathcal P(t)\big|_{p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}}
=
(1-t)\log\varphi
\qquad\text{for all }t\in\RR.
\end{equation}
\item \textup{(Fluctuation--dissipation identity.)} Near the Parry point,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fdt}
\sigma_\tau^2(\beta)
=
2\bigl(\log\varphi-h_\beta\bigr)+O(\epsilon^3),
\qquad
\epsilon:=p_\beta-\varphi^{-1}.
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $\sigma_\tau^2=2\,d_{\mathrm{KL}}(\mu_\beta\Vert\mu_*)+O(\epsilon^3)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
(i)\enspace Using $P_{*,00}=\varphi^{-1}$, $P_{*,01}=\varphi^{-2}$, $P_{*,10}=1$ and the stationary distribution \eqref{eq:bridge-stationary-law}:
\[
d_{\mathrm{KL}}
=
\frac{1}{2-p_\beta}
\bigl[
p_\beta\log(p_\beta\varphi)
+
(1-p_\beta)\log\bigl((1-p_\beta)\varphi^2\bigr)
\bigr].
\]
Since $p_\beta+2(1-p_\beta)=2-p_\beta$, the $\log\varphi$ terms collapse:
\[
d_{\mathrm{KL}}
=
\frac{p_\beta\log p_\beta+(1-p_\beta)\log(1-p_\beta)}{2-p_\beta}
+\log\varphi
=
\log\varphi-h_\beta.
\]

(ii)\enspace At $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$, the identity $1-\varphi^{-1}=\varphi^{-2}$ gives $p_\beta^{\,2t}+4(1-p_\beta)^t=\varphi^{-2t}+4\varphi^{-2t}=5\varphi^{-2t}$. The discriminant is a perfect square for every $t$:
\[
\lambda_{\max}(t)
=
\frac{\varphi^{-t}(1+\sqrt 5)}{2}
=
\varphi^{1-t}.
\]

(iii)\enspace Write $\sigma_\tau^2=A(p)L(p)^2$ with $A(p):=p(1-p)/(2-p)^3$ and $L(p):=\log\bigl((1-p)/p^2\bigr)$. Since $L(\varphi^{-1})=0$,
\[
\sigma_\tau^2
=
A(\varphi^{-1})\bigl[L'(\varphi^{-1})\bigr]^2\epsilon^2+O(\epsilon^3).
\]
Now $L'(p)=-(2-p)/\bigl(p(1-p)\bigr)$, so
\[
A(\varphi^{-1})\bigl[L'(\varphi^{-1})\bigr]^2
=
\frac{\varphi^{-3}}{(2-\varphi^{-1})^3}\cdot
\frac{(2-\varphi^{-1})^2}{\varphi^{-6}}
=
\frac{\varphi^3}{2-\varphi^{-1}}
=
\frac{15+7\sqrt 5}{10}.
\]
The entropy deficit coefficient is $(15+7\sqrt 5)/20$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:parry-kms}. The ratio is $2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Fisher information at the exact-clock point]\label{prop:fisher}
The per-symbol Fisher information for estimating $p_\beta$ from the stationary output of the ideal dead-time detector is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fisher-general}
J(p_\beta)
=
\frac{1}{p_\beta(1-p_\beta)(2-p_\beta)}.
\end{equation}
At the Parry point,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fisher-parry}
J(\varphi^{-1})
=
\frac{15+7\sqrt 5}{10}
=
-h''(\varphi^{-1}),
\end{equation}
where $h(p)=H_2(p)/(2-p)$ is the entropy rate viewed as a function of $p$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Fisher information for one transition of a Markov chain with transition matrix $P(p)$ and stationary distribution $\pi(p)$ is $J(p)=\sum_{i,j}\pi_i\,(\partial_p P_{ij})^2/P_{ij}$. For the golden-mean chain, $\partial_p P_{00}=1$, $\partial_p P_{01}=-1$, $\partial_p P_{10}=0$, so
\[
J(p)
=
\frac{1}{2-p}\left[\frac{1}{p}+\frac{1}{1-p}\right]
=
\frac{1}{p(1-p)(2-p)}.
\]
At $p=\varphi^{-1}$, $p(1-p)(2-p)=\varphi^{-3}(2-\varphi^{-1})=(2-\varphi^{-1})/\varphi^3$, so $J=(15+7\sqrt 5)/10$ by the rationalization of Theorem~\ref{thm:parry-kms}. The identity $J=-h''$ follows from comparison with the computation of $f''(\varphi^{-1})$ in that proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:information-geometry}
Theorem~\ref{thm:variational} and Proposition~\ref{prop:fisher} show that the exact-clock point is rigid in three compatible senses: the entropy deficit is exactly the relative entropy rate to the critical law, the pressure becomes affine, and the leading term of the clock jitter is governed by the Fisher metric. In particular,
\[
\sigma_\tau^2
=
J(\varphi^{-1})\,\epsilon^2+O(\epsilon^3),
\qquad
d_{\mathrm{KL}}
=
\tfrac12\,J(\varphi^{-1})\,\epsilon^2+O(\epsilon^3):
\]
the clock jitter is the Fisher-information norm of the displacement from the Parry point, while the relative entropy carries the usual factor~$1/2$ \cite{CoverThomas2006}. The corresponding Cram\'er--Rao bound for estimating the local inverse temperature $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}$ from $n$ consecutive samples is
\[
\Var(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{loc}})
\ge
\bigl[n\,J(p_\beta)\,p_\beta^2\,(\partial_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}\Gamma_+)^2(\Delta\tau)^2\bigr]^{-1}.
\]
Under Proposition~\ref{prop:kms-response},
\[
\partial_{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}}\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}},\Omega)
=
-\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\,
\frac{\Omega e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}}{(e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\Omega}-1)^2},
\]
and in the massless $(3+1)$-dimensional scalar model of Example~\ref{ex:rindler} one has $\mathcal S(\Omega)=\Omega/(2\pi)$.
\end{remark}

\begin{assumption}[Static Killing background]\label{ass:tolman-profile}
In addition to Assumption~\ref{ass:kms-bridge}, suppose the detector follows the orbits of a hypersurface-orthogonal timelike Killing field $\xi$ in a static region. Let $t$ denote the Killing parameter and
\[
N(x):=\sqrt{-\xi^\mu\xi_\mu(x)}
\]
the lapse. Assume that the field state is a global KMS state of inverse temperature $\beta_\infty>0$ with respect to the Killing flow.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Proper-time KMS scaling and Tolman law]\label{prop:tolman-kms}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:tolman-profile}, fix a static detector orbit through $x$. If $\alpha_t$ denotes the Killing-time evolution, then the proper-time evolution along that orbit is
\[
\alpha^{(x)}_\tau:=\alpha_{\tau/N(x)}.
\]
The global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state is therefore a $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)$-KMS state for the detector proper-time evolution, where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-law}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $A,B$ be observables localized along the detector worldline. Because the state is $\beta_\infty$-KMS for the Killing flow, the function
\[
F_{A,B}(z):=\omega\bigl(A\,\alpha_z(B)\bigr)
\]
extends analytically to the strip $0<\operatorname{Im} z<\beta_\infty$ and satisfies the boundary relation
\[
F_{A,B}(t+i\beta_\infty)=\omega\bigl(\alpha_t(B)A\bigr).
\]
Define
\[
\widetilde F_{A,B}^{(x)}(\zeta):=F_{A,B}\!\left(\frac{\zeta}{N(x)}\right).
\]
Then $\widetilde F_{A,B}^{(x)}$ is analytic in the strip $0<\operatorname{Im}\zeta<\beta_\infty N(x)$ and obeys
\[
\widetilde F_{A,B}^{(x)}(\tau+i\beta_\infty N(x))
=
\omega\bigl(\alpha_{\tau/N(x)}(B)A\bigr)
=
\omega\bigl(\alpha^{(x)}_\tau(B)A\bigr).
\]
This is exactly the KMS condition for the proper-time evolution $\alpha^{(x)}_\tau$ at inverse temperature $\beta_\infty N(x)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Exact-clock hypersurface in a static KMS background]\label{thm:static-parry-locus}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:kms-bridge} and \ref{ass:tolman-profile}, define the local commutator spectral weight
\begin{equation}\label{eq:local-spectral-weight}
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)
:=
\widehat{W}_{x,\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)}(-\Omega)
-
\widehat{W}_{x,\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)}(\Omega),
\end{equation}
where the subscript $x$ indicates the pullback to the static worldline through $x$. Assume $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ on the region of interest. Then the ideal dead-time detector realizes the exact-clock law, equivalently the Parry measure, at $x$ if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:static-parry-locus}
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}
     {e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=
\log\varphi.
\end{equation}
Equivalently,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:static-parry-effective}
\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega
=
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right).
\end{equation}
Hence the exact-clock locus is the codimension-one zero set
\[
\Sigma_\varphi
=
\{x:\mathcal V_\varphi(x;\Omega)=0\},
\]
where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:effective-parry-potential}
\mathcal V_\varphi(x;\Omega)
:=
\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega
-
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right).
\end{equation}
If $\nabla\mathcal V_\varphi\neq 0$ on $\Sigma_\varphi$, then $\Sigma_\varphi$ is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:parry-kms}, the ideal dead-time detector realizes the exact-clock law exactly when
\[
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi.
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:tolman-kms}, $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x)$. By Proposition~\ref{prop:kms-response}, the local excitation rate is
\[
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)
=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)\Omega}-1}
=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}.
\]
Substituting into the exact-clock condition proves \eqref{eq:static-parry-locus}. Rearranging gives \eqref{eq:static-parry-effective}, hence the zero-set representation \eqref{eq:effective-parry-potential}. The final statement follows from the implicit-function theorem.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Critical-temperature hypersurfaces in static KMS backgrounds]\label{thm:tolman-parry}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus}, assume in addition that the local spectral weight is constant on the static region:
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv \mathcal S(\Omega)>0.
\]
Then the exact-clock locus depends only on the redshift factor. Defining the critical local inverse temperature
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-parry}
\beta_\varphi
:=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-redshift-level}
\Sigma_\varphi
=
\{x:N(x)=N_\varphi\},
\qquad
N_\varphi:=\frac{\beta_\varphi}{\beta_\infty}.
\end{equation}
In particular:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
\item \textup{(Thermal shell criterion.)} The exact-clock condition is a pure redshift condition, equivalently a fixed local-temperature condition.
\item \textup{(Smoothness.)} If $\nabla N\neq 0$ on $\Sigma_\varphi$, then $\Sigma_\varphi$ is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface.
\item \textup{(Radial uniqueness.)} If $N=N(r)$ on a static spherically symmetric region and $N'(r)$ does not change sign there, then there is at most one radius $r_\varphi$ in that region for which the detector output realizes the exact-clock law.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Under the constancy assumption, equation \eqref{eq:static-parry-effective} becomes
\[
\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega
=
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right)
=
\beta_\varphi\Omega,
\]
which is equivalent to \eqref{eq:parry-redshift-level}. The smoothness and radial-uniqueness statements follow exactly as in the proof of the corresponding statements for lapse level sets.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Calibrated exact-clock thermometry]\label{thm:calibrated-thermometry}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry}, fix a detector design
\[
D=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau)
\]
and define its calibrated critical inverse temperature by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibrated-beta}
\beta_c(D)
:=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right).
\end{equation}
Then for every point $x$ in the static region,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibrated-isotherm}
x\in\Sigma_\varphi(D)
\iff
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_c(D).
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the exact-clock hypersurface selected by the detector is the Tolman isotherm at the calibrated inverse temperature $\beta_c(D)$.

If $\{D_s\}_{s\in I}$ is a family of detector designs whose calibrated values $\beta_c(D_s)$ fill an interval $J\subset(0,\infty)$ and if $\nabla\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\neq 0$ on
\[
U_J:=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)\in J\},
\]
then the corresponding exact-clock hypersurfaces $\Sigma_\varphi(D_s)$ foliate $U_J$ by level sets of the Tolman temperature field.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:tolman-kms}, $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x)$. Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry} states that the exact-clock locus is given by
\[
\beta_\infty N(x)=\beta_\varphi,
\]
where $\beta_\varphi$ is defined by \eqref{eq:beta-parry}. For the detector design $D$, the right-hand side is exactly $\beta_c(D)$ as defined in \eqref{eq:calibrated-beta}. Hence
\[
x\in\Sigma_\varphi(D)
\iff
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_c(D),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:calibrated-isotherm}. The foliation statement follows because the family of hypersurfaces is precisely the family of level sets of $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}$ with values in $J$, and $\nabla\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}\neq 0$ excludes level-set degeneracy on $U_J$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Operational identification of Tolman profiles]\label{cor:tolman-reconstruction}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:calibrated-thermometry}. Suppose in addition that the static region is spherically symmetric and that the lapse $N(r)$ is monotone on a radial interval $I_r$. For each detector design $D$ with $\beta_c(D)\in \beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(I_r)$, let $r(D)$ denote the unique radius at which the detector realizes the exact-clock law. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-profile-reconstruction}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(r(D))=\beta_c(D).
\end{equation}
Therefore the measured pairs
\[
\bigl(r(D),\beta_c(D)\bigr)
\]
operationally identify the Tolman temperature profile on $I_r$ within the calibrated homogeneous spectral-weight regime. If $\beta_\infty$ is known, then the same data identify the lapse profile through
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-profile-reconstruction}
N(r(D))=\frac{\beta_c(D)}{\beta_\infty}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:calibrated-thermometry}, the exact-clock condition at radius $r$ is equivalent to $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)=\beta_c(D)$. Monotonicity of $N(r)$ implies monotonicity of $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)=\beta_\infty N(r)$, hence uniqueness of the solution radius $r(D)$ on $I_r$. This proves \eqref{eq:tolman-profile-reconstruction}. Equation \eqref{eq:lapse-profile-reconstruction} follows by dividing by $\beta_\infty$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral stability of the critical-temperature hypersurface]\label{thm:parry-surface-stability}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus}, fix a positive reference spectral weight $\mathcal S(\Omega)$ and let $\Sigma_\varphi^0=\{x:N(x)=N_\varphi\}$ be the corresponding homogeneous critical-temperature hypersurface from Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry}. Let $Y\subset\Sigma_\varphi^0$ be a compact patch. Assume that on a tubular neighborhood of $Y$ the local spectral weight has the form
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)
=
\mathcal S(\Omega)\bigl(1+\varepsilon\eta(x)\bigr),
\]
where $\eta$ is smooth and $|\varepsilon|$ is sufficiently small. If $\nabla N\neq 0$ on $Y$, then there exists $\varepsilon_0>0$ such that for every $|\varepsilon|<\varepsilon_0$ the exact-clock locus is represented near $Y$ by a unique smooth normal graph
\[
x_\varepsilon(y)=\exp_y\bigl(u_\varepsilon(y)n(y)\bigr),
\qquad
y\in Y,
\]
where $n=\nabla N/|\nabla N|$. Writing
\[
\chi
:=
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi},
\]
one has on $Y$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-surface-shift}
u_\varepsilon(y)
=
\frac{\varepsilon\,\chi}{\beta_\infty\Omega(1+\chi)\,|\nabla N(y)|}\,
\eta(y)
+
O(\varepsilon^2).
\end{equation}
Thus the homogeneous critical-temperature hypersurface is structurally stable, and its first-order displacement is controlled explicitly by the local spectral inhomogeneity.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define
\[
F(x,\varepsilon)
:=
\beta_\infty\Omega N(x)
-
\log\!\bigl(1+\chi(1+\varepsilon\eta(x))\bigr).
\]
By Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus}, the exact-clock locus is precisely the zero set of $F(\,\cdot\,,\varepsilon)$. At $\varepsilon=0$, the definition of $N_\varphi$ gives
\[
F(x,0)
=
\beta_\infty\Omega\bigl(N(x)-N_\varphi\bigr),
\]
so $Y\subset F^{-1}(0,0)$. Along the unit normal $n=\nabla N/|\nabla N|$ one has
\[
\partial_nF(\,\cdot\,,0)
=
\beta_\infty\Omega\,|\nabla N|
\neq
0
\]
on $Y$. The implicit-function theorem therefore yields, after shrinking the tubular neighborhood if necessary, a unique smooth normal graph $u_\varepsilon(y)$ for all sufficiently small $|\varepsilon|$.

Differentiating the identity $F(x_\varepsilon(y),\varepsilon)=0$ at $\varepsilon=0$ gives
\[
\partial_nF(y,0)\,\dot u_0(y)
+
\partial_\varepsilon F(y,0)
=
0.
\]
Since
\[
\partial_\varepsilon F(y,0)
=
-\frac{\chi}{1+\chi}\eta(y),
\qquad
\partial_nF(y,0)
=
\beta_\infty\Omega\,|\nabla N(y)|,
\]
it follows that
\[
\dot u_0(y)
=
\frac{\chi}{\beta_\infty\Omega(1+\chi)\,|\nabla N(y)|}\eta(y).
\]
Hence
\[
u_\varepsilon(y)
=
\varepsilon\,\dot u_0(y)
+
O(\varepsilon^2),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:parry-surface-shift}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Near-horizon critical shell]\label{cor:near-horizon-parry}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus}. Suppose in addition that the static region ends at a nonextremal Killing horizon of surface gravity $\kappa_{\mathrm H}$, that the field state is the Hartle--Hawking state so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$, and that along static worldlines parametrized by the proper distance $\rho$ to the horizon one has
\[
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3),
\qquad
\mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)=\mathcal S_H(\Omega)+O(\rho^2),
\qquad
\mathcal S_H(\Omega)>0.
\]
Then the exact-clock shell satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:near-horizon-parry}
\rho_\varphi
=
\frac{1}{2\pi\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right)
+O(\rho_\varphi^2).
\end{equation}
If, moreover,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-spectral-weight}
\mathcal S_H(\Omega)=\frac{\Omega}{2\pi},
\end{equation}
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:near-horizon-parry-planck}
\rho_\varphi
=
\frac{1}{2\pi\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega\,\Delta\tau}{2\pi\log\varphi}
\right)
+O(\rho_\varphi^2).
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus},
\[
\beta_\infty N(\rho)\Omega
=
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right).
\]
Using $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and the horizon expansion of $N(\rho)$ gives
\[
2\pi\Omega\,\rho+O(\rho^3)
=
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right)
+O(\rho^2),
\]
which implies \eqref{eq:near-horizon-parry}. Equation \eqref{eq:near-horizon-parry-planck} follows by substituting \eqref{eq:horizon-spectral-weight}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Finite recovery and the memory-transition shell}

The exact-clock shell is an ideal dead-time phenomenon. Finite recovery does not merely perturb it: it forbids exact symbolic thermality and simultaneously produces a distinct correlation-based shell. To avoid conflict with horizon surface gravity, we write $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ for the recovery rate throughout the remainder of this section.

\begin{theorem}[Finite-recovery obstruction to exact symbolic thermality]\label{thm:finite-recovery-obstruction}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:kms-bridge} and \ref{ass:tolman-profile}, fix a finite recovery rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$ and a sampling step $\Delta\tau>0$. For each static point $x$, let
\[
\Gamma_x:=\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega),
\]
let $g^{(x)}$ be the finite-recovery gap law from Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-recovery-renewal} with $\Gamma=\Gamma_x$, and let
\[
\rho_{\mathrm{fr}}(x),
\qquad
h_{\mathrm{fr}}(x),
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{fr}}^2(x)
\]
denote the associated click density, entropy rate, and regenerative jitter. Define the geometric law at the same density by
\[
u_k^{(\rho_{\mathrm{fr}}(x))}
:=
\rho_{\mathrm{fr}}(x)\bigl(1-\rho_{\mathrm{fr}}(x)\bigr)^k,
\qquad
k\ge 0.
\]
Then for every $x$ in the static region,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:finite-recovery-static-obstruction}
g^{(x)}\neq u^{(\rho_{\mathrm{fr}}(x))},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{fr}}^2(x)>0,
\qquad
H_2\!\bigl(\rho_{\mathrm{fr}}(x)\bigr)-h_{\mathrm{fr}}(x)>0.
\end{equation}
Hence no finite-recovery stationary detector realizes exact symbolic thermality in the full-support renewal class: zero regenerative jitter and the Bernoulli entropy benchmark are excluded throughout the static region.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each fixed $x$, the local excitation rate $\Gamma_x>0$ determines a finite-recovery renewal law through Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-recovery-renewal}. Corollary~\ref{cor:finite-recovery-hazard} shows that its hazard is strictly increasing, so the local gap law $g^{(x)}$ cannot equal any geometric law, in particular not $u^{(\rho_{\mathrm{fr}}(x))}$. Corollary~\ref{cor:finite-recovery-jitter-barrier} then applies pointwise and gives the strict inequalities in \eqref{eq:finite-recovery-static-obstruction}. These statements hold for every static point $x$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Memory-transition hypersurface]\label{thm:memory-surface}
Under Assumptions~\ref{ass:kms-bridge} and \ref{ass:tolman-profile}, fix a finite recovery rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$ and a sampling step $\Delta\tau>0$. Define
\[
\Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r})
:=
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
\qquad
(\Gamma\neq\kappa_{\mathrm r}),
\]
with continuous extension $\Delta_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\kappa_{\mathrm r})=1/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. Then the map $\Gamma\mapsto \Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ is continuous and strictly decreasing from $(0,\infty)$ onto $(0,\infty)$. Hence there exists a unique number $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>0$ such that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:memory-critical-rate}
\Delta\tau=\Delta_c\bigl(\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),\kappa_{\mathrm r}\bigr).
\end{equation}

Define the memory-transition potential
\begin{equation}\label{eq:memory-potential}
\mathcal V_{\mathrm{mem}}(x;\Omega)
:=
\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega
-
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right),
\end{equation}
and the corresponding locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:memory-surface}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}
:=
\{x:\mathcal V_{\mathrm{mem}}(x;\Omega)=0\}
=
\{x:\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)\}.
\end{equation}
Then $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ is the finite-recovery memory-transition hypersurface. Writing $(A_n^{(x)})_{n\in\mathbb Z}$ for the stationary finite-recovery output process at $x$, one has:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
\item If $\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)<\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, then
\[
\operatorname{Cov}\!\bigl(A_0^{(x)},A_n^{(x)}\bigr)<0
\qquad
\text{for every }n\ge 1.
\]
\item If $x\in\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, then the process is exact $1$-dependent:
\[
\sigma\!\bigl(A_i^{(x)}:i\le 0\bigr)\ \perp\ \sigma\!\bigl(A_j^{(x)}:j\ge 2\bigr).
\]
\item If $\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, then
\[
(-1)^{n-1}\operatorname{Cov}\!\bigl(A_0^{(x)},A_n^{(x)}\bigr)<0
\qquad
\text{for every }n\ge 1.
\]
\end{enumerate}
If $\nabla\mathcal V_{\mathrm{mem}}\neq 0$ on $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, then $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime,
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv \mathcal S(\Omega)>0,
\]
it reduces to the lapse level set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:memory-lapse-level}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}
=
\{x:N(x)=N_{\mathrm{mem}}\},
\qquad
N_{\mathrm{mem}}
:=
\frac{1}{\beta_\infty\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set $r:=\Gamma/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. Then
\[
\Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r})
=
\frac{1}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}}\,\frac{\log r}{r-1}.
\]
The derivative of $f(r):=\log r/(r-1)$ is
\[
f'(r)
=
\frac{1-1/r-\log r}{(r-1)^2}.
\]
The numerator vanishes at $r=1$ and is strictly negative for $r\neq 1$, because the function $g(r):=1-1/r-\log r$ has derivative $g'(r)=(1-r)/r^2$ and a strict maximum $g(1)=0$. Hence $f$ is strictly decreasing on $(0,\infty)$, so $\Gamma\mapsto \Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ is continuous and strictly decreasing. Its limits are
\[
\Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r})\to\infty
\quad
(\Gamma\downarrow 0),
\qquad
\Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r})\to 0
\quad
(\Gamma\uparrow\infty),
\]
which proves existence and uniqueness of $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.

By Proposition~\ref{prop:tolman-kms} and Proposition~\ref{prop:kms-response},
\[
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)
=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}.
\]
Solving the equation $\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ for $\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega$ gives \eqref{eq:memory-potential} and \eqref{eq:memory-surface}. The smoothness statement follows from the implicit-function theorem.

For each fixed $x$, Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-recovery-correlation} applies with $\Gamma=\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)$. Because $\Gamma\mapsto\Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ is strictly decreasing, the inequalities
\[
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)<\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\qquad
\Gamma_+(\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x),\Omega)>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\]
are equivalent to
\[
\Delta\tau<\Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r}),
\qquad
\Delta\tau>\Delta_c(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r}),
\]
respectively. The sign claims therefore follow from Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-recovery-correlation}, while exact $1$-dependence on $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ is Corollary~\ref{cor:finite-recovery-1dependent}. If the spectral weight is homogeneous, the rate equation rearranges directly to \eqref{eq:memory-lapse-level}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Finite-record detection of the memory shell]\label{rem:memory-shell-detection}
Operationally, the memory-transition shell is visible through the vanishing of all covariances beyond lag one, together with the sign pattern on either side of the shell. For a finite binary record $A_0,\dots,A_{N-1}$, the natural estimators are the empirical click density
\[
\widehat\rho_N:=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=0}^{N-1}A_t
\]
and the lag-$n$ sample covariances
\[
\widehat\gamma_n
:=
\frac{1}{N-n}\sum_{t=0}^{N-n-1}(A_t-\widehat\rho_N)(A_{t+n}-\widehat\rho_N),
\qquad n=1,2,3,\dots.
\]
A practical decision rule is immediate from Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}: on the colder side one expects $\widehat\gamma_2<0$ and $\widehat\gamma_3<0$, on the hotter side one expects $\widehat\gamma_2>0$ and $\widehat\gamma_3<0$, while near the shell one expects $\widehat\gamma_n\approx 0$ for every $n\ge 2$ with $\widehat\gamma_1<0$ still separated from zero. At fixed lag the standard errors scale heuristically like $N^{-1/2}$, so the transition is easiest to resolve away from the critical shell or by combining several low-lag covariances with the low-frequency spectral slope from Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-recovery-spectrum}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[What survives beyond homogeneous spectral weight]\label{rem:beyond-homogeneous-spectral-weight}
The exact-clock and memory shells do not require the homogeneous spectral-weight regime in order to exist. In general they are the zero sets of the potentials from Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus} and Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}. Homogeneous spectral weight is used only to collapse those shell equations to pure lapse level sets and thereby obtain the simple shell-order and calibration-light ratio laws. Small spatial variations in $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ therefore deform the shell locations rather than destroy them. What fails outside that regime is not the existence of detector-defined shells, but the exact reduction to Tolman isotherms and the exact cancellation of the common amplitude in Theorem~\ref{thm:calibration-free-ratio}.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Universal shell-order law]\label{thm:shell-order}
Under the homogeneous spectral-weight assumptions of Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry} and Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}, let
\[
\Gamma_\varphi:=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau},
\]
and let $N_\varphi$ and $N_{\mathrm{mem}}$ denote the lapse values of the exact-clock and memory-transition hypersurfaces, respectively. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-order-less}
N_\varphi<N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_\varphi>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-order-equal}
N_\varphi=N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_\varphi=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-order-greater}
N_\varphi>N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_\varphi<\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\end{equation}
Hence the relative ordering of the exact-clock shell and the memory-transition shell is detector-controlled; the background geometry supplies only the lapse foliation on which the two selectors are placed.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime, the local excitation rate is
\[
\Gamma_+(N)
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N\Omega}-1},
\]
which is a strictly decreasing function of $N$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry},
\[
\Gamma_+(N_\varphi)=\Gamma_\varphi,
\]
and by Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface},
\[
\Gamma_+(N_{\mathrm{mem}})=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\]
Strict monotonicity of $\Gamma_+(N)$ makes the order of the lapse values exactly opposite to the order of the corresponding rates, proving \eqref{eq:shell-order-less}--\eqref{eq:shell-order-greater}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Inverse shell geometry and near-horizon universality}

The two shells admit a second reading. Besides selecting distinguished observers on a fixed background, they determine universal near-horizon scales and, in the Schwarzschild case, support an exact inverse problem.

\begin{theorem}[Fast-sampling separation of the two shells]\label{thm:fast-sampling-shell-separation}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}, define
\[
s:=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau,
\qquad
\Gamma_\varphi:=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}.
\]
As $s\downarrow 0$, the finite-recovery critical rate satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-gammac}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\qquad
L:=\log\frac{1}{s}.
\end{equation}
In particular,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-ratio}
\frac{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\Gamma_\varphi}
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\log\varphi}
\longrightarrow \infty.
\end{equation}
Hence, in the homogeneous spectral-weight regime of Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry}, one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-hotter}
N_{\mathrm{mem}}<N_\varphi
\end{equation}
for all sufficiently small $s$. If the lapse is monotone toward a nonextremal horizon, the memory-transition shell therefore lies on the hotter side of the exact-clock shell, hence closer to the horizon.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
x:=\frac{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}}.
\]
Then \eqref{eq:memory-critical-rate} is equivalent to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-critical}
s=\frac{\log x}{x-1}.
\end{equation}
As $s\downarrow 0$, the right-hand side can tend to $0$ only if $x\to\infty$. Rewriting \eqref{eq:fast-sampling-critical} gives
\[
sx=\log x+s.
\]
Let
\[
L:=\log\frac{1}{s},
\qquad
x=\frac{L+u}{s}.
\]
Substitution yields
\[
L+u-s
=
\log(L+u)-\log s
=
L+\log(L+u),
\]
hence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-u}
u=\log(L+u)+s.
\end{equation}
This implies $u=O(\log L)$, so $u/L\to 0$. Therefore \eqref{eq:fast-sampling-u} gives
\[
u=\log L+\log\!\left(1+\frac{u}{L}\right)+s=\log L+o(1).
\]
Thus
\[
x=\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{s},
\]
and multiplying by $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=s/\Delta\tau$ proves \eqref{eq:fast-sampling-gammac}. Dividing by $\Gamma_\varphi=\log\varphi/\Delta\tau$ gives \eqref{eq:fast-sampling-ratio}. For sufficiently small $s$, one has $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>\Gamma_\varphi$, so Theorem~\ref{thm:shell-order} yields $N_{\mathrm{mem}}<N_\varphi$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Near-horizon universality of the two shells]\label{thm:two-shell-near-horizon}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface} and suppose in addition that the static region ends at a nonextremal Killing horizon of surface gravity $\kappa_{\mathrm H}$, that the field state is Hartle--Hawking so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$, and that along static worldlines parametrized by proper distance $\rho$ to the horizon one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-horizon-data}
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3),
\qquad
\mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)=\mathcal S_H(\Omega)+O(\rho^2),
\qquad
\mathcal S_H(\Omega)>0.
\end{equation}
Then the exact-clock and memory-transition shells satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-rho-phi}
\rho_\varphi
=
\frac{1}{2\pi\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right)
+O(\rho_\varphi^2),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-rho-mem}
\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}
=
\frac{1}{2\pi\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right)
+O(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^2).
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-ratio}
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\rho_\varphi}
=
\frac{
\log\!\left(1+\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)/\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)\right)
}{
\log\!\left(1+\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau/\log\varphi\right)
}
+o(1).
\end{equation}

If, moreover, the horizon spectral weight is Planckian,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-planckian}
\mathcal S_H(\Omega)=\frac{\Omega}{2\pi},
\end{equation}
then in the fast-sampling regime $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\downarrow 0$ one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-rho-phi-fast}
\rho_\varphi
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2}{4\pi^2\log\varphi}\,\Delta\tau
+O((\Delta\tau)^2),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-rho-mem-fast}
\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2}{4\pi^2}\,
\frac{\Delta\tau}{L+\log L}\,
\bigl(1+o(1)\bigr),
\qquad
L=\log\frac{1}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-rho-ratio-fast}
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\rho_\varphi}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow 0.
\end{equation}

In Schwarzschild, if $A_H=16\pi M^2$ is the horizon area and $A_i$ denotes the area of the sphere at $\rho_i$ for $i\in\{\varphi,\mathrm{mem}\}$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-shell-area-general}
A_i-A_H
=
2\pi\rho_i^2+O(M^{-2}\rho_i^4).
\end{equation}
Hence the leading horizon-area excess of both shells is detector-controlled and independent of the black-hole mass.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Equation \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-phi} is exactly Corollary~\ref{cor:near-horizon-parry}. For the memory shell, Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface} gives
\[
\beta_\infty N(\rho)\Omega
=
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right)
\]
on $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Using \eqref{eq:two-shell-horizon-data} and $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ yields
\[
2\pi\Omega\,\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}+O(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^3)
=
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right)
+O(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^2),
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-mem}. The ratio formula \eqref{eq:two-shell-ratio} follows immediately.

If \eqref{eq:two-shell-planckian} holds, then \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-phi} gives
\[
\rho_\varphi
=
\frac{1}{2\pi\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega\,\Delta\tau}{2\pi\log\varphi}
\right)
+O(\rho_\varphi^2)
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2}{4\pi^2\log\varphi}\,\Delta\tau+O((\Delta\tau)^2),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-phi-fast}. Likewise,
\[
\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}
=
\frac{1}{2\pi\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega}{2\pi\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right)
+O(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^2).
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:fast-sampling-shell-separation} gives
\[
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\qquad
L=\log\frac{1}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\]
so the logarithm linearizes and yields \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-mem-fast}. Equation \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-ratio-fast} follows by dividing by \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-phi-fast}.

For Schwarzschild, write $r=2M+\delta r$. Standard near-horizon expansions give
\[
\rho^2=8M\,\delta r+O(\delta r^2),
\qquad
A(r)=4\pi r^2=16\pi M^2+16\pi M\,\delta r+O(\delta r^2).
\]
Eliminating $\delta r$ yields
\[
A(r)-16\pi M^2
=
2\pi\rho^2+O(M^{-2}\rho^4),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:two-shell-area-general}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Near-horizon shell tomography of the horizon spectral weight]\label{cor:horizon-spectral-tomography}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:two-shell-near-horizon},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-spectral-from-phi}
\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)
=
\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}
\bigl(e^{2\pi\Omega\rho_\varphi}-1\bigr)
+O(\rho_\varphi^2),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-spectral-from-mem}
\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)
=
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\bigl(e^{2\pi\Omega\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}-1\bigr)
+O(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^2).
\end{equation}
If, moreover, \eqref{eq:two-shell-planckian} holds and $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\downarrow 0$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-shell-collapse-phi}
\frac{\rho_\varphi}{\Delta\tau}
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2}{4\pi^2\log\varphi}
+O(\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-shell-collapse-mem}
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(L+\log L)}{\Delta\tau}
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2}{4\pi^2}\bigl(1+o(1)\bigr),
\qquad
L=\log\frac{1}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}.
\end{equation}
Hence near the horizon the shell distances provide leading-order algebraic readouts of the horizon spectral weight, and in the Planckian fast-sampling regime the rescaled shell family collapses onto hardware-controlled lines.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Exponentiating \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-phi} gives
\[
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_H(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
=
e^{2\pi\Omega\rho_\varphi+O(\rho_\varphi^2)}
=
e^{2\pi\Omega\rho_\varphi}+O(\rho_\varphi^2),
\]
which yields \eqref{eq:horizon-spectral-from-phi}. The derivation of \eqref{eq:horizon-spectral-from-mem} from \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-mem} is identical. Equations \eqref{eq:horizon-shell-collapse-phi} and \eqref{eq:horizon-shell-collapse-mem} are immediate restatements of \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-phi-fast} and \eqref{eq:two-shell-rho-mem-fast}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Dual-shell inversion in Schwarzschild]\label{thm:schwarzschild-inversion}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry} and Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}. For a detector design
\[
D=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r}),
\]
define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse-beta-phi}
\beta_\varphi(D)
:=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\right),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse-beta-mem}
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)
:=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right).
\end{equation}
If the exact-clock and memory-transition shells for the same design $D$ occur at radii $r_\varphi$ and $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in a static spherically symmetric background, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse-general-shells}
\beta_\infty N(r_\varphi)=\beta_\varphi(D),
\qquad
\beta_\infty N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D).
\end{equation}

If the background is Schwarzschild, $N(r)=\sqrt{1-2M/r}$, and $r_\varphi\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$, then the shell pair uniquely determines both $\beta_\infty$ and $M$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse-beta-infty}
\beta_\infty^2
=
\frac{\beta_\varphi(D)^2\,r_\varphi-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\,r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inverse-mass}
M
=
\frac{
r_\varphi r_{\mathrm{mem}}
\bigl(\beta_\varphi(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)
}{
2\bigl(\beta_\varphi(D)^2\,r_\varphi-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\,r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)
}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first statement is just the combination of Theorem~\ref{thm:calibrated-thermometry} with the homogeneous spectral-weight specialization of Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}. In Schwarzschild,
\[
\beta_\varphi(D)^2
=
\beta_\infty^2\left(1-\frac{2M}{r_\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2
=
\beta_\infty^2\left(1-\frac{2M}{r_{\mathrm{mem}}}\right).
\]
Set
\[
A:=\beta_\infty^2,
\qquad
B:=2M\beta_\infty^2.
\]
Then
\[
\beta_\varphi(D)^2=A-\frac{B}{r_\varphi},
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2=A-\frac{B}{r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\]
which is a linear system in $(A,B)$. Solving gives
\[
A
=
\frac{\beta_\varphi(D)^2\,r_\varphi-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\,r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\]
\[
B
=
\frac{
r_\varphi r_{\mathrm{mem}}
\bigl(\beta_\varphi(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)
}
{r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}}}.
\]
Since $M=B/(2A)$, equations \eqref{eq:inverse-beta-infty} and \eqref{eq:inverse-mass} follow.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Status of the Schwarzschild inversion]\label{rem:schwarzschild-inversion-status}
Theorem~\ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion} is an exact algebraic inversion within the homogeneous-spectral-weight detector response model. In the explicit black-hole application of Example~\ref{ex:schwarzschild}, that model is further specialized to the geometric-optics static-detector regime. Accordingly, the theorem should be read as a detector-dependent inverse statement inside a fixed response class, not as detector-independent metric reconstruction.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Explicit Schwarzschild shell radii and proper distances]\label{cor:schwarzschild-shell-formulas}
Under the Schwarzschild hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion}, define
\[
\eta_\varphi:=\frac{\beta_\varphi(D)}{\beta_\infty},
\qquad
\eta_{\mathrm{mem}}:=\frac{\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)}{\beta_\infty}.
\]
If $0<\eta_\varphi,\eta_{\mathrm{mem}}<1$, then the shell radii are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-shell-radii-explicit}
r_\varphi=\frac{2M}{1-\eta_\varphi^2},
\qquad
r_{\mathrm{mem}}=\frac{2M}{1-\eta_{\mathrm{mem}}^2}.
\end{equation}
If $\rho_i$ denotes the proper distance from the horizon to the shell at radius $r_i$ for $i\in\{\varphi,\mathrm{mem}\}$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-shell-distance-exact}
\rho_i
=
\frac{2M\eta_i}{1-\eta_i^2}
+
M\log\frac{1+\eta_i}{1-\eta_i}.
\end{equation}
In particular, in the Hartle--Hawking case $\beta_\infty=8\pi M$ one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-shell-distance-hh}
\rho_i
=
\frac{\beta_i(D)}{2\pi}
+
O\!\left(\frac{\beta_i(D)^3}{M^2}\right),
\qquad
r_i-2M
=
\frac{\beta_i(D)^2}{8\pi^2 M}
+
O\!\left(\frac{\beta_i(D)^4}{M^3}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\beta_i(D)$ stands for $\beta_\varphi(D)$ or $\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The radius formulas are just the identities
\[
\sqrt{1-\frac{2M}{r_i}}=\eta_i
\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
r_i=\frac{2M}{1-\eta_i^2}.
\]
For Schwarzschild,
\[
\rho(r)
=
\int_{2M}^{r}\frac{dr'}{\sqrt{1-2M/r'}}
=
\sqrt{r(r-2M)}
+
2M\log\frac{\sqrt r+\sqrt{r-2M}}{\sqrt{2M}}.
\]
Substituting \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-shell-radii-explicit} gives
\[
\sqrt{r_i(r_i-2M)}=\frac{2M\eta_i}{1-\eta_i^2},
\qquad
2M\log\frac{\sqrt{r_i}+\sqrt{r_i-2M}}{\sqrt{2M}}
=
M\log\frac{1+\eta_i}{1-\eta_i},
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-shell-distance-exact}. In the Hartle--Hawking case, $\eta_i=\beta_i(D)/(8\pi M)$, so the stated expansions follow by Taylor expansion at $\eta_i=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Sensitivity of Schwarzschild shell locations to detector thresholds]\label{cor:schwarzschild-threshold-sensitivity}
Under the hypotheses of Corollary~\ref{cor:schwarzschild-shell-formulas}, fix the background parameters $(\beta_\infty,M)$ and write
\[
\Gamma_c:=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\]
For $i\in\{\varphi,\mathrm{mem}\}$, the first-order perturbations of the shell radius and proper distance induced by a perturbation of the effective threshold $\beta_i(D)$ are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-shell-radius-sensitivity}
\frac{\delta r_i}{r_i}
:=
\frac{2\eta_i}{1-\eta_i^2}\,\delta\eta_i
:=
\frac{2\eta_i}{\beta_\infty(1-\eta_i^2)}\,\delta\beta_i(D),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-shell-distance-sensitivity}
\delta\rho_i
:=
\frac{4M}{(1-\eta_i^2)^2}\,\delta\eta_i
:=
\frac{4M}{\beta_\infty(1-\eta_i^2)^2}\,\delta\beta_i(D).
\end{equation}
If $A:=\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-phi-sensitivity}
\delta\beta_\varphi(D)
:=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\delta A+A\,\delta(\Delta\tau)}
     {\Omega\bigl(\log\varphi+A\Delta\tau\bigr)},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-mem-sensitivity}
\delta\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)
:=
\frac{\delta A}{\Omega(A+\Gamma_c)}
-
\frac{A}{\Omega\Gamma_c(A+\Gamma_c)}\,\delta\Gamma_c,
\end{equation}
with
\[
\delta\Gamma_c
:=
\partial_{\kappa_{\mathrm r}}\Gamma_c\,\delta\kappa_{\mathrm r}
+
\partial_{\Delta\tau}\Gamma_c\,\delta(\Delta\tau).
\]
Hence near-horizon shells with $\eta_i\ll 1$ are first-order stable in areal radius, while the memory-transition shell inherits additional sensitivity through the critical-rate map $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-shell-radii-explicit} and \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-shell-distance-exact} at fixed $(\beta_\infty,M)$. This gives \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-shell-radius-sensitivity} and \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-shell-distance-sensitivity}. Equations \eqref{eq:beta-phi-sensitivity} and \eqref{eq:beta-mem-sensitivity} follow by differentiating \eqref{eq:inverse-beta-phi} and \eqref{eq:inverse-beta-mem}. The final statement is immediate from the displayed first-order formulas.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Conditioning of Schwarzschild shell inversion]\label{prop:schwarzschild-conditioning}
Under the Schwarzschild hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion}, set
\[
c_\varphi:=\beta_\varphi(D)^2,
\qquad
c_{\mathrm{mem}}:=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2,
\qquad
X:=c_\varphi r_\varphi-c_{\mathrm{mem}}r_{\mathrm{mem}},
\qquad
\Delta r:=r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}}.
\]
Then the Jacobian of the forward map $(\beta_\infty,M)\mapsto(r_\varphi,r_{\mathrm{mem}})$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-jacobian}
\det\frac{\partial(r_\varphi,r_{\mathrm{mem}})}{\partial(\beta_\infty,M)}
=
\frac{
8M\beta_\infty^5\bigl(c_\varphi-c_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)
}{
\bigl(\beta_\infty^2-c_\varphi\bigr)^2
\bigl(\beta_\infty^2-c_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)^2
}.
\end{equation}
In particular, the shell map is locally identifiable whenever $c_\varphi\neq c_{\mathrm{mem}}$.

Moreover, the first-order perturbations of the inverse formulas are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-dbeta-linear}
\delta(\beta_\infty^2)
=
\frac{r_{\mathrm{mem}}(c_{\mathrm{mem}}-c_\varphi)}{(\Delta r)^2}\,\delta r_\varphi
+
\frac{r_\varphi(c_\varphi-c_{\mathrm{mem}})}{(\Delta r)^2}\,\delta r_{\mathrm{mem}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-dmass-linear}
\delta M
=
-\frac{(c_\varphi-c_{\mathrm{mem}})c_{\mathrm{mem}}r_{\mathrm{mem}}^2}{2X^2}\,\delta r_\varphi
+
\frac{(c_\varphi-c_{\mathrm{mem}})c_\varphi r_\varphi^2}{2X^2}\,\delta r_{\mathrm{mem}}.
\end{equation}
Hence the inversion is locally stable provided both $\Delta r$ and $X$ stay bounded away from zero, and it becomes ill-conditioned as the two shells approach degeneracy.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Writing
\[
r_i=\frac{2M\beta_\infty^2}{\beta_\infty^2-c_i},
\qquad i\in\{\varphi,\mathrm{mem}\},
\]
one finds
\[
\frac{\partial r_i}{\partial M}
=
\frac{2\beta_\infty^2}{\beta_\infty^2-c_i},
\qquad
\frac{\partial r_i}{\partial\beta_\infty}
=
-\frac{4M\beta_\infty c_i}{(\beta_\infty^2-c_i)^2}.
\]
Substituting these derivatives gives \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-jacobian}. Since $M>0$ and $\beta_\infty>0$, the determinant vanishes exactly when $c_\varphi=c_{\mathrm{mem}}$.

For the inverse map, differentiate \eqref{eq:inverse-beta-infty} and \eqref{eq:inverse-mass}. Direct quotient-rule algebra yields \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-dbeta-linear} and \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-dmass-linear}. The conditioning statement follows immediately from these first-order formulas.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Hartle--Hawking consistency law for a Schwarzschild shell pair]\label{cor:schwarzschild-hh-consistency}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion}, assume in addition that the Schwarzschild background is Hartle--Hawking, so that
\[
\beta_\infty=8\pi M.
\]
Define
\[
X:=\beta_\varphi(D)^2\,r_\varphi-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\,r_{\mathrm{mem}},
\qquad
\Delta r:=r_\varphi-r_{\mathrm{mem}},
\]
\[
Y:=r_\varphi r_{\mathrm{mem}}
\bigl(\beta_\varphi(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr).
\]
Then the shell data satisfy the mass-independent constraint
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hh-consistency}
X^3=16\pi^2\,\Delta r\,Y^2.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
With the notation of the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion}, one has
\[
A=\beta_\infty^2=\frac{X}{\Delta r},
\qquad
B=2M\beta_\infty^2=\frac{Y}{\Delta r}.
\]
The Hartle--Hawking relation $\beta_\infty=8\pi M$ implies
\[
B^2=(2M\beta_\infty^2)^2=4M^2\beta_\infty^4=\frac{\beta_\infty^6}{16\pi^2}=\frac{A^3}{16\pi^2}.
\]
Substituting $A=X/\Delta r$ and $B=Y/\Delta r$ gives \eqref{eq:hh-consistency}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Dual-shell ratio law with exact common-amplitude cancellation]\label{thm:calibration-free-ratio}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry} and Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}. Fix a detector design
\[
D=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r}),
\]
and suppose its exact-clock and memory-transition shells occur at radii $r_\varphi$ and $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in a static spherically symmetric background with lapse $N(r)$ and global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration-free-ratio}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
In particular, the shell ratio law is independent of the unknown prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Under the homogeneous spectral-weight assumptions,
\[
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi)}-1
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
\]
by Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry}, while Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface} gives
\[
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S(\Omega)}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}.
\]
Dividing the two identities proves \eqref{eq:calibration-free-ratio}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[What cancels in the shell-ratio law]\label{rem:ratio-law-parameters}
The cancellation in Theorem~\ref{thm:calibration-free-ratio} is exact but specific. The unknown overall amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels because it multiplies both shell conditions in the same way. What remains is the detector dependence encoded in the gap $\Omega$ and in the sampling/recovery parameters $(\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ through $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. Thus the ratio law removes the common calibration amplitude but does not eliminate detector design entirely. If switching effects or spectral inhomogeneities change the two shell equations by more than a common multiplicative factor, this exact cancellation is lost.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[General shell-pair spectral-transport identity]\label{cor:spectral-transport-identity}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus} and Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}, fix a detector design
\[
D=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r}),
\]
and suppose $x_\varphi(D)$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)$ are points on the exact-clock and memory-transition shells for that design. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-transport-identity}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_\varphi)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}\,
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}
Hence the homogeneous shell-ratio law of Theorem~\ref{thm:calibration-free-ratio} is the special case in which the local spectral weights agree at the two shell points.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
At $x_\varphi$, Theorem~\ref{thm:static-parry-locus} gives
\[
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)\,\Delta\tau}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_\varphi)}-1}
=
\log\varphi.
\]
At $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$, Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface} gives
\[
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\]
Dividing the two relations proves \eqref{eq:spectral-transport-identity}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Ratio-law anomalies as spectral-transport diagnostics]\label{rem:spectral-transport-diagnostic}
For a shell pair generated by one detector design, define
\[
R_D
:=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_\varphi)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1},
\qquad
C_D
:=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\]
Then Corollary~\ref{cor:spectral-transport-identity} states that
\[
\frac{R_D}{C_D}
=
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}.
\]
Thus failure of the homogeneous ratio law is not an unstructured residual: it measures spectral transport between the two selected shell observers. If the static region is parametrized by a radial coordinate $\rho$ and $\rho\mapsto \mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)$ is positive and $C^1$, the mean-value theorem yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-gradient-identity}
\log\frac{R_D}{C_D}
=
\bigl(\rho_\varphi-\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)
\partial_\rho\log\mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)\big|_{\rho=\xi_D}
\end{equation}
for some intermediate point $\xi_D$. Under the near-horizon hypothesis \eqref{eq:two-shell-horizon-data}, if moreover
\[
\mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)
=
\mathcal S_H(\Omega)+\frac12 \mathcal S_H''(\Omega)\rho^2+O(\rho^4),
\]
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-curvature-identity}
\log\frac{R_D}{C_D}
=
\frac{\mathcal S_H''(\Omega)}{2\mathcal S_H(\Omega)}
\bigl(\rho_\varphi^2-\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^2\bigr)
+O(\rho_\varphi^4+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^4).
\end{equation}
The leading near-horizon violation of the homogeneous ratio law therefore probes the normal curvature of the local spectral weight rather than a linear slope.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Two-mode local shell inversion with shared hardware]\label{thm:self-calibrating-two-mode}
Let $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ parametrize a $C^1$ family of static spherically symmetric backgrounds, with parameter $\theta\in\Theta$ determining both the global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty(\theta)$ and the lapse $N(r;\theta)$. Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime at fixed gap $\Omega$ and fixed hardware prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.

Fix one detector hardware design $(\Omega,\lambda_c)$ and operate it in two modes
\[
D_m=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m}),
\qquad
m=1,2,
\]
with corresponding observed shell radii
\[
r_{\varphi,m},
\qquad
r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}.
\]
Define the known mode constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-constants}
C_m
:=
\frac{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}{\log\varphi},
\qquad
m=1,2,
\end{equation}
and the shell-ratio functions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-functions}
F_m(\theta)
:=
\frac{
e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1
}{
e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1
}
-C_m.
\end{equation}
If $\theta_*\in\Theta$ satisfies
\[
F_1(\theta_*)=F_2(\theta_*)=0
\]
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian}
\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq 0,
\end{equation}
then the two shell pairs determine $\theta$ locally uniquely near $\theta_*$. In particular, the reconstruction is self-calibrating: no prior knowledge of $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is required.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each operating mode $D_m$, Theorem~\ref{thm:calibration-free-ratio} gives
\[
F_m(\theta)=0
\]
at the true background parameter. Thus the shell data define a smooth map
\[
F:=(F_1,F_2):\Theta\to\RR^2
\]
whose zero set contains the physical solution. Condition \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian} is exactly the hypothesis of the inverse-function theorem, so $F$ is locally invertible near $\theta_*$. Hence the shell data determine $\theta$ uniquely in a neighborhood of $\theta_*$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Self-calibrating Schwarzschild inversion]\label{cor:self-calibrating-schwarzschild}
In the geometric-optics static-detector regime of Example~\ref{ex:schwarzschild}, let the background parameters be
\[
\theta=(\beta_\infty,M),
\qquad
N(r;\theta)=\sqrt{1-\frac{2M}{r}}.
\]
For two operating modes $D_1,D_2$ of the same detector hardware, define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-schwarzschild-functions}
F_m(\beta_\infty,M)
:=
\frac{
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega\sqrt{1-2M/r_{\varphi,m}}}-1
}{
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega\sqrt{1-2M/r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}}}-1
}
-C_m,
\qquad
m=1,2,
\end{equation}
with $C_m$ from \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-constants}. If
\[
F_1(\beta_\infty,M)=F_2(\beta_\infty,M)=0
\]
and
\[
\det D(F_1,F_2)(\beta_\infty,M)\neq 0,
\]
then the two shell pairs determine $(\beta_\infty,M)$ locally without prior calibration.

If, in addition, the state is Hartle--Hawking so that $\beta_\infty=8\pi M$, then a single shell pair from one operating mode determines $M$ locally at every point where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-hh-derivative}
\frac{d}{dM}
\left[
\frac{
e^{8\pi M\Omega\sqrt{1-2M/r_\varphi}}-1
}{
e^{8\pi M\Omega\sqrt{1-2M/r_{\mathrm{mem}}}}-1
}
\right]
\neq 0.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
This is Theorem~\ref{thm:self-calibrating-two-mode} specialized to the two-parameter Schwarzschild family. In the Hartle--Hawking subfamily, the unknown parameter space is one-dimensional, so the one-variable inverse-function theorem applied to the shell-ratio function yields the final statement.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:inverse-hierarchy}
Theorem~\ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion} gives a closed-form inversion in the simplest black-hole family, but the broader inverse content is already carried by Theorem~\ref{thm:calibration-free-ratio} and Theorem~\ref{thm:self-calibrating-two-mode}. In the homogeneous regime, once two operating modes determine the background parameter $\theta$, any third mode supplies an internal consistency condition $F_3(\theta)=0$ for the shared response model; outside that regime, Corollary~\ref{cor:spectral-transport-identity} identifies the corresponding residual with spectral transport rather than unstructured error. Schwarzschild is therefore the first algebraic specialization of a more general shell-geometry inverse principle, always within the fixed detector-response model.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Examples}

\begin{example}[Rindler space realization]\label{ex:rindler}
For a uniformly accelerated detector in the Minkowski vacuum, the field state restricted to the Rindler wedge is a KMS state at inverse temperature $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}=2\pi/a$, where $a$ is the proper acceleration \cite{Unruh1976,BirDav1982,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008}. The excitation rate of an Unruh--DeWitt detector with gap $\Omega$ coupled to a massless scalar field in $(3\!+\!1)$ dimensions is \cite{Takagi1986,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008} (see \cite{HodgkinsonLouko2012} for higher-dimensional generalizations)
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rindler-excitation}
\Gamma_+(\Omega,a)
=
\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega}{2\pi}\,
\frac{1}{e^{2\pi\Omega/a}-1}.
\end{equation}
The exact-clock condition $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ then determines a critical proper acceleration
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rindler-parry-acceleration}
a_*
=
\frac{2\pi\Omega}
     {\log\!\left(1+\dfrac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega\,\Delta\tau}{2\pi\log\varphi}\right)}.
\end{equation}
The memory-transition condition $\Gamma_+(\Omega,a)=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ similarly gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rindler-memory-acceleration}
a_{\mathrm{mem}}
=
\frac{2\pi\Omega}
     {\log\!\left(1+\dfrac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega}{2\pi\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right)}.
\end{equation}
This is the Rindler realization of Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry} and Theorem~\ref{thm:memory-surface}: the lapse is $N(\rho)=\rho$, the Hartle--Hawking inverse temperature at the boost horizon is $\beta_\infty=2\pi$, the exact-clock shell is the hyperbola $\rho=\rho_\varphi=\beta_\varphi/(2\pi)=1/a_*$, and the memory-transition shell is the hyperbola $\rho=\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}=1/a_{\mathrm{mem}}$. On $\rho=\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}$ the finite-recovery output is exact $1$-dependent; for $\rho<\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}$ the covariance sequence alternates in sign, whereas for $\rho>\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}$ it stays strictly negative.
For a fixed detector design $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau)$, the condition $a=a_*$ selects a unique Rindler trajectory whose symbolic output realizes the exact-clock law. In the fine-sampling regime $\lambda_c^2\Omega\Delta\tau\ll\log\varphi$, one recovers $a_*\approx 4\pi^2\log\varphi/(\lambda_c^2\Delta\tau)$, so finer temporal resolution demands larger acceleration.
\[
\rho_\varphi \lessgtr \rho_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_\varphi \gtrless \Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\]
which is the Rindler specialization of Theorem~\ref{thm:shell-order}.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Schwarzschild shell pair in the geometric-optics static-detector regime]\label{ex:schwarzschild}
As a concrete illustration, consider a static Unruh--DeWitt detector at Schwarzschild coordinate radius $r>2M$ in the Hartle--Hawking state of a black hole of mass $M$. The local inverse temperature is $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)=8\pi M\sqrt{1-2M/r}$ \cite{KayWald1991}. In the geometric-optics static-detector regime \cite{BirDav1982,Takagi1986}, the excitation rate retains the Planckian form of \eqref{eq:rindler-excitation} with $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(r)$ in place of $2\pi/a$. In this approximation greybody factors, finite-bandwidth distortions, and switching corrections are compressed into the effective spectral weight and are not resolved beyond the homogeneous-spectral-weight model. Within that response model, the exact-clock condition $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ determines a unique critical radius defined implicitly by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-parry}
\sqrt{1-\frac{2M}{r_*}}
=
\frac{\log(1+\chi)}{8\pi M\Omega},
\qquad
\chi:=\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega\,\Delta\tau}{2\pi\log\varphi},
\end{equation}
while the memory-transition sphere is determined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-memory}
\sqrt{1-\frac{2M}{r_{\mathrm{mem}}}}
=
\frac{1}{8\pi M\Omega}
\log\!\left(
1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\,\Omega}{2\pi\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
\right).
\end{equation}
provided $\log(1+\chi)<8\pi M\Omega$. This is the spherically symmetric instance of Theorem~\ref{thm:tolman-parry}, with lapse $N(r)=\sqrt{1-2M/r}$ and global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty=8\pi M$. The locus $r=r_*$ is a $2$-sphere on which the dead-time detector output realizes the exact-clock law, while Corollary~\ref{cor:schwarzschild-shell-formulas} supplies the corresponding explicit radius and proper-distance formulas. If $\rho$ denotes proper distance from the horizon, then Corollary~\ref{cor:near-horizon-parry} and Theorem~\ref{thm:two-shell-near-horizon} yield the near-horizon asymptotic
\begin{equation}\label{eq:schwarzschild-rho-star}
\rho_*
\sim
\frac{1}{2\pi\Omega}\log(1+\chi)
\qquad
(\rho_*\downarrow 0),
\end{equation}
which is independent of the black-hole mass at leading order. Equivalently,
\[
r_*-2M
\sim
\frac{[\log(1+\chi)]^2}{32\pi^2 M\Omega^2},
\]
so the selected Schwarzschild sphere is the areal manifestation of the universal near-horizon critical shell. The finite-recovery shell is the sphere $r=r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ from \eqref{eq:schwarzschild-memory}; at that radius the output becomes exact $1$-dependent, with alternating correlations on the hotter side and purely negative correlations on the colder side. Because $N(r)$ is strictly increasing for $r>2M$,
\[
r_* \lessgtr r_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
N_\varphi \lessgtr N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_\varphi \gtrless \Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\]
which is the Schwarzschild specialization of Theorem~\ref{thm:shell-order}. Thus the question of which shell lies closer to the horizon is fixed by detector calibration, not by the background geometry. For a single detector design, the pair $(r_*,r_{\mathrm{mem}})$ also carries model-conditional inverse content through Theorem~\ref{thm:schwarzschild-inversion}, Proposition~\ref{prop:schwarzschild-conditioning}, and Corollary~\ref{cor:schwarzschild-hh-consistency}.
\end{example}
