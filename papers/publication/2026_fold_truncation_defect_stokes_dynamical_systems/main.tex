\begin{abstract}
For $m\ge 1$, let $\Fold_m$ map a binary word of length $m$ to the first $m$
digits of the Zeckendorf expansion of its Fibonacci value.  For $n\ge m$ we
compare $\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))$ with $\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))$
and write $D_{n\to m}$ for the resulting truncation defect.  We prove that the
adjacent defect at scale $m+1\to m$ is nonzero exactly when the appended digit
is $1$ and the prefix value is at least $F_{m+1}$; this yields a telescoping
formula expressing $D_{n\to m}$ as the xor-sum of the projected adjacent
defects.  The main deterministic result is a one-bit tail collapse: if
$\omega=(p,q)\in\Omega_m\times\Omega_{n-m}$, then
$D_{n\to m}(p,q)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q))$ for an explicit bit
$\beta(q)\in\{0,1\}$.  Consequently
\[
\mu_{n,m}=(1-\rho_{n-m})\delta_0+\rho_{n-m}\lambda_m,
\qquad
\rho_\ell=\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell},
\]
where $\mu_{n,m}$ is the law of $D_{n\to m}$ under $\Unif_n$ and $\lambda_m$
is the adjacent defect law with top digit fixed to $1$.  In particular,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\frac{(2^{n-m}-(-1)^{n-m})\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{3\cdot 2^n}.
\]
The tail bit is the parity of the initial run of $1$'s in the upper block and
is the minimal exact tail statistic for the full defect family.  The same
description yields an explicit stationary one-step Markov analogue, again as a
two-component mixture, now with coefficients
$p_{a1}(1-(-p_{11})^\ell)/(1+p_{11})$ at tail length $\ell$, and hence with
exponential convergence to the limiting law.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Let
\[
\Fold_m:\Omega_m=\{0,1\}^m\longrightarrow X_m,
\]
where $\Fold_m$ evaluates a binary word in the Fibonacci basis, replaces it by
its Zeckendorf expansion, and keeps the first $m$ digits.  Here $X_m$ denotes
the set of admissible words of length $m$, namely those with no adjacent
$1$'s.  If $m\le n$ and $\omega\in\Omega_n$, there are two natural ways to
produce an admissible word of length $m$,
\[
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega)).
\]
The first truncates the raw word and normalizes only the lower window, whereas
the second normalizes at the top scale and then projects.  These two
procedures need not agree.  We therefore write
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\oplus
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))
\in \{0,1\}^m
\]
for the resulting defect vector.  The purpose of the paper is to describe this
observable exactly at finite scale.

The starting point is an exact one-step criterion.  If
$\eta=(p,b)\in\Omega_{m+1}$ and $s=N(p)$, then
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} shows that
\[
K_{m+1\to m}(p,b)=b\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\ge F_{m+1}\}}.
\]
Thus a new defect is created only when the appended digit is $1$ and the lower
block already lies beyond the threshold $F_{m+1}$.  This is the only
arithmetic input needed for the multiscale analysis.  Once it is available,
the global defect is recovered by the telescoping identity of
Section~\ref{sec:telescoping} and the associated cocycle formula.

The main theorem says that the entire upper block can be compressed to a single
bit.  If $\omega=(p,q)\in\Omega_m\times\Omega_{n-m}$ is split into its lower
$m$-digit prefix and upper tail, Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} constructs a
recursively defined bit $\beta(q)\in\{0,1\}$ such that
\[
D_{n\to m}(p,q)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q)).
\]
In particular, the law of $D_{n\to m}$ under the uniform measure is the
mixture $(1-\rho_{n-m})\delta_0+\rho_{n-m}\lambda_m$, where $\lambda_m$ is the
adjacent law with the top digit fixed to $1$ and
\[
\rho_\ell=\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}
=
\frac13-\frac{(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\rho_{n-m}\,\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^m},
\]
and Proposition~\ref{thm:explicit-count} identifies the adjacent factor on the
right-hand side.  For fixed $m$, this yields the exact limiting law
\[
\frac23\delta_0+\frac13\lambda_m
\qquad (\ell\to\infty),
\]
with an explicit oscillatory error of order $2^{-\ell}$.  The exact
distributional formulas are therefore consequences of a deterministic collapse,
not of independent probabilistic arguments.

The tail bit also has an intrinsic interpretation.  Section~\ref{sec:markov}
shows that $\beta(q)$ is the parity of the initial run of $1$'s in the tail
and proves that no strictly coarser tail statistic determines the entire
family of defects $D_{n\to m}$ exactly.  This minimality statement is
finite-state in spirit and may be compared with Myhill--Nerode minimization
\cite{Myhill1957,Nerode1958}.  The same identification yields an exact
stationary one-step Markov extension: after conditioning on the boundary digit
$\omega_m$, the defect law is again a two-component mixture, now with
coefficient $p_{a1}(1-(-p_{11})^\ell)/(1+p_{11})$ at tail length $\ell$, and
hence converges exponentially.  We do not pursue higher-order dependence or
other linear-recurrence numeration systems here.  Standard Markov-chain
background may be found in
\cite{Parry1964,Norris1997,LevinPeresWilmer2009MarkovMixing}.

Classical background on Zeckendorf expansions goes back to
\cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972,Carlitz1968,CarlitzScovHoggatt1972,%
Fraenkel1985}.  Automata-theoretic normalization in Fibonacci numeration was
developed in
\cite{Frougny1991,Frougny1992,FrougnySolomyak1996,FrougnySakarovitch1999,%
Frougny1999,FrougnyS2010,ShallitShan2024}, and finite-state or rational-series
methods for related observables may be compared with
\cite{BerstelReutenauer2011}.  Successor carries and other scalar statistics
have been studied in
\cite{Frougny1996Successor,Frougny1997Sequential,AhlbachUsatineFrougnyPippenger2013,%
BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020}, while probabilistic and combinatorial
questions for Zeckendorf expansions appear in
\cite{KologluKoppMillerWang2011,DrmotaSteiner2002,Steiner2005,MillerWang2012,%
Griffiths2010,Hosten2024,BaratGrabner2014}.  For the symbolic-dynamical
viewpoint we use only standard material on the golden-mean shift and inverse
limits \cite{LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}.  The observable considered here is
of a different type: it is a vector-valued defect comparing the two
normalization/truncation routes above, and its exact law is not determined by
the top adjacent event alone; see the appendix for small examples.

The paper is organized as follows.  Section~\ref{sec:adjacent} introduces the
finite-window Fold map, records the elementary normalization facts needed later
on, and proves the threshold theorem for the adjacent defect.
Section~\ref{sec:telescoping} derives the telescoping and cocycle identities.
Section~\ref{sec:uniform} proves the collapse theorem and the exact uniform-law
distribution.  Section~\ref{sec:markov} identifies the tail bit, proves its
minimality as an exact factor, and extends the defect law to stationary
one-step Markov inputs.  The appendix collects examples and exact small-scale
values.

\section{Finite-window normalization and adjacent defects}
\label{sec:adjacent}

We begin by fixing notation and recording the elementary normalization facts
used throughout.  Digits are indexed from the low-order end: position $k$
carries Fibonacci weight $F_{k+1}$, where $F_1=F_2=1$ and
$F_{j+2}=F_{j+1}+F_j$.  Thus $N(10)=F_2=1$ and $N(01)=F_3=2$, and the prefix
map $r_{n\to m}$ keeps the first $m$ low-order digits.

For $m\ge 1$ set
\[
\Omega_m:=\{0,1\}^m,
\qquad
X_m:=\{x=(x_1,\dots,x_m)\in\{0,1\}^m:\ x_ix_{i+1}=0\text{ for }1\le i<m\}.
\]
For $\omega=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m\in\Omega_m$, define its Fibonacci value by
\[
N(\omega):=\sum_{k=1}^m\omega_kF_{k+1}.
\]
For $N\ge 0$, let $Z(N)=(z_k)_{k\ge 1}\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}$ be the unique finitely
supported sequence such that
\[
N=\sum_{k\ge 1}z_kF_{k+1}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
z_kz_{k+1}=0\ \text{ for all }k\ge 1;
\]
we set $Z(0)=0^\infty$.  The Fold map is
\[
\Fold_m(\omega):=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(N(\omega)))\in X_m,
\]
where $\pi_{\infty\to m}(x_1,x_2,\dots)=(x_1,\dots,x_m)$.

For $n\ge m$ we use the truncation maps
\[
r_{n\to m}(\omega_1\cdots\omega_n):=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m,
\qquad
\pi_{n\to m}(x_1,\dots,x_n):=(x_1,\dots,x_m),
\]
and, on the ambient cube,
\[
\tau_{n\to m}(u_1,\dots,u_n):=(u_1,\dots,u_m).
\]
We use $\tau_{n\to m}$ only for defect vectors in the ambient cube.

The following elementary facts will be used repeatedly.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:max-prefix-value}
For $m\ge 1$,
\[
\max_{x\in X_m}N(x)=F_{m+2}-1.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $M_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}N(x)$ and set $M_0:=0$.  Then $M_1=1=F_3-1$.  For
$m\ge 2$, either $x_m=0$, in which case $N(x)\le M_{m-1}$, or $x_m=1$, in
which case $x_{m-1}=0$ and
\[
N(x)=F_{m+1}+N(x_1,\dots,x_{m-2})\le F_{m+1}+M_{m-2}.
\]
Thus
\[
M_m=\max\{M_{m-1},\,F_{m+1}+M_{m-2}\}.
\]
Using the induction hypothesis,
\[
M_m=\max\{F_{m+1}-1,\,F_{m+1}+F_m-1\}=F_{m+2}-1.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:fold-residue}
For every $m\ge 1$ and every $\omega\in\Omega_m$, write
\[
N(\omega)=qF_{m+2}+r,
\qquad
q\in\{0,1\},
\qquad
0\le r<F_{m+2}.
\]
Then
\[
\Fold_m(\omega)=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(r)).
\]
Equivalently, for $x\in X_m$,
\[
\Fold_m(\omega)=x
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
N(\omega)\equiv N(x)\pmod{F_{m+2}}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since
\[
N(\omega)\le \sum_{k=1}^mF_{k+1}=F_{m+3}-2<2F_{m+2},
\]
we have $q\in\{0,1\}$.  If $q=0$, there is nothing to prove.  If $q=1$, then
\[
0\le r=N(\omega)-F_{m+2}\le F_{m+1}-2<F_{m+1}.
\]
Hence $Z(r)$ has no nonzero digit in positions $m,m+1,\dots$, so appending a
single $1$ in position $m+1$ produces the Zeckendorf expansion of
$N(\omega)=F_{m+2}+r$.  Its first $m$ digits are exactly those of $Z(r)$,
which proves the first assertion.

For the equivalence, note that any $x\in X_m$ is already the Zeckendorf
expansion of its own value $N(x)$, and Lemma~\ref{lem:max-prefix-value} gives
$N(x)\le F_{m+2}-1$.  Thus $\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(r))=x$ if and only if
$r=N(x)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:prefix-functoriality}
If $n\ge k\ge m$, then
\[
\pi_{n\to m}=\pi_{k\to m}\circ \pi_{n\to k},
\qquad
r_{n\to m}=r_{k\to m}\circ r_{n\to k},
\qquad
\tau_{n\to m}=\tau_{k\to m}\circ \tau_{n\to k}.
\]
Moreover, for all $u,v\in\{0,1\}^n$,
\[
\tau_{n\to m}(u\oplus v)=\tau_{n\to m}(u)\oplus\tau_{n\to m}(v).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is immediate from the definitions of the truncation maps.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
\label{ex:noncommuting-square}
Take $n=2$, $m=1$, and $\omega=11$.  Then
\[
r_{2\to 1}(11)=1,
\qquad
\Fold_1(r_{2\to 1}(11))=1.
\]
On the other hand, $N(11)=F_2+F_3=3=F_4$, so the Zeckendorf expansion of
$N(11)$ begins with $00$.  Hence
\[
\Fold_2(11)=00,
\qquad
\pi_{2\to 1}(\Fold_2(11))=0.
\]
Thus truncation does not commute with normalization.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:adjacent-defect}
For $m\ge 1$ and $\eta\in\Omega_{m+1}$, the adjacent defect is
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(\eta)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{m+1\to m}(\eta))
\oplus
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\eta))
\in\{0,1\}^m.
\]
Its indicator is
\[
K_{m+1\to m}(\eta):=\mathbf{1}_{\{\kappa_{m+1\to m}(\eta)\ne 0\}}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Threshold criterion for the adjacent defect]
\label{thm:adjacent-threshold}
Let $m\ge 1$, let $p\in\Omega_m$, let $b\in\{0,1\}$, and write
\[
\eta=(p,b)\in\Omega_{m+1},
\qquad
s:=N(p).
\]
Then
\[
K_{m+1\to m}(\eta)=b\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\ge F_{m+1}\}}.
\]
More precisely,
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,0)=0,
\]
and
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=
\begin{cases}
0,& s<F_{m+1},\\[4pt]
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2}))
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s-F_{m+1})),
& s\ge F_{m+1}.
\end{cases}
\]
When $s\ge F_{m+1}$, the two admissible words in the second line are distinct;
in particular the defect is nonzero.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If $b=0$, then $N(p,0)=s$, so
\[
\Fold_{m+1}(p,0)=\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(Z(s))
\]
and therefore
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,0))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\Fold_m(p).
\]
Thus $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,0)=0$.

Assume now that $b=1$.  If $s<F_{m+1}$, then the Zeckendorf expansion of $s$
has no nonzero digit in positions $m,m+1,\dots$.  Consequently
$Z(s+F_{m+2})$ is obtained from $Z(s)$ by inserting a single $1$ in position
$m+1$, and hence
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,1))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\Fold_m(p).
\]
So the defect vanishes.

Suppose finally that $s\ge F_{m+1}$.  Write
\[
s=F_{m+1}+t
\qquad\text{with}\qquad
0\le t<F_{m+2}.
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue},
\[
\Fold_m(p)=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2})).
\]
Since
\[
N(p,1)=s+F_{m+2}=F_{m+3}+t,
\]
the same proposition at level $m+1$ gives
\[
\Fold_{m+1}(p,1)=\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(Z(t)),
\]
and therefore
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,1))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(t))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s-F_{m+1})).
\]
This is the displayed formula.

If the two admissible words in the second line were equal, then
Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue} at level $m$ would imply
$s\bmod F_{m+2}=t=s-F_{m+1}$, which is impossible because
$0<F_{m+1}<F_{m+2}$.  Hence the defect is nonzero.
\end{proof}

\section{Telescoping and cocycle identities}
\label{sec:telescoping}

We now pass from the adjacent defect to the full defect.  The point of this
section is formal: once the one-step defect is fixed, the global defect is
obtained by iterating truncation and projection identities.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:global-defect}
Let $n\ge m$.  For $\omega\in\Omega_n$, define the global defect
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\oplus
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))
\in\{0,1\}^m.
\]
\end{definition}

When $n=m$, the definition gives $D_{m\to m}=0$.  For $n>m$, the defect
measures the accumulated noncommutation between the two routes from $\Omega_n$
to $X_m$.

For $v\in\{0,1\}^m$, write
\[
\lvert v\rvert_0:=\#\{i:\ v_i\ne 0\}.
\]

No new arithmetic enters here.  Once
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} is available, the identities below follow
by composing successive truncation and projection maps.  We record them
because they isolate the scales at which noncommutation is created and provide
the bookkeeping needed in Section~\ref{sec:uniform}.

For fixed $n>m$ and $\omega\in\Omega_n$, set
\[
A_j(\omega):=\Fold_j(r_{n\to j}(\omega)),
\qquad
B_j(\omega):=\pi_{n\to j}(\Fold_n(\omega)),
\]
and
\[
\Delta_j(\omega):=A_j(\omega)\oplus B_j(\omega)
\qquad (m\le j\le n).
\]
Then $\Delta_m(\omega)=D_{n\to m}(\omega)$ and $\Delta_n(\omega)=0$.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:adjacent-recursion}
For every $m<j\le n$,
\[
\Delta_{j-1}(\omega)
=
\kappa_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{j\to j-1}(\Delta_j(\omega)).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:prefix-functoriality},
\[
B_{j-1}(\omega)=\pi_{j\to j-1}(B_j(\omega)),
\qquad
A_{j-1}(\omega)=\Fold_{j-1}(r_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))).
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
\Delta_{j-1}(\omega)
&=A_{j-1}(\omega)\oplus B_{j-1}(\omega) \\
&=
\Bigl(A_{j-1}(\omega)\oplus \pi_{j\to j-1}(A_j(\omega))\Bigr)
\oplus
\Bigl(\pi_{j\to j-1}(A_j(\omega))\oplus \pi_{j\to j-1}(B_j(\omega))\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
The first bracket is $\kappa_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))$, while the second
equals $\tau_{j\to j-1}(\Delta_j(\omega))$ because $\tau_{j\to j-1}$ agrees
with $\pi_{j\to j-1}$ on admissible words and is an xor homomorphism.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Telescoping identity]
\label{thm:telescoping}
For every $n>m$ and every $\omega\in\Omega_n$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
\bigoplus_{j=m}^{n-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We claim that for each $j\in\{m,\dots,n\}$,
\[
\Delta_m(\omega)
=
\tau_{j\to m}(\Delta_j(\omega))
\oplus
\bigoplus_{t=m}^{j-1}
\tau_{t\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{t+1\to t}(r_{n\to t+1}(\omega))\bigr).
\]
For $j=m$ this is tautological.  Assume it holds at some
$j\in\{m,\dots,n-1\}$.  Using Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-recursion},
\[
\Delta_j(\omega)
=
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{j+1\to j}(\Delta_{j+1}(\omega)).
\]
Applying $\tau_{j\to m}$ and using Lemma~\ref{lem:prefix-functoriality} gives
\[
\tau_{j\to m}(\Delta_j(\omega))
=
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr)
\oplus
\tau_{j+1\to m}(\Delta_{j+1}(\omega)).
\]
Substituting this into the induction hypothesis proves the claim for $j+1$.
Taking $j=n$ and recalling that $\Delta_n(\omega)=0$ finishes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Cocycle identity]
\label{prop:cocycle}
If $m<k<n$, then for every $\omega\in\Omega_n$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{k\to m}(D_{n\to k}(\omega)).
\]
Consequently,
\[
\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0
\le
\lvert D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))\rvert_0
+
\lvert D_{n\to k}(\omega)\rvert_0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Split the sum in Proposition~\ref{thm:telescoping} at the intermediate scale $k$:
\[
\begin{aligned}
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
&=
\bigoplus_{j=m}^{k-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr) \\
&\quad\oplus
\bigoplus_{j=k}^{n-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
The first block is exactly $D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))$.  In the second
block, Lemma~\ref{lem:prefix-functoriality} gives
$\tau_{j\to m}=\tau_{k\to m}\circ\tau_{j\to k}$, so the second block is
$\tau_{k\to m}(D_{n\to k}(\omega))$.  This proves the cocycle identity.

The Hamming estimate follows from
\[
\lvert u\oplus v\rvert_0\le \lvert u\rvert_0+\lvert v\rvert_0
\]
and the fact that prefix projection cannot increase Hamming weight.
\end{proof}

\section{Tail collapse and the uniform law}
\label{sec:uniform}

We now specialize to the uniform law on raw words.  The key point is that the
upper block contributes only through a recursively defined bit.  We begin with
the one-step count that enters the final mixture formula.

\begin{proposition}[Exact one-step defect count]
\label{thm:explicit-count}
For $m\ge 1$, let
\[
C_m:=\#\{\alpha\in\Omega_m:\ N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}\}.
\]
Then
\[
C_1=1,
\qquad
C_2=2,
\qquad
C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}\quad (m\ge 3).
\]
Consequently,
\[
C_{2r}=\frac{2^{2r}+2}{3},
\qquad
C_{2r+1}=\frac{2^{2r+1}+1}{3}
\qquad (r\ge 1),
\]
and
\[
\PP_{\Unif_{m+1}}[K_{m+1\to m}=1]
=
\frac{2^m-C_m}{2^{m+1}}
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^{m+1}}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Consider the last two digits of $\alpha\in\Omega_m$.

If $\alpha_m=1$, then $N(\alpha)\ge F_{m+1}$, so no such word contributes to
$C_m$.

If $\alpha_m=0$ and $\alpha_{m-1}=0$, then
\[
N(\alpha)=N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})
\le
\sum_{k=1}^{m-2}F_{k+1}
=
F_{m+1}-2
<
F_{m+1},
\]
so every choice of the first $m-2$ digits is allowed.  This contributes
$2^{m-2}$ words.

If $\alpha_m=0$ and $\alpha_{m-1}=1$, then
\[
N(\alpha)=F_m+N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2}),
\]
and the condition $N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}$ becomes
$N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})<F_{m-1}$.  This contributes $C_{m-2}$ words.

Hence $C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}$ for $m\ge 3$.  The initial values are immediate:
$C_1=1$ because only $0$ has value $<F_2=1$, and $C_2=2$ because the words
$00$ and $10$ are exactly those of value $<F_3=2$.

The closed forms follow by unrolling the recurrence.  Setting $C_0:=1$, one
finds
\[
C_{2r}=2^{2r-2}+2^{2r-4}+\cdots+2^0+C_0=\frac{2^{2r}+2}{3},
\]
and
\[
C_{2r+1}=2^{2r-1}+2^{2r-3}+\cdots+2^1+C_1=\frac{2^{2r+1}+1}{3}.
\]
Finally, Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} shows that
$K_{m+1\to m}(\alpha,b)=1$ exactly when $b=1$ and $N(\alpha)\ge F_{m+1}$, so
there are $2^m-C_m$ defect-producing words in $\Omega_{m+1}$.  Dividing by
$2^{m+1}$ gives the probability formula.
\end{proof}

For $n>m$ and $v\in\{0,1\}^m$, define
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v):=2^{-n}\#\{\omega\in\Omega_n:\ D_{n\to m}(\omega)=v\}.
\]
Thus $\mu_{n,m}$ is the exact defect law under the uniform raw-word measure.
Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} shows that, after the upper block has been
marginalized, a single recursively defined tail bit is sufficient to recover
the full distribution.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:zero-barrier}
Let $m\ge 1$, let $p\in\Omega_m$, and let $b\in\{0,1\}$.  Then
\[
\tau_{m+1\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,b)\bigr)=0.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $b=0$, Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} gives
$\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,0)=0$.

If $b=1$, write
\[
s:=N(p,0)=N(p).
\]
Since the $(m+1)$-st digit of $(p,0)$ is zero,
\[
s\le \sum_{k=1}^m F_{k+1}=F_{m+2}-2<F_{m+2}.
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} at level $m+2\to m+1$ therefore gives
$\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,1)=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:double-one-reduction}
Let $m\ge 1$, let $p\in\Omega_m$, and let $b\in\{0,1\}$.  Then
\[
\tau_{m+2\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,1,b)\bigr)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,b).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $b=0$, both sides vanish by Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold}.

Assume $b=1$ and write
\[
s:=N(p).
\]
Since $p\in\Omega_m$,
\[
0\le s\le F_{m+3}-2.
\]
At level $m+3\to m+2$, the prefix $(p,1,1)$ has value
\[
N(p,1,1)=s+F_{m+2}+F_{m+3}=s+F_{m+4}.
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} therefore yields
\[
\tau_{m+2\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,1,1)\bigr)
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2})).
\]

If $s<F_{m+1}$, then $s+F_{m+2}<F_{m+3}$ and the Zeckendorf expansion of
$s+F_{m+2}$ is obtained by inserting a single $1$ in position $m+1$ above
$Z(s)$.  Hence
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2}))=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s)),
\]
so the left-hand side is zero.  Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} gives
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=0$ in the same range.

Assume now that $s\ge F_{m+1}$.  Since $s<F_{m+3}$,
\[
0\le s-F_{m+1}<F_{m+2},
\]
and
\[
s+F_{m+2}=F_{m+3}+(s-F_{m+1}).
\]
Thus the first $m$ digits of $Z(s+F_{m+2})$ are exactly those of
$Z(s-F_{m+1})$, so
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2}))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s-F_{m+1})).
\]
Also,
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2}))
\]
because $s<F_{m+3}$.  Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} at level
$m+1\to m$ therefore gives
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2}))
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1).
\]
This proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Tail collapse]
\label{thm:tail-collapse}
Fix $n>m$ and write $\ell:=n-m$.  Define
\[
\beta:\bigsqcup_{r\ge 0}\Omega_r\longrightarrow\{0,1\}
\]
by the recursion
\[
\beta(\varnothing)=0,
\qquad
\beta(1)=1,
\qquad
\beta(0\alpha)=0,
\qquad
\beta(10\alpha)=1,
\qquad
\beta(11\alpha)=\beta(\alpha).
\]
Every nonempty finite binary word has exactly one of the forms $0\alpha$,
$1$, $10\alpha$, or $11\alpha$, so the recursion is unambiguous.
Then for every $p\in\Omega_m$ and every $q\in\Omega_\ell$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(p,q)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q)).
\]
For $v\in\{0,1\}^m$, set
\[
\lambda_m(v):=2^{-m}\#\{p\in\Omega_m:\ \kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=v\},
\]
and for $\ell\ge 0$ set
\[
\rho_\ell:=2^{-\ell}\#\{q\in\Omega_\ell:\ \beta(q)=1\}.
\]
Then
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v)=
\begin{cases}
(1-\rho_\ell)+\rho_\ell\lambda_m(0),& v=0,\\[4pt]
\rho_\ell\lambda_m(v),& v\ne 0.
\end{cases}
\]
Equivalently, if $\delta_0$ denotes the point mass at $0\in\{0,1\}^m$, then
\[
\mu_{n,m}=(1-\rho_\ell)\delta_0+\rho_\ell\lambda_m.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\rho_\ell=\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}
\qquad (\ell\ge 0),
\]
and therefore
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\rho_{n-m}\,\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^m}
=
\frac{(2^{n-m}-(-1)^{n-m})\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{3\cdot 2^n}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $r\ge 0$, for $p\in\Omega_m$, and for $q\in\Omega_r$, write
\[
E_r(p,q):=D_{m+r\to m}(p,q).
\]
The identity
\[
E_r(p,q)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q))
\]
is established by induction on $r$.

For $r=0$, one has $q=\varnothing$, $E_0(p,\varnothing)=0$, and
$\beta(\varnothing)=0$.

Assume $r\ge 1$ and that the claim holds for shorter tails.

If $q=1$, then
\[
E_1(p,1)=D_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(1)).
\]
This settles the length-one tail.

If $q=0\alpha$, then Proposition~\ref{prop:cocycle} with the intermediate scale
$m+1$ gives
\[
E_r(p,0\alpha)
=
D_{m+1\to m}(p,0)
\oplus
\tau_{m+1\to m}(D_{m+r\to m+1}(p,0,\alpha)).
\]
The first term is zero.  The induction hypothesis at level $m+1$ gives
\[
D_{m+r\to m+1}(p,0,\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,\beta(\alpha)).
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:zero-barrier} then implies $E_r(p,0\alpha)=0$, which equals
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(0\alpha))$.

If $q=10\alpha$, then Proposition~\ref{prop:cocycle} with the intermediate
scale $m+2$ gives
\[
E_r(p,10\alpha)
=
D_{m+2\to m}(p,1,0)
\oplus
\tau_{m+2\to m}(D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,0,\alpha)).
\]
Since
\[
N(p,1,0)=N(p,1),
\]
the first term is exactly $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)$.  The induction hypothesis
at level $m+2$ yields
\[
D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,0,\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,0,\beta(\alpha)),
\]
and Lemma~\ref{lem:zero-barrier}, applied with the $(m+1)$-digit prefix
$(p,1)$, shows that its projection to level $m$ is zero.  Hence
\[
E_r(p,10\alpha)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(10\alpha)).
\]

If $q=11\alpha$, then Proposition~\ref{prop:cocycle} with the intermediate
scale $m+2$ gives
\[
E_r(p,11\alpha)
=
D_{m+2\to m}(p,1,1)
\oplus
\tau_{m+2\to m}(D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,1,\alpha)).
\]
Now
\[
N(p,1,1)=N(p)+F_{m+2}+F_{m+3}=N(p)+F_{m+4}.
\]
Since $N(p)<F_{m+3}$, the Zeckendorf expansion of $N(p,1,1)$ is obtained by
placing an isolated digit $1$ in position $m+3$ above $Z(N(p))$.  Therefore
\[
D_{m+2\to m}(p,1,1)=0.
\]
The induction hypothesis at level $m+2$ gives
\[
D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,1,\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,1,\beta(\alpha)).
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:double-one-reduction} then yields
\[
E_r(p,11\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(\alpha))
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(11\alpha)).
\]
This completes the induction.

Set
\[
B_\ell:=\#\{q\in\Omega_\ell:\ \beta(q)=1\}.
\]
The defining recursion for $\beta$ gives
\[
B_0=0,
\qquad
B_1=1,
\qquad
B_\ell=2^{\ell-2}+B_{\ell-2}\quad (\ell\ge 2),
\]
because
\[
\Omega_\ell=(0\Omega_{\ell-1})\sqcup(10\Omega_{\ell-2})\sqcup(11\Omega_{\ell-2})
\]
is a disjoint union.  The closed form
\[
B_\ell=\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3}
\]
follows by induction, and therefore
\[
\rho_\ell=\frac{B_\ell}{2^\ell}
=
\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}.
\]

If $v\ne 0$, the established identity shows that
$D_{n\to m}(p,q)=v$ holds if and only if $\beta(q)=1$ and
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=v$.  Hence
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v)=\rho_\ell\lambda_m(v)
\qquad (v\ne 0).
\]
The formula at $v=0$ follows from the decomposition into the cases
$\beta(q)=0$ and $\beta(q)=1$.

Finally,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\rho_\ell\sum_{v\ne 0}\lambda_m(v).
\]
By Proposition~\ref{thm:explicit-count},
\[
\sum_{v\ne 0}\lambda_m(v)
=
2^{-m}\#\{p\in\Omega_m:\ \kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)\ne 0\}
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^m}.
\]
Substituting the formula for $\rho_\ell$ proves the last identity.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:limit-law}
For fixed $m$, the preceding theorem gives the exact asymptotic law along
increasing tail length:
\[
\mu_{m+\ell,m}
=
\left(\frac23+\frac{(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}\right)\delta_0
+
\left(\frac13-\frac{(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}\right)\lambda_m.
\]
Hence
\[
\mu_{m+\ell,m}\xrightarrow[\ell\to\infty]{}\nu_m
:=
\frac23\delta_0+\frac13\lambda_m.
\]
Moreover,
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\mu_{m+\ell,m},\nu_m)
=
\frac{1-\lambda_m(0)}{3\cdot 2^\ell}
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{3\cdot 2^{m+\ell}},
\]
because
\[
1-\lambda_m(0)
=
2^{-m}\#\{p\in\Omega_m:\ \kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)\ne 0\}
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^m}
\]
by Proposition~\ref{thm:explicit-count}.
\end{remark}

\section{The tail bit and stationary Markov inputs}
\label{sec:markov}

We first identify the tail bit explicitly and deduce its minimality as an
exact factor for the family of defects.  Once this is done, the stationary
one-step Markov law follows from a direct computation of the parity of the
initial run of $1$'s.

\begin{theorem}[Identification and minimality of the tail bit]
\label{thm:tail-rigidity}
For a finite word $q=q_1\cdots q_r$, let
\[
\iota(q):=\max\{s\in\{0,\dots,r\}:\ q_1=\cdots=q_s=1\}
\]
be the length of its initial run of $1$'s, with $\iota(\varnothing):=0$.
Then
\[
\beta(q)\equiv \iota(q)\pmod 2
\qquad
\text{for every finite tail word }q.
\]
Define an equivalence relation on the disjoint union
$\bigsqcup_{r\ge 0}\Omega_r$ by
\[
q\sim q'
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
D_{m+|q|\to m}(p,q)=D_{m+|q'|\to m}(p,q')
\text{ for every }m\ge 1\text{ and }p\in\Omega_m.
\]
Then
\[
q\sim q'
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\beta(q)=\beta(q').
\]
Consequently, if $S$ is any set, if
$\gamma:\bigsqcup_{r\ge 0}\Omega_r\to S$, and if for each $m\ge 1$ there is a
map $G_m:\Omega_m\times S\to\{0,1\}^m$ such that
\[
D_{m+r\to m}(p,q)=G_m(p,\gamma(q))
\qquad
(r\ge 0,\ p\in\Omega_m,\ q\in\Omega_r),
\]
then there exists a unique map
\[
\psi:\gamma\!\left(\bigsqcup_{r\ge 0}\Omega_r\right)\to\{0,1\}
\]
satisfying
\[
\beta=\psi\circ\gamma.
\]
In particular, every exact tail factor for the full defect has at least two
states, and the factor furnished by Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} is minimal
up to relabeling of $\{0,1\}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If $q=\varnothing$, then $\beta(q)=0$ and $\iota(q)=0$.  Assume that $q$ is
nonempty.

If $\iota(q)=0$, then $q=0\alpha$ for some $\alpha$, so $\beta(q)=0$ by
definition.  If $\iota(q)=2t+1$ is odd, then either $q=1^{2t+1}$ or
$q=1^{2t+1}0\alpha$.  Repeated use of the rule $\beta(11\alpha)=\beta(\alpha)$
reduces both words to the case $\beta(1)=1$, so $\beta(q)=1$.  If
$\iota(q)=2t$ is positive and even, then either $q=1^{2t}$ or
$q=1^{2t}0\alpha$.  The same reduction gives
$\beta(q)=\beta(\varnothing)=0$.  Hence $\beta(q)\equiv\iota(q)\pmod 2$.

If $\beta(q)=\beta(q')$, Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} yields
\[
D_{m+|q|\to m}(p,q)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q))
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q'))
=
D_{m+|q'|\to m}(p,q')
\]
for every $m$ and $p$, so $q\sim q'$.

Conversely, assume $\beta(q)\ne\beta(q')$.  Set $m:=1$ and $p:=1$.  Then
Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} gives
\[
D_{1+|q|\to 1}(1,q)=\kappa_{2\to 1}(1,\beta(q)),
\qquad
D_{1+|q'|\to 1}(1,q')=\kappa_{2\to 1}(1,\beta(q')).
\]
Also,
\[
\kappa_{2\to 1}(1,0)=0,
\qquad
\kappa_{2\to 1}(1,1)=1,
\]
because $\Fold_1(1)=1$ and $\Fold_2(11)=00$.  Therefore the two defects are
different, so $q\nsim q'$.

For the factorization claim, define
\[
\psi:\gamma\!\left(\bigsqcup_{r\ge 0}\Omega_r\right)\to\{0,1\}
\]
by choosing $q$ with $\gamma(q)=s$ and setting $\psi(s):=\beta(q)$.  This is
well defined because
$\gamma(q)=\gamma(q')$ implies
\[
D_{m+|q|\to m}(p,q)=G_m(p,\gamma(q))=G_m(p,\gamma(q'))=D_{m+|q'|\to m}(p,q')
\]
for every $m$ and $p$, hence $q\sim q'$ and therefore $\beta(q)=\beta(q')$.
The identity $\beta=\psi\circ\gamma$ is immediate from the definition of
$\psi$, and uniqueness holds on the image of $\gamma$.
\end{proof}

The preceding theorem converts the deterministic collapse into an exact
non-uniform law for one-step Markov inputs.  The only tail information that
survives is the parity of the initial run of $1$'s, and that parity has a
closed form under Markov sampling.

\begin{theorem}[Stationary one-step Markov extension]
\label{thm:markov-defect-law}
Let $\PP_P$ be the stationary irreducible one-step Markov measure on
$\{0,1\}^{\NN}$ with transition matrix
\[
P=(p_{ab})_{a,b\in\{0,1\}}
\]
and stationary distribution $\pi=(\pi_0,\pi_1)$.  Let
$\omega^\infty=(\omega_1,\omega_2,\dots)$ denote the coordinate process, and
for $m\ge 1$ write
\[
r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty):=(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m)\in\Omega_m.
\]
Fix $m\ge 1$ and $\ell\ge 0$, and set $n:=m+\ell$.  For $a\in\{0,1\}$ define
probability measures on $\{0,1\}^m$ by
\[
\lambda_{m,a}(v)
:=
\PP_P\!\left[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty),1)=v
\ \middle|\
\omega_m=a
\right],
\]
and define
\[
\rho_\ell^{(a)}
:=
\PP_P\!\left[
\beta(\omega_{m+1}\cdots\omega_{m+\ell})=1
\ \middle|\
\omega_m=a
\right].
\]
Then
\[
\Law_{\PP_P}\!\bigl(D_{n\to m}(r_{\infty\to n}(\omega^\infty))\bigr)
=
\sum_{a=0}^1
\pi_a\bigl((1-\rho_\ell^{(a)})\delta_0+\rho_\ell^{(a)}\lambda_{m,a}\bigr),
\]
and
\[
\rho_0^{(a)}=0,
\qquad
\rho_\ell^{(a)}
=
\frac{p_{a1}(1-(-p_{11})^\ell)}{1+p_{11}}
\qquad (\ell\ge 1).
\]
Consequently
\[
\PP_P\!\bigl[D_{n\to m}(r_{\infty\to n}(\omega^\infty))\ne 0\bigr]
=
\sum_{a=0}^1
\pi_a\,\rho_\ell^{(a)}\bigl(1-\lambda_{m,a}(0)\bigr),
\]
the defect law converges as $\ell\to\infty$ to
\[
\nu_{m,P}
:=
\sum_{a=0}^1
\pi_a\left(
\left(1-\frac{p_{a1}}{1+p_{11}}\right)\delta_0
\;+\;
\frac{p_{a1}}{1+p_{11}}\lambda_{m,a}
\right),
\]
and
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}
\!\left(
\Law_{\PP_P}\!\bigl(D_{m+\ell\to m}(r_{\infty\to m+\ell}(\omega^\infty))\bigr),
\nu_{m,P}
\right)
\le
\frac{\pi_1}{1+p_{11}}\,p_{11}^{\ell}.
\]
If $p_{01}=p_{11}=u$, then
\[
\rho_\ell^{(0)}=\rho_\ell^{(1)}
=
\frac{u(1-(-u)^\ell)}{1+u},
\]
and the uniform law is recovered at $u=\frac12$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
P_{m,\ell}
:=
\Law_{\PP_P}\!\bigl(D_{m+\ell\to m}(r_{\infty\to m+\ell}(\omega^\infty))\bigr).
\]
Because the chain is irreducible on two states, $\pi_a>0$ for $a=0,1$, so the
conditional laws $\lambda_{m,a}$ are well defined.
Fix $p\in\Omega_m$ and write $a:=p_m$.  By the Markov property, conditioned on
$r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty)=p$, the future block
$q:=(\omega_{m+1},\dots,\omega_{m+\ell})$ is independent of the past and is
distributed as a length-$\ell$ Markov chain started from state $a$.
Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} therefore gives
\[
\Law_{\PP_P}\!\bigl(D_{m+\ell\to m}(p,q)\,\big|\,r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty)=p\bigr)
=
(1-\rho_\ell^{(a)})\delta_0+\rho_\ell^{(a)}\delta_{\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)}.
\]
Conditioning further on $\omega_m=a$ and averaging over prefixes with last
digit $a$ yields
\[
\Law_{\PP_P}\!\bigl(
D_{m+\ell\to m}(r_{\infty\to m+\ell}(\omega^\infty))
\ \big|\ \omega_m=a
\bigr)
=
(1-\rho_\ell^{(a)})\delta_0+\rho_\ell^{(a)}\lambda_{m,a}.
\]
Since $\PP_P[\omega_m=a]=\pi_a$ by stationarity, averaging over $a$ proves the
first displayed formula for $P_{m,\ell}$.  The formula for the nonzero defect
probability is immediate.

Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-rigidity} identifies $\beta(q)$ with the parity of the
initial run of $1$'s.  Under the Markov law started from state $a$, the event
$\{\beta(q)=1\}$ is therefore the disjoint union of the cylinder events
corresponding to odd initial run lengths.  For $\ell=0$, this probability is
zero.  For $\ell\ge 1$, write $\ell=2r$ or $\ell=2r+1$.

If $\ell=2r$, then an odd initial run has length $2j+1$ for some
$0\le j\le r-1$, so
\[
\rho_{2r}^{(a)}
=
\sum_{j=0}^{r-1} p_{a1}p_{11}^{2j}(1-p_{11})
=
\frac{p_{a1}(1-p_{11}^{2r})}{1+p_{11}}
=
\frac{p_{a1}(1-(-p_{11})^{2r})}{1+p_{11}}.
\]
If $\ell=2r+1$, then either the odd initial run has length $2j+1$ with
$0\le j\le r-1$ and is followed by $0$, or the entire block is $1^{2r+1}$.
Hence
\[
\rho_{2r+1}^{(a)}
=
\sum_{j=0}^{r-1} p_{a1}p_{11}^{2j}(1-p_{11})
+ p_{a1}p_{11}^{2r}
=
\frac{p_{a1}(1+p_{11}^{2r+1})}{1+p_{11}}
=
\frac{p_{a1}(1-(-p_{11})^{2r+1})}{1+p_{11}}.
\]
This proves the closed form for $\rho_\ell^{(a)}$.

Let
\[
\rho_\infty^{(a)}:=\frac{p_{a1}}{1+p_{11}}.
\]
Since the chain is irreducible, one has $p_{11}<1$.  Therefore
\[
\nu_{m,P}
=
\sum_{a=0}^1
\pi_a\bigl((1-\rho_\infty^{(a)})\delta_0+\rho_\infty^{(a)}\lambda_{m,a}\bigr),
\]
so
\[
P_{m,\ell}-\nu_{m,P}
=
\sum_{a=0}^1
\pi_a\bigl(\rho_\ell^{(a)}-\rho_\infty^{(a)}\bigr)(\lambda_{m,a}-\delta_0).
\]
Since $d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\lambda_{m,a},\delta_0)\le 1$,
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}(P_{m,\ell},\nu_{m,P})
\le
\sum_{a=0}^1 \pi_a\bigl|\rho_\ell^{(a)}-\rho_\infty^{(a)}\bigr|
=
\frac{p_{11}^{\ell}}{1+p_{11}}
\sum_{a=0}^1 \pi_a p_{a1}.
\]
Stationarity gives $\pi_0p_{01}+\pi_1p_{11}=\pi_1$, hence
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}(P_{m,\ell},\nu_{m,P})
\le
\frac{\pi_1}{1+p_{11}}\,p_{11}^{\ell}.
\]
If $p_{01}=p_{11}=u$, then the chain is i.i.d.\ Bernoulli$(u)$, so
$\rho_\ell^{(0)}=\rho_\ell^{(1)}$ and, at $u=\frac12$, the measures
$\lambda_{m,0}$ and $\lambda_{m,1}$ both coincide with the uniform-law measure
$\lambda_m$.  This recovers Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse}.
\end{proof}

\section{Examples and small values}
\label{app:supplementary}

We collect here two small examples and a table of exact values that are not
used in the proofs but help separate the present defect from the scalar
indicators naturally associated with normalization and with the top adjacent
step.

\begin{example}[Normalization cascade without a truncation defect]
\label{ex:cascade-without-defect}
Take $m=4$, $p=1100$, and append $b=1$.  Normalizing the raw word
$(p,1)=11001$ produces $\Fold_5(11001)=00101$, so the normalization does
involve a nontrivial carry cascade.  Nevertheless
\[
\Fold_4(p)=0010
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{5\to 4}(\Fold_5(11001))=0010,
\]
hence $\kappa_{5\to 4}(p,1)=0$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[A lower-level defect with trivial top indicator]
\label{ex:defect-without-top-indicator}
Let $n=4$, $m=2$, and $\omega=0110$.  Direct computation gives
\[
D_{4\to 2}(\omega)=(0,1)\ne 0,
\]
while the top adjacent indicator satisfies
\[
K_{4\to 3}(\omega)=0.
\]
Thus the global defect may be created strictly below the top scale.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:small-values}
The exact probabilities $P_{n,m}:=\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]$ obtained by
direct enumeration for $n\le 5$ are listed in Table~\ref{tab:small-values}.
In particular,
\[
P_{2,1}=\frac14,
\qquad
P_{3,1}=\frac18,
\qquad
P_{4,1}=\frac{3}{16},
\]
so the scalar probabilities are not monotone in $n$ at fixed $m$.  Combined
with Example~\ref{ex:defect-without-top-indicator}, this is enough to see that
the full defect law cannot be recovered from the scalar event
$\{D_{n\to m}\ne 0\}$ alone.
\end{remark}
