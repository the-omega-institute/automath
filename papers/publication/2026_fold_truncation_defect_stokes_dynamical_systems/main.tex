\begin{abstract}
For each $m\ge 1$ let $\Fold_m$ be Fibonacci evaluation of an $m$-bit word,
followed by Zeckendorf normalization and truncation back to length $m$.  Given
$m\le n$ and $\omega\in\Omega_n$, we compare
\[
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega)).
\]
Their xor difference $D_{n\to m}$ measures the failure of truncation to commute
with normalization.  We prove that the adjacent defect at level $m+1\to m$ is
nonzero exactly when the appended digit is $1$ and the prefix value crosses the
threshold $F_{m+1}$.  This yields an exact telescoping formula expressing
$D_{n\to m}$ as the xor sum of the projected adjacent defects along the whole
resolution band.  As consequences we obtain a cocycle identity, quantitative
coupling and Hamming bounds, an exact formula
$\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor/2^{m+1}$ for the one-step defect probability under
the uniform raw-word law, and an exact collapse theorem for the full global
defect law: after the raw word is split into a lower $m$-digit block and an
upper tail, the entire multiscale defect is obtained by feeding a recursively
defined one-bit tail state into the adjacent defect at level $m+1\to m$.  Under
the uniform raw-word law this yields a factorization of the full defect
distribution and the exact formula
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\frac{(2^{n-m}-(-1)^{n-m})\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{3\cdot 2^n}.
\]
The final section gives a criterion for eventual compatibility of folded
prefixes of an infinite binary sequence.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

For each $m\ge 1$ we consider the map
\[
\Fold_m:\Omega_m=\{0,1\}^m\longrightarrow X_m,
\]
obtained by evaluating a binary word in the Fibonacci basis, replacing it by
its Zeckendorf expansion, and then truncating back to the first $m$ digits.
Here $X_m$ denotes the set of admissible words of length $m$, that is, those
with no adjacent $1$'s.  If $m\le n$ and $\omega\in\Omega_n$, there are two
obvious ways to produce an admissible word of length $m$:
\[
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega)).
\]
The first truncates the raw word and folds only the lower-resolution window;
the second folds at the top scale and then projects.  They need not agree.  We
write
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\oplus
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))
\in \{0,1\}^m
\]
for the resulting defect vector.

The local picture turns out to be completely rigid.  If
$\eta=(p,b)\in\Omega_{m+1}$ and $s=N(p)$ is the Fibonacci value of the
$m$-digit prefix, Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} shows that the adjacent
defect $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(\eta)$ is nonzero if and only if
$b=1$ and $s\ge F_{m+1}$.  In other words, an adjacent defect appears exactly
when the final digit forces a threshold crossing.  Once this one-step
criterion is available, the global defect becomes a formal object:
Theorem~\ref{thm:telescoping} expresses $D_{n\to m}$ as the xor sum of the
projected adjacent defects along the chain of resolutions
$m,m+1,\dots,n$.

The remaining arithmetic input is a count of threshold crossings under the
uniform raw-word law.  Writing
\[
C_m=\#\{\alpha\in\Omega_m:N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}\},
\]
we show in Theorem~\ref{thm:explicit-count} that
$C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}$ for $m\ge 3$, whence
\[
\PP_{\Unif_{m+1}}[K_{m+1\to m}=1]
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^{m+1}}.
\]
The full law turns out to admit a substantially sharper description.  If
$\omega=(p,q)\in\Omega_m\times\Omega_{n-m}$ is split into its lower $m$-digit
prefix and upper tail, Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} defines a recursively
determined tail bit $\beta(q)\in\{0,1\}$ and proves
\[
D_{n\to m}(p,q)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q)).
\]
Thus every multiscale defect is already an adjacent defect, and the entire tail
enters only through a one-bit activation state.  Under the uniform raw-word law
the nonzero part of the full vector law is exactly a scalar multiple of the
adjacent one-step law; the scalar factor is
\[
\rho_\ell=\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell},
\qquad
\ell=n-m.
\]
In particular,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\rho_{n-m}\,\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^m}.
\]
An inverse-limit criterion characterizing when the folded prefixes of an
infinite raw sequence eventually stabilize into a compatible family is also
obtained.

Classical background on Zeckendorf representations is contained in
\cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972,Fraenkel1985}.  Automata-theoretic
normalization in Fibonacci numeration goes back to
\cite{Frougny1991,Frougny1992,Frougny1999}.  Successor carries and related
observables are studied in
\cite{Frougny1996Successor,Frougny1997Sequential,AhlbachUsatineFrougnyPippenger2013,%
BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020}, while digit and block statistics for
Zeckendorf expansions appear in
\cite{KologluKoppMillerWang2011,MillerWang2012,DrmotaSteiner2002,%
Griffiths2010,Hosten2024}.  The defect considered here is of a different kind:
it compares two natural morphisms between finite windows and is inherently
vector-valued.  Appendix~\ref{app:supplementary} contains two short examples
showing that it is neither a disguised carry length nor a consequence of the
top adjacent indicator alone.

The paper is organized as follows.  Section~\ref{sec:adjacent} introduces the
finite-window Fold map and proves the threshold theorem for the adjacent
defect.  Section~\ref{sec:telescoping} derives the telescoping formula, the
cocycle relation, and general coupling bounds.  Section~\ref{sec:uniform}
treats uniform counts, exact finite-$n$ enumeration, and the collapse theorem
for the full defect law under the uniform measure.  Section~\ref{sec:lifts}
turns to eventual compatibility along infinite raw sequences.  Appendix~A
collects the routine finite-window facts used in the main text, and
Appendix~B records supplementary examples and exact small-scale values.

\section{The Fold Map and the Adjacent Defect}
\label{sec:adjacent}

Throughout, digits are indexed from the low-order end: position $k$ carries
Fibonacci weight $F_{k+1}$, where $F_1=F_2=1$ and
$F_{j+2}=F_{j+1}+F_j$.  Thus $N(10)=F_2=1$ and $N(01)=F_3=2$, and the prefix
map $r_{n\to m}$ keeps the first $m$ low-order digits.

For $m\ge 1$ set
\[
\Omega_m:=\{0,1\}^m,
\qquad
X_m:=\{x=(x_1,\dots,x_m)\in\{0,1\}^m:\ x_ix_{i+1}=0\text{ for }1\le i<m\}.
\]
For $\omega=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m\in\Omega_m$, define its Fibonacci value by
\[
N(\omega):=\sum_{k=1}^m\omega_kF_{k+1}.
\]
For $N\ge 0$, let $Z(N)=(z_k)_{k\ge 1}\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}$ be the unique finitely
supported sequence such that
\[
N=\sum_{k\ge 1}z_kF_{k+1}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
z_kz_{k+1}=0\ \text{ for all }k\ge 1;
\]
we set $Z(0)=0^\infty$.  The Fold map is
\[
\Fold_m(\omega):=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(N(\omega)))\in X_m,
\]
where $\pi_{\infty\to m}(x_1,x_2,\dots)=(x_1,\dots,x_m)$.

For $n\ge m$ we use the truncation maps
\[
r_{n\to m}(\omega_1\cdots\omega_n):=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m,
\qquad
\pi_{n\to m}(x_1,\dots,x_n):=(x_1,\dots,x_m),
\]
and, on the ambient cube,
\[
\tau_{n\to m}(u_1,\dots,u_n):=(u_1,\dots,u_m).
\]
The map $\tau_{n\to m}$ will only be used on defect vectors.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:noncommuting-square}
Take $n=2$, $m=1$, and $\omega=11$.  Then
\[
r_{2\to 1}(11)=1,
\qquad
\Fold_1(r_{2\to 1}(11))=1.
\]
On the other hand, $N(11)=F_2+F_3=3=F_4$, so the Zeckendorf expansion of
$N(11)$ begins with $00$.  Hence
\[
\Fold_2(11)=00,
\qquad
\pi_{2\to 1}(\Fold_2(11))=0.
\]
Thus truncation does not commute with normalization.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:adjacent-defect}
For $m\ge 1$ and $\eta\in\Omega_{m+1}$, the adjacent defect is
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(\eta)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{m+1\to m}(\eta))
\oplus
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\eta))
\in\{0,1\}^m.
\]
Its indicator is
\[
K_{m+1\to m}(\eta):=\mathbf{1}_{\{\kappa_{m+1\to m}(\eta)\ne 0\}}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Threshold classification of the adjacent defect]
\label{thm:adjacent-threshold}
Let $m\ge 1$, let $p\in\Omega_m$, let $b\in\{0,1\}$, and write
\[
\eta=(p,b)\in\Omega_{m+1},
\qquad
s:=N(p).
\]
Then
\[
K_{m+1\to m}(\eta)=b\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\ge F_{m+1}\}}.
\]
More precisely,
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,0)=0,
\]
and
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=
\begin{cases}
0,& s<F_{m+1},\\[4pt]
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2}))
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s-F_{m+1})),
& s\ge F_{m+1}.
\end{cases}
\]
When $s\ge F_{m+1}$, the two admissible words in the second line are distinct;
in particular the defect is nonzero.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
If $b=0$, then $N(p,0)=s$, so
\[
\Fold_{m+1}(p,0)=\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(Z(s))
\]
and therefore
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,0))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\Fold_m(p).
\]
Thus $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,0)=0$.

Assume now that $b=1$.  If $s<F_{m+1}$, then the Zeckendorf expansion of $s$
has no nonzero digit in positions $m,m+1,\dots$.  Consequently
$Z(s+F_{m+2})$ is obtained from $Z(s)$ by inserting a single $1$ in position
$m+1$, and hence
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,1))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\Fold_m(p).
\]
So the defect vanishes.

Suppose finally that $s\ge F_{m+1}$.  Write
\[
s=F_{m+1}+t
\qquad\text{with}\qquad
0\le t<F_{m+2}.
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue},
\[
\Fold_m(p)=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2})).
\]
Since
\[
N(p,1)=s+F_{m+2}=F_{m+3}+t,
\]
the same proposition at level $m+1$ gives
\[
\Fold_{m+1}(p,1)=\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(Z(t)),
\]
and therefore
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,1))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(t))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s-F_{m+1})).
\]
This is the displayed formula.

If the two admissible words in the second line were equal, then
Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue} at level $m$ would imply
$s\bmod F_{m+2}=t=s-F_{m+1}$, which is impossible because
$0<F_{m+1}<F_{m+2}$.  Hence the defect is nonzero.
\end{proof}

\section{Telescoping Across Resolutions}
\label{sec:telescoping}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:global-defect}
Let $n\ge m$.  For $\omega\in\Omega_n$, define the global defect
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\oplus
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))
\in\{0,1\}^m.
\]
\end{definition}

When $n=m$, the definition gives $D_{m\to m}=0$.  For $n>m$ the defect measures
the accumulated noncommutation between the two routes from $\Omega_n$ to $X_m$.

For fixed $n>m$ and $\omega\in\Omega_n$, set
\[
A_j(\omega):=\Fold_j(r_{n\to j}(\omega)),
\qquad
B_j(\omega):=\pi_{n\to j}(\Fold_n(\omega)),
\]
and
\[
\Delta_j(\omega):=A_j(\omega)\oplus B_j(\omega)
\qquad (m\le j\le n).
\]
Then $\Delta_m(\omega)=D_{n\to m}(\omega)$ and $\Delta_n(\omega)=0$.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:adjacent-recursion}
For every $m<j\le n$,
\[
\Delta_{j-1}(\omega)
=
\kappa_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{j\to j-1}(\Delta_j(\omega)).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:prefix-functoriality},
\[
B_{j-1}(\omega)=\pi_{j\to j-1}(B_j(\omega)),
\qquad
A_{j-1}(\omega)=\Fold_{j-1}(r_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))).
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
\Delta_{j-1}(\omega)
&=A_{j-1}(\omega)\oplus B_{j-1}(\omega) \\
&=
\Bigl(A_{j-1}(\omega)\oplus \pi_{j\to j-1}(A_j(\omega))\Bigr)
\oplus
\Bigl(\pi_{j\to j-1}(A_j(\omega))\oplus \pi_{j\to j-1}(B_j(\omega))\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
The first bracket is $\kappa_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))$, while the second
equals $\tau_{j\to j-1}(\Delta_j(\omega))$ because $\tau_{j\to j-1}$ agrees
with $\pi_{j\to j-1}$ on admissible words and is an xor homomorphism.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Telescoping formula]
\label{thm:telescoping}
For every $n>m$ and every $\omega\in\Omega_n$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
\bigoplus_{j=m}^{n-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr).
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We claim that for each $j\in\{m,\dots,n\}$,
\[
\Delta_m(\omega)
=
\tau_{j\to m}(\Delta_j(\omega))
\oplus
\bigoplus_{t=m}^{j-1}
\tau_{t\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{t+1\to t}(r_{n\to t+1}(\omega))\bigr).
\]
For $j=m$ this is tautological.  Assume it holds at some
$j\in\{m,\dots,n-1\}$.  Using Lemma~\ref{lem:adjacent-recursion},
\[
\Delta_j(\omega)
=
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{j+1\to j}(\Delta_{j+1}(\omega)).
\]
Applying $\tau_{j\to m}$ and using Lemma~\ref{lem:prefix-functoriality} gives
\[
\tau_{j\to m}(\Delta_j(\omega))
=
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr)
\oplus
\tau_{j+1\to m}(\Delta_{j+1}(\omega)).
\]
Substituting this into the induction hypothesis proves the claim for $j+1$.
Taking $j=n$ and recalling that $\Delta_n(\omega)=0$ finishes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}
\label{ex:worked-telescoping}
Take $n=3$, $m=1$, and $\omega=111$.  Then
\[
\Fold_1(r_{3\to 1}(111))=1.
\]
Also
\[
N(111)=F_2+F_3+F_4=6=F_2+F_5,
\]
so the Zeckendorf expansion begins with the digits $1001\cdots$.  Hence
\[
\Fold_3(111)=100,
\qquad
\pi_{3\to 1}(\Fold_3(111))=1,
\]
and therefore $D_{3\to 1}(111)=0$.

At the adjacent scales,
\[
\kappa_{2\to 1}(11)=\Fold_1(1)\oplus\pi_{2\to 1}(\Fold_2(11))=1,
\]
while
\[
\kappa_{3\to 2}(111)=\Fold_2(11)\oplus\pi_{3\to 2}(\Fold_3(111))=10.
\]
Applying $\tau_{2\to 1}$ to the second term gives $\tau_{2\to 1}(10)=1$, and
thus
\[
\kappa_{2\to 1}(11)\oplus\tau_{2\to 1}(\kappa_{3\to 2}(111))
=
1\oplus 1
=
0
=
D_{3\to 1}(111).
\]
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Cocycle identity]
\label{prop:cocycle}
If $m<k<n$, then for every $\omega\in\Omega_n$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{k\to m}(D_{n\to k}(\omega)).
\]
Consequently,
\[
\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0
\le
\lvert D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))\rvert_0
+
\lvert D_{n\to k}(\omega)\rvert_0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Split the sum in Theorem~\ref{thm:telescoping} at the intermediate scale $k$:
\[
\begin{aligned}
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
&=
\bigoplus_{j=m}^{k-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr) \\
&\quad\oplus
\bigoplus_{j=k}^{n-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
The first block is exactly $D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))$.  In the second
block, Lemma~\ref{lem:prefix-functoriality} gives
$\tau_{j\to m}=\tau_{k\to m}\circ\tau_{j\to k}$, so the second block is
$\tau_{k\to m}(D_{n\to k}(\omega))$.  This proves the cocycle identity.

The Hamming estimate follows from
\[
\lvert u\oplus v\rvert_0\le \lvert u\rvert_0+\lvert v\rvert_0
\]
and the fact that prefix projection cannot increase Hamming weight.
\end{proof}

For $v\in\{0,1\}^m$, write
\[
\lvert v\rvert_0:=\#\{i:\ v_i\ne 0\}.
\]
Let $d_{\mathrm H}(x,y):=\lvert x\oplus y\rvert_0$ be Hamming distance on
$X_m$.

\begin{corollary}[Coupling bound]
\label{cor:tv-bound}
Let $n>m$, let $\mu$ be a probability measure on $\Omega_n$, and set
\[
X:=\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega)),
\qquad
Y:=\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))
\]
on the probability space $(\Omega_n,\mu)$.  Then
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\Law(X),\Law(Y))
\le
\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}(\omega)\ne 0].
\]
Moreover,
\[
\{D_{n\to m}\ne 0\}
\subseteq
\bigcup_{j=m}^{n-1}
\{\omega_{j+1}=1,\ N(r_{n\to j}(\omega))\ge F_{j+1}\},
\]
and therefore
\[
\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
\le
\min\Biggl\{
1,\
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
\PP_\mu[\omega_{j+1}=1,\ N(r_{n\to j}(\omega))\ge F_{j+1}]
\Biggr\}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Since $X$ and $Y$ are defined on the same probability space, they form a
coupling, and the standard coupling inequality yields
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}(\Law(X),\Law(Y))
\le
\PP_\mu[X\ne Y]
=
\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}\ne 0].
\]
If $D_{n\to m}(\omega)\ne 0$, Theorem~\ref{thm:telescoping} shows that some
local defect
$\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))$ is nonzero.  Writing
$r_{n\to j+1}(\omega)=(r_{n\to j}(\omega),\omega_{j+1})$ and applying
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} proves the event inclusion.  The last
estimate is the union bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Hamming bound]
\label{cor:hamming-bound}
Let $n>m$, let $\mu$ be a probability measure on $\Omega_n$, and let
$f:X_m\to\RR$ be $L$-Lipschitz with respect to $d_{\mathrm H}$.  Then
\[
\bigl|
\EE_\mu f(\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega)))
-
\EE_\mu f(\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega)))
\bigr|
\le
L\,\EE_\mu\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0,
\]
and
\[
\EE_\mu\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0
\le
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
\EE_\mu
\bigl\lvert \kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))\bigr\rvert_0.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The first inequality is immediate from the Lipschitz property:
\[
\bigl|f(X)-f(Y)\bigr|
\le
L\,d_{\mathrm H}(X,Y)
=
L\,\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0.
\]
For the second, use Theorem~\ref{thm:telescoping},
\[
\lvert u\oplus v\rvert_0\le \lvert u\rvert_0+\lvert v\rvert_0,
\]
and the fact that prefix projection cannot increase Hamming weight.
\end{proof}

\section{Uniform Counts and Exact Enumeration}
\label{sec:uniform}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:uniform-dp}
For $j\ge 0$ and $t\in\ZZ$, with the conventions
$\Omega_0=\{\varnothing\}$ and $N(\varnothing)=0$, define
\[
A_j(t):=\#\{p\in\Omega_j:\ N(p)=t\},
\]
and set $A_j(t)=0$ for $t<0$.  Then
\[
A_0(0)=1,
\qquad
A_0(t)=0\ \text{ for }t\ne 0,
\]
and for every $j\ge 0$,
\[
A_{j+1}(t)=A_j(t)+A_j(t-F_{j+2})
\qquad (t\in\ZZ).
\]
If $j\ge 1$, then under the uniform law on $\Omega_{j+1}$,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_{j+1}}[K_{j+1\to j}=1]
=
2^{-(j+1)}
\sum_{t\ge F_{j+1}}A_j(t).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each word in $\Omega_{j+1}$ has the form $(p,b)$ with $p\in\Omega_j$ and
$b\in\{0,1\}$, and
\[
N(p,b)=N(p)+bF_{j+2}.
\]
This gives the recursion for $A_{j+1}(t)$.

For the probability formula, Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} shows that
$K_{j+1\to j}(p,b)=1$ if and only if $b=1$ and $N(p)\ge F_{j+1}$.  Counting
such pairs under the uniform law on $\Omega_{j+1}$ yields the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Exact one-step count under the uniform law]
\label{thm:explicit-count}
For $m\ge 1$, let
\[
C_m:=\#\{\alpha\in\Omega_m:\ N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}\}.
\]
Then
\[
C_1=1,
\qquad
C_2=2,
\qquad
C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}\quad (m\ge 3).
\]
Consequently,
\[
C_{2r}=\frac{2^{2r}+2}{3},
\qquad
C_{2r+1}=\frac{2^{2r+1}+1}{3}
\qquad (r\ge 1),
\]
and
\[
\PP_{\Unif_{m+1}}[K_{m+1\to m}=1]
=
\frac{2^m-C_m}{2^{m+1}}
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^{m+1}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider the last two digits of $\alpha\in\Omega_m$.

If $\alpha_m=1$, then $N(\alpha)\ge F_{m+1}$, so no such word contributes to
$C_m$.

If $\alpha_m=0$ and $\alpha_{m-1}=0$, then
\[
N(\alpha)=N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})
\le
\sum_{k=1}^{m-2}F_{k+1}
=
F_{m+1}-2
<
F_{m+1},
\]
so every choice of the first $m-2$ digits is allowed.  This contributes
$2^{m-2}$ words.

If $\alpha_m=0$ and $\alpha_{m-1}=1$, then
\[
N(\alpha)=F_m+N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2}),
\]
and the condition $N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}$ becomes
$N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})<F_{m-1}$.  This contributes $C_{m-2}$ words.

Hence $C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}$ for $m\ge 3$.  The initial values are immediate:
$C_1=1$ because only $0$ has value $<F_2=1$, and $C_2=2$ because the words
$00$ and $10$ are exactly those of value $<F_3=2$.

The closed forms follow by unrolling the recurrence.  Setting $C_0:=1$, one
finds
\[
C_{2r}=2^{2r-2}+2^{2r-4}+\cdots+2^0+C_0=\frac{2^{2r}+2}{3},
\]
and
\[
C_{2r+1}=2^{2r-1}+2^{2r-3}+\cdots+2^1+C_1=\frac{2^{2r+1}+1}{3}.
\]
Finally, Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} shows that
$K_{m+1\to m}(\alpha,b)=1$ exactly when $b=1$ and $N(\alpha)\ge F_{m+1}$, so
there are $2^m-C_m$ defect-producing words in $\Omega_{m+1}$.  Dividing by
$2^{m+1}$ gives the probability formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:uniform-global-bound}
For $n>m$,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
\le
\min\Biggl\{
1,\
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}\frac{\lfloor 2^{j+1}/3\rfloor}{2^{j+1}}
\Biggr\}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Corollary~\ref{cor:tv-bound} with $\mu=\Unif_n$.  For each $j$, the
prefix $r_{n\to j+1}(\omega)$ is uniform on $\Omega_{j+1}$, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:explicit-count} computes the corresponding one-step defect
probability.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:uniform-hamming}
For $n>m$,
\[
\EE_{\Unif_n}\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0
\le
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
2^{-(j+1)}
\sum_{\substack{p\in\Omega_j\\ N(p)\ge F_{j+1}}}
\bigl\lvert \kappa_{j+1\to j}(p,1)\bigr\rvert_0.
\]
Consequently, if $f:X_m\to\RR$ is $L$-Lipschitz with respect to
$d_{\mathrm H}$, then
\[
\bigl|
\EE_{\Unif_n}f(\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega)))
-
\EE_{\Unif_n}f(\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega)))
\bigr|
\le
L
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
2^{-(j+1)}
\sum_{\substack{p\in\Omega_j\\ N(p)\ge F_{j+1}}}
\bigl\lvert \kappa_{j+1\to j}(p,1)\bigr\rvert_0.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Corollary~\ref{cor:hamming-bound} with $\mu=\Unif_n$.  For fixed $j$, the
prefix $r_{n\to j+1}(\omega)$ is uniform on $\Omega_{j+1}$, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} shows that only words of the form $(p,1)$
with $N(p)\ge F_{j+1}$ contribute.
\end{proof}

For $n>m$ and $v\in\{0,1\}^m$, define
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v):=2^{-n}\#\{\omega\in\Omega_n:\ D_{n\to m}(\omega)=v\}.
\]
Thus $\mu_{n,m}$ is the exact defect law under the uniform raw-word measure.

\begin{proposition}[Exact recursion for the defect law]
\label{prop:vector-recursion}
For $n>m$ and $v\in\{0,1\}^m$,
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v)
=
2^{-n}
\sum_{p\in\Omega_{n-1}}
\sum_{b\in\{0,1\}}
\mathbf{1}
\Bigl\{
D_{n-1\to m}(p)
\oplus
\tau_{n-1\to m}(\kappa_{n\to n-1}(p,b))
=
v
\Bigr\}.
\]
In particular,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\sum_{v\ne 0}\mu_{n,m}(v).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:cocycle} with $k=n-1$:
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
D_{n-1\to m}(r_{n\to n-1}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{n-1\to m}(\kappa_{n\to n-1}(\omega)).
\]
Under the uniform law on $\Omega_n$, the prefix $r_{n\to n-1}(\omega)$ runs
uniformly through $\Omega_{n-1}$, and each prefix $p$ is paired with the two
choices $b\in\{0,1\}$.  Counting the pairs $(p,b)$ producing a prescribed
vector $v$ gives the formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:zero-barrier}
Let $m\ge 1$, let $p\in\Omega_m$, and let $b\in\{0,1\}$.  Then
\[
\tau_{m+1\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,b)\bigr)=0.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $b=0$, Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} gives
$\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,0)=0$.

If $b=1$, write
\[
s:=N(p,0)=N(p).
\]
Since the $(m+1)$-st digit of $(p,0)$ is zero,
\[
s\le \sum_{k=1}^m F_{k+1}=F_{m+2}-2<F_{m+2}.
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} at level $m+2\to m+1$ therefore gives
$\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,1)=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:double-one-reduction}
Let $m\ge 1$, let $p\in\Omega_m$, and let $b\in\{0,1\}$.  Then
\[
\tau_{m+2\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,1,b)\bigr)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,b).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If $b=0$, both sides vanish by Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold}.

Assume $b=1$ and write
\[
s:=N(p).
\]
Since $p\in\Omega_m$,
\[
0\le s\le F_{m+3}-2.
\]
At level $m+3\to m+2$, the prefix $(p,1,1)$ has value
\[
N(p,1,1)=s+F_{m+2}+F_{m+3}=s+F_{m+4}.
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} therefore yields
\[
\tau_{m+2\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,1,1)\bigr)
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2})).
\]

If $s<F_{m+1}$, then $s+F_{m+2}<F_{m+3}$ and the Zeckendorf expansion of
$s+F_{m+2}$ is obtained by inserting a single $1$ in position $m+1$ above
$Z(s)$.  Hence
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2}))=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s)),
\]
so the left-hand side is zero.  Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} gives
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=0$ in the same range.

Assume now that $s\ge F_{m+1}$.  Since $s<F_{m+3}$,
\[
0\le s-F_{m+1}<F_{m+2},
\]
and
\[
s+F_{m+2}=F_{m+3}+(s-F_{m+1}).
\]
Thus the first $m$ digits of $Z(s+F_{m+2})$ are exactly those of
$Z(s-F_{m+1})$, so
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2}))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s-F_{m+1})).
\]
Also,
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2}))
\]
because $s<F_{m+3}$.  Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold} at level
$m+1\to m$ therefore gives
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s+F_{m+2}))
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1).
\]
This proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Collapse of the multiscale defect law]\textbf{[New-1 | 2026-04-17]}
\label{thm:tail-collapse}
Fix $n>m$ and write $\ell:=n-m$.  Define
\[
\beta:\bigsqcup_{r\ge 0}\Omega_r\longrightarrow\{0,1\}
\]
by the recursion
\[
\beta(\varnothing)=0,
\qquad
\beta(0\alpha)=0,
\qquad
\beta(10\alpha)=1,
\qquad
\beta(11\alpha)=\beta(\alpha).
\]
Then for every $p\in\Omega_m$ and every $q\in\Omega_\ell$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(p,q)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q)).
\]
For $v\in\{0,1\}^m$, set
\[
\lambda_m(v):=2^{-m}\#\{p\in\Omega_m:\ \kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=v\},
\]
and for $\ell\ge 0$ set
\[
\rho_\ell:=2^{-\ell}\#\{q\in\Omega_\ell:\ \beta(q)=1\}.
\]
Then
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v)=
\begin{cases}
(1-\rho_\ell)+\rho_\ell\lambda_m(0),& v=0,\\[4pt]
\rho_\ell\lambda_m(v),& v\ne 0.
\end{cases}
\]
Moreover,
\[
\rho_\ell=\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}
\qquad (\ell\ge 0),
\]
and therefore
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\rho_{n-m}\,\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^m}
=
\frac{(2^{n-m}-(-1)^{n-m})\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{3\cdot 2^n}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $r\ge 0$, for $p\in\Omega_m$, and for $q\in\Omega_r$, write
\[
E_r(p,q):=D_{m+r\to m}(p,q).
\]
The identity
\[
E_r(p,q)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(q))
\]
is established by induction on $r$.

For $r=0$, one has $q=\varnothing$, $E_0(p,\varnothing)=0$, and
$\beta(\varnothing)=0$.

Assume $r\ge 1$ and that the claim holds for shorter tails.

If $q=0\alpha$, then Proposition~\ref{prop:cocycle} with the intermediate scale
$m+1$ gives
\[
E_r(p,0\alpha)
=
D_{m+1\to m}(p,0)
\oplus
\tau_{m+1\to m}(D_{m+r\to m+1}(p,0,\alpha)).
\]
The first term is zero.  The induction hypothesis at level $m+1$ gives
\[
D_{m+r\to m+1}(p,0,\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+2\to m+1}(p,0,\beta(\alpha)).
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:zero-barrier} then implies $E_r(p,0\alpha)=0$, which equals
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(0\alpha))$.

If $q=10\alpha$, then Proposition~\ref{prop:cocycle} with the intermediate
scale $m+2$ gives
\[
E_r(p,10\alpha)
=
D_{m+2\to m}(p,1,0)
\oplus
\tau_{m+2\to m}(D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,0,\alpha)).
\]
Since
\[
N(p,1,0)=N(p,1),
\]
the first term is exactly $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)$.  The induction hypothesis
at level $m+2$ yields
\[
D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,0,\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,0,\beta(\alpha)),
\]
and Lemma~\ref{lem:zero-barrier}, applied with the $(m+1)$-digit prefix
$(p,1)$, shows that its projection to level $m$ is zero.  Hence
\[
E_r(p,10\alpha)=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(10\alpha)).
\]

If $q=11\alpha$, then Proposition~\ref{prop:cocycle} with the intermediate
scale $m+2$ gives
\[
E_r(p,11\alpha)
=
D_{m+2\to m}(p,1,1)
\oplus
\tau_{m+2\to m}(D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,1,\alpha)).
\]
Now
\[
N(p,1,1)=N(p)+F_{m+2}+F_{m+3}=N(p)+F_{m+4}.
\]
Since $N(p)<F_{m+3}$, the Zeckendorf expansion of $N(p,1,1)$ is obtained by
placing an isolated digit $1$ in position $m+3$ above $Z(N(p))$.  Therefore
\[
D_{m+2\to m}(p,1,1)=0.
\]
The induction hypothesis at level $m+2$ gives
\[
D_{m+r\to m+2}(p,1,1,\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+3\to m+2}(p,1,1,\beta(\alpha)).
\]
Lemma~\ref{lem:double-one-reduction} then yields
\[
E_r(p,11\alpha)
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(\alpha))
=
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,\beta(11\alpha)).
\]
This completes the induction.

Set
\[
B_\ell:=\#\{q\in\Omega_\ell:\ \beta(q)=1\}.
\]
The defining recursion for $\beta$ gives
\[
B_0=0,
\qquad
B_1=1,
\qquad
B_\ell=2^{\ell-2}+B_{\ell-2}\quad (\ell\ge 2),
\]
because
\[
\Omega_\ell=(0\Omega_{\ell-1})\sqcup(10\Omega_{\ell-2})\sqcup(11\Omega_{\ell-2})
\]
is a disjoint union.  The closed form
\[
B_\ell=\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3}
\]
follows by induction, and therefore
\[
\rho_\ell=\frac{B_\ell}{2^\ell}
=
\frac{2^\ell-(-1)^\ell}{3\cdot 2^\ell}.
\]

If $v\ne 0$, the established identity shows that
$D_{n\to m}(p,q)=v$ holds if and only if $\beta(q)=1$ and
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=v$.  Hence
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v)=\rho_\ell\lambda_m(v)
\qquad (v\ne 0).
\]
The formula at $v=0$ follows from the decomposition into the cases
$\beta(q)=0$ and $\beta(q)=1$.

Finally,
\[
\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]
=
\rho_\ell\sum_{v\ne 0}\lambda_m(v).
\]
By Theorem~\ref{thm:explicit-count},
\[
\sum_{v\ne 0}\lambda_m(v)
=
2^{-m}\#\{p\in\Omega_m:\ \kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)\ne 0\}
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^m}.
\]
Substituting the formula for $\rho_\ell$ proves the last identity.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:scalar-recursion-failure}
The recursion in Proposition~\ref{prop:vector-recursion} is exact, but it does
not close on the scalar event $\{D_{n\to m}\ne 0\}$ after the upper tail is
completely marginalized.  Theorem~\ref{thm:tail-collapse} shows that adjoining
the single tail bit $\beta$ is sufficient: the full vector law then collapses
to the adjacent one-step law, and the scalar event acquires an exact closed
form.  Appendix~\ref{app:supplementary} records explicit small-scale
cancellations before this reduction is imposed.
\end{remark}

\section{Compatible Lifts}
\label{sec:lifts}

Let
\[
\Xinf:=\{x=(x_1,x_2,\dots)\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}:\ x_ix_{i+1}=0\text{ for all }i\ge 1\}
\]
be the one-sided golden-mean shift.  For a raw sequence
$\omega^\infty=(\omega_1,\omega_2,\dots)\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}$, write
\[
r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty):=(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m)\in\Omega_m.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Eventual compatibility]
\label{prop:eventual-compatibility}
Let $\omega^\infty\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}$ and define
\[
x_m:=\Fold_m(r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty))\in X_m.
\]
For each $m\ge 1$, set
\[
\delta_m:=x_m\oplus \pi_{m+1\to m}(x_{m+1})\in\{0,1\}^m.
\]
Then
\[
\delta_m=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(r_{\infty\to m+1}(\omega^\infty)).
\]
Fix $M\ge 1$.  There exists $x^\infty\in\Xinf$ with
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)=x_m
\qquad\text{for all }m\ge M
\]
if and only if
\[
\delta_m=0
\qquad\text{for all }m\ge M.
\]
When these equivalent conditions hold, the lift $x^\infty$ is unique.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If such an $x^\infty$ exists, then for every $m\ge M$,
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(x_{m+1})
=
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(x^\infty))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)
=
x_m,
\]
so $\delta_m=0$.

Conversely, assume that $\delta_m=0$ for all $m\ge M$.  Then
\[
x_m=\pi_{m+1\to m}(x_{m+1})
\qquad\text{for every }m\ge M,
\]
so the family $(x_m)_{m\ge M}$ is compatible.  For each $i\ge 1$, choose any
$j\ge \max\{M,i\}$ and set
\[
x^\infty_i:=(x_j)_i.
\]
This is well defined because compatibility implies
$\pi_{j'\to j}(x_{j'})=x_j$ whenever $j'\ge j\ge M$.  Hence
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)=x_m
\qquad (m\ge M).
\]
Since every adjacent pair $(x^\infty_i,x^\infty_{i+1})$ appears in some
admissible prefix $x_j\in X_j$, we have $x^\infty\in\Xinf$.  Uniqueness is
obvious from the compatibility of the prefixes.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:summable-defects}
In the setting of Proposition~\ref{prop:eventual-compatibility}, the following
conditions are equivalent:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item $\sum_{m\ge 1}\lvert \delta_m\rvert_0<\infty$;
\item $\delta_m=0$ for all sufficiently large $m$;
\item there exists $M\ge 1$ such that for every $m\ge M$,
\[
\omega^\infty_{m+1}=0
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
N(r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty))<F_{m+1}.
\]
\end{enumerate}
When these conditions hold, there exists a unique $x^\infty\in\Xinf$ such that
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)=x_m
\qquad\text{for all sufficiently large }m.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The equivalence of \textup{(i)} and \textup{(ii)} is immediate because each
$\lvert \delta_m\rvert_0$ is a nonnegative integer.

For \textup{(ii)}$\Leftrightarrow$\textup{(iii)}, use
\[
\delta_m=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(r_{\infty\to m+1}(\omega^\infty))
\]
and apply Theorem~\ref{thm:adjacent-threshold}.

The final assertion is exactly Proposition~\ref{prop:eventual-compatibility}.
\end{proof}

\section{Finite-window Normalization}
\label{app:finite-window}

We collect here the routine finite-window facts used in the body of the paper.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:max-prefix-value}
For $m\ge 1$,
\[
\max_{x\in X_m}N(x)=F_{m+2}-1.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $M_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}N(x)$ and set $M_0:=0$.  Then $M_1=1=F_3-1$.  For
$m\ge 2$, either $x_m=0$, in which case $N(x)\le M_{m-1}$, or $x_m=1$, in
which case $x_{m-1}=0$ and
\[
N(x)=F_{m+1}+N(x_1,\dots,x_{m-2})\le F_{m+1}+M_{m-2}.
\]
Thus
\[
M_m=\max\{M_{m-1},\,F_{m+1}+M_{m-2}\}.
\]
Using the induction hypothesis,
\[
M_m=\max\{F_{m+1}-1,\,F_{m+1}+F_m-1\}=F_{m+2}-1.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:fold-residue}
For every $m\ge 1$ and every $\omega\in\Omega_m$, write
\[
N(\omega)=qF_{m+2}+r,
\qquad
q\in\{0,1\},
\qquad
0\le r<F_{m+2}.
\]
Then
\[
\Fold_m(\omega)=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(r)).
\]
Equivalently, for $x\in X_m$,
\[
\Fold_m(\omega)=x
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
N(\omega)\equiv N(x)\pmod{F_{m+2}}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since
\[
N(\omega)\le \sum_{k=1}^mF_{k+1}=F_{m+3}-2<2F_{m+2},
\]
we have $q\in\{0,1\}$.  If $q=0$, there is nothing to prove.  If $q=1$, then
\[
0\le r=N(\omega)-F_{m+2}\le F_{m+1}-2<F_{m+1}.
\]
Hence $Z(r)$ has no nonzero digit in positions $m,m+1,\dots$, so appending a
single $1$ in position $m+1$ produces the Zeckendorf expansion of
$N(\omega)=F_{m+2}+r$.  Its first $m$ digits are exactly those of $Z(r)$,
proving the first claim.

For the equivalence, note that any $x\in X_m$ is already the Zeckendorf
expansion of its own value $N(x)$, and Lemma~\ref{lem:max-prefix-value} gives
$N(x)\le F_{m+2}-1$.  Thus $\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(r))=x$ if and only if
$r=N(x)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:prefix-functoriality}
If $n\ge k\ge m$, then
\[
\pi_{n\to m}=\pi_{k\to m}\circ \pi_{n\to k},
\qquad
r_{n\to m}=r_{k\to m}\circ r_{n\to k},
\qquad
\tau_{n\to m}=\tau_{k\to m}\circ \tau_{n\to k}.
\]
Moreover, for all $u,v\in\{0,1\}^n$,
\[
\tau_{n\to m}(u\oplus v)=\tau_{n\to m}(u)\oplus\tau_{n\to m}(v).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Everything is immediate from the definition of prefix truncation.
\end{proof}

\section{Supplementary Examples and Small Values}
\label{app:supplementary}

The next two examples explain why the defect observable is not captured by the
usual scalar indicators associated with normalization or with the top adjacent
step.

\begin{example}[Normalization cascade without a truncation defect]
\label{ex:cascade-without-defect}
Take $m=4$, $p=1100$, and append $b=1$.  Normalizing the raw word
$(p,1)=11001$ produces $\Fold_5(11001)=00101$, so the normalization does
involve a nontrivial carry cascade.  Nevertheless
\[
\Fold_4(p)=0010
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{5\to 4}(\Fold_5(11001))=0010,
\]
hence $\kappa_{5\to 4}(p,1)=0$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[A lower-level defect with trivial top indicator]
\label{ex:defect-without-top-indicator}
Let $n=4$, $m=2$, and $\omega=0110$.  Direct computation gives
\[
D_{4\to 2}(\omega)=(0,1)\ne 0,
\]
while the top adjacent indicator satisfies
\[
K_{4\to 3}(\omega)=0.
\]
Thus the global defect may be created strictly below the top scale.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:small-values}
The exact probabilities $P_{n,m}:=\PP_{\Unif_n}[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]$ obtained by
direct enumeration for $n\le 5$ are listed in Table~\ref{tab:small-values}.
In particular,
\[
P_{2,1}=\frac14,
\qquad
P_{3,1}=\frac18,
\qquad
P_{4,1}=\frac{3}{16},
\]
so the scalar probabilities are not monotone in $n$ at fixed $m$.  Combined
with Example~\ref{ex:defect-without-top-indicator}, this is enough to see that
the exact recursion does not close on scalar events alone.
\end{remark}
