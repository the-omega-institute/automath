\begin{abstract}
Let $\Fold_m$ denote Zeckendorf evaluation of a binary window followed by
normalization and truncation back to length $m$.  For $m\le n$ and
$\omega\in\Omega_n$ we compare $\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))$ with
$\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))$.  Their xor difference is a global defect
$D_{n\to m}$ obtained by summing adjacent local defects
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}$ across the resolution band $m,\dots,n-1$.  The only new
arithmetic inputs of this short note are (i) the threshold criterion of
Proposition~3.2, which states that $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,b)$ is nonzero exactly
when $b=1$ and the Fibonacci value of $p$ crosses $F_{m+1}$, and
(ii) the closed-form adjacent defect probability of Theorem~5.3, namely
$\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor/2^{m+1}$ via the recurrence $C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}$ for
below-threshold words.  Once these local statements are recorded, the remaining
sections mechanically unpack the xor-telescoping identity: deterministic bounds
on total variation and Hamming distance, a cocycle relation across intermediate
levels, an inverse-limit compatibility test, and tables of the exact small-scale
probabilities $P_{n,m}=\PP[D_{n\to m}\ne 0]$ that illustrate how quickly
scalar heuristics saturate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

This paper isolates one obstruction in the resolution tower of the Zeckendorf
normalization/truncation maps informally denoted by $\Fold$.  For
$\omega\in\Omega_n$ and
$m\le n$, we compare the two words
\[
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega)).
\]
Here $\Fold_m$ means: evaluate the $m$-bit window in the Fibonacci basis,
normalize the result to an admissible Zeckendorf word, and truncate back to
length $m$ (see Section~\ref{sec:folding-naive-truncation} for the formal
definition).
Their xor-difference is the global defect $D_{n\to m}(\omega)$.

Direct truncation on raw windows does not commute with Zeckendorf
normalization-plus-truncation, so the mismatch $D_{n\to m}$ is genuine and
structured.  At each adjacent step $m+1\to m$ there is a local defect
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}$, and across a whole resolution band there is the global
defect $D_{n\to m}$.  The bookkeeping backbone is the formal xor-telescoping
identity
\[
\text{global defect}
\;=\;
\text{sum of prefix-truncated local defects}.
\]
The sum is taken in the xor group $(\ZZ/2\ZZ)^m$, so the decomposition is exact
and completely explicit.  The Fold-specific arithmetic content lies in the
threshold classification of the adjacent defect and in the exact counts derived
from that threshold.

The new content of this note consists entirely of two local inputs and their
immediate consequences.  First, Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold}
provides the sharp threshold description: for $\eta=(p,b)\in\Omega_{m+1}$,
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,b)$ is nonzero exactly when $b=1$ and the prefix value
$N(p)$ crosses the level $F_{m+1}$.  Second, Theorem~\ref{thm:fold-defect-count-explicit}
gives the exact closed-form count for that event under the uniform raw-word law.
Everything else is formal unpacking of the xor decomposition: recording the
adjacent defect observable, propagating it across a finite band, and tabulating
the resulting global probabilities for small $(n,m)$.  Among the two inputs,
Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold} is the conceptual heart of the
note, while Theorem~\ref{thm:fold-defect-count-explicit} packages its
uniform-law consequences.

These statements should be compared with two classical one-parameter families
of Zeckendorf observables: successor carry statistics and digit-frequency laws.
Carry lengths and sequentiality for admissible words have been analyzed in
detail in the transducer literature
\cite{Frougny1996Successor,Frougny1997Sequential,FrougnySolomyak1996,Frougny1999,%
AhlbachUsatineFrougnyPippenger2013,BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020,FrougnySakarovitch1999},
while block distributions and digit proportions go back to
\cite{Grabner1995,Griffiths2010,DrmotaSteiner2002,KologluKoppMillerWang2011,MillerWang2012,%
CoquetVanDenBosch1986,Steiner2005}.  The observable recorded here compares two
competing morphisms---truncate then normalize versus normalize then project---so
it takes values in $(\ZZ/2\ZZ)^m$ and cannot be recovered from a scalar carry
length or from the individual digit densities considered in the references
above.  Proposition-level evidence for that distinction appears already in
Examples~\ref{ex:carry-without-defect} and~\ref{ex:defect-without-carry}, which
exhibit a nonzero carry with no truncation defect and vice versa.

The point of this paper is therefore narrow and fully deterministic:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{naive truncation}
&\not\!\circ\,\text{Fold} \\
&\Longrightarrow
\text{local defect} \\
&\Longrightarrow
\text{threshold classification and exact counts} \\
&\Longrightarrow
\text{formal telescoping law} \\
&\Longrightarrow
\text{explicit bounds and compatibility criteria}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Sections~4--6 are therefore recorded as a short compendium of deterministic
consequences: each statement is a remark-level corollary of the threshold and
count inputs, included only so that the telescoping dictionary is available in
one place.

We use only minimal background on Zeckendorf normalization and symbolic
systems; see
\cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972,Carlitz1968,CarlitzScovHoggatt1972,%
Frougny1991,Frougny1992,Frougny1999,FrougnyS2010,Berstel1986,FrougnySakarovitch1999}
and
\cite{LabbeLepsova2023,MarquesTrojovsky2025,Fraenkel1985,%
AhlbachUsatineFrougnyPippenger2013,BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020,%
HieronymiTerry2018,ShallitShan2024,Hosten2024,KologluKoppMillerWang2011,MillerWang2012,%
DrmotaSteiner2002,Griffiths2010,CoquetVanDenBosch1986,Steiner2005} for Fibonacci

\cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,WaltersErgodicTheory} for the

\subsection{Related Work and Positioning}

Zeckendorf representations and Fibonacci numeration have a substantial
literature.  The foundational uniqueness results go back to
\cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}, with representation-theoretic
developments in \cite{Carlitz1968,CarlitzScovHoggatt1972} and automata-theoretic
and normalization viewpoints in
\cite{Frougny1991,Frougny1992,Frougny1999,FrougnyS2010,Fraenkel1985}.  Efficient
algorithms for Zeckendorf addition and carry normalization are studied in
\cite{AhlbachUsatineFrougnyPippenger2013}, and the successor/carry observable in
non-classical numeration systems is analyzed in
\cite{Frougny1996Successor,Frougny1997Sequential,FrougnySolomyak1996,BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020}.
The Ostrowski numeration perspective and its connection to finite automata,
including the identification of Zeckendorf as the golden-ratio Ostrowski case,
appear in \cite{HieronymiTerry2018}.  Automata-theoretic properties of Fibonacci
representations are developed further in \cite{ShallitShan2024}, and digit
variation and mixing in the Zeckendorf setting in \cite{Hosten2024}.  Recent
Fibonacci-transducer developments appear in
\cite{LabbeLepsova2023,MarquesTrojovsky2025}.  Broader numeration-system
perspectives may be found in
\cite{Grabner1995,Berstel1986,BerstelReutenauer2011}.

The necessary literature comparisons can be organized into three parallel lanes:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{Normalization/successor analyses} focus on how carries propagate

\item \emph{Numeration dynamics and odometers} model normalization via finite

\cite{Parry1964,WaltersErgodicTheory,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,Boyle1983,KitchensSchmidt1989,BertheRigo2007,BaratGrabner2014}.

\item \emph{Zeckendorf digit-block and density questions} deal with local digit

The defect observable $\kappa_{m+1\to m}$ is therefore a bookkeeping tool for

\subsection{Concrete Comparison with Carry-type Observables}

Two short computations make the distinction precise.

\begin{example}[Carry propagation without a truncation defect]
\label{ex:carry-without-defect}
Take $m=4$, $p=1100$, and append $b=1$.  Normalizing the raw word
$(p,b)=11001$ produces $\Fold_5(11001)=00101$, so the standard successor/carry
observable registers a nontrivial propagation.  Nevertheless
$\Fold_4(p)=0010$ and $\pi_{5\to 4}(\Fold_5(11001))=0010$, hence
$\kappa_{5\to 4}(p,1)=0$.  A genuine carry event can therefore leave the
adjacent truncation defect unchanged.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Nonzero defect without a top-level carry indicator]
\label{ex:defect-without-carry}
Let $n=4$, $m=2$, and $\omega=0110$.  Direct computation gives
$D_{4\to 2}(\omega)=(0,1)\neq 0$, while the adjacent indicator satisfies
$K_{4\to 3}(\omega)=0$.  The xor-valued global defect records an obstruction
arising two levels below the top, so a scalar carry-length statistic at the top
scale does not determine $D_{n\to m}$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Theorem-by-theorem comparison with prior work}

Lemma~\ref{lem:max-value-admissible-prefix} is a standard finite-window
corollary of the Lekkerkerker--Zeckendorf existence/uniqueness theorem
\cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}, and we use it only to pin down the
maximal admissible prefix value $F_{m+2}-1$.  Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue-characterization}
packages the normalization viewpoint of Fibonacci-representation automata
\cite{Frougny1991} into a paper-specific residue criterion for the finite-window
map $\Fold_m$.  Lemma~\ref{lem:pi-functorial-golden} is the formal
functoriality of prefix projections in one-sided symbolic dynamics
\cite{LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}; it is recorded only because the later xor
identities invoke it repeatedly.

Definition~\ref{def:fold-local-curvature-defect} is the first genuinely
paper-specific observable: relative to the scalar carry measurements studied for
the successor function \cite{Frougny1996Successor,BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020},
it records an xor vector comparing truncate-then-fold against fold-then-project.
Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold} is the main local increment,
because it isolates the exact Fibonacci threshold $N(p)\ge F_{m+1}$ for a
nonzero adjacent defect and gives the corresponding explicit xor formula,
rather than an averaged carry statistic or a sequentiality statement
\cite{Frougny1997Sequential,BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020}.  Definition~\ref{def:fold-global-stokes-defect}
then lifts that new observable from one adjacent scale to an entire resolution
band; we are not aware of an earlier Fibonacci-normalization paper that treats
this band-scale xor defect as a separate object.

Lemma~\ref{lem:fold-defect-adjacent-recursion} is a formal one-step identity
once the defect observable and prefix maps are fixed, so we do not claim an
independent literature increment there.  Proposition~\ref{thm:fold-discrete-stokes-defect}
is the corresponding exact band identity: its role is not to import new
arithmetic, but to expose the global defect as the xor sum of projected local
defects.  Proposition~\ref{prop:uniform-adjacent-defect-counts} is likewise a
bookkeeping corollary that converts the threshold event into a raw-word counting
recursion reminiscent of automata-based Zeckendorf counting methods
\cite{Frougny1991,AhlbachUsatineFrougnyPippenger2013}.  Theorem~\ref{thm:fold-defect-count-explicit}
is the second genuine increment of the paper, since it closes that recursion to
the exact uniform-law formula $\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor/2^{m+1}$, which does
not appear in the carry-propagation literature
\cite{BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020}.

Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-defect-cocycle} is another formal consequence of the
telescoping decomposition, included because it clarifies how the defect
factorizes across intermediate resolutions.  Proposition~\ref{prop:exact-vector-recursion}
records the full defect-vector recursion needed for exact finite-$n$ counts;
its contribution is bookkeeping rather than new numeration theory.
Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-naive-prefix-lift} specializes the standard
compatibility-of-prefixes principle for one-sided shifts
\cite{LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998} to the Fold-generated family $(x_m)$, so the
paper-specific gain is the reformulation in terms of eventual vanishing of the
adjacent discrepancies.

\section{Folding and Naive Truncation}
\label{sec:folding-naive-truncation}

\subsection{Minimal Setup}

\noindent\textbf{Indexing convention.}
Throughout this paper, digits are indexed \emph{low-order first}: position $k$
carries Fibonacci weight $F_{k+1}$, where $F_1=F_2=1$ and
$F_{j+2}=F_{j+1}+F_j$.  Thus position $1$ has weight $F_2=1$ and position $2$
has weight $F_3=2$.  For a two-bit word, $N(10)=F_2=1$ (the nonzero digit sits
at position $1$, the lowest-order place) and $N(01)=F_3=2$ (the nonzero digit
sits at position $2$).  The word $10$ represents the number $1$; the word $01$
represents the number $2$.  The map ``$r_{n\to m}$'' (prefix truncation) retains
the \emph{low-order} end: $r_{n\to m}(\omega_1\cdots\omega_n)=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m$.
This convention is opposite to that of several carry-propagation papers which index
from the high-order end; every numerical example in this paper follows the
low-order-first convention.

\[
\begin{array}{c|cccc}
\text{position} & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ \hline
\text{weight} & F_2=1 & F_3=2 & F_4=3 & F_5=5
\end{array}
\qquad
\text{(low-order digits sit on the left).}
\]

For $m\ge 1$, let
\[
\Omega_m:=\{0,1\}^m
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
X_m:=\{x=(x_1,\dots,x_m)\in\{0,1\}^m:\ x_ix_{i+1}=0\text{ for }1\le i<m\}.
\]
Thus $X_m$ is the set of admissible words in the one-sided golden-mean shift.

For $\omega=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m\in\Omega_m$, define its Fibonacci value by
\[
N(\omega):=\sum_{k=1}^m \omega_k F_{k+1}.
\]
Here $(F_j)_{j\ge 1}$ denotes the Fibonacci sequence
$F_1=F_2=1$, $F_{j+2}=F_{j+1}+F_j$.
For $N\ge 0$, define
\[
Z(N)=(z_k)_{k\ge 1}\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}
\]
to be the unique finitely supported sequence satisfying
\[
N=\sum_{k\ge 1} z_k F_{k+1}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
z_k z_{k+1}=0\text{ for all }k\ge 1.
\]
Thus digit position $k$ carries Fibonacci weight $F_{k+1}$, and $Z(N)$ is the
Zeckendorf expansion of $N$ \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}; we set
$Z(0)=0^\infty$.  The existence and uniqueness of $Z(N)$ for every $N\ge 0$
are classical \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972}.  When a finite admissible
word $x=(x_1,\dots,x_m)\in X_m$ is compared with $Z(N)$, it is identified with
the zero-padded infinite sequence $(x_1,\dots,x_m,0,0,\dots)\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}$.  We define the fold map by
\[
\Fold_m(\omega):=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(N(\omega)))\in X_m,
\]
where $\pi_{\infty\to m}$ truncates an infinite legal sequence to its first $m$
digits.  Equivalently, $\Fold_m$ is Fibonacci evaluation followed by
Zeckendorf normalization and truncation.

\begin{lemma}[Maximum Fibonacci value on $X_m$]
\label{lem:max-value-admissible-prefix}
For $m\ge 1$,
\[
\max_{x\in X_m} N(x)=F_{m+2}-1.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $M_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}N(x)$, and set $M_0:=0$.  Then $M_1=1=F_3-1$.  For
$m\ge 2$, every $x\in X_m$ satisfies either $x_m=0$, in which case
$N(x)\le M_{m-1}$, or $x_m=1$, in which case $x_{m-1}=0$ and
\[
N(x)=F_{m+1}+N(x_1,\dots,x_{m-2})\le F_{m+1}+M_{m-2}.
\]
Hence
\[
M_m=\max\{M_{m-1},\,F_{m+1}+M_{m-2}\}.
\]
Using the induction hypothesis $M_{m-1}=F_{m+1}-1$ and
$M_{m-2}=F_m-1$, we obtain
\[
M_m=\max\{F_{m+1}-1,\,F_{m+1}+F_m-1\}=F_{m+2}-1.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Finite-window residue characterization of $\Fold_m$]
\label{prop:fold-residue-characterization}
For every $m\ge 1$ and every $\omega\in\Omega_m$, write
\[
N(\omega)=qF_{m+2}+r,
\qquad
q\in\{0,1\},
\qquad
0\le r<F_{m+2}.
\]
Then
\[
\Fold_m(\omega)=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(r)).
\]
Equivalently, for $x\in X_m$,
\[
\Fold_m(\omega)=x
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
N(\omega)\equiv N(x)\pmod{F_{m+2}}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since
\[
N(\omega)\le \sum_{k=1}^m F_{k+1}=F_{m+3}-2<2F_{m+2},
\]
the quotient $q$ is necessarily in $\{0,1\}$.  If $q=0$, then $r=N(\omega)$ and
the claim is immediate.  If $q=1$, then
\[
0\le r=N(\omega)-F_{m+2}\le F_{m+1}-2<F_{m+1},
\]
so the Zeckendorf expansion of $r$ has no nonzero digit in positions
$m,m+1,m+2,\dots$.  Appending a digit $1$ in position $m+1$ therefore produces a
legal Zeckendorf expansion of $N(\omega)=F_{m+2}+r$, whose first $m$ digits are
exactly those of $Z(r)$.  This proves the first formula.

For $x\in X_m$, admissibility implies that $x$ is already the Zeckendorf
expansion of its own value $N(x)$, and Lemma~\ref{lem:max-value-admissible-prefix}
gives $N(x)\le F_{m+2}-1$.  Hence
$\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(r))=x$ if and only if $r=N(x)$, which is equivalent to
$N(\omega)\equiv N(x)\pmod{F_{m+2}}$.
\end{proof}

For $n\ge m$ we use two truncation maps:
\[
r_{n\to m}:\Omega_n\to\Omega_m,
\qquad
r_{n\to m}(\omega_1\cdots\omega_n):=\omega_1\cdots\omega_m,
\]
and
\[
\pi_{n\to m}:X_n\to X_m,
\qquad
\pi_{n\to m}(x_1,\dots,x_n):=(x_1,\dots,x_m).
\]
The second map is the natural restriction on already folded words.  We also use
the ambient cube-prefix map
\[
\tau_{n\to m}:\{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}^m,
\qquad
\tau_{n\to m}(u_1,\dots,u_n):=(u_1,\dots,u_m).
\]
This is the map that acts on xor-valued defect vectors.  It agrees with
$r_{n\to m}$ on $\Omega_n$ and with $\pi_{n\to m}$ on $X_n$.
The distinction is essential: $\pi_{n\to m}$ is defined only on admissible
words in $X_n$, whereas defect vectors such as $\kappa_{j+1\to j}$ and
$D_{n\to m}$ take values in the full cube $\{0,1\}^j$.  Accordingly, every
truncation of a defect vector is performed with $\tau_{n\to m}$.

\begin{lemma}[Functoriality of prefix maps]
\label{lem:pi-functorial-golden}
If $n\ge k\ge m$, then
\[
\pi_{n\to m}=\pi_{k\to m}\circ \pi_{n\to k}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
r_{n\to m}=r_{k\to m}\circ r_{n\to k}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\tau_{n\to m}=\tau_{k\to m}\circ \tau_{n\to k}.
\]
Moreover, for all $u,v\in\{0,1\}^n$,
\[
\tau_{n\to m}(u\oplus v)=\tau_{n\to m}(u)\oplus\tau_{n\to m}(v).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Both identities are immediate from the definition of prefix truncation.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Failure of Commutation}

The square formed by $r_{n\to m}$ and $\pi_{n\to m}$ does not commute in
general:
\[
\begin{CD}
\Omega_n @>{\Fold_n}>> X_n \\
@V{r_{n\to m}}VV @VV{\pi_{n\to m}}V \\
\Omega_m @>{\Fold_m}>> X_m.
\end{CD}
\]
The basic obstruction already appears at the smallest scales.

\begin{example}[A noncommuting square at $m=1$]
\label{ex:fold-noncommuting-square}
Take $n=2$, $m=1$, and $\omega=11$.  Then
\[
r_{2\to 1}(11)=1,
\qquad
\Fold_1(r_{2\to 1}(11))=\Fold_1(1)=1.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
N(11)=F_2+F_3=3=F_4,
\]
so the Zeckendorf normal form of $N(11)$ has its first two digits equal to
$00$.  Hence
\[
\Fold_2(11)=00,
\qquad
\pi_{2\to 1}(\Fold_2(11))=0.
\]
Therefore
\[
\Fold_1\circ r_{2\to 1}\neq \pi_{2\to 1}\circ \Fold_2.
\]
\end{example}

Example~\ref{ex:fold-noncommuting-square} is exactly the defect that we
formalize below.

\section{Local and Global Defects}

\subsection{Local Defect}

We work in the xor group structure on $\{0,1\}^m$, written with $\oplus$.
For $v\in\{0,1\}^m$, let
\[
\lvert v\rvert_0:=\#\{i:\ v_i\neq 0\}
\]
denote its Hamming weight.

\begin{definition}[Local defect]
\label{def:fold-local-curvature-defect}
For $m\ge 1$ and $\eta\in\Omega_{m+1}$, define the local defect
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(\eta)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{m+1\to m}(\eta))
\oplus
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(\eta))
\in \{0,1\}^m.
\]
Its zero/nonzero indicator is
\[
K_{m+1\to m}(\eta):=\mathbf{1}_{\{\kappa_{m+1\to m}(\eta)\neq 0\}}.
\]
\end{definition}

The local defect records the failure of commutation at a single adjacent
resolution step.

\begin{proposition}[Threshold classification of the adjacent defect]
\label{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold}
Let $m\ge 1$, let $p\in\Omega_m$, let $b\in\{0,1\}$, and write
\[
\eta=(p,b)\in\Omega_{m+1},
\qquad
s:=N(p).
\]
Then
\[
K_{m+1\to m}(\eta)=b\,\mathbf{1}_{\{s\ge F_{m+1}\}}.
\]
More precisely,
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,0)=0,
\]
and
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=
\begin{cases}
0, & s<F_{m+1},\\[4pt]
\pi_{\infty\to m}\!\bigl(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2})\bigr)
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}\!\bigl(Z(s-F_{m+1})\bigr),
& s\ge F_{m+1}.
\end{cases}
\]
When $s\ge F_{m+1}$, the two summands above are distinct elements of $X_m$ and
hence their xor is nonzero.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $b=0$, then $N(p,0)=s$, so
\[
\Fold_{m+1}(p,0)=\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(Z(s)).
\]
After applying $\pi_{m+1\to m}$ we recover
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,0))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\Fold_m(p),
\]
hence $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,0)=0$.

Assume now that $b=1$.  If $s<F_{m+1}$, then
\[
N(p,1)=s+F_{m+2}<F_{m+1}+F_{m+2}=F_{m+3}.
\]
Since $s<F_{m+1}$, the Zeckendorf expansion $Z(s)$ has no nonzero digit in
positions $m,m+1,m+2,\dots$, so $Z(s+F_{m+2})$ is obtained by inserting a
single $1$ in position $m+1$ and leaving the first $m$ coordinates equal to
$Z(s)$.  Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue-characterization} at level $m+1$
therefore gives
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,1))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s))
=
\Fold_m(p),
\]
so $\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)=0$.

Finally, suppose $b=1$ and $s\ge F_{m+1}$.  By
Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue-characterization} at level $m$,
\[
\Fold_m(p)=\pi_{\infty\to m}\!\bigl(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2})\bigr).
\]
Write
\[
s=F_{m+1}+t
\qquad\text{with}\qquad
0\le t<F_{m+2},
\]
which is possible because
$s\le \sum_{k=1}^m F_{k+1}=F_{m+3}-2$.  Then
\[
N(p,1)=s+F_{m+2}=F_{m+3}+t,
\]
so Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue-characterization} at level $m+1$ yields
\[
\Fold_{m+1}(p,1)=\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(Z(t)).
\]
After truncating to length $m$ we obtain the displayed formula
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\Fold_{m+1}(p,1))=\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(t)),
\]
and hence
\[
\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}\!\bigl(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2})\bigr)
\oplus
\pi_{\infty\to m}\!\bigl(Z(s-F_{m+1})\bigr).
\]
If the two summands were equal, then applying
Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-residue-characterization} at level $m$ to the
admissible word $\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(t))$ would imply
$s\bmod F_{m+2}=t$.  But $s=t+F_{m+1}$ and
$0<F_{m+1}<F_{m+2}$, a contradiction.  Therefore
$\kappa_{m+1\to m}(p,1)\neq 0$, and the indicator formula follows.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Global Tower Defect}

\begin{definition}[Global tower defect]
\label{def:fold-global-stokes-defect}
Let $n\ge m$.  For $\omega\in\Omega_n$, define
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
:=
\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))
\oplus
\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))
\in \{0,1\}^m.
\]
\end{definition}

When $n=m$, this definition gives $D_{m\to m}(\omega)=0$.  For general
$n\ge m$, the defect $D_{n\to m}$ measures the aggregate mismatch accumulated
when one
compares the two paths from $\Omega_n$ down to $X_m$ in the noncommuting square.

\section{A Telescoping Decomposition}
\label{sec:telescoping}

\subsection{Telescoping Structure}

For fixed $n>m$ and $\omega\in\Omega_n$, define
\[
A_j(\omega):=\Fold_j(r_{n\to j}(\omega)),
\qquad
B_j(\omega):=\pi_{n\to j}(\Fold_n(\omega)),
\]
and
\[
\Delta_j(\omega):=A_j(\omega)\oplus B_j(\omega)\in\{0,1\}^j
\qquad (m\le j\le n).
\]
Then $\Delta_m(\omega)=D_{n\to m}(\omega)$ and $\Delta_n(\omega)=0$.

\begin{lemma}[Adjacent recursion for the telescoping defect]
\label{lem:fold-defect-adjacent-recursion}
For every $m<j\le n$,
\[
\Delta_{j-1}(\omega)
=
\kappa_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{j\to j-1}(\Delta_j(\omega)).
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:pi-functorial-golden},
\[
B_{j-1}(\omega)=\pi_{j\to j-1}(B_j(\omega))
\]
and
\[
A_{j-1}(\omega)=\Fold_{j-1}(r_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))).
\]
Therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
\Delta_{j-1}(\omega)
&=A_{j-1}(\omega)\oplus B_{j-1}(\omega) \\
&=\Bigl(A_{j-1}(\omega)\oplus \pi_{j\to j-1}(A_j(\omega))\Bigr)
\oplus
\Bigl(\pi_{j\to j-1}(A_j(\omega))\oplus \pi_{j\to j-1}(B_j(\omega))\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
The first bracket is
$\kappa_{j\to j-1}(r_{n\to j}(\omega))$, while the second is
\[
\tau_{j\to j-1}(A_j(\omega)\oplus B_j(\omega))
=
\tau_{j\to j-1}(\Delta_j(\omega)),
\]
because $\tau_{j\to j-1}$ agrees with $\pi_{j\to j-1}$ on admissible words and
is a homomorphism for xor.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Telescoping decomposition of the global defect]
\label{thm:fold-discrete-stokes-defect}
For every $n>m$ and every $\omega\in\Omega_n$,
\[
\boxed{
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
\bigoplus_{j=m}^{n-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\!\left(
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\right).
}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We claim that for every $j\in\{m,\dots,n\}$,
\[
\Delta_m(\omega)
=
\tau_{j\to m}(\Delta_j(\omega))
\oplus
\bigoplus_{t=m}^{j-1}
\tau_{t\to m}\!\left(
\kappa_{t+1\to t}(r_{n\to t+1}(\omega))
\right).
\]
For $j=m$ this is tautological.  Assume the identity holds at some
$j\in\{m,\dots,n-1\}$.  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:fold-defect-adjacent-recursion},
\[
\Delta_j(\omega)
=
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{j+1\to j}(\Delta_{j+1}(\omega)).
\]
Applying $\tau_{j\to m}$ and using
$\tau_{j\to m}\circ \tau_{j+1\to j}=\tau_{j+1\to m}$, we obtain
\[
\tau_{j\to m}(\Delta_j(\omega))
=
\tau_{j\to m}\!\left(
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\right)
\oplus
\tau_{j+1\to m}(\Delta_{j+1}(\omega)).
\]
Substituting this into the induction hypothesis proves the displayed formula at
$j+1$.  Setting $j=n$ and using $\Delta_n(\omega)=0$ yields the proposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation as a telescoping cocycle sum]
The quantity
$D_{n\to m}(\omega)$ is a boundary-level discrepancy between two routes from
resolution $n$ to resolution $m$, while the adjacent defects
$\kappa_{j+1\to j}$ are the interior contributions picked up one scale at a
time.  Proposition~\ref{thm:fold-discrete-stokes-defect} is therefore a finite
telescoping identity, or equivalently a cocycle decomposition along the
resolution tower.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[A worked telescoping computation at $n=3$, $m=1$]
\label{ex:fold-stokes-computation}
Take $\omega=111$.  Then
\[
r_{3\to 1}(111)=1,
\qquad
\Fold_1(r_{3\to 1}(111))=1.
\]
Also
\[
N(111)=F_2+F_3+F_4=6=F_2+F_5,
\]
so the Zeckendorf expansion begins with the digits $1001\cdots$, and hence
\[
\Fold_3(111)=100,
\qquad
\pi_{3\to 1}(\Fold_3(111))=1.
\]
Therefore the global defect vanishes:
\[
D_{3\to 1}(111)=1\oplus 1=0.
\]
At the adjacent scales we have
\[
\kappa_{2\to 1}(r_{3\to 2}(111))
=
\kappa_{2\to 1}(11)
=
\Fold_1(1)\oplus \pi_{2\to 1}(\Fold_2(11))
=
1\oplus 0
=
1,
\]
while
\[
\kappa_{3\to 2}(111)
=
\Fold_2(11)\oplus \pi_{3\to 2}(\Fold_3(111))
=
00\oplus 10
=
10.
\]
Applying $\tau_{2\to 1}$ to the second term gives $\tau_{2\to 1}(10)=1$, so
\[
\kappa_{2\to 1}(11)\oplus \tau_{2\to 1}(\kappa_{3\to 2}(111))
=
1\oplus 1
=
0
=
D_{3\to 1}(111).
\]
This is the first nontrivial resolution band in which two adjacent defects
contribute to the telescoping identity.
\end{example}

\subsection{Explicit Consequences}

\begin{remark}[Total-variation bound from local threshold events]
\label{rem:fold-discrete-stokes-auditable-bound}
Let $n>m$ and let $\mu$ be any probability measure on $\Omega_n$.  Setting
\[
X:=\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega)),
\qquad
Y:=\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega)),
\]
on the probability space $(\Omega_n,\mu)$, the pair $(X,Y)$ is a coupling and
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}\!\bigl(\Law(X),\Law(Y)\bigr)
\le
\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}(\omega)\neq 0].
\]
Moreover,
\[
\{D_{n\to m}\neq 0\}
\subseteq
\bigcup_{j=m}^{n-1}
\left\{
\,\omega_{j+1}=1,\;
N(r_{n\to j}(\omega))\ge F_{j+1}
\right\},
\]
and therefore
\[
\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}\neq 0]
\le
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
\PP_\mu\!\left[
\,\omega_{j+1}=1,\;
N(r_{n\to j}(\omega))\ge F_{j+1}
\right].
\]
For any bounded $f:X_m\to\RR$, the duality characterization of total variation
gives
\[
\left|
\EE_\mu f(X)
-
\EE_\mu f(Y)
\right|
\le
2\lVert f\rVert_\infty\,
d_{\mathrm{TV}}\!\bigl(\Law(X),\Law(Y)\bigr)
\le
2\lVert f\rVert_\infty\,
\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}(\omega)\neq 0].
\]
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
Since $X$ and $Y$ are defined on the common probability space $(\Omega_n,\mu)$,
they form a coupling.  By the coupling characterization of total variation,
\[
d_{\mathrm{TV}}\!\bigl(\Law(X),\Law(Y)\bigr)
\le
\PP_\mu[X\neq Y]
=
\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}(\omega)\neq 0].
\]

If $D_{n\to m}(\omega)\neq 0$, then
Proposition~\ref{thm:fold-discrete-stokes-defect} implies that some local defect
$\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))$ is nonzero.  Writing
$r_{n\to j+1}(\omega)=(r_{n\to j}(\omega),\omega_{j+1})$ and applying
Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold}, this can happen only when
$\omega_{j+1}=1$ and $N(r_{n\to j}(\omega))\ge F_{j+1}$.  The event inclusion
follows, and the bound on $\PP_\mu[D_{n\to m}\neq 0]$ is the union bound.  The
test-function estimate follows from the duality bound
$|Ef(X)-Ef(Y)|\le 2\|f\|_\infty\,\PP_\mu[X\neq Y]$.
\end{proof}

\section{Budget Bounds and Cocycle Structure}

\subsection{Uniform Raw-Word Counts}

\begin{proposition}[Dynamic programming for uniform adjacent-defect counts]
\label{prop:uniform-adjacent-defect-counts}
For $j\ge 0$ and $t\in\ZZ$, with the conventions
$\Omega_0=\{\varnothing\}$ and $N(\varnothing)=0$, define
\[
A_j(t):=\#\{p\in\Omega_j:\ N(p)=t\},
\]
and set $A_j(t)=0$ for $t<0$.  Then
\[
A_0(0)=1,
\qquad
A_0(t)=0\quad (t\neq 0),
\]
and for every $j\ge 0$,
\[
A_{j+1}(t)=A_j(t)+A_j(t-F_{j+2})
\qquad (t\in\ZZ).
\]
If $j\ge 1$ and $\mathsf{Unif}_{j+1}$ denotes the uniform law on $\Omega_{j+1}$,
then
\[
\PP_{\mathsf{Unif}_{j+1}}[K_{j+1\to j}=1]
=
2^{-(j+1)}
\sum_{t\ge F_{j+1}} A_j(t).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Every word in $\Omega_{j+1}$ has the form $(p,b)$ with $p\in\Omega_j$ and
$b\in\{0,1\}$.  Since
\[
N(p,b)=N(p)+bF_{j+2},
\]
the words of value $t$ split into those with $b=0$ and those with $b=1$, which
gives the recursion
\[
A_{j+1}(t)=A_j(t)+A_j(t-F_{j+2}).
\]

For the probability formula, with $j\ge 1$,
Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold}
shows that $K_{j+1\to j}(p,b)=1$ exactly when $b=1$ and $N(p)\ge F_{j+1}$.
Under the uniform law on $\Omega_{j+1}$, this yields
\[
\PP_{\mathsf{Unif}_{j+1}}[K_{j+1\to j}=1]
=
2^{-(j+1)}
\#\{p\in\Omega_j:\ N(p)\ge F_{j+1}\},
\]
which is the stated sum over $A_j(t)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Uniform-law bound for the global defect]
\label{rem:uniform-global-defect-bound}
Let $\mathsf{Unif}_n$ be the uniform law on $\Omega_n$.  Then
\[
\PP_{\mathsf{Unif}_n}[D_{n\to m}\neq 0]
\le
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
2^{-(j+1)}
\sum_{t\ge F_{j+1}} A_j(t).
\]
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
Apply Remark~\ref{rem:fold-discrete-stokes-auditable-bound} with
$\mu=\mathsf{Unif}_n$.  For each $j$, the prefix $r_{n\to j+1}(\omega)$ is
uniform on $\Omega_{j+1}$, so
\[
\PP_{\mathsf{Unif}_n}\!\left[
K_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))=1
\right]
=
\PP_{\mathsf{Unif}_{j+1}}[K_{j+1\to j}=1].
\]
Proposition~\ref{prop:uniform-adjacent-defect-counts} gives the explicit value
of this probability, and summing over $j$ proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Explicit defect count under the uniform law]
\label{thm:fold-defect-count-explicit}
For $m\ge 1$, let
\[
C_m:=\#\{\alpha\in\Omega_m:\ N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}\}.
\]
Then
\[
C_1=1,\qquad C_2=2,\qquad C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}\quad(m\ge 3).
\]
Solving the recurrence gives
\[
C_{2r}=\frac{2^{2r}+2}{3},
\qquad
C_{2r+1}=\frac{2^{2r+1}+1}{3}
\qquad(r\ge 1).
\]
Consequently, under the uniform law on $\Omega_{m+1}$,
\[
\PP_{\mathsf{Unif}_{m+1}}[K_{m+1\to m}=1]
=
\frac{2^m - C_m}{2^{m+1}}
=
\frac{\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor}{2^{m+1}},
\]
and the global coupling bound becomes
\[
\PP_{\mathsf{Unif}_n}[D_{n\to m}\neq 0]
\le
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
\frac{\lfloor 2^{j+1}/3\rfloor}{2^{j+1}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Recurrence.}  We derive $C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}$ for $m\ge 3$ by
conditioning on the last two coordinates $(\alpha_{m-1},\alpha_m)$.

If $\alpha_m=1$, then $N(\alpha)\ge F_{m+1}$, so $N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}$ is
impossible; no such word contributes to $C_m$.

If $\alpha_m=0$ and $\alpha_{m-1}=0$, then $N(\alpha)=N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})
\le\sum_{k=1}^{m-2}F_{k+1}=F_{m+1}-2<F_{m+1}$, so all $2^{m-2}$ choices of
$(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})$ qualify.

If $\alpha_m=0$ and $\alpha_{m-1}=1$, then $N(\alpha)=F_m+N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})$,
and the condition $N(\alpha)<F_{m+1}$ becomes $N(\alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_{m-2})<F_{m-1}$,
which holds for exactly $C_{m-2}$ words in $\Omega_{m-2}$.

Summing the three cases gives $C_m=0+2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}$.

\textit{Base cases.}  With Fibonacci values $F_2=1$, $F_3=2$: for $m=1$,
$N(0)=0<1=F_2$ and $N(1)=1\not<1$, so $C_1=1$; for $m=2$,
$N(00)=0<2=F_3$, $N(10)=1<2$, $N(01)=2\not<2$, $N(11)=3\not<2$, so $C_2=2$.
(Recall: position $k$ has weight $F_{k+1}$, so $N(10)=F_2=1$ and $N(01)=F_3=2$.)

\textit{Closed form.}  Unrolling the recurrence with the convention $C_0:=1$
(the empty word has value $0<F_1=1$): for $m=2r$,
\[
C_{2r}=2^{2r-2}+2^{2r-4}+\cdots+2^0+C_0=\frac{4^r-1}{3}+1=\frac{2^{2r}+2}{3}.
\]
For $m=2r+1$,
\[
C_{2r+1}=2^{2r-1}+2^{2r-3}+\cdots+2^1+C_1=\frac{2(4^r-1)}{3}+1=\frac{2^{2r+1}+1}{3}.
\]

\textit{Probability formula.}  By Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold},
$K_{m+1\to m}(\alpha,b)=1$ iff $b=1$ and $N(\alpha)\ge F_{m+1}$, so the number
of pairs $(\alpha,b)\in\Omega_{m+1}$ with $K_{m+1\to m}=1$ equals the number
of $\alpha\in\Omega_m$ with $N(\alpha)\ge F_{m+1}$, which is $2^m-C_m$.
Dividing by $2^{m+1}$ gives the stated probability.

To verify $2^m-C_m=\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor$: for $m=2r$,
$(2^{2r+1}-2)/3=\lfloor 2^{2r+1}/3\rfloor$; for $m=2r+1$,
$(2^{2r+2}-1)/3=\lfloor 2^{2r+2}/3\rfloor$.

The global bound follows from the union bound in
Remark~\ref{rem:fold-discrete-stokes-auditable-bound}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:fold-defect-count-explicit} gives the precise probability that
a single adjacent step produces a defect under the uniform raw-word law.  The
fraction $\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor/2^{m+1}$ converges to $1/3$ as
$m\to\infty$, reflecting the fact that roughly one-third of all $(m+1)$-bit
words trigger a carry across the threshold $F_{m+1}$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Hamming--Lipschitz Budget}

\begin{remark}[Hamming--Lipschitz budget]
\label{rem:fold-hamming-lipschitz-budget}
Let $n>m$, let $\mu$ be any probability measure on $\Omega_n$, and let
$d_{\mathrm H}(x,y):=\lvert x\oplus y\rvert_0$ be Hamming distance on $X_m$.
If $f:X_m\to\RR$ is $L$-Lipschitz with respect to $d_{\mathrm H}$, then
\[
\left|
\EE_\mu f\bigl(\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))\bigr)
-
\EE_\mu f\bigl(\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))\bigr)
\right|
\le
L\,\EE_\mu \lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0
\]
and
\[
\EE_\mu \lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0
\le
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
\EE_\mu\left\lvert
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\right\rvert_0.
\]
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
For the same $x$ and $y$ as in the proof of
Remark~\ref{rem:fold-discrete-stokes-auditable-bound},
\[
\lvert f(x)-f(y)\rvert
\le
L\,d_{\mathrm H}(x,y)
=
L\,\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0.
\]
Taking expectation gives the first inequality.

For the second, apply Proposition~\ref{thm:fold-discrete-stokes-defect} and use the
subadditivity
\[
\lvert u\oplus v\rvert_0\le \lvert u\rvert_0+\lvert v\rvert_0
\]
together with the fact that prefix projection cannot increase Hamming weight.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Uniform-law Hamming budget]
\label{rem:uniform-hamming-budget}
Let $\mathsf{Unif}_n$ be the uniform law on $\Omega_n$.  Then
\[
\EE_{\mathsf{Unif}_n}\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0
\le
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
2^{-(j+1)}
\sum_{\substack{p\in\Omega_j\\ N(p)\ge F_{j+1}}}
\left\lvert \kappa_{j+1\to j}(p,1)\right\rvert_0.
\]
Consequently, if $f:X_m\to\RR$ is $L$-Lipschitz with respect to Hamming
distance, then
\[
\left|
\EE_{\mathsf{Unif}_n} f\bigl(\Fold_m(r_{n\to m}(\omega))\bigr)
-
\EE_{\mathsf{Unif}_n} f\bigl(\pi_{n\to m}(\Fold_n(\omega))\bigr)
\right|
\le
L
\sum_{j=m}^{n-1}
2^{-(j+1)}
\sum_{\substack{p\in\Omega_j\\ N(p)\ge F_{j+1}}}
\left\lvert \kappa_{j+1\to j}(p,1)\right\rvert_0.
\]
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
Apply Remark~\ref{rem:fold-hamming-lipschitz-budget} with
$\mu=\mathsf{Unif}_n$.  For each fixed $j$, the prefix
$r_{n\to j+1}(\omega)$ is uniform on $\Omega_{j+1}$, and
Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold} shows that
$\kappa_{j+1\to j}$ can be nonzero only on words of the form $(p,1)$ with
$N(p)\ge F_{j+1}$.  Averaging over the uniform law on $\Omega_{j+1}$ yields the
stated bound on $\EE_{\mathsf{Unif}_n}\lvert D_{n\to m}\rvert_0$, and the
Lipschitz estimate then follows from the first inequality in
Remark~\ref{rem:fold-hamming-lipschitz-budget}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Cocycle and Triangle Structure}

\begin{proposition}[Cocycle identity across intermediate scales]
\label{prop:fold-defect-cocycle}
If $m<k<n$, then for every $\omega\in\Omega_n$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{k\to m}(D_{n\to k}(\omega)).
\]
Consequently, if
\[
\Delta_{n,m}(\omega):=\lvert D_{n\to m}(\omega)\rvert_0,
\]
then
\[
\Delta_{n,m}(\omega)
\le
\Delta_{k,m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))
+
\Delta_{n,k}(\omega).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Split the telescoping sum of Proposition~\ref{thm:fold-discrete-stokes-defect} at the
intermediate scale $k$:
\[
\begin{aligned}
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
&=
\bigoplus_{j=m}^{k-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\!\left(
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\right) \\
&\quad\oplus
\bigoplus_{j=k}^{n-1}
\tau_{j\to m}\!\left(
\kappa_{j+1\to j}(r_{n\to j+1}(\omega))
\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
The first block depends only on the prefix $r_{n\to k}(\omega)$ and is exactly
$D_{k\to m}(r_{n\to k}(\omega))$.  In the second block we use
$\tau_{j\to m}=\tau_{k\to m}\circ \tau_{j\to k}$ from
Lemma~\ref{lem:pi-functorial-golden} and factor out $\tau_{k\to m}$, obtaining
$\tau_{k\to m}(D_{n\to k}(\omega))$.  This proves the cocycle identity.

The triangle inequality follows from
\[
\lvert u\oplus v\rvert_0\le \lvert u\rvert_0+\lvert v\rvert_0
\]
and the nonexpansiveness of prefix projection in Hamming weight.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Exact enumeration identity for the global defect law}

The union bound in Remark~\ref{rem:uniform-global-defect-bound} ignores
cancellations between different local defects.  Capturing those cancellations
requires the full xor distribution.  For $n>m$, define
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v):=2^{-n}\#\{\omega\in\Omega_n:\ D_{n\to m}(\omega)=v\},
\qquad
v\in\{0,1\}^m.
\]
Equivalently, $\mu_{n,m}$ is the law of the $m$-bit defect vector under the
uniform raw-word law.  The next proposition only records the exact enumeration
identity provided by the telescoping decomposition; it still references the full
prefix data $p\in\Omega_{n-1}$ and therefore is not a closed finite-state
recursion on $(\mu_{n,m})_{n>m}$.

\begin{remark}[Scalar events do not determine $P_{n,m}$]
\label{rem:scalar-recursion-failure}
A scalar recursion that only tracks the events
$\{D_{n-1\to m}\neq 0\}$ and $\{K_{n\to n-1}=1\}$ cannot recover $P_{n,m}$.
Indeed, for $n=3$, $m=1$, and $\omega=011$ we have
$K_{3\to 2}(\omega)=1$ while $D_{3\to 1}(\omega)=0$, so the top indicator
does not determine whether a truncation defect survives the final projection.
Conversely, Example~\ref{ex:defect-without-carry} shows that $K_{4\to 3}=0$
does not preclude the appearance of a lower-level defect.
Therefore the xor-valued law in Proposition~\ref{prop:exact-vector-recursion}
is the minimal data set that closes the recursion.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Exact enumeration identity]
\label{prop:exact-vector-recursion}
For $n>m$ and $v\in\{0,1\}^m$,
\[
\mu_{n,m}(v)
=
2^{-n}
\sum_{p\in\Omega_{n-1}}
\sum_{b\in\{0,1\}}
\mathbf{1}\!\left\{
D_{n-1\to m}(p)
\oplus
\tau_{n-1\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{n\to n-1}(p,b)\bigr)
=
v
\right\}.
\]
Consequently, the scalar probabilities satisfy
\[
P_{n,m}
=
\sum_{v\neq 0}\mu_{n,m}(v).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-defect-cocycle} with $k=n-1$,
\[
D_{n\to m}(\omega)
=
D_{n-1\to m}(r_{n\to n-1}(\omega))
\oplus
\tau_{n-1\to m}\bigl(\kappa_{n\to n-1}(\omega)\bigr).
\]
Under the uniform law on $\Omega_n$, the prefix $r_{n\to n-1}(\omega)$ ranges
uniformly over $\Omega_{n-1}$, and each choice of prefix $p\in\Omega_{n-1}$ can
be paired with either terminal bit $b\in\{0,1\}$.  Counting the number of pairs
$(p,b)$ that produce a prescribed vector $v$ gives the stated formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Finite-state compression]
\label{rem:finite-state-compression}
Proposition~\ref{prop:exact-vector-recursion} is exact but requires a table of
size $2^{n-1}$ to advance one step.  Compressing the prefix $p$ with a
Fibonacci-normalization transducer
\cite{Frougny1991,FrougnyS2010} while carrying the current projected defect
would turn the identity into a finite-state convolution amenable to spectral
analysis.  Building that automaton and analyzing its spectrum is a separate
project; the present note only records the explicit xor enumeration.
\end{remark}

\section{A Simple Lift Criterion}

\subsection{Consistent Lifts}

Let
\[
\Xinf:=\{x=(x_1,x_2,\dots)\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}: x_ix_{i+1}=0\text{ for all }i\ge 1\}
\]
be the one-sided golden-mean shift.  For a raw infinite binary sequence
$\omega^\infty=(\omega_1,\omega_2,\dots)\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}$, let
\[
r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty):=(\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m)\in\Omega_m,
\]
and for $x\in\Xinf$ let
\[
\pi_{\infty\to m}(x):=(x_1,\dots,x_m)\in X_m.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Exact eventual compatibility criterion]
\label{prop:fold-naive-prefix-lift}
Let $\omega^\infty\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}$ and define
\[
x_m:=\Fold_m(r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty))\in X_m.
\]
For each $m\ge 1$, define the adjacent discrepancy
\[
\delta_m:=x_m\oplus \pi_{m+1\to m}(x_{m+1})\in\{0,1\}^m.
\]
Note that
\[
\delta_m=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(r_{\infty\to m+1}(\omega^\infty)),
\]
and let $M\ge 1$.  Then
\[
\exists\,x^\infty\in\Xinf
\text{ such that }
\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)=x_m
\text{ for all }m\ge M
\]
if and only if
\[
\delta_m=0
\qquad\text{for all }m\ge M.
\]
If these equivalent conditions hold, then the lift $x^\infty$ is unique.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume first that there exists $x^\infty\in\Xinf$ with
$\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)=x_m$ for all $m\ge M$.  Then for every $m\ge M$,
\[
\pi_{m+1\to m}(x_{m+1})
=
\pi_{m+1\to m}(\pi_{\infty\to m+1}(x^\infty))
=
\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)
=
x_m,
\]
so $\delta_m=0$.

Conversely, assume that $\delta_m=0$ for all $m\ge M$.  Then
\[
x_m=\pi_{m+1\to m}(x_{m+1})
\qquad\text{for every }m\ge M,
\]
so $(x_m)_{m\ge M}$ is a compatible family of admissible prefixes.  Therefore
there exists a unique $x^\infty\in\Xinf$ with
$\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)=x_m$ for all $m\ge M$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Explicit Fold-specific compatibility criterion]
\label{rem:summable-defects-eventual-compatibility}
In the setting of Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-naive-prefix-lift}, the following
are equivalent.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item $\sum_{m\ge 1}\lvert \delta_m\rvert_0<\infty$.
\item $\delta_m=0$ for all sufficiently large $m$.
\item There exists $M\ge 1$ such that for all $m\ge M$,
\[
\omega^\infty_{m+1}=0
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
N(r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty))<F_{m+1}.
\]
\end{enumerate}
When these conditions hold, there exists a unique $x^\infty\in\Xinf$ such that
$\pi_{\infty\to m}(x^\infty)=x_m$ for all $m\ge M$.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
The equivalence (i)$\Leftrightarrow$(ii) holds because each $|\delta_m|_0$ is
a nonneg\-ative integer: their sum is finite if and only if all but finitely
many terms vanish.

For (ii)$\Leftrightarrow$(iii): since
$\delta_m=\kappa_{m+1\to m}(r_{\infty\to m+1}(\omega^\infty))$,
Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold} gives
\[
\delta_m=0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\omega^\infty_{m+1}=0
\;\text{ or }\;
N(r_{\infty\to m}(\omega^\infty))<F_{m+1}.
\]
So $\delta_m=0$ for all $m\ge M$ iff condition~(iii) holds.

The existence and uniqueness of $x^\infty$ then follows from
Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-naive-prefix-lift}.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion}

This note isolates a concrete deterministic obstruction in the Zeckendorf normalization/truncation tower.
Once the telescoping framework of Section~\ref{sec:telescoping} is fixed, every
subsequent corollary follows formally from the xor bookkeeping.  The genuinely
arithmetical inputs are Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold} and
Theorem~\ref{thm:fold-defect-count-explicit}.

Proposition~\ref{prop:adjacent-defect-threshold} gives a sharp threshold
description of the adjacent defect: once $s=N(p)$ is fixed, the two competing
routes are explicitly
$\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s\bmod F_{m+2}))$ and
$\pi_{\infty\to m}(Z(s-F_{m+1}))$, and a local defect occurs exactly when
appending the final bit $1$ pushes the prefix value across the Fibonacci level
$F_{m+1}$.  This carry-threshold criterion is a local fact peculiar to Fibonacci
numeration; it has a precise analogue in the carry-propagation literature
\cite{BertheFrougnyRigoSakarovitch2020} but the xor-valued defect observable
and the non-commutativity it encodes are new.

Theorem~\ref{thm:fold-defect-count-explicit} converts the threshold into an
exact closed-form count: the number of below-threshold words in $\Omega_m$
satisfies $C_m=2^{m-2}+C_{m-2}$, giving the adjacent-defect probability
$\lfloor 2^{m+1}/3\rfloor/2^{m+1}\to 1/3$ as $m\to\infty$.  Beyond the union
bound, Proposition~\ref{prop:exact-vector-recursion} records an exact recursion
for the entire distribution of $D_{n\to m}$, and
Table~\ref{tab:small-values} tabulates the resulting scalar probabilities for
small $(n,m)$.  No asymptotic claim is made beyond what those explicit formulas
and tables show.

Proposition~\ref{prop:fold-naive-prefix-lift} and
Remark~\ref{rem:summable-defects-eventual-compatibility} give the exact
finite-to-infinite compatibility criterion, restated in terms of the same
carry-threshold condition: the sequence of folded prefixes converges in the
inverse limit exactly when, for all large $m$, either the $(m+1)$-th bit of
the raw sequence is $0$ or the truncated prefix value falls below $F_{m+1}$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Limitations and scope.}
The vector recursion in Proposition~\ref{prop:exact-vector-recursion} is exact
but exponentially large; a practical recursion would compress prefixes using a
finite Fibonacci-normalization transducer as suggested in
Remark~\ref{rem:finite-state-compression}.  Carrying out that construction and
deriving spectral information from the resulting finite-state model is beyond
the scope of this short note.  Likewise, everything beyond the telescoping
framework is deterministic and local; no global probabilistic limit claim is
asserted without an explicit computation or table.
