\section{Relations among the three levels}
\label{sec:strictness}

The terminology above compares structures, not classes of
functions.  The useful facts are the conditional compilation
statement below, the Richardson obstruction for EML, and the
distinction between formal finite-fiber certificates and
finite-state transfer certificates.

\subsection{Compatible compilation}
\label{ssec:L1-geq-L0}

\begin{proposition}[Semantic compilation into a protocol]
\label{prop:L1-compiles-L0}
Let \((\sigma,c_0)\) be expression-universal for a family
\(\mathcal F\).  Let \(T\) be the corresponding admissible term
algebra, and let \(\sim\) denote semantic equivalence of terms on
their specified domains.  Suppose
\((D',\mathrm{NF}',\{\Phi'_\ell\})\) is a protocol-universal
system and that there is a computable map
\(\mathrm{eval}\colon T\to D'\) such that, for all
\(t_1,t_2\in T\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:semantic-compatibility}
  t_1\sim t_2
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \mathrm{NF}'(\mathrm{eval}(t_1))
  =\mathrm{NF}'(\mathrm{eval}(t_2)).
\end{equation}
Then \(\mathrm{NF}'\circ\mathrm{eval}\) descends to an injection
\(T/{\sim}\to D'\), and equivalence of expression terms is
decidable by the word problem in the protocol.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The condition \eqref{eq:semantic-compatibility} says exactly that
\(\mathrm{NF}'\circ\mathrm{eval}\) is constant on each
\(\sim\)-class and separates distinct \(\sim\)-classes.  It
therefore induces an injective map on \(T/{\sim}\).  To decide
whether \(t_1\sim t_2\), compute the two normal forms in \(D'\) and
use the decidable equality of protocol normal forms.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:compile-scope}
This is a compatibility statement, not an embedding theorem for all
expression-universal grammars.  The hypothesis is precisely the
missing canonical-form information.  When equivalence in the
expression language is undecidable, no computable evaluator
satisfying \eqref{eq:semantic-compatibility} can exist into a
protocol with decidable equality.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Richardson obstruction}
\label{ssec:L0-not-L1}

\begin{theorem}[Expression universality does not give normal forms]
\label{thm:separation}
The EML system \((\eml,1)\) is expression-universal for
\(\mathcal B_{36}\).  Nevertheless, the real elementary EML
fragment obtained by translating Richardson expressions has no
computable normal form \(\mathrm{NF}\) satisfying
\[
  \mathrm{NF}(t_1)=\mathrm{NF}(t_2)
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  t_1 \text{ and } t_2
  \text{ define the same elementary function}
\]
for all translated terms, with equality understood on their common
real domains of definition.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Richardson proved that the identity problem for real elementary
expressions is undecidable in a language generated by rational
constants, a real variable, arithmetic operations, composition, and
elementary functions including exponential, logarithmic,
trigonometric, and absolute-value operations
\cite{Richardson1968}.  Odrzywo{\l}ek's construction gives
explicit EML terms for the corresponding scientific-calculator
operations, including arithmetic, \(\exp\), \(\operatorname{Log}\),
square root, and trigonometric functions \cite{Odrzywolek2026}.
On real inputs, the principal square root gives the absolute value
occurring in Richardson's language by \(|x|=\sqrt{x^2}\).

Thus each Richardson expression can be translated effectively into
an admissible EML term by replacing each primitive operation by
the corresponding cited EML expression.  The replacements agree
with the primitive real operations on their principal real domains,
and composition preserves the common domain and value of the
Richardson expression.  If the asserted normal form existed, then
for a Richardson expression \(E\) one could translate \(E\) and the
zero expression into EML terms and compare their normal forms.  This
would decide whether \(E\) is identically zero in Richardson's
sense, contradicting Richardson's theorem.  Hence the expression
grammar, although universal in the sense of \Cref{def:expr-univ},
cannot by itself supply the decidable canonical-form component of
\Cref{def:prot-univ}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:strict-L0-L1}
Expression universality does not imply protocol universality in
general.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The EML grammar is expression-universal by
\Cref{thm:eml-universality}, while \Cref{thm:separation} rules out
the required decidable normal forms for the full elementary
fragment.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Certificate data}
\label{ssec:L2-strict}

\begin{proposition}[Generic and additional certificate clauses]
\label{prop:L2-generic-vs-structural}
For any protocol system whose normalization map has finite fibers
at each resolution, the Hankel positivity and finite spectral
reconstruction clauses in \Cref{def:cert-univ} follow from the
fiber sizes alone.  Finite-state certificate universality is an
additional transfer-operator hypothesis of the kind described in
\Cref{thm:star-kernel}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(d_m(x)\) be the finite fiber sizes and set
\[
  S_q(m)=\sum_x d_m(x)^q .
\]
For every finitely supported coefficient vector \((c_i)\),
\[
  \sum_{i,j}c_i c_j S_{i+j}(m)
  =\sum_x\Bigl(\sum_i c_i d_m(x)^i\Bigr)^2\ge0,
\]
which is Hankel positivity.  Since
\(\sum_x\delta_{d_m(x)}\) has finite support, its support points
and multiplicities are recovered from finitely many moments by the
Prony argument of \Cref{thm:prony}.  Neither argument constructs a
finite-state transfer operator.  That operator is extra structure;
when it has the tensor form of \Cref{thm:star-kernel}, it admits
the stated symmetric compression.
\end{proof}

A finite-fiber enlargement below is meant only at the presentation
level: the finite sets that map to normal forms are replaced by
computable finite covers of the same normal-form sets, while the
normal forms, valuation, and readout operations are kept fixed.
Thus the enlargement changes the certificate fibers but not the
arithmetic protocol on normal forms.

\begin{theorem}[Strictness of finite-state certificates]
\label{thm:finite-state-strictness}
Let \(a_m\ge1\) be any computable integer sequence.  There is a
computable finite-fiber enlargement of the finite presentation
sets in the Zeckendorf protocol with
exactly the same normal forms and arithmetic readouts as in
\Cref{thm:semiring,thm:two-level}, hence with the same
protocol-universality for \(\{+,\times\}\), whose enlarged second
collision moment is
\[
  S_2^\sharp(m)=(a_m+1)^2+F_{m+2}-1 .
\]
Consequently finite Hankel--Prony certificates impose no
finite-state growth law on the resolution parameter.  In
particular, choosing \(a_m=m!\) gives a certificate-universal
finite-fiber enlargement that is not finite-state
certificate-universal in the sense of \Cref{def:cert-univ}: already
its second collision moment admits no representation
\[
  S_2^\sharp(m)=u^{\mathsf T}A^m v\qquad(m\ge1)
\]
with \(A\) a fixed finite matrix and \(u,v\) fixed finite vectors.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each \(m\ge1\), let \(z_m\in X_m\) be the all-zero normal form.
Define the enlarged resolution set
\[
  \Omega_m^\sharp
  =
  X_m\sqcup\bigl(\{z_m\}\times\{1,\ldots,a_m\}\bigr)
\]
and the normalization map
\[
  \Fold_m^\sharp(x)=x\quad(x\in X_m),\qquad
  \Fold_m^\sharp(z_m,j)=z_m .
\]
This construction is computable in \(m\): membership in
\(\Omega_m^\sharp\) and the map \(\Fold_m^\sharp\) are decidable
because the sequence \(a_m\) is computable.  Every fiber is finite.
The stable normal-form space
\(X_{\infty}^{\mathrm{fin}}\), the valuation map, and the
readouts \(\oplus,\otimes\) are inherited unchanged.  Hence the
protocol-universality statement for addition and multiplication is
identical to the one in \Cref{thm:semiring,thm:two-level}.
Certificate-universality follows from
\Cref{prop:L2-generic-vs-structural}.

The enlarged fiber sizes are
\[
  d_m^\sharp(z_m)=a_m+1,\qquad
  d_m^\sharp(x)=1\quad(x\in X_m,\ x\ne z_m).
\]
Since \(|X_m|=F_{m+2}\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inflated-second-moment}
  S_2^\sharp(m)=(a_m+1)^2+F_{m+2}-1 .
\end{equation}
This proves the asserted arbitrary computable one-spike
realization.

For the asserted obstruction, specialize to \(a_m=m!\).  If the
enlargement were finite-state certificate-universal, then the case
\(q=2\) in \Cref{def:cert-univ} would give a representation of the
following form.  Suppose that
\(S_2^\sharp(m)=u^{\mathsf T}A^m v\) for a fixed finite matrix
\(A\) and fixed vectors \(u,v\).  For any
submultiplicative matrix norm there are constants \(C>0\) and
\(R>0\) such that
\[
  |u^{\mathsf T}A^m v|\le C R^m\qquad(m\ge1).
\]
Equation \eqref{eq:inflated-second-moment} contradicts this bound,
because \((m!)^2/R^m\to\infty\) for every fixed \(R\).  Therefore no
fixed finite-dimensional transfer-matrix realization exists for
the second moment.  In particular, finite Hankel--Prony
certificates are strictly weaker than the finite-state transfer
property.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:hankel-psd-generic}
The certificate level therefore has two parts.  Finite Hankel
positivity and Prony reconstruction are formal consequences of a
finite fiber partition.  Polynomial-size finite-state computation
of the same moments is a stronger property and must be proved from
the particular transfer representation at hand.
\end{remark}
