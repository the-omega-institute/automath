\section{Separations}
\label{sec:strictness}

The levels compare structure, not only the functions eventually
represented.  The proof uses three elementary ingredients: a
conditional compilation observation, Richardson's obstruction for
the EML fragment, and a finite-fiber cover showing that finite-state
certificate data are additional.

\subsection{Compatible compilation}
\label{ssec:L1-geq-L0}

\begin{proposition}[Semantic compilation into a protocol]
\label{prop:L1-compiles-L0}
Let \((\sigma,c_0)\) be expression-universal for a family
\(\mathcal F\).  Let \(T\) be the corresponding admissible term
algebra, and let \(\sim\) denote semantic equivalence of terms on
their specified domains.  Suppose
\((C',N',\mathrm{NF}',\{\Phi'_\ell\})\) is the code, normal-form,
normalization, and readout part of a protocol-universal system, and
that there is a computable map
\(\mathrm{eval}\colon T\to C'\) such that, for all
\(t_1,t_2\in T\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:semantic-compatibility}
  t_1\sim t_2
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \mathrm{NF}'(\mathrm{eval}(t_1))
  =\mathrm{NF}'(\mathrm{eval}(t_2)).
\end{equation}
Then \(\mathrm{NF}'\circ\mathrm{eval}\) descends to an injection
\(T/{\sim}\to N'\), and equivalence of expression terms is
decidable by the word problem in the protocol.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The condition \eqref{eq:semantic-compatibility} says precisely that
\(\mathrm{NF}'\circ\mathrm{eval}\) is constant on each
\(\sim\)-class and separates distinct \(\sim\)-classes.  It
therefore induces an injection on \(T/{\sim}\).  To decide whether
\(t_1\sim t_2\), compute the two normal forms in \(N'\) and use the
decidable equality of protocol normal forms.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:compile-scope}
This is a compatibility statement, not an embedding theorem for an
arbitrary expression-universal grammar.  The hypothesis is exactly
the missing canonical-form information.  If equivalence in the
expression language is undecidable, no computable evaluator
satisfying \eqref{eq:semantic-compatibility} can exist into a
protocol with decidable equality.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Richardson obstruction}
\label{ssec:L0-not-L1}

Let \(\mathcal R\) denote Richardson's real elementary language
generated by a real variable, rational constants, the constants
\(\pi\) and \(\log 2\), the operations \(+,-,\times\),
composition, and the total real functions \(\exp\), \(\sin\), and
\(|\cdot|\).  The identity problem asks whether such an expression
defines the zero function on all of \(\RR\).

\begin{lemma}[Richardson fragment and equality reduction]
\label{lem:richardson-fragment}
For the language \(\mathcal R\) just specified, there is no
algorithm which decides, from an expression \(E\in\mathcal R\),
whether \(E(x)=0\) for every \(x\in\RR\).  Consequently the
functional equality problem for pairs of \(\mathcal R\)-expressions
is undecidable.  More precisely, an identity-to-zero decision
procedure would decide equality by applying it to \(E_1-E_2\), and
an equality decision procedure would decide identity-to-zero by
comparing \(E\) with the zero expression.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The undecidability of the identity-to-zero problem for this real
elementary fragment is Richardson's theorem
\cite{Richardson1968}: the fragment contains the identity function,
rational constants, \(\pi\), \(\log 2\), \(\exp\), \(\sin\), and
absolute value, and is closed under addition, subtraction,
multiplication, and composition.  Since the grammar is closed under
subtraction, two expressions define the same real function exactly
when their difference defines the zero function.  Conversely,
identity-to-zero is the special case of equality against the
rational constant \(0\).  Thus the two decision problems are
many-one equivalent for this fragment, and both are undecidable.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Richardson-compatible EML witnesses]
\label{lem:eml-real-witness-library}
There is a finite, effectively fixed library of principal-branch EML
terms over the constant \(1\) with the following real-domain
properties.
\begin{enumerate}
\item For all \(u,v\in\RR\), the chosen witnesses for
  \(u+v\), \(u-v\), and \(uv\) are defined and have those real
  values; the chosen witness for \(u/v\) is defined and has that
  value whenever \(v\ne0\).
\item For all \(u\in\RR\), the chosen witnesses for
  \(\exp u\) and \(\sin u\) are defined and have those real values.
\item The chosen square-root witness is defined for every
  \(t\in\RR_{\ge0}\) and returns the nonnegative square root.
\item There are fixed witnesses for \(\pi\) and \(\log 2\), and
  there is an effective construction of a witness for every rational
  constant.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Odrzywo{\l}ek's principal-branch EML table gives finite terms for
the standard arithmetic operations, elementary unary functions, and
scientific-calculator constants used here
\cite[Table~1 and Supplementary Information, Part~II]{Odrzywolek2026}.
We choose once and for all the entries for \(+\), \(-\),
\(\times\), \(\div\), \(\exp\), \(\sin\), square root, and \(\pi\),
restricted to the displayed real domains.  The real domains in the
first two items are the usual real domains of those operations.  For
\(\exp\) this is also immediate from the displayed formula
\(e^u=\eml(u,1)\), since the second EML input is the nonzero
constant \(1\).

The square-root entry is used only on \(\RR_{\ge0}\), where the
principal square root is the ordinary nonnegative real square root.
The constant \(0\) is obtained as \(1-1\), integers are obtained by
iterated addition and subtraction, and a rational \(a/b\) with
\(b>0\) is obtained by applying the chosen division witness to the
integer witnesses for \(a\) and \(b\); the denominator witness is
nonzero.  Finally, \(\log 2\) is obtained from the logarithm witness
in \Cref{thm:eml-universality} applied to the positive real constant
\(2=1+1\).  These are finite syntactic constructions, uniform in the
rational input, and each is evaluated only on the real domain on
which the corresponding principal-branch witness is defined.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Effective Richardson translation]
\label{lem:richardson-translation}
Using the witnesses of \Cref{lem:eml-real-witness-library}, there is
an effective map \(E\mapsto\tau(E)\) from Richardson expressions to
principal-branch EML terms over \((\eml,1)\), together with a
transported real semantics
\(\llbracket\tau(E)\rrbracket_{\RR}\), such that
\[
  \llbracket\tau(E)\rrbracket_{\RR}(x)
  =E(x)\qquad(x\in\RR)
\]
for every Richardson expression \(E\).  Absolute value is represented
by
\[
  |F|=\sqrt{F^2}
\]
using the principal square root on the nonnegative real axis.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Define \(\tau\) by structural recursion.  A variable is sent to the
same variable.  Constants are sent to the constant witnesses from
\Cref{lem:eml-real-witness-library}.  If an expression is formed by
\(+,-,\times,\exp\), or \(\sin\), apply the corresponding chosen EML
witness to the translated subexpressions.  If an expression is a
composition \(G\circ F\), substitute the translated inner expression
\(\tau(F)\) into the translated outer expression \(\tau(G)\).  If
\(E=|F|\), translate it as the square-root witness applied to
\(\tau(F)^2\), where the square is built using the multiplication
witness.

This recursion is algorithmic because all primitive witnesses are
finite terms and syntactic substitution is computable.  We prove the
semantic assertion, including definedness on every real input, by
induction on the expression tree.  The variable and constant cases
are immediate from the witness library.  For
\(+,-,\times,\exp\), and \(\sin\), the induction hypothesis says
that the translated subexpressions are defined at the given real
input and evaluate to the corresponding real subexpression values;
\Cref{lem:eml-real-witness-library} then gives definedness and the
correct value for the chosen primitive witness on those real
arguments.  For a composition \(G\circ F\), the induction hypothesis
first gives \(\llbracket\tau(F)\rrbracket_{\RR}(x)=F(x)\in\RR\);
applying the induction hypothesis for \(G\) at the real input
\(F(x)\) proves the composition case.  Finally, for
\(E=|F|\), the induction hypothesis gives
\(\llbracket\tau(F)\rrbracket_{\RR}(x)=F(x)\in\RR\), so the
multiplication witness gives \(F(x)^2\in\RR_{\ge0}\).  The
square-root witness is defined on this input and returns the
nonnegative square root, hence
\(\sqrt{F(x)^2}=|F(x)|\).  Thus every translated term agrees with
the original Richardson expression as a real function.  The
statement concerns this transported real semantics; it makes no
branch-independent assertion about equality of arbitrary raw EML
terms on a complex domain.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Expression universality does not give normal forms]
\label{thm:separation}
The EML system \((\eml,1)\) is expression-universal for
\(\mathcal B_{36}\).  Let \(\tau(E)\) be the effective
principal-branch EML representative of a Richardson expression
given by \Cref{lem:richardson-translation}.
There is no computably presented normal-form codomain
\(N_{\mathcal R}\) of finite objects with decidable equality and no
total computable map
\(\mathrm{NF}\colon\tau(\mathcal R)\to N_{\mathcal R}\) satisfying
\[
  \mathrm{NF}(\tau(E_1))=\mathrm{NF}(\tau(E_2))
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  E_1 \text{ and } E_2
  \text{ define the same function } \RR\to\RR
\]
for all Richardson expressions \(E_1,E_2\in\mathcal R\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The expression-universality assertion is \Cref{thm:eml-universality}.
By \Cref{lem:richardson-translation}, the map \(E\mapsto\tau(E)\)
is effective and preserves the real function defined by \(E\).  If
the asserted normal form existed on this translated fragment, then
for a Richardson expression \(E\) one could translate \(E\) and the
zero expression into EML terms and compare their normal forms.  The
decidable equality predicate on \(N_{\mathcal R}\), together with
the displayed equivalence, would decide whether \(E\) is identically
zero in Richardson's sense, as a real function.  This contradicts
\Cref{lem:richardson-fragment}.  Thus the expression grammar, although
universal in the sense of \Cref{def:expr-univ}, cannot by itself
supply the decidable canonical-form component of
\Cref{def:prot-univ}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:strict-L0-L1}
Expression universality does not imply protocol universality in
general.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The EML grammar is expression-universal by
\Cref{thm:eml-universality}, while \Cref{thm:separation} rules out
the required decidable normal forms already on the translated
Richardson fragment, with equality understood in the transported
real semantics of the represented functions.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Certificate data}
\label{ssec:L2-strict}

\begin{proposition}[Generic and additional certificate clauses]
\label{prop:L2-generic-vs-structural}
For any protocol system with effective finite resolution data whose
normalization map has finite fibers at each resolution, the Hankel
positivity and finite spectral reconstruction clauses in
\Cref{def:cert-univ} follow from the fiber sizes alone, with the
a priori finite bound supplied by the computable number of normal
forms at that resolution.  Finite-state certificate universality is
an additional transfer-operator hypothesis of the kind described in
\Cref{thm:star-kernel}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(N_m\) be the finite normal-form set at resolution \(m\), listed
as in \Cref{def:effective-resolution-data}.  Let \(d_m(x)\) be the
finite fiber sizes and set
\[
  S_q(m)=\sum_{x\in N_m} d_m(x)^q .
\]
For every finitely supported coefficient vector \((c_i)\),
\[
  \sum_{i,j}c_i c_j S_{i+j}(m)
  =\sum_{x\in N_m}
    \Bigl(\sum_i c_i d_m(x)^i\Bigr)^2\ge0,
\]
which is Hankel positivity.  Since
\(\sum_{x\in N_m}\delta_{d_m(x)}\) has finite support of size at
most \(|N_m|\), its support points and multiplicities are recovered
from finitely many moments, with the explicit bound
\(2|N_m|-1\) from \Cref{thm:prony}.  The value \(|N_m|\) and the
finite moment segment are computable by bounded enumeration in the
effective resolution data.  Neither argument constructs a
finite-state transfer operator.  That operator is additional
structure; when it has the tensor form of
\Cref{thm:star-kernel}, it admits the stated symmetric compression.
\end{proof}

The next construction is only a presentation-level modification.
The finite sets mapping to normal forms are replaced by computable
finite covers of the same normal-form sets.  The normal forms,
valuation, and readout operations are fixed; only the certificate
fibers change.  Thus the theorem is not a claim that the unmodified
Zeckendorf fold lacks a finite-state transfer operator.

\begin{definition}[Computable finite-fiber cover]
\label{def:computable-cover}
A \emph{computable finite-fiber cover} of the Zeckendorf resolution
data is a sequence
\[
  (\Omega_m^\sharp,\Fold_m^\sharp)_{m\ge1}
\]
such that each \(\Omega_m^\sharp\) is a finite decidable set,
the relation \(\omega\in\Omega_m^\sharp\) is decidable uniformly in
\((m,\omega)\), and a uniform algorithm outputs a finite
no-repetition list of all elements of \(\Omega_m^\sharp\), hence the
cardinality \(|\Omega_m^\sharp|\).  Moreover
\[
  \Fold_m^\sharp\colon\Omega_m^\sharp\to X_m
\]
is a uniformly computable surjection.  The covered collision moments
are
\[
  S_q^\sharp(m)
  =\sum_{x\in X_m}|\bigl(\Fold_m^\sharp\bigr)^{-1}(x)|^q .
\]
By definition this operation changes only the finite certificate
fibers over the finite normal-form sets \(X_m\).  The stable
normal-form space \(X_{\infty}^{\mathrm{fin}}\), the decoding
\(\Val\), the operations \(\oplus,\otimes\), and the composition
readouts \(\Phi_0,\Phi_1\) remain the Zeckendorf protocol data.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Covers preserve readouts]
\label{lem:cover-preserves-protocol}
Let \((\Omega_m^\sharp,\Fold_m^\sharp)\) be a computable
finite-fiber cover of the Zeckendorf resolution data.  Every
operation realized by the Zeckendorf protocol with normal forms
\(X_{\infty}^{\mathrm{fin}}\), decoding \(\Val\), and readouts
\(\Phi_0,\Phi_1\) is realized by the covered presentation with the
same realizing map on normal forms, the same level, and the same
single-operation readout term.  In particular, protocol universality
for \(\{+,\times\}\) is preserved.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The realization equations in \Cref{def:prot-univ} are equations on
the stable normal forms and their readouts:
\[
  \nu(\widehat\theta(n_1,\ldots,n_r))
  =\theta(\nu(n_1),\ldots,\nu(n_r))
\]
and
\[
  \Phi_\ell(\widehat\theta(n_1,\ldots,n_r))
  =
  R_\theta(\Phi_\ell(n_1),\ldots,\Phi_\ell(n_r)).
\]
A finite-fiber cover leaves \(X_{\infty}^{\mathrm{fin}}\), \(\Val\),
\(\oplus,\otimes\), and the maps \(\Phi_0,\Phi_1\) unchanged.  It
changes only the cardinalities of the finite preimages over the
finite sets \(X_m\), which enter the collision moments but not the
readout equations.  Therefore the same realization data continue to
realize the same operations.  Applying this to
\Cref{thm:zeckendorf-protocol-univ} gives preservation of
protocol universality for \(\{+,\times\}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite-fiber certificates do not force finite-state transfer]
\label{thm:finite-state-strictness}
Let \(a_m\ge1\) be any computable integer sequence.  There is a
computable finite-fiber cover of the finite normal-form sets in the
Zeckendorf protocol with exactly the same normal forms and
arithmetic readouts as in
\Cref{thm:semiring,thm:two-level}, hence with the same
protocol-universality for \(\{+,\times\}\), whose resulting second
collision moment is
\[
  S_2^\sharp(m)=(a_m+1)^2+F_{m+2}-1 .
\]
Consequently finite Hankel--Prony certificates, by themselves,
impose no finite-state growth law on the resolution parameter.  In
particular, choosing \(a_m=m!\) gives a certificate-universal
finite-fiber cover that is not finite-state
certificate-universal in the sense of \Cref{def:cert-univ}: already
its second collision moment admits no representation
\[
  S_2^\sharp(m)=u^{\mathsf T}A^m v\qquad(m\ge1)
\]
with \(A\) a fixed finite matrix and \(u,v\) fixed finite vectors.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each \(m\ge1\), let \(z_m\in X_m\) be the all-zero normal form.
Define the covered resolution set
\[
  \Omega_m^\sharp
  =
  X_m\sqcup\bigl(\{z_m\}\times\{1,\ldots,a_m\}\bigr)
\]
and the normalization map
\[
  \Fold_m^\sharp(x)=x\quad(x\in X_m),\qquad
  \Fold_m^\sharp(z_m,j)=z_m .
\]
This construction is computable in \(m\): encode the disjoint union
by tags, use decidability of \(X_m\), and decide
\((z_m,j)\) by computing \(a_m\) and checking \(1\le j\le a_m\).
The required finite list is obtained by concatenating the
lexicographic list of \(X_m\) with the list
\((z_m,1),\ldots,(z_m,a_m)\).
The map \(\Fold_m^\sharp\) is then uniformly computable and
surjective onto \(X_m\), and every fiber is finite.  Thus
\((\Omega_m^\sharp,\Fold_m^\sharp)\) is a computable finite-fiber
cover in the sense of \Cref{def:computable-cover}.  By
\Cref{lem:cover-preserves-protocol}, the protocol-universality
statement for addition and multiplication is unchanged from
\Cref{thm:zeckendorf-protocol-univ}.
Certificate-universality follows from
\Cref{prop:L2-generic-vs-structural}.

The covered fiber sizes are
\[
  d_m^\sharp(z_m)=a_m+1,\qquad
  d_m^\sharp(x)=1\quad(x\in X_m,\ x\ne z_m).
\]
Since \(|X_m|=F_{m+2}\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inflated-second-moment}
  S_2^\sharp(m)=(a_m+1)^2+F_{m+2}-1 .
\end{equation}
This proves the asserted computable one-spike cover and preserves
the protocol data.

For the asserted obstruction, specialize to \(a_m=m!\).  If the
cover were finite-state certificate-universal, then the case \(q=2\)
in \Cref{def:cert-univ} would give a representation
\(S_2^\sharp(m)=u^{\mathsf T}A^m v\) for a fixed finite matrix
\(A\) and fixed vectors \(u,v\).  For any
submultiplicative matrix norm there are constants \(C>0\) and
\(R>0\) such that
\[
  |u^{\mathsf T}A^m v|\le C R^m\qquad(m\ge1).
\]
Equation \eqref{eq:inflated-second-moment} contradicts this bound,
because \((m!)^2/R^m\to\infty\) for every fixed \(R\).  Hence no
fixed finite-dimensional transfer-matrix realization exists for the
second moment.  The finite-fiber certificate clauses are therefore
strictly weaker, as logical requirements, than the finite-state
transfer property.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:hankel-psd-generic}
The certificate level has two parts.  Finite Hankel positivity and
Prony reconstruction are formal consequences of a finite fiber
partition.  Polynomial-size finite-state computation of the same
moments is stronger and must come from the particular transfer
representation at hand; it is not certified by moment positivity and
reconstruction alone.
\end{remark}
