\section{Separations}
\label{sec:strictness}

The levels compare structures, not only the functions eventually
represented.  We shall use a conditional compilation observation,
Richardson's obstruction for EML, and a finite-fiber cover showing
that finite-state certificate data are additional.

\subsection{Compatible compilation}
\label{ssec:L1-geq-L0}

\begin{proposition}[Semantic compilation into a protocol]
\label{prop:L1-compiles-L0}
Let \((\sigma,c_0)\) be expression-universal for a family
\(\mathcal F\).  Let \(T\) be the corresponding admissible term
algebra, and let \(\sim\) denote semantic equivalence of terms on
their specified domains.  Suppose
\((C',N',\mathrm{NF}',\{\Phi'_\ell\})\) is the code, normal-form,
normalization, and readout part of a protocol-universal system, and
that there is a computable map
\(\mathrm{eval}\colon T\to C'\) such that, for all
\(t_1,t_2\in T\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:semantic-compatibility}
  t_1\sim t_2
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  \mathrm{NF}'(\mathrm{eval}(t_1))
  =\mathrm{NF}'(\mathrm{eval}(t_2)).
\end{equation}
Then \(\mathrm{NF}'\circ\mathrm{eval}\) descends to an injection
\(T/{\sim}\to N'\), and equivalence of expression terms is
decidable by the word problem in the protocol.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The condition \eqref{eq:semantic-compatibility} says precisely that
\(\mathrm{NF}'\circ\mathrm{eval}\) is constant on each
\(\sim\)-class and separates distinct \(\sim\)-classes.  It
therefore induces an injection on \(T/{\sim}\).  To decide whether
\(t_1\sim t_2\), compute the two normal forms in \(N'\) and use the
decidable equality of protocol normal forms.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:compile-scope}
This is a compatibility statement, not an embedding theorem for an
arbitrary expression-universal grammar.  The hypothesis is precisely
the missing canonical-form information.  If equivalence in the
expression language is undecidable, no computable evaluator
satisfying \eqref{eq:semantic-compatibility} can exist into a
protocol with decidable equality.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Richardson obstruction}
\label{ssec:L0-not-L1}

Let \(\mathcal R\) denote Richardson's real elementary language
generated by a real variable, rational constants, the constants
\(\pi\) and \(\log 2\), the operations \(+,-,\times\),
composition, and the total real functions \(\exp\), \(\sin\), and
\(|\cdot|\).  The identity problem asks whether such an expression
defines the zero function on all of \(\RR\).

\begin{theorem}[Expression universality does not give normal forms]
\label{thm:separation}
The EML system \((\eml,1)\) is expression-universal for
\(\mathcal B_{36}\).  Fix an effective choice of the EML
representatives supplied by Odrzywo{\l}ek's construction for the
primitives occurring in \(\mathcal R\), and let \(\tau(E)\) denote
the resulting EML representative of a Richardson expression \(E\).
There is no computable normal form \(\mathrm{NF}\) on the
translated set \(\tau(\mathcal R)\) satisfying
\[
  \mathrm{NF}(\tau(E_1))=\mathrm{NF}(\tau(E_2))
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  E_1 \text{ and } E_2
  \text{ define the same function } \RR\to\RR
\]
for all Richardson expressions \(E_1,E_2\in\mathcal R\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Richardson proved that the identity problem for this real language
is undecidable \cite{Richardson1968}.

Odrzywo{\l}ek's construction gives explicit EML terms for the
scientific-calculator primitives needed in this sublanguage:
constants, \(+,-,\times\), \(\exp\), square root, and the
trigonometric functions, with the stated principal-branch
interpretation on the corresponding real domains
\cite[Table~1 and Supplementary Information, Part~II]{Odrzywolek2026}.
The constant \(\log 2\) is obtained from the positive-real
logarithm formula in \Cref{thm:eml-universality}.  On real inputs,
\(|x|=\sqrt{x^2}\), with the principal square root on the
nonnegative real axis, so absolute value is translated through the
square-root witness.  By structural recursion, each Richardson
expression has an effective EML representative whose transported
semantics is the source real function.  This is the only semantics
used in the reduction.  Since EML formulas are partial and
principal-branch dependent, the argument makes no
branch-independent assertion about equality of arbitrary raw EML
terms on a complex domain.

If the asserted normal form existed on this translated fragment,
then for a Richardson expression \(E\) one could translate \(E\)
and the zero expression into EML terms and compare their normal
forms.  This would decide whether \(E\) is identically zero in
Richardson's sense, as a real function, contradicting Richardson's
theorem.  Thus the expression grammar, although universal in the
sense of \Cref{def:expr-univ}, cannot by itself supply the
decidable canonical-form component of \Cref{def:prot-univ}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:strict-L0-L1}
Expression universality does not imply protocol universality in
general.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The EML grammar is expression-universal by
\Cref{thm:eml-universality}, while \Cref{thm:separation} rules out
the required decidable normal forms already on the translated
Richardson fragment, with equality understood in the transported
real semantics of the represented functions.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Certificate data}
\label{ssec:L2-strict}

\begin{proposition}[Generic and additional certificate clauses]
\label{prop:L2-generic-vs-structural}
For any protocol system whose normalization map has finite fibers
at each resolution, the Hankel positivity and finite spectral
reconstruction clauses in \Cref{def:cert-univ} follow from the
fiber sizes alone, with the a priori finite bound supplied by the
number of normal forms at that resolution.  Finite-state
certificate universality is an additional transfer-operator
hypothesis of the kind described in \Cref{thm:star-kernel}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(d_m(x)\) be the finite fiber sizes and set
\[
  S_q(m)=\sum_x d_m(x)^q .
\]
For every finitely supported coefficient vector \((c_i)\),
\[
  \sum_{i,j}c_i c_j S_{i+j}(m)
  =\sum_x\Bigl(\sum_i c_i d_m(x)^i\Bigr)^2\ge0,
\]
which is Hankel positivity.  Since
\(\sum_x\delta_{d_m(x)}\) has finite support, its support points
and multiplicities are recovered from finitely many moments, with
the explicit bound in \Cref{thm:prony}.  Neither argument
constructs a finite-state transfer operator.  That operator is
additional structure; when it has the tensor form of
\Cref{thm:star-kernel}, it admits the stated symmetric compression.
\end{proof}

A finite-fiber cover below is meant only at the presentation level.
In the next theorem the finite sets mapping to normal forms are
replaced by computable finite covers of the same normal-form sets.
The normal forms, valuation, and readout operations are kept fixed.
Thus the cover changes the certificate fibers, but not the
arithmetic protocol on normal forms.  The theorem is not a claim
that the unmodified Zeckendorf fold lacks a finite-state transfer
operator.

\begin{theorem}[Finite-fiber certificates do not force finite-state transfer]
\label{thm:finite-state-strictness}
Let \(a_m\ge1\) be any computable integer sequence.  There is a
computable finite-fiber cover of the finite normal-form sets in the
Zeckendorf protocol with exactly the same normal forms and
arithmetic readouts as in
\Cref{thm:semiring,thm:two-level}, hence with the same
protocol-universality for \(\{+,\times\}\), whose resulting second
collision moment is
\[
  S_2^\sharp(m)=(a_m+1)^2+F_{m+2}-1 .
\]
Consequently finite Hankel--Prony certificates, by themselves,
impose no finite-state growth law on the resolution parameter.  In
particular, choosing \(a_m=m!\) gives a certificate-universal
finite-fiber cover that is not finite-state
certificate-universal in the sense of \Cref{def:cert-univ}: already
its second collision moment admits no representation
\[
  S_2^\sharp(m)=u^{\mathsf T}A^m v\qquad(m\ge1)
\]
with \(A\) a fixed finite matrix and \(u,v\) fixed finite vectors.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each \(m\ge1\), let \(z_m\in X_m\) be the all-zero normal form.
Define the covered resolution set
\[
  \Omega_m^\sharp
  =
  X_m\sqcup\bigl(\{z_m\}\times\{1,\ldots,a_m\}\bigr)
\]
and the normalization map
\[
  \Fold_m^\sharp(x)=x\quad(x\in X_m),\qquad
  \Fold_m^\sharp(z_m,j)=z_m .
\]
This construction is computable in \(m\): membership in
\(\Omega_m^\sharp\) and the map \(\Fold_m^\sharp\) are decidable,
because the sequence \(a_m\) is computable.  Every fiber is finite.
The stable normal-form space
\(X_{\infty}^{\mathrm{fin}}\), the valuation map, and the
readouts \(\oplus,\otimes\) are inherited unchanged.  Hence the
protocol-universality statement for addition and multiplication is
identical to the one in
\Cref{thm:semiring,thm:two-level,cor:minimal}.
Certificate-universality follows from
\Cref{prop:L2-generic-vs-structural}.

The covered fiber sizes are
\[
  d_m^\sharp(z_m)=a_m+1,\qquad
  d_m^\sharp(x)=1\quad(x\in X_m,\ x\ne z_m).
\]
Since \(|X_m|=F_{m+2}\),
\begin{equation}\label{eq:inflated-second-moment}
  S_2^\sharp(m)=(a_m+1)^2+F_{m+2}-1 .
\end{equation}
This proves the asserted computable one-spike cover and the
claimed preservation of the protocol data.

For the asserted obstruction, specialize to \(a_m=m!\).  If the
cover were finite-state certificate-universal, then the case \(q=2\)
in \Cref{def:cert-univ} would give a representation of the
following form.  Suppose that
\(S_2^\sharp(m)=u^{\mathsf T}A^m v\) for a fixed finite matrix
\(A\) and fixed vectors \(u,v\).  For any
submultiplicative matrix norm there are constants \(C>0\) and
\(R>0\) such that
\[
  |u^{\mathsf T}A^m v|\le C R^m\qquad(m\ge1).
\]
Equation \eqref{eq:inflated-second-moment} contradicts this bound,
because \((m!)^2/R^m\to\infty\) for every fixed \(R\).  Therefore
no fixed finite-dimensional transfer-matrix realization exists for
the second moment.  In particular, the finite-fiber certificate
clauses are strictly weaker, as logical requirements, than the
finite-state transfer property.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:hankel-psd-generic}
The certificate level therefore has two parts.  Finite Hankel
positivity and Prony reconstruction are formal consequences of a
finite fiber partition.  Polynomial-size finite-state computation of
the same moments is stronger and must come from the particular
transfer representation at hand; it is not certified by the moment
positivity and reconstruction statements alone.
\end{remark}
