\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Single-operation descriptions are a familiar way of making a
language look small.  The Sheffer stroke represents all Boolean
operations \cite{Sheffer1913}; lambda abstraction gives a formal
calculus for effective computation \cite{Church1936}; and
combinatory logic replaces variables by a small finite basis, with
one-combinator encodings already implicit in Sch\"onfinkel's work
\cite{Schonfinkel1924}.  Such examples are often grouped together
under the phrase ``single primitive'', although they do not all
assert the same thing.

Throughout the paper \(\NN=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}\).

A recent analytic instance makes the distinction particularly
sharp.  Odrzywo{\l}ek showed that the binary operation
\[
  \eml(x,y)=e^x-\operatorname{Log}y
\]
together with the constant \(1\) represents the functions in a
standard scientific-calculator basis \cite{Odrzywolek2026}.  The
terms are binary trees whose internal vertices are labelled by
\(\eml\).  This is a genuine collapse of expression syntax.  It is
not, however, a normalization theorem: two different trees may
define the same function, and the grammar itself gives no procedure
for choosing between them.

The contribution of this paper is to make the distinctions precise,
not to reprove the classical ingredients.  We separate three kinds
of structure.  At the first level, expression universality only asks
that the desired functions have terms over one operation.  At the
second level, a protocol also includes computable normal forms and
readout maps through which operations are obtained from the single
primitive.  The Zeckendorf model used below is the guiding example:
binary words are folded to legal Fibonacci words, and addition and
multiplication are read from two levels of composition.  At the
third level one keeps finite audit data attached to the fibers of
the normalizing map.  In the fold model these data are the fiber
sizes, their power sums, the associated Hankel moment matrices, and
the finite Prony reconstruction of the fiber-size spectrum.  The
last two facts are formal for finite fibers; the non-formal
certificate condition is the existence of a finite-state transfer
operator computing the moments uniformly in the resolution.

Three separations are established.  First, the EML operator is
expression-universal, but the full elementary EML language cannot
have a computable canonical form deciding analytic equivalence.
Indeed, Odrzywo{\l}ek's formulas effectively encode the elementary
operations appearing in Richardson's undecidability theorem
\cite{Richardson1968}; such a canonical form would therefore
decide Richardson's identity problem.  Thus expression
universality does not imply the normal-form component of protocol
universality.

Second, the two-level Church readout is not merely a presentation
choice: multiplication cannot be represented on a single free
monogenic orbit by the ambient monoid product under any injective
recoding of the positive integers.  Third, finite Hankel
certificates and Prony recovery do not by themselves impose a
finite-state law.  A computable finite-fiber enlargement with the
same Zeckendorf normal forms and readouts can force the second
collision moment to grow faster than every exponential sequence,
excluding every fixed finite-dimensional transfer-matrix
representation.

The organization is as follows.  Section~\ref{sec:L0} records the
expression-level EML example.  Section~\ref{sec:L1} gives the
Zeckendorf protocol, the two-level composition readout, and the
level-zero obstruction to multiplicative readout.
Section~\ref{sec:L2} proves the finite certificate statements.
Section~\ref{sec:strictness} compares the three levels and proves
strictness of the finite-state certificate condition.
