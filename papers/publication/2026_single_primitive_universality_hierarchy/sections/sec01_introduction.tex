\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Universality statements often hide a difference in kind.  The
Sheffer stroke generates the Boolean operations \cite{Sheffer1913};
lambda abstraction gives a calculus of effective computation
\cite{Church1936}; and combinatory logic replaces variables by a
finite stock of combinators \cite{Schonfinkel1924}.  In such
examples it matters whether the assertion is merely that expressions
exist, or also that representatives can be chosen and compared
effectively.

The same distinction occurs for recent one-operation presentations
of elementary functions.  Odrzywo{\l}ek showed that
\[
  \eml(x,y)=e^x-\operatorname{Log}y
\]
together with the constant \(1\) represents the operations in a
standard scientific-calculator basis \cite{Odrzywolek2026}.  Thus a
large elementary language can be encoded by binary trees with a
single internal label.  This is a useful expression theorem, but it
does not itself choose a normal form among the many trees defining
the same function.

We shall distinguish three levels.  First, expression universality
only asks for some term over a single binary operation.  Second,
protocol universality asks in addition for computable normal forms
and readout maps.  Our model example uses Zeckendorf normal forms:
after normalization, the elementary Church-iteration identities read
addition and multiplication on \(\NN=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}\) at two
successive composition levels.  Third, one may attach finite
certificates to the fibers of a normalizing map.  Hankel positivity
and finite Prony reconstruction are then automatic finite-fiber
facts, whereas a finite-state transfer formula for the same moments
is extra structure.

The results below are separations of these structures, not new
proofs of Zeckendorf arithmetic, Church iteration, elementary
function identities, or finite moment reconstruction.  The EML
operation is expression-universal for Odrzywo{\l}ek's basis, but
Richardson's theorem rules out a computable canonical form on the
translated Richardson fragment when equality is understood as
equality of real functions.  In the Zeckendorf protocol, addition is
read on a free monogenic orbit, while multiplication cannot be read
there by the ambient product, even after injectively recoding the
positive integers.  Finally, finite Hankel--Prony certificates do
not imply a finite-state transfer law: one can replace the finite
sets over the same normal forms by computable finite-fiber covers,
leaving the arithmetic readouts unchanged and forcing the second
collision moments to grow faster than any exponential sequence.

Section~\ref{sec:L0} records the expression-level EML input.
Section~\ref{sec:L1} gives the Zeckendorf protocol and the
level-zero obstruction for multiplication.  Section~\ref{sec:L2}
sets up the finite-fiber certificates.  Section~\ref{sec:strictness}
proves the separations.  The appendix collects several standard
consequences of the finite fiber measure.

\begin{theorem}[Main hierarchy theorem]\label{thm:main-hierarchy}
With the definitions made below, the following hierarchy statements
hold.  In the protocol and certificate clauses, \(A\) is any
nonempty set and \(g\colon A\to A\) is any endomorphism of infinite
order.
\begin{enumerate}
\item The EML system \((\eml,1)\) is expression-universal for
  Odrzywo{\l}ek's scientific-calculator basis
  \(\mathcal B_{36}\).
\item The Zeckendorf address system, equipped with its stable normal
  forms and the two composition readouts
  \(c_n\mapsto g^{\circ n}\) and \(c_n\mapsto I_n\), is
  protocol-universal for \(\{+,\times\}\) on \(\NN\).
\item Multiplication on \(\NN_{\ge1}\) cannot be read from the
  ambient product on one free monogenic orbit, even after an
  injective recoding of the positive integers.
\item On the principal-branch EML translation of Richardson's real
  elementary fragment, no computable normal form into a decidable
  finite-object codomain can classify equality of the transported
  real functions.
\item The Zeckendorf fold system is certificate-universal in the
  finite-fiber sense.  Nevertheless finite-fiber certificate
  universality is strictly weaker than finite-state certificate
  universality: computable finite-fiber covers can preserve the
  normal forms and readouts while forcing the second collision
  moment to grow faster than every exponential sequence.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The expression input is \Cref{thm:eml-universality}.  The positive
protocol construction is \Cref{thm:zeckendorf-protocol-univ}, and
the monogenic-orbit obstruction is
\Cref{thm:level-zero-obstruction}.  The Richardson obstruction is
proved in \Cref{thm:separation}, using the effective translation
lemma \Cref{lem:richardson-translation}.  The finite-fiber
certificate assertion is \Cref{prop:zeckendorf-cert-univ}; the
strictness from finite-state transfer is
\Cref{thm:finite-state-strictness}, with preservation of the
underlying protocol supplied by \Cref{lem:cover-preserves-protocol}.
\end{proof}
