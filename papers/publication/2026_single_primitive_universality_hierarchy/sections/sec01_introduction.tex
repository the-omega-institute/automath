\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Several familiar universality statements say that a large algebra
of operations is generated by a very small supply of primitives.
The Sheffer stroke generates the Boolean operations
\cite{Sheffer1913}; lambda abstraction gives a calculus of effective
computation \cite{Church1936}; and combinatory logic removes
variables at the cost of a finite basis \cite{Schonfinkel1924}.
The phrase ``single primitive'' is often used in this neighborhood,
but by itself it leaves open a basic question: has one obtained only
terms, a decidable system of canonical representatives, or further
finite certificates attached to those representatives?

Throughout the paper \(\NN=\{0,1,2,\ldots\}\).  The distinction just
mentioned appears already in a recent construction of
Odrzywo{\l}ek.  He showed that
\[
  \eml(x,y)=e^x-\operatorname{Log}y
\]
together with the constant \(1\) represents all operations in a
standard scientific-calculator basis \cite{Odrzywolek2026}.  Thus a
large elementary syntax may be written as binary trees with one
internal label.  This is an expression theorem.  It does not provide
a word problem or choose one representative among the many trees
which may define the same function.

We isolate three levels of structure.  At the first, expression
universality, every function in a prescribed class has some term
over one binary operation.  At the second, protocol universality,
codes have computable normal forms and readout maps into semantic
domains carrying the single operation.  The example used here is
the Zeckendorf normal form for finite binary words: after
normalization, the usual laws for Church iteration read addition
and multiplication on \(\NN\) at two successive composition
levels.  At the third, one records finite data on the fibers of a
normalizing map.  For finite fibers the basic Hankel and Prony
certificates are formal consequences of finiteness; the strictly
stronger certificate condition considered here is a uniform
finite-state transfer representation of the same moment data.

The point is not to reprove Zeckendorf arithmetic, Church
iteration, elementary-function identities, or finite moment
reconstruction.  These are used as standard inputs.  The results
proved below concern only the extra structure: the absence of
canonical forms in the Richardson fragment after EML translation,
the obstruction to reading multiplication on one free monogenic
orbit, and the logical non-implication from finite-fiber
certificates to finite-state transfer.

The separations are as follows.  First, the EML operation is
expression-universal for Odrzywo{\l}ek's elementary basis, but the
Richardson elementary fragment, transported into EML terms, admits
no computable canonical form for equality as real functions.  Such a
form would decide Richardson's identity problem
\cite{Richardson1968}.  Second, the two readout levels in the
Zeckendorf protocol are forced in a precise sense: multiplication on
the positive integers cannot be represented by the ambient product
on a single free monogenic orbit, even after an arbitrary injective
recoding of the numerals.  Third, the finite Hankel and Prony
certificates supplied by finite fibers do not imply a finite-state
transfer law.  This last separation is deliberately
presentation-level: by replacing the finite sets mapping to the
same normal forms with computable finite-fiber covers, one keeps the
normal forms and arithmetic readouts fixed while making the second
collision moments grow faster than every exponential sequence.

Section~\ref{sec:L0} treats expression universality through the EML
example.  Section~\ref{sec:L1} constructs the Zeckendorf protocol
and proves the level-zero obstruction to multiplicative readout.
Section~\ref{sec:L2} records the finite-fiber certificates.
Section~\ref{sec:strictness} proves the separations.
