\section{Expression universality}\label{sec:L0}

The expression-level model is recorded first.  Throughout this
section \(\operatorname{Log}\) denotes the principal branch of the
complex logarithm.

\begin{definition}[EML operator]\label{def:eml}
The \emph{Exp-Minus-Log} operation is the partial binary operation
on \(\CC\) with domain \(\CC\times\CC^*\) given by
\[
  \eml \colon \CC\times\CC^*\longrightarrow \CC,
  \qquad
  \eml(x,y)=e^x-\operatorname{Log}y .
\]
\end{definition}

The complex domain is part of the construction.  Constants such as
\(i\), and consequently the trigonometric functions, are obtained
through the usual exponential identities; branch choices are always
understood in this principal-branch sense.  Thus an EML term is
interpreted on the set of points for which every occurrence of the
second argument of \(\eml\) is nonzero and every cited
principal-branch identity is valid.

\begin{proposition}[Odrzywo{\l}ek]
\label{thm:eml-universality}
Let \(\mathcal B_{36}\) be Odrzywo{\l}ek's scientific-calculator
basis: the indicated constants and variables, the usual elementary
unary functions, and the standard binary operations
\(+,-,\times,\div\), logarithm, power, average, and hypotenuse.
Then every operation in \(\mathcal B_{36}\) is represented, on its
corresponding principal-branch domain, by a finite expression in
\(\eml\) and the constant \(1\) \cite[Theorem~1]{Odrzywolek2026}.
In particular, for \(x\) in the right half-plane,
\begin{align}
  e^x &= \eml(x,1), \label{eq:eml-exp}\\
  \operatorname{Log}x
      &= \eml\!\bigl(1,\eml(\eml(1,x),1)\bigr),
         \label{eq:eml-ln}\\
  e   &= \eml(1,1). \label{eq:eml-e}
\end{align}
Away from the principal domain the same formulas have the expected
branch dependence; for instance the right side of
\eqref{eq:eml-ln} gives \(-i\pi\) at \(x=-1\), while
\(\operatorname{Log}(-1)=i\pi\).
\end{proposition}

Thus the relevant syntax is simply
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eml-grammar}
  S\longrightarrow 1 \mid x_i \mid \eml(S,S),
\end{equation}
where the \(x_i\) are input variables.  This motivates the following
terminology.

\begin{definition}[Expression universality]\label{def:expr-univ}
Let \(\mathcal F\) be a family of functions.  Let
\(\sigma\colon\dom(\sigma)\subseteq D^2\to D\) be a partial binary
operation on a set \(D\), and let \(c_0\in D\).  Terms in the
grammar
\[
  S\longrightarrow c_0 \mid x_i \mid \sigma(S,S).
\]
are interpreted on their admissible domains.  The pair
\((\sigma,c_0)\) is \emph{expression-universal for
\(\mathcal F\)} if, for every \(f\in\mathcal F\), some such term
agrees with \(f\) on the specified domain of \(f\).
\end{definition}

This definition is intentionally existential.  It says that a term
exists; it does not specify a preferred term.  The grammar
\eqref{eq:eml-grammar} therefore gives no word problem, no
canonical representative, and no internal operation on equivalence
classes of terms.  For example, the identity function can be written
as
\[
  x=\eml(\operatorname{Log}x,\eml(1,x))
\]
on the corresponding principal domain; substituting the EML term
for \(\operatorname{Log}x\) from \eqref{eq:eml-ln} gives a tree in
the grammar.  There are many less economical representations
obtained by inserting cancelling subexpressions.

\begin{remark}\label{rem:eml-variants}
Odrzywo{\l}ek also notes closely related one-operation
representations, including
\(\mathrm{edl}(x,y)=e^x/\ln y\) paired with \(e\), and the negated
form \(-\eml(y,x)=\ln x-e^y\) paired with \(-\infty\)
\cite{Odrzywolek2026}.  The phenomenon is best regarded as a
feature of the \(\exp\)--\(\log\) pair, not as an isolated formula.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:no-canon}
Nothing in expression universality supplies analytic
normalization.  In the full elementary EML language this is not
merely an omission: \Cref{thm:separation} shows, using
Richardson's theorem, that a computable canonical form deciding
analytic equivalence cannot exist.
\end{remark}
