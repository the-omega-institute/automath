\section{Expression universality}\label{sec:L0}

We begin with the expression-level input.  Throughout this section
\(\operatorname{Log}\) denotes the principal branch of the complex
logarithm.

\begin{definition}[EML operator]\label{def:eml}
The \emph{Exp-Minus-Log} operation is the partial binary operation
on \(\CC\) with domain \(\CC\times\CC^*\) given by
\[
  \eml \colon \CC\times\CC^*\longrightarrow \CC,
  \qquad
  \eml(x,y)=e^x-\operatorname{Log}y .
\]
\end{definition}

The complex domain is part of the convention.  Constants such as
\(i\), and hence the trigonometric functions, are obtained from the
usual exponential identities.  All branch choices below are made in
this principal-branch sense.  Thus an EML term is interpreted only at
points where every second argument of \(\eml\) is nonzero and the
principal-branch identities used in its construction are valid.  We
do not assert a branch-independent calculus of arbitrary elementary
expressions.

\begin{proposition}[Odrzywo{\l}ek]
\label{thm:eml-universality}
Let \(\mathcal B_{36}\) be Odrzywo{\l}ek's scientific-calculator
basis, consisting of the indicated constants and variables, the
usual elementary unary functions, and the standard binary operations
\(+,-,\times,\div\), logarithm, power, average, and hypotenuse.
Then each operation in \(\mathcal B_{36}\) is represented, on its
corresponding principal-branch domain, by a finite expression built
from \(\eml\) and the constant \(1\)
\cite[Table~1 and Supplementary Information, Part~II]{Odrzywolek2026}.
In particular, for \(x\) in the right half-plane,
\begin{align}
  e^x &= \eml(x,1), \label{eq:eml-exp}\\
  \operatorname{Log}x
      &= \eml\!\bigl(1,\eml(\eml(1,x),1)\bigr),
         \label{eq:eml-ln}\\
  e   &= \eml(1,1). \label{eq:eml-e}
\end{align}
Away from the principal domain the same formulas have the expected
branch dependence.  For instance, the right side of
\eqref{eq:eml-ln} gives \(-i\pi\) at \(x=-1\), while
\(\operatorname{Log}(-1)=i\pi\).
\end{proposition}

The induced syntax has the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eml-grammar}
  S\longrightarrow 1 \mid x_i \mid \eml(S,S),
\end{equation}
where the \(x_i\) are input variables.

\begin{definition}[Expression universality]\label{def:expr-univ}
Let \(\mathcal F\) be a family of functions.  Let
\(\sigma\colon\dom(\sigma)\subseteq D^2\to D\) be a partial binary
operation on a set \(D\), and let \(c_0\in D\).  Terms in the
grammar
\[
  S\longrightarrow c_0 \mid x_i \mid \sigma(S,S).
\]
are interpreted on their admissible domains.  We say that the pair
\((\sigma,c_0)\) is \emph{expression-universal for
\(\mathcal F\)} if, for every \(f\in\mathcal F\), some such term
agrees with \(f\) on the specified domain of \(f\).
\end{definition}

The definition is deliberately existential.  It asserts that a term
exists, but does not select one.  The grammar
\eqref{eq:eml-grammar} therefore gives no word problem, no canonical
representative, and no internal operation on semantic equivalence
classes.  For example, the identity function can be written as
\[
  x=\eml(\operatorname{Log}x,\eml(1,x))
\]
on the corresponding principal domain.  Substituting the EML term
for \(\operatorname{Log}x\) from \eqref{eq:eml-ln} gives a tree in
the grammar.  Further representatives are obtained by inserting
cancelling subexpressions.

\begin{remark}\label{rem:eml-variants}
Odrzywo{\l}ek also gives closely related one-operation
representations, for instance
\(\mathrm{edl}(x,y)=e^x/\ln y\) paired with \(e\), and the negated
form \(-\eml(y,x)=\ln x-e^y\) paired with \(-\infty\)
\cite{Odrzywolek2026}.  The phenomenon is a feature of the
\(\exp\)--\(\log\) pair rather than of one isolated formula.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:no-canon}
Expression universality supplies no analytic normalization.  This is
not merely a missing step in the construction: by
\Cref{thm:separation}, the EML representatives obtained from
Richardson's elementary fragment cannot carry a computable canonical
form for their transported real semantics.
\end{remark}
