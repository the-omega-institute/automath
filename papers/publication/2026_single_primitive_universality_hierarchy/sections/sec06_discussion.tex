\section{Final remarks}\label{sec:discussion}

The hierarchy separates auxiliary structure.  Expression
universality is a representation statement.  Protocol universality
adds computable canonical forms and readouts.  Finite-fiber
certificates attach moment data to normalization fibers, and the
finite-state version adds a uniform transfer computation of those
moments.

The obstructions are correspondingly narrow.  Odrzywo{\l}ek's
operator gives a one-label grammar for a large elementary library,
but Richardson's theorem prevents the translated Richardson fragment
from having a computable canonical form for equality as real
functions.  Likewise, the Zeckendorf protocol reads addition on a
free monogenic orbit, while \Cref{thm:level-zero-obstruction} shows
that multiplication cannot be read there by an injective recoding of
the positive integers.  Under that level-zero orbit restriction, one
must pass from the iterates \(g^{\circ n}\) to the power operators
\(f\mapsto f^{\circ n}\).

The finite-fiber statements have the same character.  Hankel
positivity and finite Prony reconstruction follow formally from
finiteness of the fibers at each resolution.  They become uniform
computational statements only after a finite-state transfer
representation, such as the tensor form in \Cref{thm:star-kernel},
has been supplied.  \Cref{thm:finite-state-strictness} proves the
corresponding logical non-implication: protocol data and finite-fiber
certificates do not by themselves force such a transfer
representation.

Thus the three levels may describe the same operations while carrying
different kinds of evidence for them.  Restricted expression classes,
such as polynomials or rational functions, may have decidable
equivalence by other methods; the obstruction here is specific to a
Richardson-undecidable elementary fragment inside the EML-representable
setting.
