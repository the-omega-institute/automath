\section{Concluding remarks}\label{sec:discussion}

The hierarchy should be read as a distinction among kinds of data.
Expression universality is a representation theorem.  Protocol
universality adds computable canonical forms and readouts for
operations.  Certificate universality attaches finite moment data
to the normalization fibers, while finite-state certificate
universality additionally requires a uniform transfer computation
of those moments.

The obstruction at the first step is essential.  Odrzywo{\l}ek's
operator gives a one-node expression grammar for a large elementary
library, but Richardson's theorem prevents the corresponding full
elementary language from carrying a computable canonical form for
analytic equivalence.  A single grammar node and a decidable
protocol are therefore different mathematical structures.

The protocol example also contains a genuine lower bound.  On a
free monogenic orbit, ambient composition can encode addition, but
\Cref{thm:level-zero-obstruction} shows that multiplication cannot
be encoded there by any injective recoding of the positive
integers.  The passage from iterates \(g^{\circ n}\) to the power
operators \(f\mapsto f^{\circ n}\) is therefore not cosmetic under
the level-zero orbit restriction.

The certificate layer has a similar qualification.  Hankel
positivity and finite Prony reconstruction are automatic once the
fibers at each resolution are finite.  They become computationally
substantive only after one also supplies a finite-state transfer
representation, such as the tensor form in \Cref{thm:star-kernel}.
That representation is not a consequence of protocol universality:
\Cref{thm:finite-state-strictness} gives a computable finite-fiber
enlargement of the finite presentation sets in the Zeckendorf
protocol whose second moment grows too fast for any fixed transfer
matrix.

Finally, this is not a comparison of computational power.  The
three levels may describe the same functions or operations.  What
changes is the presence or absence of normal forms, decidable
equivalence, finite fiber certificates, and finite-state transfer
data.  Restricted expression classes, for instance polynomials or
rational functions, may have decidable equivalence by other
methods; the obstruction here concerns the full elementary setting
in which EML is expression-universal.
