\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Formal semantics is often presented as truth at a fully specified point of evaluation. That picture is exact when the relevant assignment or world has already been fixed. It is too coarse, however, when information is only partial. One may have ruled out some realizations without having selected a unique one; one may have an admissible reference before a global object has formed; or one may have compatible local data that fail to glue. In such situations the natural semantic notion is not pointwise truth but forcing at an information state.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Related work.}
Several familiar traditions isolate parts of this picture.

\emph{Team semantics and dependence logic.}
Hodges introduced compositional semantics for a language of imperfect information, evaluating formulas on sets of assignments rather than on single valuations \cite{Hodges1997}. V\"a\"an\"anen developed this into dependence logic \cite{Vaananen2007}, and the resulting family of team-based and inquisitive logics now forms an active area; see \cite{CiardelliGroenendijsenRoelofsen2019} for the inquisitive-semantics perspective and \cite{AlbertGradel2022,CiardelliGrilletti2022} for recent APAL contributions. Kripke and Beth semantics replace absolute truth by forcing at stages of information and impose persistence under refinement \cite{Beth1956,Kripke1965,TroelstraVanDalen1988}. The lower-layer forcing in the present paper coincides with flat team semantics on nonempty teams; what is new begins only after the local-object predicates are added.

\emph{Sheaf-theoretic contextuality and cohomological obstructions.}
Sheaf-theoretic semantics separates local from global existence and treats gluing failure as a structural defect rather than as a mere lack of value \cite{MacLaneMoerdijk1994}. Abramsky and Brandenburger gave the foundational sheaf-theoretic formulation of non-locality and contextuality \cite{AbramskyBrandenburger2011}. The cohomological refinement of that picture, using \v{C}ech cohomology of the support presheaf to detect obstructions to global sections, was developed by Abramsky, Mansfield, and Barbosa \cite{AbramskyMansfield2012} and extended to a logical/topological setting by Abramsky et al.\ \cite{AbramskyEtAl2015}. Car\`u \cite{Caru2017} showed that the cohomological obstruction can be incomplete, a phenomenon we identify in \S\ref{sec:homological-visibility} as the pure $\operatorname{Ext}$-case. The present paper does not claim to improve on the Abramsky--Brandenburger obstruction theory itself; our contribution is a conditional comparison (\Cref{thm:unique-branch-contextuality-comparison}) showing that, when a compatible realization prestack exists, the formalism developed here recovers the no-global-section picture and refines the blind residue.

\emph{Gerbes and obstruction language.}
The gerbe-theoretic and cohomological machinery used in \S\ref{sec:gerbe-obstruction} is standard \cite{Giraud1971,Johnstone2002,Bredon1997,StacksProject}. Our contribution is not in the categorical formalism but in its semantic use: the branch-gerbe decomposition of gluing failure relative to a chosen realization prestack and the identification of the visible quotient.

The present paper combines these themes in order to address a single question: once an admitted reference carries a finite abelian gluing obstruction relative to a chosen realization prestack, what part of that obstruction is semantically visible without enlarging the state space, and what part survives only as a blind residue?

The paper is deliberately narrow and is best read as a focused note. We retain only the lower-layer pointwise and information-state semantics, the local-object layer needed to express compatible local presence and gluing failure, and the sheaf/gerbe machinery used to interpret branch obstructions. We do not attempt here to derive the site-theoretic layer from an underlying measurable recursive-addressing hierarchy, and we do not claim that an arbitrary set-valued local object functor canonically determines a branch obstruction class. The dynamic refinement, support, complexity, symbolic-dynamical cylinder, and inverse-limit material of the longer manuscript is omitted here; standard background for those omitted directions may be found in \cite{LindMarcus1995,RibesZalesskii2010}.

Against that background, the novelty of the present paper is two-sided. On lower-layer formulas, the information-state semantics is just flat team semantics on nonempty sets of assignments. The new phenomena begin only when local-object predicates are added. At that point, the logic can express compatible local presence, global absence, and gluing failure, and these predicates are invisible to the lower flat fragment. This is why the paper is not merely a team-semantical definability note and also not merely a sheaf-theoretic repackaging of contextuality.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main result A: forcing necessity.}
After separating admissible reference, local sectionability, compatible local sectionability, and global sectionability, we show that compatible local existence is characterized by sheafification (\Cref{thm:sheafification-characterization}). The key logical separation theorem is that compatible local sectionability and gluing-level absence are not definable by any formula of the lower information-state forcing language without the local-object layer (\Cref{thm:pointwise-irreducibility}). This is the result that makes the enrichment logically necessary rather than merely convenient.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main result B: visible branches and gluing failure.}
After fixing an admitted reference and working with a chosen realization prestack with abelian band on the slice site, one can compare that lift with the canonical split realization prestack of \Cref{prop:split-realization-prestack} (which requires the local object functor to be separated). The substantive hypotheses are semantic: we work with gluing-sensitive lifts whose stackifications recover the sheafification of isomorphism classes (\Cref{prop:stackification-components}), identify visible value classes with the global connected components of the realization stack (\Cref{thm:visible-value-components}), and are globally conservative at the terminal fibre when stack-global objects are compared back to the original local object functor. In that setting, each visible branch determines a banded component gerbe (\Cref{thm:component-gerbe-decomposition}), and gluing failure occurs exactly when visible branches exist but every component gerbe is non-neutral (\Cref{thm:gerbe-null-semantics}). Under branch constancy, the realization stack decomposes into those component gerbes (\Cref{cor:branch-decomposition}).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main result C: visible quotient and pure-Ext blindness.}
For a finite abelian branch obstruction attached to such a chosen visible branch on a finite \v{C}ech nerve, the homological evaluation map
\[
\mathrm{ev}_{\omega}:H_2(N(\mathcal U),\mathbb Z)\to A
\]
defines the visible quotient relative to the chosen presentation as
\[
A_{\mathrm{vis}}^{\omega}:=\operatorname{coker}(\mathrm{ev}_{\omega}),
\]
and \Cref{thm:intrinsic-visible-quotient} proves that this quotient is exactly the one detected by the class-admissible character channels. We prove in \Cref{prop:visible-quotient-lift-invariance,prop:visible-quotient-refinement-invariance} that the visible quotient is invariant under equivalence of chosen lifts and common refinement of covers, so it depends only on the equivalence class of the realization data. The blind part is equally sharp: \Cref{thm:character-blind-obstructions} identifies complete character-blindness with the case where the surviving obstruction class lies entirely in the pure $\operatorname{Ext}$-summand of the universal coefficient sequence. In the unique-branch support-presheaf setting, \Cref{thm:unique-branch-contextuality-comparison} is a conditional comparison theorem: when a compatible realization prestack exists and is globally conservative, it recovers the Abramsky--Brandenburger no-global-section picture and turns Car\`u-type blind cases into a theorem about pure $\operatorname{Ext}$-residuals. No construction from an arbitrary empirical model to a realization prestack is claimed here.

Standard inputs and new contributions should be kept separate. Sheafification, stackification, banded gerbes, and the universal coefficient sequence are not claimed as new. Several of the results below (the sheafification characterization, the identification of visible presentations with sections of the sheafification, the banded gerbe decomposition, the \v{C}ech cocycle construction, the universal coefficient algebra) are routine consequences of standard machinery; they are included as propositions and lemmas to keep the paper self-contained. The genuinely new contributions are: (1)~the forcing-necessity theorem (\Cref{thm:pointwise-irreducibility}), showing that gluing predicates are not definable in the lower flat layer; (2)~the semantic synthesis of \Cref{thm:gerbe-null-semantics}, characterizing gluing failure as non-neutrality of component gerbes relative to a chosen lift; and (3)~the conditional contextuality comparison of \Cref{thm:unique-branch-contextuality-comparison}.

The main notation is as follows. The lower semantic layers are denoted by $\mathbb L_0$ and $\mathbb L_1$. For an admitted reference $r$, the realization prestack is $\mathfrak P_r$, its stackification is $\mathfrak L_r$, the component gerbe of a visible branch $v$ is $\mathfrak G_v$, the branch obstruction class attached to a chosen branch of a chosen gluing-sensitive lift is $\omega_v$, the hidden subgroup is $K_{\omega}$, and the visible quotient is $A_{\mathrm{vis}}^{\omega}$.

The paper is organized accordingly. \Cref{sec:preliminaries} and \Cref{sec:information-states} isolate the lower-layer semantics and its conservative-extension behavior. \Cref{sec:null-decomposition} develops local objects, sheafification, visible value classes, and the forcing-necessity theorem. \Cref{sec:gerbe-obstruction} gives the branch-gerbe semantics of gluing failure. \Cref{sec:homological-visibility} develops the visible quotient, the pure-Ext blindness theorem, invariance under change of presentation, three worked examples (including one end-to-end), and the conditional unique-branch support-presheaf comparison with contextuality.
