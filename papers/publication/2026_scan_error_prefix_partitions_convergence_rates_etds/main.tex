\begin{abstract}
We study how much the decision error for a measurable event can be reduced by
refining the available prefix information.  Given an increasing filtration
$(\mathcal{F}_m)_{m\ge 0}$ generated by a binary-valued process, the scan
error profile $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$ is the smallest classification
error achievable using only $\mathcal{F}_m$-measurable rules.  Our first
contribution is a discrete Tanaka representation
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]$
in terms of a nonnegative discrete local-time functional.  Our second
contribution is a finite-state posterior theorem for mixing SFTs
with Parry measure: a finite observer graph with bounded positive and negative
resolving windows yields both the boundary thickness constant and a
higher-block invariant ambiguity exponent directly from the ambiguous
subgraph.  Our third contribution is a pair of boundary-controlled rate laws
converting those boundary-count and thickness inputs
into two-sided scan-error bounds.  A fourth theorem extends the count-based law
to log-regular cylinder measures and shows that polynomial losses are genuine
in a nonuniform product example.  As a fully worked example, we give an
exact parity-resolved formula for an explicit event $\mathsf{E}$ on the golden-mean
shift with its Parry measure, where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt{5})/2$ and the
higher-block invariant normalized ambiguity exponent is
$\delta_\partial(\mathsf{E})=1/2$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The purpose of this paper is narrow.  We fix a binary readout process and ask
how refinement of prefix information improves the best possible decision error
for a target event.  Let $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu)$ be a probability space with an
increasing filtration $(\mathcal{F}_m)_{m\ge 0}$ generated by $\{0,1\}$-valued
functions $s_0,s_1,\ldots$  The scan error profile
\[
m\longmapsto\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
:=\inf\bigl\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{Q}):\mathsf{Q}\in\mathcal{F}_m\bigr\}
\]
records the smallest classification error at each prefix depth.

The atom decomposition of Bayes risk on the finite partitions
$\mathcal{F}_m$ (equivalently, the cylinder decomposition on the symbolic
readout space) and the optimality of the threshold classifier
$p_m\ge 1/2$ are classical
\cite{Tsybakov2009Nonparametric,DevroyeGyorfiLugosi1996}.  We recall them in
Section~\ref{sec:scan} to make the paper self-contained.  The new contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item A \emph{discrete Tanaka representation}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka}) connecting error decay to the accumulation
  of threshold crossings of the martingale $(p_m)$.
\item A \emph{finite-state posterior theorem}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-state-posterior}) showing that, for a mixing SFT
  with Parry measure, a finite observer graph with bounded resolving windows
  determines both the thickness parameter and, together with
  Proposition~\ref{prop:higher-block-invariance}, a higher-block invariant
  normalized ambiguity exponent through the spectral radius of its ambiguous
  subgraph.
\item \emph{Boundary-cylinder rate laws}
  (Theorems~\ref{thm:boundary-dimension} and~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling})
  giving upper and two-sided error bounds once the boundary count and
  thickness are available.
\item A \emph{nonuniform-profile extension}
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:nonuniform-cylinder}) replacing the uniform cylinder-mass
  assumption by a log-regular condition, together with an example where the
  polynomial correction is unavoidable.
\item A \emph{fully worked example} on the golden-mean shift
  (Example~\ref{ex:golden-mean-specialization}) with
  $\delta_\partial(\mathsf{E})=1/2$ and a
  closed-form parity-resolved scan-error formula.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Heuristic comparison with margin assumptions}
\label{sub:margin}

The thickness hypothesis in Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} is
best compared heuristically with margin and low-noise conditions in
nonparametric classification.
Mammen and Tsybakov \cite{MammenTsybakov1999} introduce a margin condition
with exponent $\alpha\ge 0$: for the regression function
$p_\infty(x)=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_\infty)(x)$,
\[
\mu\!\left(\left|p_\infty(x)-\tfrac12\right|\le t\right)\le C_0\,t^\alpha
\qquad\text{for all }t>0.
\]
Tsybakov \cite{Tsybakov2004} extends this to a general noise-exponent
framework, showing that margin conditions control the rate of optimal
plug-in classifiers.

The exact boundary decomposition is
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
=\sum_{C\in \partial_m\mathsf{P}}
\mu(C)\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}.
\]
Hence the uniform thickness condition
$\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}\ge\theta>0$ on
boundary cylinders implies the lower bound
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\ge
\theta\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}}\mu(C),
\]
so thickness controls scan error through the \emph{mass} of the depth-$m$
boundary.  What it does \emph{not} control is the global distribution of the
terminal posterior $p_\infty$ near the threshold $1/2$; that global
distribution is exactly what the Mammen--Tsybakov condition measures.  Thus
the two notions live at different scales: thickness is a cylinderwise lower
bound on ambiguous atoms, while the margin condition is an integrated bound on
how much of the space has $p_\infty$ close to $1/2$.  Without additional
geometric information, neither hypothesis implies the other, so the comparison
should be read only as orientation.

The closer multiscale analogue is the dyadic tree analysis of
Scott and Nowak \cite{ScottNowak2006Dyadic}, where partition geometry and
near-boundary noise are coupled in a multiscale classifier.  Tsybakov's noise
framework remains the right broad backdrop for fast-rate phenomena, but it
should not be read as giving a direct substitute for the thickness hypothesis,
and we do not identify either $\theta$ or the boundary-growth exponents with a
classical margin exponent.
The precise relationship between the higher-block invariant ambiguity exponent
in the finite-state symbolic setting, or the raw filtration-level boundary
count exponent in the general setting, and the margin exponent $\alpha$
remains open.

\subsection{Related work}
\label{sub:related}

Bayes risk on partitions and optimality of threshold classifiers are classical;
see \cite{DevroyeGyorfiLugosi1996} and \cite{AudibertTsybakov2007} for refined
plug-in rates.  For dyadic tree methods that explicitly couple multiscale
partition geometry and low-noise behavior near a decision boundary, see
\cite{ScottNowak2006Dyadic}.  The underlying martingale convergence is Doob's
upward theorem \cite{Doob1953,Williams1991,Durrett2019}.  The discrete Tanaka
formula is a lattice analogue of Tanaka's formula for Brownian local time
\cite{Tanaka1963,RevuzYor1999}.

Cylinder partition structure in symbolic dynamics is treated in
\cite{LindMarcus1995}; boundary combinatorics connect to
\cite{Fischer1975,Krieger1984,Boyle1983}.  Same-length cylinder bounds for the
Parry (maximal-entropy) measure are in \cite{Parry1964}; Gibbs estimates with
potential-dependent weights for H\"older equilibrium states are in
\cite{Bowen1975,WaltersErgodicTheory}.
Finite-state observable factors of Markov chains go back to Blackwell and
Heller \cite{Blackwell1957,Heller1965}; for sofic shifts this observer
viewpoint is standard in Fischer and Krieger presentations
\cite{Fischer1975,Krieger1984,LindMarcus1995}.  Our finite-state posterior
theorem uses this observer philosophy for a different object: not the output
process itself, but the evolving ambiguity class of a fixed event
$\mathsf{P}$, from which thickness and higher-block invariant boundary growth
become finite-state spectral quantities.
For the literature closest to the symbolic-dynamics side of our setup, Boyle
and Petersen \cite{BoylePetersen2011} survey hidden Markov (sofic) measures
and the factor-map questions around them; Chazottes and Ugalde
\cite{ChazottesUgalde2003,ChazottesUgalde2011}, Pollicott and Kempton
\cite{PollicottKempton2011}, and Piraino \cite{Piraino2018} study when
Markov or H\"older Gibbs measures remain Gibbsian after projection and what
regularity survives in the induced potential.  Those papers concern the
thermodynamic regularity of factor measures.  Our paper does not prove a
factor-closure theorem for Gibbs classes, nor does it identify induced
potentials or relative pressure.  Instead, fixing a measurable event
$\mathsf{P}$, we study the prefix-filtration boundary of that event and the
resulting scan-error decay, with rates governed by thickness and boundary
growth rather than by a general Gibbs-factor formalism.
The Shannon--McMillan--Breiman theorem \cite{Shannon1948,McMillan1953,Breiman1957}
gives almost-sure asymptotics for the cylinder \emph{containing} a given
sample point; this is an almost-sure pointwise statement, weaker than the
uniform all-cylinder bounds assumed in our rate theorems (see
Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).  Background on dynamics and information theory
is in \cite{WaltersErgodicTheory,LindMarcus1995,CoverThomas2006,MorseHedlund1940}.

\section{The Prefix Filtration Setting}
\label{sec:prefix}

Sections~\ref{sec:prefix}--\ref{sec:tanaka} hold in the following general
setting; no invariance or symbolic structure is used there.

\begin{definition}[Prefix filtration]
\label{def:prefix-filtration}
Let $(X,\mathcal{B},\mu)$ be a probability space and $(s_t)_{t\ge 0}$ a
sequence of $\{0,1\}$-valued measurable functions.  Set $\mathcal{F}_0:=\{\varnothing,X\}$
and $\mathcal{F}_m:=\sigma(s_0,\ldots,s_{m-1})$ for $m\ge 1$.  The family
$(\mathcal{F}_m)_{m\ge 0}$ is the \emph{prefix filtration}; set
$\mathcal{F}_\infty:=\bigvee_m\mathcal{F}_m$.
\end{definition}

Let $\Omega_\infty:=\{0,1\}^{\mathbb{N}}$ and, for
$a=a_0\cdots a_{m-1}\in\{0,1\}^m$, write
\[
[a]:=\{\xi\in\Omega_\infty:\xi_0\cdots\xi_{m-1}=a\},
\qquad
\mathcal{A}_m:=\sigma([a]:a\in\{0,1\}^m).
\]
The readout map
\[
\omega_\infty:X\to\Omega_\infty,
\qquad
\omega_\infty(x):=(s_0(x),s_1(x),\ldots),
\]
is measurable and satisfies
\[
\mathcal{F}_m=\omega_\infty^{-1}(\mathcal{A}_m)
\qquad(m\ge 0).
\]
Thus the filtration-theoretic objects below live on $X$, while cylinder
language belongs to the auxiliary symbolic space $\Omega_\infty$ and is
pulled back to $X$ through $\omega_\infty$.

Two specializations appear later.  In the dynamical setting,
$s_t(x):=\ind_W(T^tx)$ for a measurable set $W$.  In the symbolic setting,
$X\subseteq\Omega_\infty$ is a one-sided shift space and $s_t(\xi):=\xi_t$; in
that case $\omega_\infty$ is the inclusion map, and the atoms of
$\mathcal{F}_m$ are exactly the positive length-$m$ cylinders of $X$.  Unless
explicitly stated otherwise, Sections~\ref{sec:prefix}--\ref{sec:tanaka} use
only the general filtration notation on $X$.

\begin{proposition}[Prefix-decidable events are clopen cylinder unions]
\label{prop:decidable-clopen}
A set $P\subseteq\Omega_\infty$ is in $\mathcal{A}_m$
if and only if it is a union of length-$m$ cylinders $[a]$, equivalently, a
finite union of clopen balls of radius $2^{-m}$ in the prefix ultrametric.
Consequently, an event $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_m$ is decided by depth $m$ if
and only if there exists $P\in\mathcal{A}_m$ such that
$\mathsf{P}=\omega_\infty^{-1}(P)$ modulo $\mu$-null sets; equivalently, the
truth of $\mathsf{P}$ is determined by the first $m$ symbols of the readout.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The atoms of $\mathcal{A}_m$ are exactly the length-$m$ cylinders, and a set
is measurable with respect to them iff it is a union of atoms.  In the prefix
ultrametric, each length-$m$ cylinder is a clopen ball of radius $2^{-m}$, so
each $\mathcal{A}_m$-measurable set is a finite union of such clopen balls.
Pulling back along $\omega_\infty$ transfers this finite-cylinder description
to depth-$m$ events on $X$.
\end{proof}

\section{The Scan Error Profile}
\label{sec:scan}

\begin{definition}[Scan error profile and admissible classifiers]
\label{def:scan-error-profile}
Let $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{B}$ be a target event.  A depth-$m$
\emph{admissible classifier} is an event $\mathsf{Q}\in\mathcal{F}_m$.
The depth-$m$ scan error is
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
:=\inf\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{Q}):\mathsf{Q}\in\mathcal{F}_m\}.
\]
Classifiers and target events are identified up to $\mu$-null symmetric
difference.  Thus statements such as $\mathsf{P}=\mathsf{Q}$, membership in
$\mathcal{F}_m$, and ``$\mathsf{P}$ is decided by depth $m$'' are understood
in the $\mu$-a.s.\ sense whenever null sets are irrelevant; this convention
does not change $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Positive-atom convention and conditional probabilities]
\label{def:positive-atoms}
For each $m$, $\mathcal{C}_m$ denotes the finite family of positive atoms of
$\mathcal{F}_m$ on $X$, after identifying atoms that agree modulo
$\mu$-null sets.
Equivalently, one may take the atoms of the finite algebra $\mathcal{F}_m$ and
discard the atoms of $\mu$-measure zero.  All sums and cardinalities over
$\mathcal{C}_m$ below are over this positive-atom family.  If
$C\in\mathcal{C}_m$, then
\[
\mu(\mathsf{A}\mid C):=\frac{\mu(\mathsf{A}\cap C)}{\mu(C)}
\]
for every event $\mathsf{A}$.  In the symbolic specialization
$X\subseteq\Omega_\infty$ with the coordinate readout, the elements of
$\mathcal{C}_m$ are exactly the positive length-$m$ cylinders in $X$.  Thus
all filtration-level statements below are atomwise on $X$; when later phrased
symbolically, they are the corresponding cylinder statements.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Exact atom decomposition]
\label{prop:scan-error-cylinder}
Let $\mathcal{C}_m$ be the positive atoms of $\mathcal{F}_m$ and
$p_m:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_m)$.
For every $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{B}$ and $m\ge 0$,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
=\sum_{C\in\mathcal{C}_m}\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}
=\EE[\min\{p_m,1-p_m\}].
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Any $\mathcal{F}_m$-measurable event $\mathsf{Q}$ is, modulo a null set, a
union of positive atoms.  On each positive atom $C$, inclusion contributes
local error $\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})$ and exclusion contributes
$\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C)$; the optimum is their minimum.  Since
$\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}=\mu(C)\min\{p_C,1-p_C\}$
where $p_C:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)$, summing over atoms gives the expectation form.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Bayes-optimality]
\label{prop:bayes-optimality}
The threshold classifier $\mathsf{P}_m^\star:=\{x:p_m(x)\ge 1/2\}$ achieves
$\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{P}_m^\star)=\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$, and
$|\int\varphi\ind_{\mathsf{P}}\,d\mu-\int\varphi\ind_{\mathsf{P}_m^\star}\,d\mu|\le\|\varphi\|_\infty\varepsilon_m$
for every bounded $\mathcal{F}_\infty$-measurable $\varphi$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On each atom the threshold rule minimizes the local error; summing gives the
global minimum.  The bound for $\varphi$ follows because
$\ind_{\mathsf{P}}-\ind_{\mathsf{P}_m^\star}$ is supported on
$\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{P}_m^\star$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Monotonicity and decidability]
\label{cor:basic-properties}
For every $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{B}$, the profile
$m\mapsto\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$ is nonincreasing, and
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=0$ iff $\mathsf{P}$ coincides $\mu$-a.s.\ with
an event in $\mathcal{F}_m$.
\leanverified{paper_prefix_scan_error_basic_properties}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Since $\mathcal{F}_m\subseteq\mathcal{F}_{m+1}$, the admissible classifiers at
depth $m+1$ contain those at depth $m$, and the infimum defining
$\varepsilon_{m+1}$ is no larger than the infimum defining $\varepsilon_m$.
For the zero-error criterion, Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}
shows that $\varepsilon_m=0$ precisely when, on every positive atom
$C\in\mathcal{C}_m$, either $\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C)=0$ or
$\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})=0$.  Let $\mathsf{Q}$ be the union of the positive
atoms $C$ for which $\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})=0$.  Then
$\mathsf{Q}\in\mathcal{F}_m$ and $\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{Q})=0$.
The converse is immediate from the definition of $\varepsilon_m$, since an
$\mathcal{F}_m$-event equal to $\mathsf{P}$ modulo null sets has zero
symmetric-difference error.
\end{proof}

\section{Discrete Tanaka Representation}
\label{sec:tanaka}

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Tanaka representation of the scan error]
\label{thm:scan-tanaka}
Let $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{B}$ and set
\[
p_m:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_m),
\qquad
p_\infty:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_\infty).
\]
For $m\ge 1$ define
\[
L_m^{1/2}
:=\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}
\left(
\left|p_{k+1}-\tfrac12\right|
-\left|p_k-\tfrac12\right|
-\sgn\!\left(p_k-\tfrac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)
\right),
\]
with $\sgn(0):=0$.  Then $L_m^{1/2}\ge 0$ and $m\mapsto L_m^{1/2}$ is
nondecreasing.  Moreover,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}],
\qquad
\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}),1-\mu(\mathsf{P})\}.
\]
As $m\to\infty$,
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\to
\varepsilon_\infty(\mathsf{P};\mu)
:=\inf\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{Q}):\mathsf{Q}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty\}
=\EE[\min\{p_\infty,1-p_\infty\}],
\]
and therefore
\[
\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]
=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\varepsilon_\infty(\mathsf{P};\mu).
\]
\leanverified{paper_prefix_scan_error_tanaka_representation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder} gives
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
=\EE[\min\{p_m,1-p_m\}]
=\frac12-\EE\left|p_m-\frac12\right|.
\]
For each $k\ge 0$, define
\[
r_k
:=\left|p_{k+1}-\frac12\right|
-\left|p_k-\frac12\right|
-\sgn\!\left(p_k-\frac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k).
\]
Since $\sgn(x-\tfrac12)\in\partial|x-\tfrac12|$, the subgradient inequality for
the convex function $x\mapsto |x-\tfrac12|$ gives $r_k\ge 0$ pointwise.  By
definition,
\[
\left|p_{k+1}-\frac12\right|
=\left|p_k-\frac12\right|
+\sgn\!\left(p_k-\frac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)+r_k,
\]
and summing from $k=0$ to $m-1$ yields
\[
\left|p_m-\frac12\right|
=\left|p_0-\frac12\right|
+\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}\sgn\!\left(p_k-\frac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)
+L_m^{1/2},
\]
where $L_m^{1/2}=\sum_{k=0}^{m-1}r_k\ge 0$.  This is the discrete analogue of
Tanaka's formula \cite{Tanaka1963,RevuzYor1999}.  Taking expectations, for each
$k$ we have
\[
\EE\!\left[\sgn\!\left(p_k-\tfrac12\right)(p_{k+1}-p_k)\right]
=\EE\!\left[\sgn\!\left(p_k-\tfrac12\right)\EE[p_{k+1}-p_k\mid\mathcal{F}_k]\right]
=0
\]
because $\sgn(p_k-\tfrac12)$ is bounded and $\mathcal{F}_k$-measurable, while
$(p_m)$ is a martingale.  Hence
\[
\EE\left|p_m-\frac12\right|
=\left|p_0-\frac12\right|+\EE[L_m^{1/2}],
\]
and therefore
\[
\varepsilon_m
=\frac12-\left|p_0-\frac12\right|-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]
=\varepsilon_0-\EE[L_m^{1/2}].
\]
L\'{e}vy's upward theorem \cite{Doob1953,Williams1991,Durrett2019} gives
$p_m\to p_\infty$ a.s.\ and in $L^1$.  Since
$u\mapsto\min\{u,1-u\}$ is $1$-Lipschitz on $[0,1]$,
Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder} yields
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\EE[\min\{p_m,1-p_m\}]
\longrightarrow
\EE[\min\{p_\infty,1-p_\infty\}].
\]
For any $\mathsf{Q}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$,
\[
\mu(\mathsf{P}\triangle\mathsf{Q})
=\EE\!\left[p_\infty\ind_{\mathsf{Q}^c}+(1-p_\infty)\ind_{\mathsf{Q}}\right]
\ge \EE[\min\{p_\infty,1-p_\infty\}],
\]
with equality for the threshold classifier
$\mathsf{Q}_\infty^\star:=\{p_\infty\ge 1/2\}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$.  Thus
$\varepsilon_\infty(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\EE[\min\{p_\infty,1-p_\infty\}]$, and the
identity for $\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]$ follows from monotone convergence applied
to $L_m^{1/2}\uparrow L_\infty^{1/2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Tail-measurable specialization]
\label{cor:scan-tanaka-tail}
If $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$, then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\to 0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\EE[L_\infty^{1/2}]=\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu).
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$, then
$p_\infty=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid\mathcal{F}_\infty)=\ind_{\mathsf{P}}$ a.s.  Hence
\[
\varepsilon_\infty(\mathsf{P};\mu)
=\EE[\min\{p_\infty,1-p_\infty\}]=0,
\]
and the conclusion follows from Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Threshold-crossing budget]
\label{prop:tanaka-crossing-budget}
For a positive atom $D\in\mathcal{C}_{k+1}$, let $D^-\in\mathcal{C}_k$ be its
parent and write
\[
q_D:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid D),\qquad q_{D^-}:=\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid D^-).
\]
Set
\[
\tau(u,v):=
\left|v-\tfrac12\right|
-\left|u-\tfrac12\right|
-\sgn\!\left(u-\tfrac12\right)(v-u).
\]
Then for every $k\ge 0$,
\[
\varepsilon_k(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\varepsilon_{k+1}(\mathsf{P};\mu)
=\EE[L_{k+1}^{1/2}-L_k^{1/2}]
=\sum_{D\in\mathcal{C}_{k+1}}\mu(D)\tau(q_{D^-},q_D).
\]
Moreover,
\[
\tau(u,v)=
\begin{cases}
2(v-\tfrac12), & u<\tfrac12<v,\\
2(\tfrac12-v), & u>\tfrac12>v,\\
|v-\tfrac12|, & u=\tfrac12,\\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\]
Thus the local-time increment is exactly the one-step budget spent by children
whose conditional probabilities cross the Bayes threshold, together with
children split from a tied parent.  Its tail is the boundary-atom error:
\[
\varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]
=\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}}
\mu(C)\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}.
\]
Consequently the boundary rate laws are also tail estimates for the Tanaka
local time.  For example, under the hypotheses of
Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling},
\[
\theta c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
\le \varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]
\le \frac{c_+}{2}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}),
\]
and under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:nonuniform-cylinder},
\[
\theta c_1m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
\le \varepsilon_0(\mathsf{P};\mu)-\EE[L_m^{1/2}]
\le \frac{c_2}{2}m^\alpha\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On each child $D$, the random variables $p_k$ and $p_{k+1}$ are equal to
$q_{D^-}$ and $q_D$, respectively.  Therefore the definition of
$L_{k+1}^{1/2}-L_k^{1/2}$ gives the displayed atomwise sum.  The identity with
$\varepsilon_k-\varepsilon_{k+1}$ follows from Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka}
applied at the two consecutive depths.  The piecewise formula is obtained by
checking the four sign possibilities for $u-\frac12$ and $v-\frac12$, with
$\sgn(0)=0$.

The tail identity is Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka} together with the atom
decomposition in Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}; non-boundary
atoms have local error zero.  The two final displays are precisely
Theorems~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} and~\ref{thm:nonuniform-cylinder}
with this tail identity substituted for $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)$.
\end{proof}

\section{Boundary-Controlled Rates}
\label{sec:rates}

\subsection{Boundary atoms and the growth scale}

\begin{definition}[Boundary atoms and cylinder growth]
\label{def:boundary-cylinder}
For $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$, the boundary family at
resolution $m$ is
\[
\partial_m\mathsf{P}
:=\{C\in\mathcal{C}_m:\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C)>0\text{ and }\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})>0\},
\]
with cardinality $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}):=\#(\partial_m\mathsf{P})$.

Let $M_m:=\#\mathcal{C}_m$.  When the limit
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}:=\lim_{m\to\infty}M_m^{1/m}$ exists and exceeds $1$,
it is the \emph{cylinder growth base}.  For a mixing SFT, $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}$
is the spectral radius of the transition matrix \cite{LindMarcus1995}.  The
\emph{upper boundary dimension} is
\[
\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{P})
:=\limsup_{m\to\infty}
\frac{\log \max\{N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}),1\}}{m\log\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}.
\]
\end{definition}

The limsup exponent in Definition~\ref{def:boundary-cylinder} is a property of
the chosen filtration and presentation.  For arbitrary filtrations we make no
representation-invariance claim at this level.  The symbolic finite-state
theorem below isolates the higher-block invariant quantity relevant for
shift-dynamical
applications: the ambiguity growth base of the observer graph, whose
normalization agrees with
$\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{P})$ and is unchanged under higher-block
recoding (Proposition~\ref{prop:higher-block-invariance}).  No broader
invariance under general finite-to-one sliding block conjugacies is claimed
here.  The maximum in Definition~\ref{def:boundary-cylinder} is only a
zero-count convention: if the boundary is eventually empty, then the resulting
exponent is $0$ and the scan error is eventually zero.
In general $\partial_m\mathsf{P}$ is a family of positive atoms of
$\mathcal{F}_m$; in the symbolic specialization those atoms are precisely the
positive cylinders, which is why the later symbolic discussion keeps the
cylinder terminology.

\subsection{Finite-state posterior presentations}
\label{sub:finite-posterior}

The next theorem supplies a finite-state symbolic source of the thickness
hypothesis and of the ambiguity exponent.  The input is a finite observer that
tracks, from a prefix alone, whether the target event is already forced true,
already forced false, or still ambiguous.

\begin{definition}[Finite-state posterior presentation]
\label{def:finite-posterior}
Let $X\subseteq\{0,1\}^{\NN}$ be a one-sided shift space, let $\mu$ be a Borel
probability measure on $X$, and let $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$.  A
\emph{finite-state posterior presentation} for $\mathsf{P}$ consists of a
finite set
\[
Q=Q_1\sqcup Q_0\sqcup Q_\partial,
\]
an initial state $q_\emptyset\in Q$, and partial update maps
$T_0,T_1:Q\to Q$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item for every positive word $a=a_0\cdots a_{m-1}$ in the language of $X$,
  the iterated state
  \[
  q(a):=T_{a_{m-1}}\circ\cdots\circ T_{a_0}(q_\emptyset)
  \]
  is defined;
\item $Q_0$ and $Q_1$ are absorbing under every defined transition;
\item for every positive word $a$ with cylinder $[a]\subset X$,
  \[
  q(a)\in Q_1 \Rightarrow \mu([a]\setminus\mathsf{P})=0,\qquad
  q(a)\in Q_0 \Rightarrow \mu([a]\cap\mathsf{P})=0,
  \]
  and
  \[
  q(a)\in Q_\partial \Longleftrightarrow [a]\in\partial_{|a|}\mathsf{P}.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Finite-state posterior theorem]
\label{thm:finite-state-posterior}
Let $X$ be a mixing one-step SFT on the alphabet $\{0,1\}$ with transition
matrix $A$, and let $\mu$ be its Parry measure.  Let
$\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$ admit a finite-state posterior presentation
as in Definition~\ref{def:finite-posterior}.  Assume $q_\emptyset\in Q_\partial$
and let $Q_\partial^{\mathrm{acc}}\subseteq Q_\partial$ be the ambiguous states
reachable from $q_\emptyset$.  Define the ambiguous transition matrix
$B=(B_{uv})_{u,v\in Q_\partial^{\mathrm{acc}}}$ by
\[
B_{uv}:=\#\{b\in\{0,1\}:T_b(u)=v\}.
\]
Assume there exists $R\ge 1$ such that for every
$u\in Q_\partial^{\mathrm{acc}}$ there are admissible words
$w_u^+,w_u^-$ of length at most $R$ with
\[
T_{w_u^+}(u)\in Q_1,
\qquad
T_{w_u^-}(u)\in Q_0.
\]
Then the following hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item For every $m\ge 1$,
  \[
  N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})=e_{q_\emptyset}^{\top}B^m\mathbf{1},
  \]
  where $e_{q_\emptyset}$ is the coordinate vector of $q_\emptyset$ and
  $\mathbf{1}$ is the all-ones column vector.
\item If $B$ is nilpotent, then $\partial_m\mathsf{P}=\varnothing$ for all
  large $m$.  Otherwise
  \[
  \Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})
  :=\limsup_{m\to\infty}\max\{N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}),1\}^{1/m}
  =\rho(B),
  \]
  where $\rho(B)$ is the spectral radius of $B$.  In the nilpotent case set
  $\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})=1$.  Then the normalized ambiguity exponent
  \[
  \delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})
  :=\frac{\log\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})}{\log\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}
  \]
  satisfies
  \[
  \delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})
  =\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{P}).
  \]
\item Let
  \[
  p_*:=\min\{\PP_\mu(x_{n+1}=j\mid x_n=i):A_{ij}=1\}>0,
  \qquad
  \theta:=p_*^R.
  \]
  Then every boundary cylinder $C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}$ satisfies
  \[
  \min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}\ge \theta.
  \]
\item There exist constants $c_-,c_+>0$ such that for every $m\ge 1$,
  \[
  \theta c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}e_{q_\emptyset}^{\top}B^m\mathbf{1}
  \le \varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
  \le \frac{c_+}{2}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}e_{q_\emptyset}^{\top}B^m\mathbf{1}.
  \]
  If $B$ is primitive, then
  \[
  \varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\asymp
  \left(\frac{\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})}{\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}\right)^m
  =\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P}))m}.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Because $Q_0$ and $Q_1$ are absorbing, a positive word $a$ of length $m$ is
boundary if and only if the run of the observer stays inside
$Q_\partial^{\mathrm{acc}}$ for $m$ steps and ends there.  The number of such
words is exactly the number of length-$m$ paths in the ambiguous automaton
starting from $q_\emptyset$, which is
$e_{q_\emptyset}^{\top}B^m\mathbf{1}$.  This proves (1).

If $B$ is nilpotent then $B^m=0$ for all large $m$, so (1) gives eventual
emptiness of the boundary.  Assume now that $B$ is not nilpotent.  The upper
bound
\[
N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})\le \|e_{q_\emptyset}^{\top}\|\,\|B^m\|\,\|\mathbf{1}\|
\]
implies
$\limsup_m N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})^{1/m}\le \rho(B)$ by Gelfand's formula.
Conversely, choose a strongly connected component of the ambiguous graph with
spectral radius $\rho(B)$ that is reachable from $q_\emptyset$.  Perron--Frobenius
theory on that component gives $c>0$ and infinitely many $m$ for which
$N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})\ge c\,\rho(B)^m$.  Hence
\[
\limsup_{m\to\infty}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})^{1/m}=\rho(B),
\]
and the dimension formula in (2) follows from
Definition~\ref{def:boundary-cylinder}.

For (3), let $C=[a]\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}$ and write
$u:=q(a)\in Q_\partial^{\mathrm{acc}}$.  By hypothesis there are admissible
words $w_u^+,w_u^-$ of lengths at most $R$ with
$T_{w_u^+}(u)\in Q_1$ and $T_{w_u^-}(u)\in Q_0$.  Therefore
$[aw_u^+]\subseteq \mathsf{P}$ modulo $\mu$-null sets and
$[aw_u^-]\subseteq C\setminus \mathsf{P}$ modulo $\mu$-null sets.  Since the
Parry measure is the Markov measure with transition matrix derived from $A$,
every admissible one-step transition has conditional probability at least
$p_*$; hence each admissible continuation of length at most $R$ has
conditional probability at least $p_*^R=\theta$.  Therefore
\[
\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\ge \mu([aw_u^+]\mid[a])\ge \theta
\]
and similarly
\[
1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\ge \mu([aw_u^-]\mid[a])\ge \theta.
\]
This is the claimed thickness estimate.

Finally, Parry's formula \cite{Parry1964} gives constants $c_-,c_+>0$ such
that
$c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}\le \mu(C)\le c_+\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$
for every positive $m$-cylinder $C$.  Combining these cylinder bounds with
Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}, the thickness estimate from (3),
and the count identity from (1), gives the two-sided inequality in (4).  If
$B$ is primitive, Perron--Frobenius theory gives
$e_{q_\emptyset}^{\top}B^m\mathbf{1}\asymp \rho(B)^m$, and substitution into
the two-sided estimate yields the displayed asymptotic law.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Higher-block invariance of the ambiguity exponent]
\label{prop:higher-block-invariance}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-state-posterior}.  Then
$\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ and
$\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ are independent of the chosen finite-state
posterior presentation.  Let $k\ge 1$, let
$\beta_k:X\to X^{[k]}$ be the standard $k$-block conjugacy to the higher-block
presentation \cite{LindMarcus1995}, let
$\mu^{[k]}:=\mu\circ\beta_k^{-1}$, and let
$\mathsf{P}^{[k]}:=\beta_k(\mathsf{P})$.  Then for every $m\ge 1$,
\[
N_m^{[k]}(\partial\mathsf{P}^{[k]})
=N_{m+k-1}(\partial\mathsf{P}),
\qquad
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P}^{[k]};\mu^{[k]})
=\varepsilon_{m+k-1}(\mathsf{P};\mu),
\]
and the cylinder growth bases satisfy
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{[k]}=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}$.  Consequently
\[
\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P}^{[k]})=\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P}),
\qquad
\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P}^{[k]})=\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P}).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-state-posterior}(2) expresses
$\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ as the limsup growth base of the boundary
counts, so any two finite-state posterior presentations yield the same
$\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ and hence the same
$\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})$.

For the higher-block claim, a length-$m$ cylinder in $X^{[k]}$ is determined by
admissible overlapping $k$-blocks
$u_0,\dots,u_{m-1}$ and therefore corresponds bijectively to a unique original
word $a_0\cdots a_{m+k-2}$ of length $m+k-1$.  If
$D=[u_0\cdots u_{m-1}]_{[k]}\subset X^{[k]}$ denotes the $k$-block cylinder and
$C=[a_0\cdots a_{m+k-2}]\subset X$ the associated original cylinder, then
$\beta_k^{-1}(D)=C$.  Therefore
\[
\mu^{[k]}(\mathsf{P}^{[k]}\cap D)=\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),
\qquad
\mu^{[k]}(D\setminus \mathsf{P}^{[k]})=\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P}),
\]
so $D$ is boundary for $\mathsf{P}^{[k]}$ if and only if $C$ is boundary for
$\mathsf{P}$.  This gives
$N_m^{[k]}(\partial\mathsf{P}^{[k]})=N_{m+k-1}(\partial\mathsf{P})$.  The same
cylinder correspondence shows
$M_m^{[k]}=M_{m+k-1}$, hence
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{[k]}=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}$.

Likewise the pullback of the depth-$m$ prefix sigma algebra on $X^{[k]}$ is the
depth-$(m+k-1)$ prefix sigma algebra on $X$:
\[
\beta_k^{-1}\bigl(\mathcal{F}_m^{[k]}\bigr)=\mathcal{F}_{m+k-1}.
\]
Taking the infimum defining the scan error over corresponding measurable sets
gives
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P}^{[k]};\mu^{[k]})
=\varepsilon_{m+k-1}(\mathsf{P};\mu)$.  The displayed invariance of
$\Lambda_\partial$ and $\delta_\partial$ now follows from the count identity
and the equality of cylinder growth bases.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Sofic and hidden-Markov origin]
\label{rem:sofic-hidden-markov}
The state $q(a)$ in Definition~\ref{def:finite-posterior} can be interpreted as
an observer state.  For a right-resolving sofic presentation, one includes the
current presentation vertex together with the ambiguity status of
$\mathsf{P}$; for a finite hidden Markov model, one may equivalently use the
finite posterior class of hidden states compatible with both
$\mathsf{P}$ and $\mathsf{P}^c$.  After recoding to an edge shift, both cases
fall under Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-state-posterior}.  Thus the theorem converts
finite observer data directly into the thickness constant and the higher-block
invariant ambiguity exponent $\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})$; compare
\cite{Blackwell1957,Heller1965,Fischer1975,Krieger1984,LindMarcus1995}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Rate theorems}

Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-state-posterior} and
Proposition~\ref{prop:higher-block-invariance} identify a higher-block
invariant symbolic exponent $\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ in the finite-state
SFT setting.  The
next results are the general filtration-level transfer principles: once
boundary count and boundary thickness are known, they turn those inputs into
scan-error bounds.  Their hypotheses are stated in terms of the raw boundary
counts $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$, with no representation-invariance claim
beyond the symbolic higher-block setting above.

\begin{theorem}[Boundary-dimension upper bound]
\label{thm:boundary-dimension}
Let $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$.  Assume that the cylinder growth base
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}$ exists and $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}>1$.  Suppose
$\mu(C)\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ for all $m\ge 1$ and every positive
depth-$m$ atom $C\in\mathcal{C}_m$.  Then
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}).
\]
If $\overline{\dim}_B(\partial\mathsf{P})=\delta<1$, then
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)\le\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-\delta)m+o(m)}$.
\leanverified{paper_prefix_scan_error_boundary_dimension_upper}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Non-boundary cylinders contribute zero to the decomposition
(Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}), since off $\partial_m\mathsf{P}$
the local term
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}
\]
vanishes.  On boundary cylinders,
$\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\cap C),\mu(C\setminus\mathsf{P})\}\le\mu(C)\le
K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$.  Summing gives
$\varepsilon_m\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
For $\delta<1$: fix $\eta>0$; for large $m$,
$N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})\le\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{(\delta+\eta)m}$, giving
$\varepsilon_m\le K\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-\delta-\eta)m}$.  Since $\eta$ and
the constant $K$ contribute $o(m)$ after taking logs, the bound follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Two-sided bounds and exponent law from boundary thickness]
\label{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}
Let $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$.  Assume that the cylinder growth base
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}$ exists and $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}>1$.  Suppose
$c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}\le\mu(C)\le c_+\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ for
all $m\ge 1$ and every positive depth-$m$ atom $C\in\mathcal{C}_m$, and suppose
there exist
$\theta\in(0,\frac12]$ and $m_0$ such that for all $m\ge m_0$ and all
$C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}$,
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}\ge\theta.
\]
Then for all $m\ge m_0$,
\[
\theta c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
\le\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
\le\frac{c_+}{2}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}).
\]
If $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{\delta m+o(m)}$, then
\[
\lim_{m\to\infty}\left(-\frac{1}{m}\log_{\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}\varepsilon_m\right)=1-\delta,
\]
so $\varepsilon_m$ has exponential decay exponent $1-\delta$, where $\delta$
is the exponential growth exponent of $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder},
$\varepsilon_m=\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}}\mu(C)\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}$.
Lower: thickness and the lower cylinder bound give
$\varepsilon_m\ge\theta c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
Upper: $\min\{p,1-p\}\le\frac12$ and the upper cylinder bound give
$\varepsilon_m\le\frac{c_+}{2}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
Inserting $N_m=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{\delta m+o(m)}$ and taking
$-\log_{\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}}/m$ yields $1-\delta$ in the limit.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Parry versus general Gibbs measures]
\label{rem:parry-vs-gibbs}
The hypotheses of Theorems~\ref{thm:boundary-dimension}
and~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} are genuinely same-length assumptions.
For a mixing SFT with Parry measure $\mu_{\max}$, every admissible word
$a=a_0\cdots a_{m-1}$ satisfies
\[
\mu_{\max}([a])
=\frac{\ell_{a_0}r_{a_{m-1}}}{\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{m-1}\langle \ell,r\rangle},
\]
where $\ell,r$ are positive left/right Perron--Frobenius eigenvectors of the
transition matrix.  Since only the endpoints appear, one obtains constants
$c_\pm>0$ with $c_-\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}\le\mu_{\max}([a])\le
c_+\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ uniformly over all admissible words of length
$m$ \cite{Parry1964}.  By contrast, a H\"older Gibbs state for a potential
$\phi$ satisfies
\[
\mu_\phi([a])\asymp \exp\!\bigl(S_m\phi(x)-mP(\phi)\bigr)
\qquad(x\in[a]),
\]
so same-length cylinders generally differ exponentially with the word
\cite{Bowen1975,WaltersErgodicTheory}.  In that regime a count-only law in
terms of $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$ is no longer natural; the corresponding
quantity is a weighted boundary partition sum.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Extension to log-regular cylinder measures}
\label{sub:nonuniform}

The uniform bounds $\mu(C)\asymp\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ hold for Parry
(maximal-entropy) measures on mixing SFTs \cite{Parry1964}; for general
H\"older Gibbs measures one has instead the potential-weighted estimate from
Remark~\ref{rem:parry-vs-gibbs}, so same-length cylinders need not have
comparable masses.  The following log-regular hypothesis isolates a weaker
count-based regime in which polynomial distortions are allowed but the
exponential scale remains $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$.

\begin{definition}[Log-regular measure]
\label{def:log-regular}
A measure $\mu$ is \emph{log-regular with exponent $\alpha>0$}, relative to an
existing cylinder growth base $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}>1$, if there exist
$0<c_1\le c_2<\infty$ such that for all $m\ge 1$ and every positive depth-$m$
atom $C\in\mathcal{C}_m$,
\[
c_1 m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}\le\mu(C)\le c_2 m^{\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Rate law for log-regular measures]
\label{thm:nonuniform-cylinder}
Let $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_\infty$.  Assume that the cylinder growth base
$\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}$ exists and $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}>1$.  Let $\mu$ be
log-regular with exponent $\alpha>0$.  Assume the thickness condition of
Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling} holds with some $\theta>0$ and
$m_0$.  Then for all $m\ge m_0$,
\[
\theta c_1 m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
\le\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
\le \frac{c_2}{2}m^{\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P}).
\]
If $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{\delta m+o(m)}$, then
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-\delta)m+o(m)}$ and the
exponential rate $1-\delta$ is unchanged from the uniform case.  More quantitatively,
if for some $\beta\ge 0$ and $A_-,A_+>0$,
\[
A_-m^{-\beta}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{\delta m}
\le N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})
\le A_+m^\beta\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{\delta m}
\]
for all large $m$, then for all large $m$,
\[
\theta c_1A_-m^{-(\alpha+\beta)}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-\delta)m}
\le\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
\le \frac{c_2A_+}{2}m^{\alpha+\beta}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-\delta)m}.
\]
\leanverified{paper_prefix_scan_error_nonuniform_cylinder}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The same decomposition argument as in Theorem~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}
applies with $c_1 m^{-\alpha}$ and $c_2 m^\alpha$ in place of $c_-$ and
$c_+/2$.  Thus
\[
\varepsilon_m
=\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}}\mu(C)
\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}
\]
is bounded below by
$\theta c_1m^{-\alpha}\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$
and above by
$\frac{c_2}{2}m^\alpha\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.
If $N_m=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{\delta m+o(m)}$, then the two bounds have logarithms
$-(1-\delta)m\log\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}+o(m)$, because
$\log m=o(m)$.  This gives
$\varepsilon_m=\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-(1-\delta)m+o(m)}$.  The final displayed
polynomial estimate follows by substituting the two-sided polynomial bounds
for $N_m$ into the two-sided estimate above.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[A log-regular measure with a genuine polynomial correction]
\label{ex:log-regular-polynomial-correction}
Let $X=\{0,1\}^{\NN}$ with its prefix filtration and let
$0<\alpha<1$.  Define the independent, non-identically distributed product
measure $\mu_\alpha$ by
\[
\mu_\alpha(x_n=0)=\frac12\left(1+\frac{\alpha}{n+2}\right),
\qquad
\mu_\alpha(x_n=1)=\frac12\left(1-\frac{\alpha}{n+2}\right).
\]
Then $\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}=2$ and $\mu_\alpha$ is log-regular with exponent
$\alpha$.  Indeed, for every word $a=a_0\cdots a_{m-1}$,
\[
\mu_\alpha([a])=2^{-m}\prod_{n=0}^{m-1}
\left(1+\sigma_n(a_n)\frac{\alpha}{n+2}\right),
\qquad \sigma_n(0)=1,\quad \sigma_n(1)=-1,
\]
and the two extremal products satisfy
\[
\prod_{n=0}^{m-1}\left(1+\frac{\alpha}{n+2}\right)\asymp m^\alpha,
\qquad
\prod_{n=0}^{m-1}\left(1-\frac{\alpha}{n+2}\right)\asymp m^{-\alpha},
\]
by the standard gamma-ratio estimate
$\Gamma(m+c)/\Gamma(m)\asymp m^c$.

Let $Y:=\{0^\infty\}$ and, for $x\notin Y$, let
$\rho(x):=\min\{n\ge 0:x_n=1\}$.  Define
\[
\mathsf{P}:=\{x\notin Y:\rho(x)\text{ is even}\}\cup Y.
\]
At depth $m$, the only boundary cylinder is $[0^m]$, so
$N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})=1$ and the raw count exponent is $\delta=0$.  If the prefix is not $0^m$, the first
$1$ has already appeared and the event is decided.  From $[0^m]$, however,
one can force either parity of the first $1$ within at most two further
symbols; since all one-coordinate probabilities are bounded below by
$q_*:=(1-\alpha/2)/2>0$,
\[
\min\{\mu_\alpha(\mathsf{P}\mid[0^m]),
1-\mu_\alpha(\mathsf{P}\mid[0^m])\}\ge q_*^2.
\]
Hence the thickness hypothesis holds and
\[
q_*^2\,\mu_\alpha([0^m])
\le \varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu_\alpha)
\le \frac12\,\mu_\alpha([0^m]).
\]
Finally,
\[
\mu_\alpha([0^m])
=2^{-m}\prod_{n=0}^{m-1}\left(1+\frac{\alpha}{n+2}\right)
\asymp m^\alpha 2^{-m}.
\]
Thus
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu_\alpha)\asymp m^\alpha 2^{-m}.
\]
The exponential rate is still $1-\delta=1$, but the polynomial factor is real; it
cannot be replaced by a uniform constant under log-regularity alone.  Reversing
the signs in the one-coordinate probabilities gives the matching
$m^{-\alpha}2^{-m}$ lower-side behaviour.
\end{example}

\section{Examples}
\label{sec:examples}

\begin{example}[Finite-horizon events]
\label{ex:finite-horizon-event}
If $\mathsf{P}\in\mathcal{F}_r$ for some finite $r$, then
$\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)=0$ for all $m\ge r$.  Indeed, $\partial_m\mathsf{P}=\varnothing$
for $m\ge r$ since membership in $\mathsf{P}$ is determined by the first $r$
symbols.  Under Definition~\ref{def:boundary-cylinder}, the upper boundary
dimension is $0$ in this eventually empty case.
\end{example}

For the golden-mean example, let
\[
X:=\{x\in\{0,1\}^{\NN}:x_ix_{i+1}\ne 11\text{ for all }i\ge 0\}
\]
and let $\mu$ be its Parry measure.  The transition matrix is
\[
A=\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}=\varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt{5}}{2}.
\]
For a finite word $a$, the cylinder $[a]$ is always understood as a cylinder
inside $X$.

\begin{lemma}[Explicit Parry cylinder bounds]
\label{lem:golden-parry-bounds}
For every admissible word $a=a_0\cdots a_{m-1}$ of length $m\ge 1$,
\[
\mu([a])=\frac{r_{a_0}r_{a_{m-1}}}{\varphi^2+1}\,\varphi^{-(m-1)},
\qquad r_0=\varphi,\quad r_1=1.
\]
Consequently
\[
\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\,\varphi^{-m}
\le \mu([a])
\le \frac{\varphi^2}{\sqrt{5}}\,\varphi^{-m}.
\]
The positive transition probabilities are
\[
\PP_\mu(0\mid 0)=\varphi^{-1},\qquad
\PP_\mu(1\mid 0)=\varphi^{-2},\qquad
\PP_\mu(0\mid 1)=1.
\]
In particular, if $m\ge 1$ and $w$ is an admissible continuation of $a$ of
length $\ell$, then
\[
\mu([aw]\mid[a])\ge p_*^\ell,\qquad p_*:=\varphi^{-2}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The vector $r=(\varphi,1)$ is a right and left Perron--Frobenius eigenvector
of $A$.  The Parry transition matrix is
$P_{ij}=A_{ij}r_j/(\varphi r_i)$, and the stationary distribution is
$\pi_i=r_i^2/(r_0^2+r_1^2)$ \cite{Parry1964}.  Hence, for an admissible word,
\[
\mu([a])=\pi_{a_0}\prod_{j=0}^{m-2}P_{a_j a_{j+1}}
=\frac{r_{a_0}r_{a_{m-1}}}{\varphi^2+1}\,\varphi^{-(m-1)}.
\]
Since $1\le r_{a_0}r_{a_{m-1}}\le\varphi^2$ and
$\varphi^2+1=\sqrt{5}\,\varphi$, the displayed cylinder bounds follow.  The
transition probabilities are obtained from the same formula for $P_{ij}$, and
the continuation estimate follows because every positive transition is at
least $p_*=\varphi^{-2}$.
\end{proof}

Let
\[
Y:=\{x\in X:x_{2j}=0\text{ for all }j\ge 0,\quad
x_{2j+1}x_{2j+3}\ne 11\text{ for all }j\ge 0\}.
\]
A finite word $a$ is called $Y$-admissible if $[a]\cap Y\ne\varnothing$.  For
$x\in X\setminus Y$, define
\[
\rho(x):=\min\{m\ge 1:x_0\cdots x_{m-1}\text{ is not }Y\text{-admissible}\}.
\]
Define $\mathsf{E}$ to be the set of points whose first obstruction at time
$\rho(x)$ is an even coordinate equal to $1$, and include $Y$ in $\mathsf{E}$.

\begin{lemma}[Bounded first-obstruction continuations]
\label{lem:golden-bounded-continuations}
Let $a$ be a $Y$-admissible word of length $m\ge 1$.  There are admissible
continuations $u_{\mathrm e}$ and $u_{\mathrm o}$, each of length at most five,
such that every proper prefix of $au_{\mathrm e}$ and $au_{\mathrm o}$ extending
$a$ is still $Y$-admissible, while $au_{\mathrm e}$ first leaves the
$Y$-language by an even-coordinate obstruction and $au_{\mathrm o}$ first
leaves it by an odd-coordinate obstruction.  Moreover,
\[
\mu([au_{\mathrm e}]\mid[a])\ge p_*^5,\qquad
\mu([au_{\mathrm o}]\mid[a])\ge p_*^5.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For the even obstruction, if the next coordinate $m$ is even and the preceding
symbol is $0$, append $1$ at coordinate $m$.  If $m$ is even and the preceding
symbol is $1$, append $001$, so the $1$ lands at the next even coordinate.  If
$m$ is odd, append $01$, so the $1$ lands at the next even coordinate.  In each
case the preceding coordinate of the new even $1$ is $0$, so the word remains
admissible in $X$, and no earlier appended prefix violates the defining
conditions of $Y$.

For the odd obstruction, let $r$ be the next odd coordinate not yet specified.
All newly specified even coordinates before the obstruction are set equal to
$0$.  If the most recent specified odd coordinate exists and carries the value
$1$, put $1$ at coordinate $r$.  Otherwise put $1$ at coordinate $r$, put $0$
at the following even coordinate, and put $1$ at the next odd coordinate.  This
creates the first adjacent pair $11$ in the induced odd-coordinate sequence and
does not create an even-coordinate obstruction beforehand.  The construction
uses at most four symbols, hence at most five.

Both constructed words are admissible in $X$.  Lemma~\ref{lem:golden-parry-bounds}
gives conditional mass at least $p_*^\ell$ for a continuation of length
$\ell\le 5$, and $p_*^\ell\ge p_*^5$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[The skeleton has zero Parry measure]
\label{lem:golden-y-null}
The set $Y$ satisfies $\mu(Y)=0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $A_m$ be the union of all $Y$-admissible cylinders of length $m$.  Then
$Y=\bigcap_{m\ge 1}A_m$.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:golden-bounded-continuations}, for
each $Y$-admissible word $a$ of length $m\ge 1$ there is a subcylinder of
$[a]$ with conditional mass at least $p_*^5$ whose points have left the
$Y$-language by time $m+5$.  Hence
\[
\mu(A_{m+5}\cap[a])\le (1-p_*^5)\mu([a]).
\]
Summing over the $Y$-admissible words of length $m$ gives
$\mu(A_{m+5})\le(1-p_*^5)\mu(A_m)$.  Iterating from $m=1$,
\[
\mu(A_{1+5n})\le (1-p_*^5)^n\mu(A_1)\to 0.
\]
Since $Y\subseteq A_{1+5n}$ for every $n$, $\mu(Y)=0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Golden-mean boundary skeleton]
\label{prop:golden-boundary-skeleton}
Let $a=a_0\cdots a_{m-1}$ be a finite word of length $m$, and let
$\mathrm{odd}(a):=a_1a_3\cdots a_{2\lfloor m/2\rfloor-1}$, interpreted as the
empty word when $\lfloor m/2\rfloor=0$.  Then $a$ is
$Y$-admissible if and only if all even coordinates visible in $a$ are $0$ and
$\mathrm{odd}(a)$ is golden-mean admissible.  For $x\in X\setminus Y$, the
first exit time $\rho(x)$ has exactly one of the following two forms:
\[
\rho(x)=2j+1\text{ and }x_{2j}=1,
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
\rho(x)=2j+4\text{ and }x_{2j+1}=x_{2j+3}=1.
\]
Finally, for every positive length-$m$ cylinder $[a]$,
\[
[a]\in\partial_m\mathsf{E}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad a
\text{ is }Y\text{-admissible}.
\]
Consequently, with $F_0=0,F_1=1$,
\[
N_{2k}(\partial\mathsf{E})=N_{2k+1}(\partial\mathsf{E})=F_{k+2}
\qquad(k\ge 0).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The characterization of $Y$-admissible words is immediate in one direction.
Conversely, if the visible even coordinates of $a$ are $0$ and
$\mathrm{odd}(a)$ has no adjacent $1$s, then extend $a$ by putting all future
even and odd coordinates equal to $0$; the extension lies in $Y$.

If $x\notin Y$, the first failure is either the first even coordinate carrying
$1$, which is seen at prefix length $2j+1$, or the first adjacent pair $11$ in
the odd-coordinate subsequence, whose second symbol is seen at prefix length
$2j+4$.  These prefix lengths have opposite parity, so the newly visible
obstruction is unique.

If $a$ is not $Y$-admissible, let $r\le m$ be the first prefix length at which
$a_0\cdots a_{r-1}$ is not $Y$-admissible.  The obstruction type at $r$ is
already fixed by $a$, and every point of $[a]$ has the same first obstruction;
therefore $[a]$ is contained either in $\mathsf{E}$ or in $\mathsf{E}^c$.
If $a$ is $Y$-admissible and $m\ge 1$, Lemma~\ref{lem:golden-bounded-continuations}
gives positive-measure subcylinders of $[a]$ contained in $\mathsf{E}$ and in
$\mathsf{E}^c$, so $[a]$ is a boundary cylinder.  For $m=0$, the empty cylinder
$X$ is boundary because the positive cylinders $[1]$ and $[0101]$ have first
even and first odd obstructions respectively.

For $m=2k$ or $m=2k+1$, the visible odd skeleton has length $k$.  The number
of golden-mean admissible words of length $k$ is $F_{k+2}$, by the recurrence
$b_k=b_{k-1}+b_{k-2}$ with $b_0=1$ and $b_1=2$.  The boundary equivalence gives
the displayed formula for $N_m(\partial\mathsf{E})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Diagonalisation of the golden transfer matrix]
\label{lem:golden-transfer-diagonalization}
Let $a=\varphi^{-1}$ and
\[
B=\begin{pmatrix}a^2&a^3\\ a&0\end{pmatrix},\qquad
v=\frac1{\sqrt{5}}(1,a),\qquad
w=\begin{pmatrix}a^2/2\\[2pt]1/2\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Define
\[
r_+=\begin{pmatrix}1\\ 1\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
r_-=\begin{pmatrix}-a^2\\ 1\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Then $Br_+=ar_+$ and $Br_-=-a^3r_-$, so
\[
B=P\begin{pmatrix}a&0\\ 0&-a^3\end{pmatrix}P^{-1},
\qquad
P=\begin{pmatrix}1&-a^2\\ 1&1\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Moreover,
\[
w=\frac{a^2}{1+a^2}\,r_+ + \frac{a}{2(1+a^2)}\,r_-,
\]
and for every $n\ge 0$,
\[
vB^n w=\frac15\,a^n-\frac1{10}\,(-a^3)^{n+1}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Using $a^2+a=1$, one checks
\[
B\begin{pmatrix}1\\ 1\end{pmatrix}
=\begin{pmatrix}a^2+a^3\\ a\end{pmatrix}
=\begin{pmatrix}a\\ a\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B\begin{pmatrix}-a^2\\ 1\end{pmatrix}
=\begin{pmatrix}0\\ -a^3\end{pmatrix}
=-a^3\begin{pmatrix}-a^2\\ 1\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Thus $r_\pm$ are eigenvectors with eigenvalues $a$ and $-a^3$ respectively,
and the displayed diagonalisation follows because $\det P=1+a^2>0$.

To decompose $w$, write $w=\alpha r_+ + \beta r_-$.  Then
\[
\alpha-a^2\beta=\frac{a^2}{2},
\qquad
\alpha+\beta=\frac12.
\]
Subtracting gives $(1+a^2)\beta=(1-a^2)/2=a/2$, hence
$\beta=\frac{a}{2(1+a^2)}$ and
$\alpha=\frac12-\beta=\frac{a^2}{1+a^2}$.

Now
\[
vr_+=\frac{1+a}{\sqrt{5}}=\frac{\varphi}{\sqrt{5}},
\qquad
vr_-=\frac{a-a^2}{\sqrt{5}}=\frac{a^3}{\sqrt{5}},
\]
since $1-a=a^2$.  Therefore
\[
vB^n w
=\alpha\,vr_+\,a^n+\beta\,vr_-\,(-a^3)^n.
\]
Using $\varphi a=1$ and
$1+a^2=1+\varphi^{-2}=\sqrt{5}\,\varphi^{-1}=\sqrt{5}\,a$, this becomes
\[
vB^n w
=\frac{a}{\sqrt{5}(1+a^2)}\,a^n
+\frac{a^4}{2\sqrt{5}(1+a^2)}\,(-a^3)^n
=\frac15\,a^n+\frac{a^3}{10}\,(-a^3)^n
=\frac15\,a^n-\frac1{10}\,(-a^3)^{n+1}.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Exact parity-resolved scan error in the golden example]
\label{prop:golden-parity-sharp}
For every $k\ge 1$,
\[
\gamma_k
:=\frac{\varphi}{5}\,\varphi^{-k}
-\frac{1}{10}\,(-\varphi^{-3})^k.
\]
\[
\varepsilon_{2k}(\mathsf{E};\mu)
=\varepsilon_{2k+1}(\mathsf{E};\mu)
=\gamma_k.
\]
Consequently
\[
\lim_{k\to\infty}\varphi^k\varepsilon_{2k}(\mathsf{E};\mu)
=\lim_{k\to\infty}\varphi^k\varepsilon_{2k+1}(\mathsf{E};\mu)
=\frac{\varphi}{5}.
\]
Equivalently, with normalization by $\varphi^{m/2}$,
\[
\varepsilon_{2k}(\mathsf{E};\mu)\sim \frac{\varphi}{5}\varphi^{-k},
\qquad
\varepsilon_{2k+1}(\mathsf{E};\mu)\sim
\frac{\varphi^{3/2}}{5}\varphi^{-(2k+1)/2}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Put $a:=\varphi^{-1}$ and $b:=\varphi^{-2}=a^2$.  Suppose the process has not
yet left the $Y$-language, and let the most recent odd-coordinate symbol be
$y\in\{0,1\}$.  Let $e_y$ be the conditional probability that the first future
obstruction is even when the next coordinate to be read is even, and let $o_y$
be the same probability when the next coordinate to be read is odd.  The Parry
transition probabilities in Lemma~\ref{lem:golden-parry-bounds} give
\[
e_0=b+ao_0,\qquad e_1=o_1,\qquad
o_0=ae_0+be_1,\qquad o_1=ae_0.
\]
Solving,
\[
e_0=\frac{\varphi}{2},\qquad e_1=\frac12,\qquad
o_0=\frac{1+b}{2},\qquad o_1=\frac12.
\]
Thus the local classification errors at an even-read state are $b/2$ and
$1/2$ for last odd symbol $0$ and $1$, respectively.  At an odd-read state the
local errors are $a/2$ and $1/2$; the corresponding length-$(2k+1)$ cylinder
masses are obtained from the length-$2k$ masses by multiplying by $a$ when the
last odd symbol is $0$ and by $1$ when it is $1$.  Hence the odd-depth
contribution has the same weights, since $a(a/2)=a^2/2=b/2$.

Let $s_k=(s_k(0),s_k(1))$ be the row vector of total Parry masses of
$Y$-admissible length-$2k$ cylinders whose last visible odd symbol is $0$ or
$1$.  Since $\pi_0=\varphi/\sqrt{5}$,
\[
s_k=vB^{k-1},\qquad
v=\frac1{\sqrt{5}}(1,a),\qquad
B=\begin{pmatrix}a^2&a^3\\ a&0\end{pmatrix}
\quad(k\ge 1).
\]
Indeed, the initial vector is
\[
s_1=\bigl(\mu([00]),\mu([01])\bigr)
=\bigl(\pi_0\PP_\mu(0\mid 0),\pi_0\PP_\mu(1\mid 0)\bigr)
=\frac1{\sqrt{5}}(1,a)=v.
\]
If a $Y$-admissible cylinder contributing to $s_k(0)$ has last visible odd
symbol $0$, then the next even symbol must be $0$, after which the next odd
symbol may be $0$ or $1$.  By Lemma~\ref{lem:golden-parry-bounds}, the
corresponding two-step cylinder-mass multipliers are
\[
\PP_\mu(0\mid 0)\PP_\mu(0\mid 0)=a^2,
\qquad
\PP_\mu(0\mid 0)\PP_\mu(1\mid 0)=a^3.
\]
If the last visible odd symbol is $1$, then the next even symbol is again
forced to be $0$, and $Y$-admissibility forbids the next odd symbol from being
$1$; hence the only allowed return is $1\to 0$, with multiplier
\[
\PP_\mu(0\mid 1)\PP_\mu(0\mid 0)=a.
\]
Therefore
\[
s_{k+1}(0)=a^2s_k(0)+as_k(1),\qquad
s_{k+1}(1)=a^3s_k(0),
\]
which is exactly the matrix recurrence $s_{k+1}=s_kB$.  With
\[
w=\begin{pmatrix}b/2\\[2pt]1/2\end{pmatrix},
\]
the preceding paragraph gives
$\varepsilon_{2k}(\mathsf{E};\mu)=\varepsilon_{2k+1}(\mathsf{E};\mu)
=vB^{k-1}w$.  Lemma~\ref{lem:golden-transfer-diagonalization} applied with
$n=k-1$ gives
\[
vB^{k-1}w=\frac{\varphi}{5}a^k-\frac{1}{10}(-a^3)^k.
\]
Substituting $a=\varphi^{-1}$ gives the displayed formula and its two
asymptotic normalizations.
\end{proof}

\begin{example}[Golden-mean shift: exact parity-resolved computation with $\delta_\partial(\mathsf{E})=1/2$]
\label{ex:golden-mean-specialization}
The set $\mathsf{E}$ belongs to $\mathcal{F}_\infty$: it is the union of the
cylinders on which a first even obstruction occurs, together with the closed
set $Y$.  The one-sided SFT Borel sigma algebra is generated by its cylinders,
so the closed set $Y$ is $\mathcal{F}_\infty$-measurable; moreover
Lemma~\ref{lem:golden-y-null} shows that this added skeleton is null.  It is
not decided by any finite prefix, because the all-zero
length-$m$ cylinder is $Y$-admissible and is therefore a boundary cylinder by
Proposition~\ref{prop:golden-boundary-skeleton}.

Proposition~\ref{prop:golden-boundary-skeleton} gives
\[
N_{2k}(\partial\mathsf{E})=N_{2k+1}(\partial\mathsf{E})=F_{k+2}
\sim\frac{\varphi^{k+2}}{\sqrt{5}},
\]
so
\[
\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{E})
=\limsup_{m\to\infty}\max\{N_m(\partial\mathsf{E}),1\}^{1/m}
=\varphi^{1/2},
\qquad
\delta_\partial(\mathsf{E})
=\frac{\log\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{E})}{\log\varphi}
=\frac{1}{2}.
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:higher-block-invariance}, these values are unchanged
under every higher-block recoding of the golden-mean shift.
The same bounded continuations give the thickness estimate
\[
\min\{\mu(\mathsf{E}\mid[a]),1-\mu(\mathsf{E}\mid[a])\}
\ge p_*^5=\varphi^{-10}
\qquad([a]\in\partial_m\mathsf{E},\ m\ge 1).
\]
The general rate theorem therefore predicts the correct exponential order.
Proposition~\ref{prop:golden-parity-sharp} sharpens this to the exact
parity-resolved formula
\[
\varepsilon_{2k}(\mathsf{E};\mu)
=\varepsilon_{2k+1}(\mathsf{E};\mu)
=\frac{\varphi}{5}\,\varphi^{-k}
-\frac{1}{10}\,(-\varphi^{-3})^k
\qquad(k\ge 1).
\]
In particular, for all sufficiently large $m$ there are constants
$C_1,C_2>0$ such that
\[
C_1\varphi^{-m/2}\le\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{E};\mu)\le C_2\varphi^{-m/2}.
\]
Thus $\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{E};\mu)\asymp\varphi^{-m/2}$.
\end{example}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We have shown that the scan error profile decomposes exactly over the positive
atoms of the prefix partition on $X$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:scan-error-cylinder}),
or equivalently over prefix cylinders on the symbolic readout space, that the
Bayes-optimal rule is the threshold classifier
(Proposition~\ref{prop:bayes-optimality}),
and that error decay is encoded by discrete local time at the threshold
(Theorem~\ref{thm:scan-tanaka}).  Theorem~\ref{thm:finite-state-posterior}
shows that, in a finite-state symbolic model, the thickness constant and the
higher-block invariant ambiguity growth base $\Lambda_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ can themselves
be read off from an observer graph, while
Proposition~\ref{prop:higher-block-invariance} shows that the normalized
exponent $\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ is unchanged by higher-block recoding.
The later rate laws then convert those quantities into scan-error decay.
Example~\ref{ex:golden-mean-specialization} computes the exact parity-resolved profile
for an explicit event with $\delta_\partial(\mathsf{E})=1/2$ on the
golden-mean shift.

\paragraph{Shannon--McMillan--Breiman and uniform bounds.}
The SMB theorem \cite{Shannon1948,McMillan1953,Breiman1957} states that for
$\mu$-a.e.\ $x$,
\[
-\frac{1}{m}\log\mu([x]_m)\to h(\mu)
\quad\text{as }m\to\infty,
\]
where $[x]_m$ is the length-$m$ cylinder containing $x$.  This is a pointwise
almost-sure statement about a single cylinder along a single orbit; it does
\emph{not} give a uniform bound over all admissible cylinders simultaneously.
The uniform bounds $\mu(C)\asymp\lambda_{\mathrm{cyl}}^{-m}$ in
Theorems~\ref{thm:boundary-dimension} and~\ref{thm:volume-boundary-scaling}
are strictly stronger.  For a mixing SFT with its Parry measure, these
same-length bounds are established by Parry \cite{Parry1964}, so the
count-based theorems apply directly.  For a general H\"older Gibbs measure,
Bowen's estimate \cite{Bowen1975} is instead
\[
\mu([a])\asymp \exp\!\bigl(S_m\phi(x)-mP(\phi)\bigr)\qquad(x\in[a]),
\]
which depends on the word $a$ through the Birkhoff sum $S_m\phi$.
Accordingly, a direct Gibbs-state analogue of our two-sided bound/exponent law should weight
boundary cylinders by a partition sum rather than by bare cardinality.

\paragraph{Relation to margin conditions.}
The exact boundary identity
\[
\varepsilon_m(\mathsf{P};\mu)
=\sum_{C\in\partial_m\mathsf{P}}
\mu(C)\min\{\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C),1-\mu(\mathsf{P}\mid C)\}
\]
shows the right comparison point.  Uniform thickness gives a lower bound by
boundary mass, but it does not by itself control how much of the ambient space
has terminal posterior $p_\infty$ close to $1/2$.  That latter question is the
domain of the Mammen--Tsybakov condition
\cite{MammenTsybakov1999,Tsybakov2004}, so the comparison is only heuristic.
A closer multiscale analogue to our setup is the dyadic tree analysis of Scott and Nowak
\cite{ScottNowak2006Dyadic}.  In the finite-state symbolic setting the relevant
exponent is the higher-block invariant quantity
$\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})$; in the general filtration-level theorems it is
only the raw count exponent of $N_m(\partial\mathsf{P})$.  We do not interpret
these quantities, or the thickness parameter $\theta$, as a classical margin
exponent.  Connecting either one to a margin exponent through filtration
geometry remains open.

\paragraph{Extensions.}
Natural generalizations include: finite alphabets of size $q$ (the discrete
Tanaka structure extends with $q-1$ threshold parameters); the continuous-time
limit to Tanaka--Meyer formula \cite{RevuzYor1999}; weakening the thickness
hypothesis to an $L^p$ condition on boundary-atom probabilities
(symbolically, boundary-cylinder probabilities); and
expressing the ambiguity exponent through conditional entropies, mutual
information, or an intrinsic pressure functional beyond the finite-state case.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The general rate laws still assume exponential growth of the positive-atom
counts $M_m=\#\mathcal{C}_m$ (equivalently, exponential cylinder complexity in
the symbolic specialization);
subexponential systems (Sturmian codings, substitution shifts) require
separate analysis.  The finite-state posterior theorem covers sofic and hidden
Markov situations with a bounded resolving window, but not arbitrary Gibbs
events whose ambiguity cannot be compressed into finitely many observer states.
For those systems one would need a weighted thermodynamic formalism for the
boundary rather than the count-based one used here.  Even in the finite-state
symbolic setting, the paper proves invariance of
$\delta_\partial(\mathsf{P})$ only under higher-block recoding; invariance
under general finite-to-one sliding block conjugacies remains open.
