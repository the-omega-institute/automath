\section{Perturbative Applications}
\label{sec:perturbation}

This section is application-level throughout. Its role is to show how the
determinant-side package from \Cref{sec:fredholm} interfaces with standard
finite-dimensional perturbation theory, spectral gaps, and twisted
determinants. None of the results below is used as a second structural
classification theorem alongside
\Cref{thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation}; the structural novelty of the paper
remains concentrated in the cyclic-block realization theorem and its sharp
trace-budget consequences.

\subsection{Branch isolation and secondary nonresonance}

The perturbative arguments are organized into two layers. The first layer is
\emph{branch isolation}: it selects a simple Riesz eigenvalue branch and
allows the reduced determinant on the complementary spectral subspace. The
second layer is \emph{secondary admissibility}: it is a separate
nonresonance hypothesis for the full factors \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\), \(k\ge2\).
No statement in the second layer is inherited from branch isolation unless
the expansion condition is explicitly added.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)}]
  \item \emph{Branch isolation} selects a simple holomorphic Riesz
  eigenvalue branch \(\lambda(\theta)\) and its complementary projector.
  It is enough for the reduced determinant \(C(A,\lambda)\).
  \item \emph{Secondary admissibility} adds absolute expansion of the
  selected branch. Together with the uniform complementary relative gap,
  this is equivalent to a subunit secondary-radius bound for the rescaled
  matrices \(\lambda^{-k}A\), \(k\ge2\). Only after this stronger
  hypothesis is imposed do we introduce the full secondary factors
  \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\), their principal trace-log branches, the functions
  \(\Phi_k\), and the full finite part \(F(A,\lambda)\).
\end{enumerate}

These are not interchangeable assumptions. If \(\mu\) is a complementary
eigenvalue, then
\[
  \abs{\lambda^{-k}\mu}
  =\abs{\mu/\lambda}\,\abs{\lambda}^{1-k},
\]
so the relative gap controls only the first factor. The distinguished
eigenvalue itself contributes the scaled eigenvalue
\(\lambda^{1-k}\), whose modulus is below \(1\) for all \(k\ge2\) exactly
when \(\abs{\lambda}>1\). Thus the principal trace-log branch for
\(I-\lambda^{-k}A\) is justified by the expanded relative branch,
equivalently by a subunit secondary radius, not by branch isolation alone.
The counterexample in
\Cref{lem:relative-gap-counterexample} shows that even
\(I-\lambda^{-2}A\) may be singular under a relative gap without
expansion.

\begin{definition}[Branch-isolating relative gap]
  \label{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap}
  Let \(\theta\mapsto A(\theta)\) be a holomorphic family of
  \(d\times d\) complex matrices near \(\theta_0\in\CC\). We say that
  \(A(\theta)\) satisfies the \emph{branch-isolating relative gap at
  \(\theta_0\)} if \(A(\theta_0)\) has a simple eigenvalue
  \(\lambda_0\ne 0\) such that
  \[
    \abs{\mu}<\abs{\lambda_0}
    \quad\text{for every }
    \mu\in\spec\!\bigl(A(\theta_0)\bigr)\setminus\{\lambda_0\}.
  \]
  Under this condition, after shrinking to a simply connected
  neighbourhood \(U\ni\theta_0\), the standard Riesz projection
  \[
    P(\theta)
    :=\frac{1}{2\pi i}
      \int_{\abs{z-\lambda_0}=\delta}
      (zI-A(\theta))^{-1}\,\dd z
  \]
  (for \(\delta>0\) chosen so that no other eigenvalue of
  \(A(\theta_0)\) lies in \(\abs{z-\lambda_0}\le\delta\)) defines a
  holomorphic rank-one projector. The corresponding eigenvalue branch
  \[
    \lambda(\theta):=\Tr(A(\theta)P(\theta))
  \]
  is the unique holomorphic function on \(U\) with \(\lambda(\theta_0)
  =\lambda_0\) and \(A(\theta)P(\theta)=\lambda(\theta)P(\theta)\).
  There exist \(q\in(0,1)\) and a neighbourhood \(U\) such that
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}
    \frac{\rho\!\left(A(\theta)\bigm|_{(I-P(\theta))\CC^d}\right)}
         {\abs{\lambda(\theta)}}
    \le q < 1.
  \]
  When \(A(\theta)\) is a primitive matrix with Perron root \(\lambda_0
  =\rho(A(\theta_0))>0\), this is exactly the Perron--Frobenius
  spectral gap, and \(\lambda(\theta)\) agrees with \(\rho(A(\theta))\)
  on the real slice where \(A(\theta)\) remains primitive. In the
  general complex case, \(\rho(A(\theta))\) is not holomorphic in
  \(\theta\), and \(\lambda(\theta)\) is the correct analytic object.
  This definition contains no assertion that \(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}
  A(\theta)\) is invertible. It is only the branch-selection hypothesis.
\end{definition}

\noindent\emph{Secondary layer.}
The next definition is the standing hypothesis for every full secondary
factor \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\), \(k\ge2\), used in this paper. It is not a
restatement of \Cref{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap}; it adds absolute
expansion of the selected branch. Until this definition has been imposed,
the symbols \(G_k\), \(\Phi_k\), and \(F(A,\lambda)\) are not used as
defined objects, except inside the counterexample and equivalence criteria
below.

For a finite matrix \(A\) with distinguished eigenvalue \(\lambda\ne0\),
define the secondary radius by
\[
  s(A,\lambda):=\sup_{k\ge2}\rho(\lambda^{-k}A).
\]
For a holomorphic branch \(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta)\) on \(U\) with
\(\lambda(\theta)\ne0\) throughout \(U\), define
\[
  s_U:=\sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
  \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right).
\]
These radii are the exact nonresonance quantities for the secondary
factors. They are recorded separately because they are not consequences of
the relative gap alone.

\noindent\emph{Strengthened hypothesis for secondary determinants.}
The secondary determinant package below is stated only after the following
definition has been imposed. This definition is the gate through which the
symbols
\[
  I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta,\qquad
  \Phi_k(\theta)=\log\det(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)^{-1},
  \qquad k\ge2,
\]
and the full finite part \(F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))\) enter the paper.
It is deliberately stronger than the branch-isolating relative gap in
\Cref{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap}.

\begin{definition}[Secondary-admissible Riesz branch]
  \label{def:secondary-admissible-pair}
  Let
  \((A_\theta,\lambda(\theta),P_\theta)_{\theta\in U}\) be a holomorphic
  Riesz branch and put \(Q_\theta:=I-P_\theta\). Define
  \[
    \lambda_\ast:=\inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)} .
  \]
  The branch is \emph{secondary-admissible on \(U\)} when the conditions
  below hold:
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(SA\arabic*)}]
    \item \label{sa:relative-gap}
      there is \(q\in(0,1)\) such that
      \[
        \sup_{\theta\in U}
        \frac{\rho\!\left(A_\theta\bigm|_{Q_\theta\CC^d}\right)}
             {\abs{\lambda(\theta)}}\le q;
      \]
    \item \label{sa:expanded-branch}
      the selected branch is uniformly expanding,
      \[
        \lambda_\ast>1 .
      \]
  \end{enumerate}
  Clause \textup{(SA1)} gives branch separation on the complementary
  spectral subspace. Clause \textup{(SA2)} is the absolute expansion that
  makes the selected eigenvalue itself nonresonant after the rescaling
  \(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}\). These two clauses imply the exact
  secondary-radius formula
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
      \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)
    =\lambda_\ast^{-1}<1,
  \]
  as proved immediately in \Cref{lem:secondary-admissibility-gate} and
  recorded again in \Cref{prop:secondary-nonresonance}. Thus the principal
  trace-log branches below are justified by the expanded relative branch,
  equivalently by this subunit secondary radius. They are not justified by
  \Cref{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap} alone.

  Pointwise, a pair \((A,\lambda)\) with \(A\) finite-dimensional and
  \(\lambda\ne0\) a distinguished simple eigenvalue is called
  \emph{relatively gapped} if every other eigenvalue \(\mu\) satisfies
  \[
    \abs{\mu}<\abs{\lambda}.
  \]
  For such a pair set
  \[
    s(A,\lambda):=\sup_{k\ge2}\rho(\lambda^{-k}A).
  \]
  A relatively gapped pair is called a
  \emph{secondary-admissible eigenvalue pair} if the distinguished
  eigenvalue is expanding:
  \[
    \abs{\lambda}>1 .
  \]
  Under the relative gap, \Cref{prop:pointwise-secondary-nonresonance}
  proves that this is equivalent to the subunit secondary-radius condition:
  \[
    s(A,\lambda)<1 .
  \]
  A relatively gapped pair with \(\abs{\lambda}\le1\) is therefore not
  secondary-admissible: the selected eigenvalue already contributes
  \(\rho(\lambda^{-k}A)=\abs{\lambda}^{1-k}\), so the secondary radius is
  \(1\) when \(\abs{\lambda}=1\) and is unbounded when
  \(\abs{\lambda}<1\).
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Immediate consequences of secondary admissibility]
  \label{lem:secondary-admissibility-gate}
  If a holomorphic Riesz branch is secondary-admissible on \(U\), set
  \[
    \lambda_\ast:=\inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)},
    \qquad
    s_U:=\sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right).
  \]
  Then
  \[
    s_U=\lambda_\ast^{-1}<1 .
  \]
  Choose \(\eta\in(\lambda_\ast^{-1},1)\). Then
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)
    \le \eta<1.
  \]
  Consequently each
  \(G_k(\theta)=I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\) is invertible, and every
  eigenvalue of \(G_k(\theta)\) lies in the fixed disc
  \[
    \{w:\abs{w-1}\le\eta\},
  \]
  which is contained in the principal-log domain. The branch
  \(\log\det G_k(\theta)^{-1}\) used below is therefore the
  finite-dimensional trace-log branch
  \[
    \sum_{m\ge1}
      \frac{\Tr((\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)^m)}{m}.
  \]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  By \textup{(SA1)}, the non-selected eigenvalues \(\nu\) satisfy
  \(\abs{\nu}\le q\abs{\lambda(\theta)}\) with \(q<1\), while the selected
  eigenvalue is \(\lambda(\theta)\). Hence for \(k\ge2\)
  \[
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)
    =\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}.
  \]
  Taking the supremum over \(k\ge2\) and \(\theta\in U\) gives
  \(s_U=\lambda_\ast^{-1}\), and \textup{(SA2)} gives
  \(\lambda_\ast^{-1}<1\). Thus every eigenvalue \(z\) of
  \(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\) satisfies \(\abs{z}\le\eta\). Therefore
  \(1-z\ne0\), so \(G_k(\theta)\) is invertible, and \(1-z\) belongs to the
  compact disc \(\abs{w-1}\le\eta\), contained in the principal-log domain.
  In finite dimension,
  \[
    -\log\det(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)
    =\sum_{m\ge1}
      \frac{\Tr((\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)^m)}{m}
  \]
  with the principal scalar logarithms, since the eigenvalues of
  \(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\) lie in the closed disc
  \(\abs{z}\le\eta<1\).
\end{proof}

\noindent\emph{No secondary factors from branch isolation alone.}
The convention for the rest of the paper is literal: except for explicit
counterexamples and gate criteria, a statement may use
\(I-\lambda^{-k}A\), \(\Phi_k\), or \(F(A,\lambda)\) only after secondary
admissibility has been assumed. Positive results made under the
branch-isolating relative gap alone are restricted to the Riesz branch, the
reduced resolvent, and the reduced determinant \(C(A,\lambda)\).

\begin{proposition}[No implication from relative gap to secondary factors]
  \label{lem:relative-gap-counterexample}
  The secondary-radius, equivalently expansion, requirement in secondary
  admissibility cannot be removed.
  For example,
  \[
    A=\begin{pmatrix}1/2&0\\0&1/4\end{pmatrix},
    \qquad \lambda=1/2,
  \]
  has a simple distinguished eigenvalue \(\lambda\) and the remaining
  eigenvalue \(\mu=1/4\) satisfies \(\abs{\mu}<\abs{\lambda}\). However
  \(I-\lambda^{-2}A=\mathrm{diag}(-1,0)\) is singular. Thus the valid
  implication is
  \[
    \text{relative gap},\quad \abs{\lambda}>1,\quad k\ge2
    \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
    \abs{\lambda^{-k}\nu}<1
  \]
  for every eigenvalue \(\nu\), and the first hypothesis alone is
  insufficient. The example may be regarded as a constant holomorphic
  family, so shrinking a parameter neighbourhood cannot repair the failure:
  the missing input is absolute expansion of the selected branch.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  The displayed matrix has spectrum \(\{1/2,1/4\}\), so \(\lambda=1/2\)
  is simple and dominates the complementary eigenvalue in the relative
  sense. For \(k=2\),
  \[
    \lambda^{-2}A
    =4A=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix},
  \]
  and hence
  \(I-\lambda^{-2}A=\mathrm{diag}(-1,0)\). Thus the secondary factor is
  singular although the branch-isolating relative gap holds. The final
  implication is the elementary estimate
  \[
    \abs{\lambda^{-k}\nu}
    \le \abs{\lambda}^{1-k}
  \]
  for the distinguished eigenvalue and all complementary eigenvalues,
  with strict inequality on the complementary part; it becomes a
  unit-disc bound only after the separate hypothesis
  \(\abs{\lambda}>1\) is imposed.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Exact pointwise secondary-factor criterion]
  \label{lem:pointwise-secondary-nonresonance}
  \label{prop:pointwise-secondary-nonresonance}
  Suppose \(A\) is a finite matrix, \(\lambda\ne0\) is a simple
  distinguished eigenvalue, and every other eigenvalue \(\mu\) satisfies
  \(\abs{\mu}<\abs{\lambda}\). Then
  \[
    \rho(\lambda^{-k}A)=\abs{\lambda}^{1-k}
    \qquad (k\ge2).
  \]
  The equality is forced by the distinguished eigenvalue \(\lambda\)
  itself; the complementary spectral gap only makes the other eigenvalues
  smaller after scaling.
  Hence
  \[
    s(A,\lambda)=
    \begin{cases}
      \abs{\lambda}^{-1}, & \abs{\lambda}>1,\\
      1, & \abs{\lambda}=1,\\
      +\infty, & \abs{\lambda}<1,
    \end{cases}
  \]
  and in particular
  \[
    s(A,\lambda)<1
    \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
    \abs{\lambda}>1.
  \]
  Thus, for a relatively gapped pair, secondary admissibility in
  \Cref{def:secondary-admissible-pair} is exactly the expanding condition
  \(\abs{\lambda}>1\), and this condition is equivalent to
  \(s(A,\lambda)<1\). The relative gap by itself leaves all
  three cases \(\abs{\lambda}>1\), \(\abs{\lambda}=1\), and
  \(\abs{\lambda}<1\) possible; it supplies secondary nonresonance only
  after the separate expanding hypothesis \(\abs{\lambda}>1\) is added. When
  \(\abs{\lambda}\le1\), the trace-log series defining the secondary
  determinants is not obtained from the relative gap, and no statement
  below that involves \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\), \(\Phi_k\), or
  \(F(A,\lambda)\) is being asserted for such a pair.
  Consequently, if \((A,\lambda)\) is secondary-admissible, equivalently
  in the relatively gapped setting if \(\abs{\lambda}>1\), then for every
  \(k\ge2\) and every \(\nu\in\spec(A)\),
  \[
    \abs{\lambda^{-k}\nu}<1.
  \]
  Hence \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\) is invertible and every scalar
  \(1-\lambda^{-k}\nu\), \(\nu\in\spec(A)\), lies in the disc
  \(\{w:\abs{w-1}<1\}\), which is contained in the principal-log domain.
  Therefore
  \[
    \log\det(I-\lambda^{-k}A)^{-1}
    :=\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{\Tr((\lambda^{-k}A)^m)}{m}
  \]
  is a single-valued finite-dimensional trace-log branch. This branch
  selection uses \(\abs{\lambda}>1\); it is not obtained from the relative
  gap alone. Moreover
  \[
    \sum_{k\ge2}\frac{\abs{\mu(k)}}{k}
    \abs{\log\det(I-\lambda^{-k}A)^{-1}}<\infty .
  \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  The spectrum of \(\lambda^{-k}A\) is
  \(\{\lambda^{-k}\nu:\nu\in\spec(A)\}\). The distinguished eigenvalue
  contributes
  \[
    \abs{\lambda^{-k}\lambda}=\abs{\lambda}^{1-k}.
  \]
  For a complementary eigenvalue \(\nu\), the relative gap gives
  \[
    \abs{\lambda^{-k}\nu}
    =\abs{\nu/\lambda}\,\abs{\lambda}^{1-k}
    <\abs{\lambda}^{1-k}.
  \]
  Therefore \(\rho(\lambda^{-k}A)=\abs{\lambda}^{1-k}\) for every
  \(k\ge2\). Taking the supremum over \(k\ge2\) gives the displayed formula
  for \(s(A,\lambda)\), and the equivalence with \(\abs{\lambda}>1\)
  follows immediately.

  The remaining assertions now apply this calculation under the expansion
  \(\abs{\lambda}>1\). For every eigenvalue \(\nu\) and every
  \(k\ge2\), the preceding bound gives
  \(\abs{\lambda^{-k}\nu}<1\).
  Thus \(1\notin\spec(\lambda^{-k}A)\), which is equivalent to
  invertibility of \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\). Since the spectrum of
  \(\lambda^{-k}A\) is contained in the open unit disc, finite-dimensional
  Jordan normal form gives convergence of
  \(\sum_{m\ge1}\Tr((\lambda^{-k}A)^m)/m\). Each scalar
  \(1-\lambda^{-k}\nu\) lies in the disc
  \(\{w:\abs{w-1}<1\}\), hence in the domain of the principal logarithm, and
  \[
    \log\det(I-\lambda^{-k}A)^{-1}
    =-\sum_{\nu\in\spec(A)}\log(1-\lambda^{-k}\nu)
  \]
  with algebraic multiplicities and principal scalar logarithms.

  To prove convergence in \(k\), choose \(k_0\) so large that
  \(\norm{\lambda^{-k}A}\le1/2\) for \(k\ge k_0\). For those \(k\),
  \[
    \abs{\log\det(I-\lambda^{-k}A)^{-1}}
    \le d\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{\norm{\lambda^{-k}A}^{m}}{m}
    \le 2d\,\norm{A}\,\abs{\lambda}^{-k}.
  \]
  Thus the tail over \(k\) is dominated by a geometric series. The
  finitely many terms \(2\le k<k_0\) are finite by the nonresonance already
  proved.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Exact uniform secondary-factor criterion]
  \label{lem:secondary-nonresonance}
  \label{prop:secondary-nonresonance}
  Let
  \[
    (A_\theta,\lambda(\theta),P_\theta)_{\theta\in U}
  \]
  be a holomorphic Riesz branch with a uniform complementary relative gap
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}
    \frac{\rho\!\left(A_\theta\bigm|_{(I-P_\theta)\CC^d}\right)}
         {\abs{\lambda(\theta)}}\le q<1 .
  \]
  Set
  \[
    \lambda_\ast:=\inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)}.
  \]
  Then for every \(\theta\in U\) and every \(k\ge2\),
  \[
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)
    =\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}.
  \]
  Consequently the uniform secondary radius is exactly
  \[
    s_U=
    \begin{cases}
      \lambda_\ast^{-1}, & \lambda_\ast>1,\\
      1, & \lambda_\ast=1,\\
      +\infty, & \lambda_\ast<1.
    \end{cases}
  \]
  In particular, the following are equivalent:
  \[
    s_U<1
    \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
    \lambda_\ast>1 .
  \]
  Therefore, under the uniform complementary relative gap, secondary
  admissibility on \(U\), as stated in
  \Cref{def:secondary-admissible-pair}, is exactly the expanded-branch
  condition \(\lambda_\ast>1\). Equivalently, after the relative gap has
  been assumed, it is the exact radius condition \(s_U<1\). This is a
  criterion under the strengthened hypothesis; it is not an implication
  from branch isolation alone.
  The displayed formula shows the obstruction: the selected eigenvalue
  itself contributes the term \(\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}\). Hence a
  uniform complementary relative gap becomes a uniform secondary-factor
  statement only after the separate absolute expansion
  \(\lambda_\ast>1\) is imposed, or equivalently after \(s_U<1\) is
  assumed. The relative gap supplies the analytic branch and the
  complementary spectral-ratio bound; it does not itself imply
  invertibility of \(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\).
  If \(\lambda_\ast>1\), then for every
  \(\theta\in U\), every \(k\ge2\), and every
  \(\nu\in\spec(A_\theta)\),
  \[
    \abs{\lambda(\theta)^{-k}\nu}
    \le \lambda_\ast^{-1}<1.
  \]
  Consequently \(1-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}\nu\) remains in the fixed disc
  \(\{w:\abs{w-1}\le\lambda_\ast^{-1}\}\), a compact subset of the
  principal-log domain. This is the uniform branch control used in the
  normal-convergence and truncation results below.
  Consequently \(s_U=\lambda_\ast^{-1}<1\), and
  \(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\) is invertible on \(U\) for all
  \(k\ge2\).

  In particular, if a holomorphic family satisfies the branch-isolating
  relative gap of \Cref{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap} at \(\theta_0\) and
  \(\abs{\lambda(\theta_0)}>1\), then after shrinking to a simply connected
  neighbourhood \(U\ni\theta_0\) the analytic Riesz branch is
  secondary-admissible on \(U\). These conclusions use absolute expansion;
  the relative gap supplies only the factor \(q<1\) on the complementary
  spectrum.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  The eigenvalues of \(A_\theta\) consist of the simple eigenvalue
  \(\lambda(\theta)\) and the eigenvalues of
  \(A_\theta|_{(I-P_\theta)\CC^d}\). The first gives, for every
  \(k\ge2\),
  \(\abs{\lambda(\theta)^{-k}\lambda(\theta)}
  =\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}\). For a
  complementary eigenvalue \(\nu\), the spectral-radius bound gives
  \(\abs{\nu}\le q\abs{\lambda(\theta)}\), hence
  \[
    \abs{\lambda(\theta)^{-k}\nu}
    \le q\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}.
  \]
  Since \(q<1\), the distinguished eigenvalue is the spectral-radius
  maximizer after scaling. This proves
  \[
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)
    =\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}.
  \]

  Taking the supremum over \(k\ge2\) and \(\theta\in U\) gives the displayed
  formula for \(s_U\). Indeed, if \(\lambda_\ast>1\), the largest value in
  \(k\) occurs at \(k=2\), so \(s_U=\sup_{\theta\in U}
  \abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{-1}=\lambda_\ast^{-1}\). If
  \(\lambda_\ast=1\), the same \(k=2\) expression has supremum \(1\). If
  \(\lambda_\ast<1\), some \(\theta\) has
  \(\abs{\lambda(\theta)}<1\), and then
  \(\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}\to+\infty\) as \(k\to\infty\).

  When \(\lambda_\ast>1\), all scaled eigenvalues are bounded by
  \[
    \lambda_\ast^{-1}<1.
  \]
  Thus \(s_U=\lambda_\ast^{-1}<1\), and
  \(1\notin\spec(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)\), which is exactly the
  invertibility of \(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\).
  Conversely, if \(s_U<1\), the displayed formula for \(s_U\) excludes the
  cases \(\lambda_\ast\le1\), hence \(\lambda_\ast>1\).

  Finally, under the branch-isolating relative gap of
  \Cref{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap} at \(\theta_0\), together with the
  separate expansion assumption \(\abs{\lambda(\theta_0)}>1\), continuity
  of the Riesz branch allows \(U\) to be shrunk so that the displayed
  uniform complementary gap holds and
  \(\inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)}>1\). The first part then gives
  secondary admissibility on \(U\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Reduced determinant under branch isolation]
  \label{def:reduced-determinant}
  Let \((A,\lambda)\) be a finite-dimensional relatively gapped pair:
  \(\lambda\ne0\) is a simple distinguished eigenvalue and every other
  eigenvalue \(\mu\) satisfies \(\abs{\mu}<\abs{\lambda}\). Set
  \(B:=I-A/\lambda\). The \emph{reduced determinant} is
  \[
    C(A,\lambda)
    :=\det\nolimits'(B)^{-1}
    :=\prod_{\mu\in\spec(A)\setminus\{\lambda\}}
      \!\!\left(1-\frac{\mu}{\lambda}\right)^{-1},
  \]
  where the product is taken with algebraic multiplicity. Its logarithm is
  the principal reduced branch
  \[
    \log C(A,\lambda)
    :=-\sum_{\mu\in\spec(A)\setminus\{\lambda\}}
      \log\!\left(1-\frac{\mu}{\lambda}\right),
  \]
  again with algebraic multiplicity. This is well-defined from branch
  isolation alone, because \(\abs{\mu/\lambda}<1\) on the complementary
  spectrum. This definition is intentionally only a reduced determinant:
  it does not introduce the secondary factors \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\), the
  functions \(\Phi_k\), or the full Abel finite part \(F(A,\lambda)\).
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Uniform power bounds for secondary factors]
  \label{lem:uniform-secondary-power-bounds}
  Let \(U\) have compact closure in the holomorphy domain of the Riesz
  branch, and assume that the branch is secondary-admissible on \(U\).
  Put
  \[
    B_k(\theta):=\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta,
    \qquad k\ge2 .
  \]
  If
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}\rho(B_k(\theta))\le\eta<1,
  \]
  then for every \(\tau\in(\eta,1)\) there is a constant
  \(C_\tau\) such that
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}\norm{B_k(\theta)^m}
    \le C_\tau\tau^m
    \qquad (m\ge0).
  \]
  Consequently, uniformly in \(\theta\in U\) and \(k\ge2\),
  \[
    \sum_{m\ge1}\frac{\Tr(B_k(\theta)^m)}{m}
  \]
  is an absolutely and normally convergent trace-log branch of
  \(\log\det(I-B_k(\theta))^{-1}\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
  Since \(\overline U\) is compact and
  \(\inf_U\abs{\lambda}>1\), the matrices \(A_\theta\) are bounded on
  \(U\) and \(B_k(\theta)\to0\) uniformly in \(\theta\) as
  \(k\to\infty\). Hence
  \[
    \mathcal K:=
    \overline{\{B_k(\theta):\theta\in U,\ k\ge2\}}
    \subset M_d(\CC)
  \]
  is compact. The spectral-radius bound extends to \(\mathcal K\) by
  continuity. Fix \(\tau\in(\eta,1)\). Then \(wI-B\) is invertible for
  every \(B\in\mathcal K\) and every \(\abs{w}=\tau\). Compactness gives
  \[
    M_\tau:=\sup_{\substack{B\in\mathcal K\\ \abs{w}=\tau}}
    \norm{(wI-B)^{-1}}<\infty .
  \]
  The holomorphic functional calculus on the circle \(\abs{w}=\tau\)
  gives
  \[
    B^m=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{\abs{w}=\tau}
      w^m(wI-B)^{-1}\,\dd w,
  \]
  and therefore
  \[
    \norm{B^m}\le M_\tau\tau^{m+1}
    \qquad (m\ge0).
  \]
  Enlarging the constant to handle \(m=0\) gives the stated bound.
  Since \(\abs{\Tr(B^m)}\le d\norm{B^m}\), the trace-log series is
  dominated by
  \[
    dC_\tau\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{\tau^m}{m}.
  \]
  The spectrum of each \(B_k(\theta)\) lies in the open unit disc, so the
  normally convergent trace-log series is the principal
  finite-dimensional branch of \(\log\det(I-B_k(\theta))^{-1}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Secondary Abel finite part]
  \label{def:algebraic-finite-part}
  \label{def:secondary-abel-finite-part}
  The full Abel finite-part functional is defined only for a
  secondary-admissible eigenvalue pair. If \((A,\lambda)\) is
  secondary-admissible, set
  \[
    \Phi_k(A,\lambda)
    :=\log\det\!\bigl(I-\lambda^{-k}A\bigr)^{-1}
    =\sum_{m\ge1}\frac{\Tr((\lambda^{-k}A)^m)}{m}
    \qquad(k\ge2),
  \]
  where the equality is the principal trace-log branch justified by
  \Cref{lem:pointwise-secondary-nonresonance}. Then
  \[
    F(A,\lambda)
    :=\log C(A,\lambda)
      +\sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}\Phi_k(A,\lambda).
  \]
  The series converges absolutely by
  \Cref{lem:pointwise-secondary-nonresonance}. If the pair is only
  relatively gapped and \(\abs{\lambda}\le1\), this definition is not made:
  the factors \(I-\lambda^{-k}A\) may be singular and the trace-log branch
  need not exist. When \(A\) is primitive with Perron root
  \(\lambda=\rho(A)>1\), one has \(C(A,\lambda)=C(A)\) in the notation of
  \eqref{eq:residue-constant-def}--\eqref{eq:reduced-det-constant} and
  \(F(A,\lambda)=\log\MM(A)\) by
  \Cref{thm:finite-part-primitive-closed-form}.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Finite-part constants under secondary admissibility}

Throughout this subsection, \(\theta\mapsto A_\theta\) is a holomorphic
family satisfying the branch-isolating relative gap of
\Cref{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap} at \(\theta_0\),
with analytic eigenvalue branch \(\lambda(\theta)\) and projector
\(P_\theta\). We write \(B_\theta:=I-A_\theta/\lambda(\theta)\) and
\(R_\theta:=B_\theta^{\#}\) for the group inverse. The reduced
determinant formula below only uses this isolated simple branch. Every
statement involving the secondary factors
\(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\), the functions \(\Phi_k\), or the full
finite part \(F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))\) is stated with the
secondary-admissible branch hypothesis on a specified neighbourhood \(U\).
By \Cref{lem:secondary-nonresonance}, this hypothesis is available after
shrinking precisely when the branch-isolating relative gap holds at
\(\theta_0\) and the distinguished branch is expanding there:
\(\abs{\lambda(\theta_0)}>1\). If the branch is only relatively gapped,
the results below stop at the reduced determinant
\(C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))\); the counterexample
\Cref{lem:relative-gap-counterexample} shows that even \(G_2\) may be
singular.

\begin{proposition}[Reduced-determinant gradient formula]
  \label{thm:finite-part-reduced-determinant-group-inverse-gradient}
  Assume only the branch-isolating relative gap for the chosen Riesz
  branch. Let \(\ell_\theta\) and
  \(r_\theta\) be left and right eigenvectors, normalized by
  \[
    \ell_\theta^{\mathsf T}r_\theta=1,
    \qquad
    P_\theta=r_\theta\ell_\theta^{\mathsf T},
  \]
  Then
  \[
    \partial_\theta\log C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
    =\Tr\!\left(
      R_\theta\left(
        \frac{\partial_\theta A_\theta}{\lambda(\theta)}
        -\frac{\partial_\theta\lambda(\theta)}{\lambda(\theta)^2}
         A_\theta\right)\right),
  \]
  with
  \[
    \partial_\theta\lambda(\theta)
    =\ell_\theta^{\mathsf T}(\partial_\theta A_\theta)r_\theta.
  \]
  This formula involves only the reduced determinant at the isolated
  eigenvalue and does not use the secondary factors
  \(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\); no expansion assumption is needed
  for this proposition.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Since \(\lambda(\theta)\) is a simple eigenvalue
  \cite[Chapter~II, \S1]{Kato1995Perturbation}, \(B_\theta\) has a
  simple zero eigenvalue and all other eigenvalues are non-zero; hence
  the group inverse \(R_\theta\) exists; see
  \cite[Chapter~4]{CampbellMeyer1979}. Because the spectrum depends
  analytically, \(P_\theta\) and \(R_\theta=B_\theta^{\#}\) vary
  analytically on \(\theta\). On \((I-P_\theta)\CC^d\), the
  restriction of \(B_\theta\) is invertible and its ordinary
  determinant equals \(\det\nolimits'(B_\theta)=C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))^{-1}\).
  Jacobi's formula on this complementary spectral subspace gives
  \[
    \partial_\theta\log\det\nolimits'(B_\theta)
    =\Tr(R_\theta\,\partial_\theta B_\theta).
  \]
  Since \(C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))=\det\nolimits'(B_\theta)^{-1}\),
  \[
    \partial_\theta\log C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
    =-\Tr(R_\theta\,\partial_\theta B_\theta),
  \]
  while
  \[
    \partial_\theta B_\theta
    =-\frac{\partial_\theta A_\theta}{\lambda(\theta)}
      +\frac{\partial_\theta\lambda(\theta)}{\lambda(\theta)^2}
       A_\theta.
  \]
  Substituting yields the stated formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Normal convergence under secondary admissibility]
  \label{prop:secondary-fredholm-normal-convergence}
  Let \(U\) be a simply connected neighbourhood with compact closure in the
  holomorphy domain of \(A_\theta\). Assume explicitly that
  \((A_\theta,\lambda(\theta),P_\theta)_{\theta\in U}\) is
  secondary-admissible on \(U\). Thus, by definition, the complementary
  relative gap holds and the selected branch is uniformly expanding:
  \[
    \lambda_\ast:=\inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)}>1 .
  \]
  The expanded relative gap gives, by
  \Cref{lem:secondary-admissibility-gate}, a number \(\eta_U<1\) such that
  \[
    s_U=\sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)
    \le \eta_U<1 .
  \]
  If only the branch-isolating relative gap is assumed, the conclusions of
  this proposition need not hold; the counterexample in
  \Cref{lem:relative-gap-counterexample} already makes \(G_2\) singular.
  Then every matrix
  \(G_k(\theta):=I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\) is invertible on \(U\)
  for every \(k\ge 2\). Define the holomorphic trace-log branch
  \[
    \Phi_k(\theta)
    :=\sum_{m\ge1}
      \frac{\Tr\!\left((\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)^m\right)}{m},
  \]
  equivalently
  \[
    \Phi_k(\theta)=\log\det G_k(\theta)^{-1}
  \]
  with the scalar logarithms from
  \Cref{def:algebraic-finite-part}. Then
  \[
    \partial_\theta\Phi_k(\theta)
    =-\Tr\!\bigl(G_k(\theta)^{-1}\partial_\theta G_k(\theta)\bigr).
  \]
  There exist constants \(C_U>0\) and \(\tau_U\in(0,1)\) such that
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\abs{\Phi_k(\theta)}
    +\sup_{\theta\in U}\abs{\partial_\theta\Phi_k(\theta)}
    \le C_U k \tau_U^k
    \qquad (k\ge 2).
  \]
  Consequently
  \[
    \sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}\Phi_k(\theta)
  \]
  converges normally on \(U\), and termwise differentiation is
  justified there. In particular,
  \[
    F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
    =\log C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
      +\sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}\Phi_k(\theta)
  \]
  on \(U\), with a normally convergent correction series whose first
  \(\theta\)-derivative is obtained termwise.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  By secondary admissibility, choose
  \(q\in(0,1)\) and \(\lambda_\ast>1\) such that
  \[
    \rho\!\left(A_\theta\bigm|_{(I-P_\theta)\CC^d}\right)
    \le q\abs{\lambda(\theta)}
    \qquad(\theta\in U),
  \]
  \[
    \inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)}\ge \lambda_\ast>1 .
  \]
  By \Cref{lem:secondary-admissibility-gate}, after enlarging
  \(\lambda_\ast^{-1}\) slightly if necessary, there is \(\eta_U<1\) such
  that
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)\le\eta_U<1 .
  \]
  Since
  \(\overline U\) is compact in the holomorphy domain, the following
  suprema are finite:
  \[
    M_0:=\sup_{\theta\in U}\norm{A_\theta}<\infty,
  \]
  and
  \[
    M_1:=\sup_{\theta\in U}
    \bigl(\norm{\partial_\theta A_\theta}
      +\abs{\partial_\theta\lambda(\theta)}\bigr)<\infty.
  \]

  The nonresonance input is the subunit secondary radius obtained from the
  expanded relative branch by \Cref{lem:secondary-nonresonance}. It gives
  \[
    1\notin\spec(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta),
  \]
  so \(G_k(\theta)\) is invertible on \(U\) for every \(k\ge 2\). This is
  a consequence of secondary admissibility, including absolute expansion;
  it is not inferred from the relative gap alone.
  Apply \Cref{lem:uniform-secondary-power-bounds} to the compact family
  \(B_k(\theta)=\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\). For every
  \(\tau\in(\eta_U,1)\), the traces of all powers are uniformly dominated
  by \(C\tau^m\). Hence the series defining \(\Phi_k\) converges normally
  in \(\theta\), uniformly in \(k\ge2\), and it equals
  \[
    -\sum_{\nu\in\spec(A_\theta)}
      \log(1-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}\nu),
  \]
  with algebraic multiplicities and the principal scalar logarithm. Thus
  \(\Phi_k\) is a single-valued holomorphic branch of
  \(\log\det G_k^{-1}\).

  Choose \(\tau_U\in(\lambda_\ast^{-1},1)\). For \(k\) large enough that
  \(\sup_{\theta\in U}\norm{I-G_k(\theta)}\le 1/2\), the Taylor series
  \(\Phi_k(\theta)=\sum_{m\ge 1}\Tr((\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)^m)/m\)
  converges absolutely and \(\sup_{\theta\in U}\norm{G_k(\theta)^{-1}}\le2\),
  giving
  \[
    \abs{\Phi_k(\theta)}
    \le d\sum_{m\ge 1}\frac{(M_0/\lambda_\ast^{k})^m}{m}
    \le C\,\tau_U^k.
  \]
  Jacobi's formula yields
  \[
    \partial_\theta\Phi_k(\theta)
    =-\Tr\!\bigl(G_k(\theta)^{-1}\partial_\theta G_k(\theta)\bigr),
  \]
  with
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\norm{\partial_\theta G_k(\theta)}
    \le M_1/\lambda_\ast^k+kM_0M_1/\lambda_\ast^{k+1}
    \le C'\,k\,\tau_U^k.
  \]
  Combining gives
  \(\abs{\partial_\theta\Phi_k(\theta)}\le C''k\tau_U^k\).
  For the finitely many smaller \(k\), the eigenvalue bound
  \(\rho(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)\le\eta_U\) keeps
  \(\det G_k(\theta)\) bounded away from zero, while the matrix entries
  are bounded on \(\overline U\). Thus \(\Phi_k\) and
  \(\partial_\theta\Phi_k\) are bounded for each such \(k\). Absorbing
  these finitely many values into the constant proves the stated bound.
  Normal convergence and termwise differentiation of
  \(\sum_{k\ge 2}\mu(k)\Phi_k/k\) follow from
  \(\sum_{k\ge 2}\tau_U^k<\infty\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Holomorphic variation of the Abel finite part]
  \label{thm:logM-holomorphic-variation}
  \leanverified{paper_fredholm_witt_logM_holomorphic_variation}
  Let \(U\) be a simply connected neighbourhood with compact closure in the
  holomorphy domain of \(A_\theta\). Assume that the Riesz branch
  \((A_\theta,\lambda(\theta),P_\theta)_{\theta\in U}\) is
  secondary-admissible on \(U\). In particular, this means a uniform
  complementary relative gap and uniform expansion of the selected branch:
  \[
    \inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)}>1 .
  \]
  Under the relative gap, \Cref{prop:secondary-nonresonance} shows that this
  is equivalent to the subunit secondary-radius bound
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)<1.
  \]
  This bound is not a consequence of the branch-isolating relative gap
  alone. If expansion, equivalently the displayed secondary-radius bound,
  fails, the statement is not asserted. Then
  \(F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))\) is holomorphic as a function of
  \(\theta\in U\), and
  \[
    \frac{\dd}{\dd\theta}F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
    =\Tr\!\left(
      R_\theta\left(
        \frac{\partial_\theta A_\theta}{\lambda(\theta)}
        -\frac{\partial_\theta\lambda(\theta)}{\lambda(\theta)^2}
         A_\theta\right)\right)
    +\sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}
      \partial_\theta\Phi_k(\theta).
  \]
  When \(A_\theta\) is a primitive matrix with Perron root
  \(\lambda(\theta)=\rho(A_\theta)>1\), this gives
  \[
    \begin{aligned}
      \frac{\dd}{\dd\theta}\log\MM(A_\theta)
      &=
      \Tr\!\left(
        R_\theta\left(
          \frac{\partial_\theta A_\theta}{\lambda(\theta)}
          -\frac{\partial_\theta\lambda(\theta)}{\lambda(\theta)^2}
           A_\theta\right)\right) \\
      &\quad
      +\sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}
        \frac{\dd}{\dd\theta}
        \log\det(I-\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)^{-1}.
    \end{aligned}
  \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  For each \(\theta\in U\),
  \Cref{def:algebraic-finite-part} gives
  \[
    F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
    =\log C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
      +\sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}\Phi_k(\theta).
  \]
  By \Cref{prop:secondary-fredholm-normal-convergence}, the correction
  series converges normally and may be differentiated termwise. The
  derivative of \(\log C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))\) is given by
  \Cref{thm:finite-part-reduced-determinant-group-inverse-gradient}.
  The specialization to primitive matrices uses
  \(F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))=\log\MM(A_\theta)\) from
  \Cref{thm:finite-part-primitive-closed-form}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Determinant truncation for the Abel finite part]
  \label{prop:logM-truncation}
  Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:logM-holomorphic-variation}, including
  the uniform complementary relative gap, the explicit uniform expansion
  \[
    \inf_{\theta\in U}\abs{\lambda(\theta)}>1,
  \]
  equivalently the secondary-radius bound
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}\sup_{k\ge2}
    \rho\!\left(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta\right)<1,
  \]
  but not under the branch-isolating relative gap alone,
  there exist a constant \(C_U>0\) and a number \(\tau_U\in(0,1)\) such
  that for
  \[
    F_N(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
    :=\log C(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))
      +\sum_{2\le k\le N}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}\Phi_k(\theta),
  \]
  one has
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}
    \abs{F(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))-F_N(A_\theta,\lambda(\theta))}
    \le C_U \tau_U^{N+1}.
  \]
  The same estimate holds after one \(\theta\)-derivative. When
  \(A_\theta\) is primitive and real, \(F\) and \(F_N\) specialize to
  \(\log\MM(A_\theta)\) and \(\log\MM_N(A_\theta)\) respectively, and
  the truncation estimate reads
  \[
    \sup_{\theta\in U}
    \abs{\log\MM(A_\theta)-\log\MM_N(A_\theta)}
    \le C_U \tau_U^{N+1}.
  \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Let \(C_0\) and \(\tau_U\) be given by
  \Cref{prop:secondary-fredholm-normal-convergence}. Then
  \[
    \sum_{k>N}\left|\frac{\mu(k)}{k}\Phi_k(\theta)\right|
    \le C_0\sum_{k>N}\tau_U^k,
  \]
  and the same estimate holds with \(\Phi_k\) replaced by
  \(\partial_\theta\Phi_k\). Summing the geometric tail gives the
  result.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Twisted determinants, periodic moments, and cumulants}

Let \((X_A^+,\sigma_A)\) be the mixing subshift determined by a primitive
matrix \(A\), let \(\mu_A\) be its Parry measure, and let
\(f:X_A^+\to\RR\) be locally constant. If \(f\) depends on the first
\(m\) coordinates, pass to the \((m-1)\)-block presentation. Then the
recoded system is again a mixing finite-state Markov shift with
primitive adjacency matrix \(B\), and there is a real-valued edge
function \(g\) on the allowed transitions of \(B\) such that
\[
  S_nf(x)=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}g(x_k,x_{k+1})
\]
on the recoded shift. For \(t\in\CC\), define the weighted matrix
\[
  M_t(i,j):=B_{ij}e^{t g(i,j)}.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Twisted determinants encode periodic-orbit moments]
  \label{prop:twisted-determinant-orbit-moments}
  For every \(n\ge 1\),
  \[
    \Tr(M_t^n)=\sum_{x\in\Fix(\sigma_A^n)}e^{tS_nf(x)}.
  \]
  Hence, for \((z,t)\) near \((0,0)\),
  \[
    \det(I-zM_t)^{-1}
    =\exp\!\left(
      \sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{z^n}{n}
      \sum_{x\in\Fix(\sigma_A^n)}e^{tS_nf(x)}\right)
    =\prod_{\mathcal O}
      \bigl(1-e^{tS_{\mathcal O}f}z^{|\mathcal O|}\bigr)^{-1},
  \]
  where \(\mathcal O\) runs over primitive periodic orbits of the
  recoded shift. For every integer \(r\ge 0\),
  \[
    \left.\partial_t^r\Tr(M_t^n)\right|_{t=0}
    =\sum_{x\in\Fix(\sigma_A^n)}(S_nf(x))^r,
  \]
  and consequently
  \[
    \left.\partial_t^r
      \log\det(I-zM_t)^{-1}\right|_{t=0}
    =\sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{z^n}{n}
      \sum_{x\in\Fix(\sigma_A^n)}(S_nf(x))^r.
  \]
  In particular, the twisted determinant determines every periodic-orbit
  moment sum.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  For each \(n\ge 1\),
  \[
    \Tr(M_t^n)=\sum_{x\in\Fix(\sigma_A^n)}e^{tS_nf(x)}.
  \]
  Applying \Cref{thm:fredholm-witt-bridge} to the finite matrix \(M_t\)
  gives the first identity. The primitive-orbit product is the
  corresponding Euler-product expansion by M\"obius inversion, exactly as
  in \Cref{prop:prelim-trace-primitive}. Differentiating the logarithm
  with respect to \(t\) at \(t=0\) yields the moment identities.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Twisted determinant rigidity for locally constant observables]
  \label{thm:twisted-determinant-rigidity}
  \leanverified{paper_fredholm_witt_twisted_determinant_rigidity}
  Let \(f\) and \(g\) be locally constant observables on mixing
  subshifts of finite type \((X_A^+,\sigma_A)\) and \((X_B^+,\sigma_B)\),
  and let \(M_t^{(f)}\) and \(M_t^{(g)}\) be one-step weighted matrix
  models as above. Assume that
  \[
    \det(I-zM_t^{(f)})=\det(I-zM_t^{(g)})
  \]
  for \((z,t)\) in a neighbourhood of \((0,0)\). Then:
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
    \item For every \(n\ge 1\) and every \(r\ge 0\),
      \[
        \sum_{x\in\Fix(\sigma_A^n)}(S_nf(x))^r
        =
        \sum_{y\in\Fix(\sigma_B^n)}(S_ng(y))^r.
      \]
    \item The dominant eigenvalue branches \(\lambda_f(t)\) and
      \(\lambda_g(t)\) agree near \(t=0\).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  Equality of determinants near \((0,0)\) implies equality of the
  reciprocal determinants and therefore, by
  \Cref{prop:twisted-determinant-orbit-moments}, equality of the trace
  functions
  \[
    \Tr\!\bigl((M_t^{(f)})^n\bigr)
    =\Tr\!\bigl((M_t^{(g)})^n\bigr)
    \qquad (n\ge 1)
  \]
  for \(t\) near \(0\). Differentiating \(r\) times at \(t=0\) proves
  \textup{(i)}.

  For part \textup{(ii)}, let \(\lambda_0=\lambda_f(0)=\rho(M_0^{(f)})
  =\rho(M_0^{(g)})=\lambda_g(0)\) be the common simple Perron
  eigenvalue at \(t=0\). Choose \(\varepsilon>0\) so that the disc
  \(\abs{z-\lambda_0}<\varepsilon\) contains no other eigenvalue of
  \(M_0^{(f)}\) or \(M_0^{(g)}\). Define Riesz projectors
  \begin{gather*}
    P_f(t)
    :=\frac{1}{2\pi i}
      \int_{\abs{z-\lambda_0}=\varepsilon}
      \bigl(zI-M_t^{(f)}\bigr)^{-1}\,\dd z,\\
    P_g(t)
    :=\frac{1}{2\pi i}
      \int_{\abs{z-\lambda_0}=\varepsilon}
      \bigl(zI-M_t^{(g)}\bigr)^{-1}\,\dd z.
  \end{gather*}
  By continuity of eigenvalues, after shrinking to \(\abs{t}<\delta\)
  each matrix has exactly one eigenvalue inside
  \(\abs{z-\lambda_0}<\varepsilon\), so
  \(\mathrm{rank}\,P_f(t)=\mathrm{rank}\,P_g(t)=1\). The corresponding
  eigenvalues are the unique analytic branches \(\lambda_f(t)\) and
  \(\lambda_g(t)\) near \(\lambda_0\). For each fixed \(t\) with
  \(\abs{t}<\delta\), the spectral-rigidity statement
  \Cref{thm:fredholm-spectral-rigidity} gives equality of the full
  spectral multisets of \(M_t^{(f)}\) and \(M_t^{(g)}\); in
  particular the unique eigenvalue inside \(\abs{z-\lambda_0}<\varepsilon\)
  must agree. Therefore \(\lambda_f(t)=\lambda_g(t)\) for \(\abs{t}<\delta\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Cumulant rigidity]
  \label{cor:twisted-determinant-cumulants}
  \leanverified{paper_fredholm_witt_twisted_determinant_cumulants}
  Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:twisted-determinant-rigidity}, the
  centered Birkhoff sums for \(f\) and \(g\) have the same asymptotic
  cumulants of every order; in particular, their asymptotic variances
  coincide.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
  By \Cref{thm:twisted-determinant-rigidity}\textup{(ii)}, the dominant
  eigenvalue branches agree near \(t=0\). Since the centered cumulant
  generating function is
  \[
    t\longmapsto
    \log\frac{\lambda_f(t)}{\lambda_f(0)}
    -t\,\frac{\lambda_f'(0)}{\lambda_f(0)},
  \]
  the asymptotic cumulants are the same for \(f\) and \(g\).
\end{proof}

\subsection{Spectral-radius gap and limit laws}

Write
\[
  \bar f:=f-\int f\,\dd\mu_A,
  \qquad
  \bar g(i,j):=g(i,j)-\int f\,\dd\mu_A,
\]
\[
  \widetilde M_u(i,j):=B_{ij}e^{iu\bar g(i,j)}
  \qquad (u\in\RR).
\]
Choose positive left and right Perron vectors \(\ell_0,r_0\) for \(B\),
normalized by
\[
  \ell_0^{\mathsf T}B=\lambda\ell_0^{\mathsf T},
  \qquad
  Br_0=\lambda r_0,
  \qquad
  \ell_0^{\mathsf T}r_0=1,
\]
where \(\lambda=\rho(B)\). Then, in the block presentation,
\[
  \phi_n(u):=
  \int e^{iu\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\bar f\circ\sigma_A^k}\,\dd\mu_A
  =\lambda^{-n}\ell_0^{\mathsf T}\widetilde M_u^n r_0.
\]

\begin{definition}[Arithmetic lattice obstruction]
  \label{def:arithmetic-lattice}
  The arithmetic lattice obstruction of \(f\) is
  \begin{multline*}
    \Lambda_f:=
    \bigl\{u\in\RR:\ \exists\,\omega\in\mathbb S^1,\
      \eta:\mathrm{state}(B)\to\mathbb S^1\text{ such that }\\
        e^{iu\bar g(i,j)}=\omega\,\eta(i)\eta(j)^{-1}
        \text{ for every allowed edge }(i,j)
      \bigr\}.
  \end{multline*}
  By the finite-state cocycle criterion of Parry
  \cite[Theorem~1]{Parry1992Cocycle}, this is exactly the set of
  frequencies \(u\) for which \(\rho(\widetilde M_u)=\lambda\). We call
  \(f\) \emph{aperiodic}, or \emph{non-lattice}, when
  \(\Lambda_f=\{0\}\).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Off-origin decay from a spectral-radius gap]
  \label{prop:off-origin-decay}
  Let \(K\Subset\RR\setminus\Lambda_f\) be compact, and set
  \[
    \kappa_K:=\sup_{u\in K}\frac{\rho(\widetilde M_u)}{\lambda}.
  \]
  The map \(u\mapsto\rho(\widetilde M_u)\) is continuous, and since
  \(\rho(\widetilde M_u)=\lambda\) if and only if \(u\in\Lambda_f\)
  (by \Cref{def:arithmetic-lattice} and Parry's criterion), we have
  \(\kappa_K<1\). Setting
  \[
    \eta_K:=\frac{1+\kappa_K}{2}\in(\kappa_K,1),
  \]
  for every \(\vartheta_K\in(\eta_K,1)\) there exists \(C_K>0\) such that
  \[
    \sup_{u\in K}\abs{\phi_n(u)}
    \le C_K\vartheta_K^n
    \qquad (n\ge 1).
  \]
  In particular, if \(f\) is aperiodic then the same estimate holds on
  every compact set \(K\Subset\RR\setminus\{0\}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  The entries of \(\widetilde M_u\) are continuous in \(u\), so the
  spectral radius \(u\mapsto\rho(\widetilde M_u)\) is continuous on
  \(\RR\). By \Cref{def:arithmetic-lattice} and
  \cite[Theorem~1]{Parry1992Cocycle},
  \(\rho(\widetilde M_u)=\lambda\) if and only if \(u\in\Lambda_f\).
  Since \(K\Subset\RR\setminus\Lambda_f\) is compact and
  \(\rho(\widetilde M_u)<\lambda\) for all \(u\in K\), the supremum
  \(\kappa_K=\sup_{u\in K}\rho(\widetilde M_u)/\lambda\) is attained
  and satisfies \(\kappa_K<1\). The explicit choice
  \(\eta_K:=(1+\kappa_K)/2\) lies in \((\kappa_K,1)\).

  Fix \(\vartheta_K\in(\eta_K,1)\) and set \(r_K:=\vartheta_K\lambda\).
  Then \(\sup_{u\in K}\rho(\widetilde M_u)<\eta_K\lambda<r_K<\lambda\).
  The compact set \(K\times\{\abs{z}=r_K\}\) avoids the spectrum of
  \(zI-\widetilde M_u\), so
  \[
    C_K^{(0)}:=
    \sup_{u\in K,\ \abs{z}=r_K}\|(zI-\widetilde M_u)^{-1}\|<\infty.
  \]
  By the holomorphic functional calculus,
  \[
    \widetilde M_u^n
    =\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_{\abs{z}=r_K}
      z^n(zI-\widetilde M_u)^{-1}\,\dd z,
  \]
  so
  \[
    \sup_{u\in K}\|\widetilde M_u^n\|
    \le 2\pi C_K^{(0)}r_K^n.
  \]
  Multiplying by \(\lambda^{-n}\|\ell_0\|\,\|r_0\|\) gives
  \[
    \sup_{u\in K}\abs{\phi_n(u)}
    \le
    2\pi\|\ell_0\|\,\|r_0\|\,C_K^{(0)}
    \left(\frac{r_K}{\lambda}\right)^n,
  \]
  which is the stated estimate with \(C_K:=2\pi\|\ell_0\|\,\|r_0\|\,C_K^{(0)}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Spectral-gap central limit theorem]
  \label{thm:clt-spectral}
  \leanverified{paper_fredholm_witt_clt_spectral}
  Assume \(f\) is not a coboundary modulo constants. Then there exists
  \(\sigma_f^2>0\) such that
  \[
    \frac{\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\bar f\circ\sigma_A^k}{\sqrt n}
    \Longrightarrow \mathcal N(0,\sigma_f^2).
  \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
  The map \(u\mapsto \widetilde M_u\) is entire. Since \(B=\widetilde
  M_0\) is primitive, analytic perturbation theory gives
  \(\delta>0\), an analytic simple eigenvalue \(u\mapsto\lambda_c(u)\),
  an analytic rank-one projector \(u\mapsto P_u\), and constants
  \(C>0\), \(\vartheta\in(0,1)\) such that
  \[
    \widetilde M_u^n=\lambda_c(u)^nP_u+N_u^n,
    \qquad
    \|N_u^n\|\le C(\vartheta\lambda)^n
  \]
  for \(|u|<\delta\) and \(n\ge 1\); see
  \cite[Chapter~II, \S2]{Kato1995Perturbation}. Because \(\bar f\) is
  centered,
  \[
    \lambda_c'(0)=0.
  \]
  The standard finite-state spectral method gives
  \[
    \log\frac{\lambda_c(u)}{\lambda}
    =-\frac{\sigma_f^2}{2}u^2+O(u^3)
    \qquad (u\to 0),
  \]
  where
  \[
    \sigma_f^2
    =-\left.\frac{\dd^2}{\dd u^2}\log\frac{\lambda_c(u)}{\lambda}
      \right|_{u=0}>0
  \]
  whenever \(f\) is not a coboundary modulo constants; see
  \cite[Chapter~5]{Ruelle1978Thermodynamic},
  \cite[Chapter~3]{Baladi2000PositiveTransfer},
  and \cite[Chapter~IV]{HennionHerve2001}. Because
  \(P_0=r_0\ell_0^{\mathsf T}\), one has
  \[
    \ell_0^{\mathsf T}P_{u_0/\sqrt n}r_0=1+O(n^{-1/2}).
  \]
  Hence, for each fixed \(u_0\in\RR\),
  \[
    \phi_n(u_0/\sqrt n)
    =\left(\frac{\lambda_c(u_0/\sqrt n)}{\lambda}\right)^n
      \ell_0^{\mathsf T}P_{u_0/\sqrt n}r_0
      +O(\vartheta^n)
    \longrightarrow e^{-\sigma_f^2u_0^2/2}.
  \]
  L\'evy's continuity theorem yields the central limit theorem.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
  The determinant package clarifies why
  the reduced-determinant formula, the secondary-factor convergence and
  truncation statements, twisted-determinant rigidity, cumulant
  rigidity, off-origin decay, and the spectral CLT belong together. In
  each case one starts from a simple dominant
  eigenvalue branch of a determinant, isolates the reduced resolvent on
  the complementary spectral subspace, and then reads a perturbative
  quantity from that reduced determinant or from the motion of the
  dominant eigenvalue. The difference between finite-part constants,
  orbit moments, and limit laws is therefore a difference of observable,
  not of mechanism.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Explicit computational examples}

\begin{remark}[Computational example: the golden-mean family]
  \label{rem:example-golden-mean}
  Let
  \[
    A_s=
    \begin{pmatrix}
      1+s & 1 \\
      1   & 0
    \end{pmatrix},
    \qquad |s|\ll 1.
  \]
  For real \(s\) near \(0\), this is a primitive matrix. The analytic
  simple eigenvalue branch from \Cref{def:branch-isolating-relative-gap} is
  \[
    \lambda(s)=\frac{1+s+\sqrt{(1+s)^2+4}}{2},
  \]
  and the second eigenvalue is \(\mu_2(s)=-\lambda(s)^{-1}\). Hence,
  by \Cref{def:reduced-determinant,def:algebraic-finite-part},
  \[
    C(A_s,\lambda(s))
    =\left(1+\lambda(s)^{-2}\right)^{-1},
    \qquad
    \partial_s\log C(A_s,\lambda(s))
    =\frac{2\lambda'(s)}{\lambda(s)(\lambda(s)^2+1)}.
  \]
  At \(s=0\), with \(\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2\),
  \[
    C(A_0,\varphi)=\frac{\varphi}{\sqrt5},
    \qquad
    \left.\partial_s\log C(A_s,\lambda(s))\right|_{s=0}
    =\frac{2}{\sqrt5(\varphi^2+1)}
    =0.2472135\ldots.
  \]
  The Abel finite part is
  \[
    \log \MM(A_0)
    =F(A_0,\varphi)
    =\log\frac{\varphi}{\sqrt5}
      +\sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}
        \log\Bigl((1-\varphi^{1-k})(1+\varphi^{-(k+1)})\Bigr)^{-1},
  \]
  and the determinant truncation from \Cref{prop:logM-truncation} is
  numerically rapid: truncation at \(k=14\) already gives
  \[
    \log\MM(A_0)=-0.82923\ldots.
  \]
  This example validates the derivative formula and the exponential
  truncation scheme in a nontrivial holomorphic family, but it does not
  test the twisted non-lattice estimates because no observable is
  involved.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Computational example: the full two-shift]
  \label{rem:example-full-two-shift}
  Let
  \[
    A=
    \begin{pmatrix}
      1 & 1 \\
      1 & 1
    \end{pmatrix}
  \]
  and let \(f(x)=\mathbf 1_{\{x_0=1\}}\). Then
  \[
    M_t=
    \begin{pmatrix}
      1 & e^t \\
      1 & e^t
    \end{pmatrix},
    \qquad
    \det(I-zM_t)=1-(1+e^t)z.
  \]
  The analytic leading eigenvalue branch is \(\lambda(t)=1+e^t\)
  (this is a simple eigenvalue with spectral gap for \(t\) near \(0\)),
  and \(C(M_t,\lambda(t))=1\). After centering by \(1/2\), the
  dominant eigenvalue becomes
  \[
    \lambda_c(t)=e^{-t/2}(1+e^t)=2\cosh(t/2).
  \]
  Therefore, for real \(u\),
  \[
    \int e^{iu\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(f-\frac12)\circ\sigma_A^k}\,\dd\mu_A
    =\left(\frac{\lambda_c(iu)}{2}\right)^n
    =\cos(u/2)^n.
  \]
  Hence
  \[
    \sigma_f^2=\frac14,
  \]
  and on every compact set \(K\Subset\RR\setminus 2\pi\ZZ\),
  \[
    \sup_{u\in K}
    \left|\int e^{iu\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(f-\frac12)\circ\sigma_A^k}\,\dd\mu_A\right|
    \le
    \left(\sup_{u\in K}\abs{\cos(u/2)}\right)^n.
  \]
  This example is arithmetic rather than aperiodic, so the lattice
  obstruction is visible at \(u\in 2\pi\ZZ\). Away from that lattice,
  the determinant package recovers the variance and the exponential
  decay exactly, not merely asymptotically. It therefore shows both what
  the off-origin estimate captures and where it stops: the decay is
  uniform only after excluding the lattice obstruction.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Comparison with classical finite-dimensional perturbation theory}

Classical finite-dimensional perturbation theory starts from a fixed
family of matrices \(A_\theta\) and studies the motion of its isolated
eigenvalues, eigenvectors, and reduced resolvent. That machinery is
already enough to prove
\Cref{thm:finite-part-reduced-determinant-group-inverse-gradient} and
\Cref{thm:clt-spectral}
for a \emph{single} symbolic model. What it does not single out is the
determinant as a common, model-independent object.

The determinant packaging adds exactly three features needed here.
First, \Cref{prop:twisted-determinant-orbit-moments} is period-graded:
one determinant organizes all periodic lengths simultaneously via the
Euler product and M\"obius inversion. Second,
\Cref{thm:twisted-determinant-rigidity} and
\Cref{cor:twisted-determinant-cumulants}
compares different shifts or different observables without putting them
on a common state space; the invariant is the determinant germ, not the
ambient matrix size. Third,
\Cref{prop:secondary-fredholm-normal-convergence} and
\Cref{prop:logM-truncation}
split the Abel finite part into a reduced determinant plus a rapidly
convergent hierarchy of secondary factors. That is the only comparison
with ordinary finite-dimensional perturbation theory needed here.
