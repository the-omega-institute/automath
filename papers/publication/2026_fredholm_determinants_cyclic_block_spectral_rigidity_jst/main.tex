\title[Cyclic-Block Trace-Class Realisations]{Cyclic-block trace-class realisations\\
for prescribed cyclic zero divisors}

\author{Haobo Ma}

\begin{abstract}
Motivated by symbolic dynamical \(\zeta\)-functions, we study which
prescribed cyclic zero divisors admit trace-class normal Fredholm
realisations. For a primitive finite adjacency matrix \(A\), the
classical primitive-orbit product
\[
  \zeta_A(z)=\prod_{n\ge1}(1-z^n)^{-p_n(A)}
\]
is a holomorphic-germ identity at the origin, but the corresponding
cyclic data \((n,1,p_n(A))\) satisfy the trace-class threshold only in
the trivial one-orbit case \(A=[1]\). Against that background, our main
structural result is a cyclic-block realization and classification
theorem for independently prescribed cyclic zero divisors satisfying
\(
  \sum_j m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty
\). The positive-exponent product
\[
  \prod_j (1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{m_j}
\]
is the Fredholm determinant of a trace-class normal cyclic-block direct
sum. This divisor data is not the Fredholm--Witt coordinate sequence of
an arbitrary trace-class operator, since
\[
  p_n(T)=\frac1n\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)\Tr(T^{n/d})
\]
need not be integers or non-negative. Conversely, any trace-class normal
operator with this Fredholm
determinant has non-zero part, up to unitary equivalence and an arbitrary
zero summand, equal to the direct sum of cyclic blocks
\(C(n_j,\alpha_j)\otimes I_{m_j}\) with \(\alpha_j^{n_j}=\beta_j\). In
equivalent terms, the determinant germ forgets the Euler-factor packaging
and remembers exactly the collapsed non-zero spectral multiset. In
addition, among all trace-class operators with the same Fredholm
determinant, the cyclic-block model attains the minimal trace norm
\(
  \sum_j m_jn_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}
\),
while the full determinant fiber is trace-norm unbounded in nonnormal
quasinilpotent directions. In particular, the total non-zero block size
is forced; for a single factor \((1-\beta z^n)^{-m}\), the closed span of
the non-zero spectral subspaces has dimension exactly \(mn\). In infinite
cyclic sums this is a closed-support statement; the algebraic range can be
proper and nonclosed.
\end{abstract}
