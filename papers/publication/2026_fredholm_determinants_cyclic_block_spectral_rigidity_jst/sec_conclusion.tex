\section{Further remarks}\label{sec:conclusion}

The three operator-theoretic statements proved here have different
logical status. The Fredholm--Witt bridge is the connective identity
that places traces, Witt coordinates, and determinants in one common
language. The main structural input is the sharp characterization theorem:
an Euler product admits a trace-class block-cyclic normal realisation if
and only if the trace-class summability threshold
\(
  \sum_j m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty
\)
holds, and in that case the canonical cyclic-block model is already
trace-norm optimal and minimal for the closed span of its non-zero
spectral subspaces. Spectral rigidity then shows that this extra
operator-theoretic freedom is geometric rather than spectral: once the
determinant is fixed, both the non-zero spectrum and the minimal closed
span of the non-zero spectral subspaces are fixed. In the infinite
direct-sum case the algebraic range is a proper dense, hence nonclosed,
subspace; the invariant used here is its closure.

The symbolic-dynamical section is included as an application of this
determinant viewpoint, not as a separate claim of novelty. In finite
dimension, the formulas for \(C(A)\), \(\MM(A)\), cumulants, and
characteristic functions remain consequences of the usual perturbative
and spectral-gap machinery, together with explicit expansion/nonresonance
where secondary factors are present. Whenever secondary determinants
\(I-\lambda^{-k}A\) enter, the proof assumes secondary admissibility:
uniform complementary relative gap together with the absolute expansion
condition \(\inf_U\abs{\lambda}>1\). Under that relative gap, this
expansion condition is equivalent to the exact subunit secondary-radius
condition \(s_U<1\). The relative gap alone supplies only branch isolation
and the complementary spectral-ratio bound. The selected eigenvalue itself
contributes
\(\rho(\lambda(\theta)^{-k}A_\theta)=\abs{\lambda(\theta)}^{1-k}\), which
is why absolute expansion is the missing hypothesis for the secondary
trace-log branches.
What the determinant package adds is a
common organizing object: it records periodic data, reduced
determinants, and twisted moment information in one invariant germ. The
explicit golden-mean and full two-shift computations in
\Cref{rem:example-golden-mean} and \Cref{rem:example-full-two-shift}
show that
these determinant-level formulas are concrete and computable, not only
formal identities: the golden-mean family validates the derivative and
truncation formulas, while the full two-shift isolates the arithmetic
obstruction in the off-origin decay statement. This is the comparison
with ordinary finite-dimensional perturbation theory highlighted in
\Cref{sec:perturbation}: the Fredholm package remembers the invariant
determinant object across different symbolic presentations.

Several natural extensions merit further investigation. First, the
cyclic-block construction itself extends formally to direct sums in
broader settings. Extending the spectral-rigidity statement beyond the
Hilbert-space trace-class case would require a precise determinant
theory and a corresponding canonical zero description, and this lies
beyond the scope of the present paper. Quantitative refinements of the
symbolic applications, such as Berry--Esseen bounds or moderate
deviations, would likewise require additional spectral input not pursued
here.
