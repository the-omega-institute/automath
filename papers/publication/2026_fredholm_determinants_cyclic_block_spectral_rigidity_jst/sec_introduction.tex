\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

For a primitive finite matrix \(A\), the dynamical \(\zeta\)-function
\[
  \zeta_A(z)=\det(I-zA)^{-1}
\]
packages periodic-orbit data, transfer-operator traces, and spectral data
into one holomorphic object. In symbolic dynamics this identity is
classical and can be viewed as the finite-state shadow of the Fredholm
determinant formalism for trace-class operators.

This paper isolates the trace-class normal realization problem behind that
identity. The primitive-orbit Euler product
\[
  \zeta_A(z)=\prod_{n\ge1}(1-z^n)^{-p_n(A)}
\]
is likewise classical as a germ at the origin. However, the trace-class
threshold relevant for the prescribed-cyclic-zero-divisor theorem below
becomes
\[
  \sum_{n\ge1} n\,p_n(A)<\infty,
\]
and \Cref{thm:primitive-symbolic-exclusion} shows that this happens only
for the trivial one-symbol fixed-point shift \(A=[1]\). Thus the
primitive symbolic packaging is the motivating local model, but in
general it does not itself define a summable cyclic zero divisor in the
normal trace-class category.

Against that background, this paper isolates the trace-class normal
realization problem for prescribed cyclic zero divisors. Given countable
Euler data
\[
  \mathcal D=\{(n_j,\beta_j,m_j)\}_{j\in J},
\]
we ask when the product
\[
  \prod_{j\in J}(1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{m_j}
\]
is the Fredholm determinant of a trace-class operator and what geometry is
forced if one restricts to normal realizations. The main point is that in
the normal trace-class category the cyclic divisor of the determinant
already determines the canonical block model, the closed non-zero spectral
support, and the exact trace budget. Beyond the normal category, the same
determinant still carries a sharp variational statement: among all
trace-class realizations with that determinant, the canonical cyclic model
has minimal trace norm, and equality forces a return to that same normal
cyclic model up to a zero summand, even though the full determinant fiber
is trace-norm unbounded once quasinilpotent nonnormal directions are
adjoined.

The Fredholm--Witt bridge and determinant rigidity enter as classical
tools. Within that framework, the main results of \Cref{sec:fredholm}
form a three-step theorem chain: first, a cyclic-block realization and
normal-classification theorem for prescribed cyclic zero divisors at the
sharp trace-class threshold; second, a
determinant-fiber theorem whose lower bound is classical but whose
equality case identifies the entire trace-norm minimizer class; third, a
determinant-classification theorem showing that the determinant germ
forgets the Euler-factor packaging and remembers exactly the collapsed
non-zero spectral multiset.

\subsection{Main results}

The first body result is the symbolic exclusion theorem
\Cref{thm:primitive-symbolic-exclusion}: for a nontrivial primitive
finite-state shift, the classical primitive-orbit data satisfy
\(\sum_n n\,p_n(A)=\infty\). This isolates the precise point at which the
finite-state symbolic Euler product ceases to fit the trace-class
cyclic-block framework and shows that, in this paper, that product is
retained only as a germ-level motivating example.

The remaining three body results concern summable prescribed cyclic zero
divisors. First, \Cref{prop:fredholm-witt-bridge} records the
Fredholm--Witt identity in
the precise form needed later: the passage from traces to Witt
coordinates is an equality of holomorphic germs at \(0\), and the proof
keeps the convergence argument explicit rather than treating the
\(-\sum_m p_m(T)\log(1-z^m)\) step as a purely formal rearrangement.
Next, \Cref{prop:cyclic-witt-compatibility} records the precise overlap
between the two parametrisations used in \Cref{sec:fredholm}: a
prescribed cyclic zero divisor determines the Witt coordinates of its
cyclic model, but in general those coordinates are complex and do not
recover the multiplicities \(m_j\). The two packages coincide only in
the special integral unit-root case
\(\mathcal D=\{(n,1,m_n)\}_{n\ge1}\).

The central structural statements are
\Cref{thm:normal-realizability-threshold,thm:cyclic-fredholm-realisation}.
The first packages the exact threshold: the summability condition
\[
  \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty
\]
holds if and only if there exists a trace-class realization, equivalently
if and only if there exists a normal trace-class realization.
The second gives the explicit canonical model and normal classification at
that threshold. Together they show that the summability condition
\[
  \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty
\]
is exactly the trace-class threshold for realizability of the prescribed
cyclic divisor, and they identify the canonical normal cyclic-block model
\[
  L_{\mathcal D}
  =\bigoplus_{j\in J}\bigl(C(n_j,\alpha_j)\otimes I_{m_j}\bigr),
  \qquad \alpha_j^{n_j}=\beta_j,
\]
starting from the independently prescribed divisor
\[
  D_{\mathcal D}(z)=\prod_{j\in J}(1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{m_j}
\]
with integer multiplicities \(m_j\) and the above summable root budget,
not from an arbitrary Fredholm--Witt coordinate sequence, and any normal
trace-class operator with the same Fredholm
determinant is unitarily equivalent to this model after adjoining an
arbitrary zero summand. The reciprocal Euler product is used only as the
reciprocal of the positive-exponent Fredholm determinant on a connected
zero-free neighbourhood of the origin.

The determinant-fiber theorem
\Cref{thm:determinant-fiber-trace-minimality} separates into a classical
lower bound and an equality-case classification. The lower bound itself is
the direct combination of determinant rigidity with Weyl/Ky Fan
majorization; see
\cite{Konig1986EigenvalueDistribution,GohbergGoldbergKaashoek1990ClassesI}
for classical eigenvalue/s-number majorization in compact-operator form:
if
\[
  \det(I-zT)=D_{\mathcal D}(z)
\]
for some trace-class operator \(T\), then
\[
  \|T\|_1
  \ge \sum_{\lambda\in\Sigma_{\mathcal D}}
    \abs{\lambda}M_{\mathcal D}(\lambda)
  =\sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j},
\]
and the canonical cyclic model \(L_{\mathcal D}\) attains this infimum.
The added content is the converse equality statement: if equality holds,
then \(T\) is already normal and is unitarily equivalent to
\(0_{H_0}\oplus L_{\mathcal D}\) for some zero summand \(H_0\). Thus the
cyclic realization is not merely a convenient normal model: it is the
unique trace-norm minimizer class in the entire determinant fiber.
\Cref{cor:nonnormal-fiber-unbounded} then shows that this lower bound is
sharp in a genuinely asymmetric sense, since the same determinant fiber is
trace-norm unbounded as soon as one allows nonnormal quasinilpotent
extensions.

The final classification step is completed in
\Cref{thm:cyclic-data-classification}: the determinant germ forgets the
particular Euler-factor packaging and remembers exactly the collapsed
non-zero spectral multiset. More explicitly, if
\[
  M_{\mathcal D}(\lambda)
  :=\sum_{\{j:\lambda^{n_j}=\beta_j\}} m_j
  \qquad (\lambda\neq 0),
\]
then the determinant classifies the normal model exactly through the
function \(M_{\mathcal D}\): two summable Euler data sets define the same
determinant if and only if these multiplicities agree for every non-zero
\(\lambda\), equivalently if and only if their cyclic-block models are
unitarily equivalent on the non-zero spectral part. The exact range
formula and the resulting support and trace-budget invariants are
isolated in \Cref{cor:block-cyclic-rigidity} and
\Cref{prop:sharp-trace-budget-support-obstruction}, while the classical
input turning determinant equality into spectral data is the cited lemma
\Cref{lem:fredholm-spectral-rigidity}.

\subsection{Scope}

The argument is organized around the following five ingredients.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
  \item The finite-state symbolic product is the motivating germ-level
  example, and \Cref{thm:primitive-symbolic-exclusion} identifies the
  obstruction that keeps its primitive-orbit packaging outside the
  summable trace-class regime except for \(A=[1]\).
  \item The Fredholm--Witt identity is connective background: it moves
  between traces, Witt coordinates, and determinant germs for
  trace-class operators.
  \item The cyclic-block theorem is the central structural result. It
  is preceded by \Cref{thm:normal-realizability-threshold}, which
  packages the exact trace-class threshold as a single `iff' statement,
  and then gives the explicit normal model and normal classification for
  prescribed cyclic zero divisors. This is not a
  converse parametrization of arbitrary Witt coordinates.
  \item The global trace-norm theorem identifies the cyclic model as the
  variational minimum throughout the full trace-class determinant fiber,
  and its equality case shows that every minimizer already lies in the
  normal cyclic-model class, while the nonnormal-fiber corollary shows
  that this fiber is nonetheless unbounded in trace norm.
  \item The determinant-classification theorem identifies the precise
  invariant seen by the determinant germ, namely the collapsed non-zero
  spectral multiset, while the spectral-rigidity lemma is only the
  classical passage from determinant equality to that multiset.
\end{enumerate}
These ingredients are exactly what is needed for the realization and
variational and rigidity results proved here.

\subsection{Previous work}

The dynamical \(\zeta\)-function viewpoint goes back at least to
Artin--Mazur \cite{ArtinMazur1965}. In the symbolic finite-state setting,
the rationality of these \(\zeta\)-functions goes back to
Bowen--Lanford and Manning
\cite{BowenLanford1970Zeta,Manning1971Axiom}; see also
\cite{Ruelle1976ZetaExpanding,Ruelle1978Thermodynamic,ParryPollicott1990Zeta,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}.
The necklace and Witt-vector algebra behind the M\"obius inversion formulas
used here is classical; see Metropolis--Rota \cite{MetropolisRota1983}.
The theory of Fredholm determinants for trace-class operators, and more
generally for operators in trace ideals, originates with Grothendieck
\cite{Grothendieck1956}, Simon's earlier account of infinite determinants
\cite{Simon1977InfiniteDeterminants}, the continuity-based treatment of
Gohberg--Goldberg--Krupnik
\cite{GohbergGoldbergKrupnik1996TracesDeterminants}, and the monograph of
Simon \cite{Simon2005TraceIdeals}. The product representation and zero-set
description for trace-class Fredholm determinants that underlie the
spectral-rigidity discussion are standard in
\cite{Simon1977InfiniteDeterminants,Simon2005TraceIdeals,GohbergGoldbergKrupnik1996TracesDeterminants}.
The eigenvalue summability, trace formula, and eigenvalue/s-number
comparison underlying the global trace-norm theorem go back to Lidskii
\cite{Lidskii1959}; see also
\cite{Konig1986EigenvalueDistribution,GohbergGoldbergKaashoek1990ClassesI}
and \cite[Chapter~1]{Simon2005TraceIdeals}. The
transfer-operator literature studies dynamical determinants whose zeros
and poles encode spectra of transfer operators on Banach or anisotropic
spaces. In the Axiom~A setting, Pollicott
\cite{Pollicott1986Meromorphic} and then Ruelle
\cite{Ruelle1990FredholmExtension} developed Fredholm determinant
formalisms for transfer operators, while Keller
\cite{Keller1989Fredholm} and Baladi--Keller
\cite{BaladiKeller1990ZetaTransfer} treated piecewise invertible and
piecewise monotone interval maps, Rugh
\cite{Rugh1996GeneralizedFredholm} treated Axiom~A systems, and
Baladi--Tsujii \cite{BaladiTsujii2008DynamicalDeterminants} treated smooth
hyperbolic diffeomorphisms on anisotropic spaces; see also Baladi's
monograph \cite{Baladi2018DynamicalZeta}. Those works begin with a given
dynamical system and a corresponding transfer operator, and then use the
resulting determinant or zeta function to study analytic continuation,
spectral data, orbit-growth asymptotics, or resonance structure. By
contrast, the present paper does not construct a transfer operator from
dynamics and does not pursue meromorphic continuation for a prescribed
system. Instead, it fixes an abstract cyclic Euler product germ and asks
which trace-class operators can realize it, which normal model is forced
by that determinant, and how the entire determinant fiber behaves under
trace-norm minimization, minimizer classification, and
spectral-rigidity constraints.

\subsection{Organisation}

\Cref{sec:preliminaries} recalls the finite-state \(\zeta\)-function,
proves the primitive-orbit identities used later as background, and
shows that nontrivial primitive symbolic Euler data fall outside the
trace-class summability threshold.
\Cref{sec:fredholm} contains the Fredholm--Witt bridge, the cyclic-block
realization and normal classification theorem, the global trace-norm
minimum and minimizer-classification theorem and the nonnormal-fiber
corollary, the
collapsed-multiset determinant classification theorem, and the sharp
support and trace-budget consequences. \Cref{sec:conclusion} summarizes
the resulting realization, variational, and rigidity picture.
