\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

For a primitive finite matrix \(A\), the dynamical \(\zeta\)-function
\[
  \zeta_A(z)=\det(I-zA)^{-1}
\]
packages periodic-orbit data, transfer-operator traces, and spectral data
into one holomorphic object. In symbolic dynamics this identity is
classical and can be viewed as the finite-state shadow of the Fredholm
determinant formalism for trace-class operators.

This paper isolates the trace-class normal realization problem behind that
identity. Given countable Euler data
\[
  \mathcal D=\{(n_j,\beta_j,m_j)\}_{j\in J},
\]
we ask when the product
\[
  \prod_{j\in J}(1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{m_j}
\]
is the Fredholm determinant of a trace-class operator and what geometry is
forced if one restricts to normal realizations. The main point is that in
the normal trace-class category the cyclic divisor of the determinant
already determines the canonical block model, the closed non-zero spectral
support, and the exact trace budget.

The claim of novelty is deliberately narrow. The Fredholm--Witt bridge and
determinant rigidity are classical connective tools. The new structural
input is the cyclic-block theorem proved in \Cref{sec:fredholm}. Further
determinant-side perturbative developments are intentionally left to
separate work: they require additional hypotheses and are not used in the
proof of the cyclic-block theorem.

\subsection{Main results}

We begin by recording the classical bridge in the form used throughout
the paper.
\begin{proposition}[Connecting Fredholm--Witt identity]
  \label{prop:intro-fredholm-witt}
  Let \(T\in \sone(H)\) be a trace-class operator on a complex Hilbert
  space. Define
  \[
    a_n(T):=\Tr(T^n),
    \qquad
    p_n(T):=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)\,a_{n/d}(T)
    \qquad (n\ge 1).
  \]
  Then for every \(\abs{z}<\min(1,\norm{T}^{-1})\),
  \[
    \det(I-zT)^{-1}
    =\exp\!\left(\sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{a_n(T)}{n}z^n\right)
    =\prod_{n\ge 1}(1-z^n)^{-p_n(T)}.
  \]
\end{proposition}

The main structural theorem records the realization statement with
necessity and closed-support optimality in the normal trace-class
category. Its determinant convention is part of the statement: finite
truncations give positive-exponent determinants, and reciprocal
negative-exponent products are used only after a zero-free domain has
been fixed.
\begin{theorem}[Positive-exponent cyclic-block determinant and closed-support optimality]
  \label{thm:intro-cyclic-characterization}
  \leanverified{paper_fredholm_witt_intro_cyclic_characterization}
  Let \(\{(n_j,\beta_j,m_j)\}_{j\in J}\) be a countable family with
  \(n_j,m_j\in\NN\), \(\beta_j\in\CC\setminus\{0\}\), and
  \[
    \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}<\infty.
  \]
  Then the positive-exponent product
  \[
    \prod_{j\in J}(1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{m_j}
  \]
  is the Fredholm determinant of the cyclic-block trace-class normal
  operator
  \[
    \bigoplus_{j\in J}\bigl(C(n_j,\alpha_j)\otimes I_{m_j}\bigr),
    \qquad \alpha_j^{n_j}=\beta_j.
  \]
  Conversely, if a trace-class normal operator has this Fredholm
  determinant, then its non-zero spectral support is unitarily equivalent
  to the non-zero part of this cyclic-block direct sum; equivalently, after
  adjoining an arbitrary zero summand it is unitarily equivalent to the
  displayed model. This is a closed-support classification, not an
  assertion that the algebraic range is the whole support. Here, in the
  infinite case, the negative-exponent product
  \[
    \prod_{j\in J}(1-\beta_j z^{n_j})^{-m_j}
  \]
  means the reciprocal of the locally uniform positive-exponent Fredholm
  determinant on a zero-free neighbourhood of the origin; it is not the
  Fredholm determinant computed on finite truncations. Equivalently,
  realising the reciprocal Euler product is always shorthand for
  realising this positive-exponent Fredholm determinant and then taking
  its reciprocal only where it is non-zero.
  Consequently every trace-class normal realization has closed span of
  non-zero spectral subspaces of dimension
  \(
    \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j
  \)
  and trace norm
  \(
    \sum_{j\in J}m_j n_j\abs{\beta_j}^{1/n_j}
  \).
  In particular, for a single factor \((1-\beta z^n)^{-m}\), the closed
  span of the non-zero spectral subspaces has dimension exactly \(mn\).
  In infinite direct sums this is a statement about the closed span, not
  about equality of the algebraic range with that span; the algebraic
  range may be a proper dense nonclosed subspace.
  These counts are attached to the chosen Euler data
  \(\mathcal D=\{(n_j,\beta_j,m_j)\}\). More generally, if two choices
  of Euler data produce the same Fredholm determinant, then
  \Cref{prop:intro-spectral-rigidity} identifies the same collapsed
  non-zero spectral multiset, so their associated normal cyclic-block
  models agree up to unitary equivalence on the non-zero spectral part.
  The source theorem chain makes this sharp through
  \Cref{prop:sharp-trace-budget-support-obstruction}: the same cyclic
  zero divisor already forces this trace norm and this closed support for
  every normal trace-class realization.
\end{theorem}

Once the determinant is fixed, the non-zero spectrum is fixed as well.
The next statement is classical but important because it is the mechanism
that turns determinant identities into rigidity.
\begin{proposition}[Spectral rigidity]
  \label{prop:intro-spectral-rigidity}
  Let \(T_1,T_2\in \sone\). If
  \[
    \det(I-zT_1)=\det(I-zT_2)
  \]
  in a neighbourhood of \(z=0\), then \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) have the same
  non-zero eigenvalues with algebraic multiplicity.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Scope}

The logical scope of the paper is as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
  \item The Fredholm--Witt identity is connective background: it moves
  between traces, Witt coordinates, and determinant germs for
  trace-class operators.
  \item The cyclic-block theorem is the central structural result. It
  gives existence at the exact trace-class threshold and a converse
  classification in the normal category.
  \item The spectral-rigidity proposition is the converse tool that turns
  equality of determinant germs into spectral information.
\end{enumerate}
Further determinant-side perturbative applications fall outside this
paper's theorem chain and are deferred to separate work.

\subsection{Previous work}

The dynamical \(\zeta\)-function viewpoint goes back at least to
Artin--Mazur \cite{ArtinMazur1965}. In the symbolic finite-state setting,
the rationality of these \(\zeta\)-functions goes back to
Bowen--Lanford and Manning
\cite{BowenLanford1970Zeta,Manning1971Axiom}; see also
\cite{Ruelle1976ZetaExpanding,Ruelle1978Thermodynamic,ParryPollicott1990Zeta,LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998}.
The necklace and Witt-vector algebra behind the M\"obius inversion formulas
used here is classical; see Metropolis--Rota \cite{MetropolisRota1983}.
The theory of Fredholm determinants for trace-class operators, and more
generally for operators in trace ideals, originates with Grothendieck
\cite{Grothendieck1956} and is developed systematically in Simon
\cite{Simon2005TraceIdeals}. The product representation for trace-class
Fredholm determinants that underlies the spectral rigidity discussion is
\cite[Theorem~3.7]{Simon2005TraceIdeals}. The operator-theoretic approach
to dynamical \(\zeta\)-functions via Fredholm determinants of transfer
operators was developed by Pollicott \cite{Pollicott1986Meromorphic} in
the Axiom~A setting, while Keller \cite{Keller1989Fredholm} treated
Fredholm theory of dynamical determinants in the trace-class sense for
piecewise invertible systems.
