\section{Abstract calibrated toy-model quotient, horizon, and fixed-radius corollaries}\label{sec:inverse}

This appendix uses the two response loci selected in
\Cref{thm:response-locus-main,thm:shells-general-main}. The default
physical comparison remains \Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}; the quotient,
near-horizon, and fixed-radius statements collected here are additional
abstract calibrated toy-model corollaries obtained only after extra
calibration and ratio-data hypotheses are imposed. Appendix
Table~\ref{tab:hypothesis-ledger} summarizes that standing input. The boxed
quotient convention below fixes the notation once for the whole appendix
section, and the standing convention paragraph below centralizes the
sampled-counter closure and the fixed response-side scope. Whenever the
relevant range and regularity conditions are also verified, these same two
loci become the corresponding shell pair.
Throughout this appendix section the response loci use the same fixed switching
convention as Section~\ref{sec:kms-shells}, so the switching dependence enters
only through $\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2=\lambda_c^2\|\chi\|_2^2$.

\paragraph{Dependency map for this appendix section.}
The appendix section is meant to be read from left to right in the following sense.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item \Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main} is the default physical statement: it
compares two points on one fixed-response locus pair and keeps the
spectral-weight transport factor explicit.
\item \Cref{thm:ratio-main,prop:spectral-stability-main} add homogeneous or
slow-variation hypotheses and therefore become algebraic corollaries of that
transport law.
\item \Cref{lem:exact-collar-ratio-main,thm:horizon-main,cor:asymptotic-horizon-main}
add horizon and small-locus assumptions on top of the homogeneous or
asymptotically homogeneous pair statements; they do not construct a new
nonconstant-lapse response model.
\item \Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main,thm:two-mode-main}
then add a fixed-radius ratio map together with an explicitly external
ratio-data asymptotic law. The single conditional proposition keeps the exact
fixed-setting shell constraint, the local inverse/sensitivity branch, and the
covariance transport for nearby estimated ratio data in one place, rather than
letting them read as separate consequences of the click record.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Standing convention for this appendix section.}
Unless a later statement explicitly adds more structure, every result below
keeps the same sampled interval instrument, the same calibration closure
$\Gamma=R_\Omega(x)$, the same switching convention, and the same
gap-regular stationary response input already fixed in
\Cref{sec:kms-shells}; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:hypothesis-ledger} is the
central scope map for those shared hypotheses. The result statements in this
section therefore restate only the extra assumptions that genuinely change the
conclusion: homogeneous or slowly varying spectral weight, small-locus or
horizon scaling, or externally supplied fixed-radius ratio-data asymptotics.
The logical order remains the boxed Model A assumption from
\Cref{ass:udw-rate-calibration-main}: the sampled counter is fixed first, and
the closure $\Gamma=R_\Omega(x)$ is invoked here only as that already-stated
calibration step.

\paragraph{Comparator boundary for the inverse corollaries.}
The quotient, near-horizon, and fixed-radius statements proved below are
conditional algebra inside one fixed calibrated response model. They are not
new intrinsic local-temperature observables in the sense of
Mistry--Pinzul \cite{MistryPinzul2024DiamondTemp}, and they are not a general
causal-measurement framework in the sense of Oeckl
\cite{Oeckl2025CausalMeasurement}. The transport and homogeneous quotient
results compare two preselected response loci for one common detector core; the
near-horizon result is the exact collar ratio, plus an additional small-gap
linearization, for that same preselected response-locus pair; and the fixed-radius
results are local inverse/sensitivity and admissibility statements for ratio
data supplied externally at one fixed radius record.

\subsection{Default transport and its abstract calibrated specializations}

The response-locus-pair ratio used below is a quotient of the two Planck
denominators, not a quotient of the two target rates. For radii
$r_\varphi$ and $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ selected by the two response-locus
equations define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:denominator-convention-main}
D_\varphi=e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi)}-1,\qquad
D_{\mathrm{mem}}=e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1,\qquad
Q_{\varphi/\mathrm{mem}}=\frac{D_\varphi}{D_{\mathrm{mem}}}.
\end{equation}
The exact-clock condition contains the sampling factor $\Delta\tau$, whereas
the memory-transition condition is already a rate condition. Keeping these two
source equations in denominator form is what fixes the quotient below.

The two members of the pair are obtained from the same field-facing detector
core: the gap, coupling scale, detector profile, and field state are fixed,
while the classical counter mode is changed from ideal one-step blanking to
the sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset mode. The physically
default comparison keeps the two response-locus points distinct inside one fixed
calibrated response profile and therefore retains the spectral-weight transport
factor. Only after that fixed-response identity has been stated do we impose
the extra homogeneous specialization
$\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ and pass to the later abstract
quotient, near-horizon, and fixed-radius corollaries.

\begin{corollary}[Default transport identity between two points on one fixed-response locus pair]\label{cor:spectral-transport-main}
\ModelAClause the two record-selected loci obey the
following default transport identity.
Work under the standing convention for this section. Let
$x_\varphi$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$ be points on the ideal-benchmark and
memory-transition loci for one common field-facing detector core with the same
gap, coupling scale, detector profile, and field state, but with the two
counter modes specified above. Thus
\[
\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)\Delta\tau}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_\varphi)}-1}
=\log\varphi,
\qquad
\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-transport-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
{\log\varphi}\,
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_{\varphi}}(\Omega)}
     {\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)} .
\end{equation}
Thus every departure from the homogeneous denominator-ratio law is exactly the
displayed spectral-weight transport factor. If the lapse model is
independently known, the same identity can be read as a measurement of that
spectral-weight ratio.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Set
\[
D_\varphi(x_\varphi)=e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_\varphi)}-1,\qquad
D_{\mathrm{mem}}(x_{\mathrm{mem}})
=e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1 .
\]
The two displayed response-locus equations are positive and therefore equivalent to
\[
D_\varphi(x_\varphi)
=\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi},
\qquad
D_{\mathrm{mem}}(x_{\mathrm{mem}})
=
\frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)} .
\]
Taking their quotient
$D_\varphi/D_{\mathrm{mem}}$ cancels the common coupling scale
$\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2$ and leaves exactly \eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main}.
\end{proof}

\Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main} is the physical fixed-response statement for
the section. Everything that follows keeps the same pair of response loci and
either cancels or perturbs its transport factor under added assumptions. The
remaining quotient, near-horizon, and fixed-radius results are therefore
additional corollaries obtained only after imposing homogeneous spectral weight
or its controlled failure on that fixed response profile. Under the homogeneous
input, the shared detector core contributes one common positive factor
\[
A=\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S(\Omega)
\]
to both source response-locus equations.

Under the calibrated homogeneous spectral-weight regime and a common
field-facing detector core, the two source equations in the fixed denominator
notation are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:denominator-shells-main}
\frac{A\Delta\tau}{D_\varphi}=\log\varphi,
\qquad
\frac{A}{D_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\qquad
A=\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S(\Omega)>0 .
\end{equation}
Here the ideal-benchmark member uses ideal one-step blanking, while the
memory-transition member uses the sampled one-recovery-opportunity
boundary-reset mode and the same sampling interval and recovery rate. Since all
factors are positive, these two source equations are equivalently
\begin{equation}\label{eq:denominator-rearranged-main}
D_\varphi=\frac{A\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi},
\qquad
D_{\mathrm{mem}}=\frac{A}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)} .
\end{equation}
The factor $\Delta\tau$ appears only in the ideal-benchmark source equation
because the ideal record threshold is $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$, whereas
the memory threshold is already a rate equation,
$\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.

\begin{corollary}[Conditional homogeneous denominator quotient inside one fixed calibrated response model]\label{thm:ratio-main}
\ModelAClause assume in addition the calibrated
homogeneous spectral-weight regime.
Work under the standing convention for this section, and assume the calibrated
homogeneous spectral-weight regime. Thus the fixed calibrated response function
$R_\Omega$ has homogeneous spectral weight. Let $r_\varphi$
and $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ be the radii of the
ideal-benchmark and memory-transition response loci, produced by one
field-facing detector core
with fixed gap, coupling scale, detector profile, and field state. The
ideal-benchmark member uses ideal one-step blanking with sampling interval
$\Delta\tau$, and the memory member uses the sampled one-recovery-opportunity
boundary-reset mode and the same
sampling interval and recovery rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. Using the fixed
denominator convention in \eqref{eq:denominator-convention-main}, the
response-locus-pair denominator quotient is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio-main}
Q_{\varphi/\mathrm{mem}}
=\frac{D_\varphi}{D_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
For the two record-selected target rates from
\eqref{eq:target-rates-main}, one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:target-quotient-main}
\frac{\gamma_\varphi}{\gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
Q_{\mathrm{mem}/\varphi}
=C_D^{-1}
=Q_{\varphi/\mathrm{mem}}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
This is a cancellation identity inside the calibrated homogeneous response
model; it does not derive the common amplitude $A$ or the homogeneity of
$\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ from the KMS condition. For a general fixed calibrated
response function without the homogeneous specialization, the default physical
statement is instead \Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The ideal-benchmark and memory-transition thresholds are supplied by
Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} and
\Cref{lem:one-dep-criterion-main}. In the calibrated homogeneous specialization of
\Cref{thm:response-locus-main,thm:shells-general-main,cor:homogeneous-main},
\[
\Gamma_+(r,\Omega)=\frac{A}{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r)}-1},
\qquad A=\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S(\Omega)>0 .
\]
Substitution gives the two source equations
\eqref{eq:denominator-shells-main}, hence the two positive denominator
identities
\[
D_\varphi=\frac{A\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi},
\qquad
D_{\mathrm{mem}}=\frac{A}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)},
\qquad
A=\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S(\Omega)>0.
\]
Dividing the first identity by the second cancels exactly the common response
amplitude $A$ and gives \eqref{eq:ratio-main}. Since the ideal-benchmark and
memory target rates are those in \eqref{eq:target-rates-main}, their quotient
is the reciprocal of \eqref{eq:ratio-main}, proving
\eqref{eq:target-quotient-main}. No further geometric hypothesis is used
beyond the stated response-model and common-core inputs.
\end{proof}

This cancellation law uses one common field-facing core and homogeneous
spectral weight; without the second input the transport factor in
\Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main} remains. The sampling and recovery
parameters still enter through the readout constant
\[
C_D=\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi},
\]
so the ratio law is amplitude-free, not detector-free.

\begin{proposition}[Quantitative local homogeneity criterion for a fixed calibrated $R_\Omega$]\label{prop:spectral-stability-main}
\ModelAClause keep the same two response-locus points
and perturb only the spectral-weight homogeneity.
Work under the standing convention for this section, and assume the hypotheses
of \Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}. Let $U$ be a geodesically convex
subset of the static slice containing $x_\varphi$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$, and
suppose $\log \mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ is $C^1$ on $U$. Fix any auxiliary
Riemannian norm $|\cdot|$ on the slice and write
\[
L_U(\Omega)=\sup_{x\in U}|d\log \mathcal S_x(\Omega)|,
\qquad
d_U=d_U(x_\varphi,x_{\mathrm{mem}})
\]
for the corresponding geodesic distance between the two response-locus points. Let
\[
C_D=\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\]
and
\[
R=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-stability-main}
e^{-L_U(\Omega)d_U}
\le
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}
     {\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
\le
e^{L_U(\Omega)d_U},
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-stability-ratio-main}
C_D e^{-L_U(\Omega)d_U}
\le R\le
C_D e^{L_U(\Omega)d_U},
\qquad
\left|\frac{R}{C_D}-1\right|
\le e^{L_U(\Omega)d_U}-1.
\end{equation}
In particular, for any family with $L_{U_t}(\Omega(t))\,d_t\to0$ one has
\[
R(t)=C_D(t)\bigl(1+O(L_{U_t}(\Omega(t))\,d_t)\bigr).
\]
Thus the homogeneous ratio law is recovered asymptotically whenever the response-locus separation is small compared with the spatial variation scale of $\log\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $\gamma:[0,d_U]\to U$ be a unit-speed minimizing geodesic from $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$ to $x_\varphi$. Then
\[
\log\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}
         {\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
=
\int_0^{d_U}
d\log\mathcal S_{\gamma(u)}(\Omega)\bigl[\dot\gamma(u)\bigr]\,du.
\]
Hence
\[
\left|
\log\frac{\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)}
         {\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}
\right|
\le
\int_0^{d_U}L_U(\Omega)\,du
=L_U(\Omega)d_U,
\]
which gives \eqref{eq:spectral-stability-main} after exponentiation. Multiplying the latter by the exact spectral-transport identity \eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} yields \eqref{eq:spectral-stability-ratio-main}. If $L_{U_t}(\Omega(t))d_t\to0$, then $e^{L_{U_t}(\Omega(t))d_t}-1=O(L_{U_t}(\Omega(t))d_t)$, giving the final estimate.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Uniform perturbative form]\label{cor:spectral-stability-perturbative-main}
\ModelAClause the previous transport estimate
specializes as follows when the spectral weight is a uniform perturbation of a
homogeneous one.
Work under the standing convention for this section, and assume the hypotheses
of \Cref{prop:spectral-stability-main}. If, on the same region $U$,
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)(1+\varepsilon(x)),
\qquad
|\varepsilon(x)|\le\varepsilon_0<1,
\]
then
\[
C_D\,\frac{1-\varepsilon_0}{1+\varepsilon_0}
\le R\le
C_D\,\frac{1+\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0},
\qquad
\left|\frac{R}{C_D}-1\right|
\le
\frac{2\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main},
\[
\frac{R}{C_D}
=
\frac{1+\varepsilon(x_\varphi)}{1+\varepsilon(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}.
\]
The two-sided estimate follows by maximizing and minimizing the numerator and denominator under $|\varepsilon|\le\varepsilon_0$. The absolute bound follows from
\[
\left|
\frac{1+\varepsilon_\varphi}{1+\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mem}}}-1
\right|
=
\left|\frac{\varepsilon_\varphi-\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mem}}}{1+\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mem}}}\right|
\le
\frac{2\varepsilon_0}{1-\varepsilon_0}.
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Near-horizon corollaries inside the abstract calibrated specialization}

Near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, the primary statement in this
subsection is already abstract: it is an exact collar identity for the
homogeneous response-locus-pair denominator ratio inside the calibrated homogeneous
specialization of one fixed response profile. That statement requires only that
the response-locus pair enter a Gaussian normal collar. The further
proper-distance hierarchy appears only after an additional linearization step,
and that step requires the dimensionless small-gap condition
$\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$. Without that extra condition the valid
near-horizon conclusion remains the exact nonlinear collar ratio; no
proper-distance ratio follows in general. A Schwarzschild area-excess
specialization is recorded separately in Appendix~\ref{app:schwarzschild-toy}.
This is the precise scope boundary relative to the recent local-temperature
analysis of Mistry--Pinzul \cite{MistryPinzul2024DiamondTemp}: the theorem here
is not a new local-temperature limit, but the exact nonlinear collar ratio for
one already selected homogeneous response-locus pair inside the fixed calibrated
response model, followed only afterwards by the extra small-gap linearization.

\begin{assumption}[Abstract calibrated small-locus horizon family]\label{ass:small-shell-main}
\ModelAClause a family of homogeneous response-locus
pairs is called \emph{small-locus} if it satisfies the following conditions.
A family of response-locus pairs in the abstract calibrated homogeneous specialization is called \emph{small-locus} if it is indexed by a parameter $t$, has detector gap $\Omega(t)>0$, and has proper distances
\[
\rho_\varphi(t)>0,\qquad \rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)>0
\]
from the horizon, satisfies the exact homogeneous ratio law
\[
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t) N(\rho_\varphi(t))}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t) N(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t))}-1}
=C_D(t),
\qquad
C_D(t)=
\frac{\Delta\tau(t)\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r}(t),\Delta\tau(t))}
{\log\varphi},
\]
and obeys
\[
\epsilon(t)=\max\{\rho_\varphi(t),\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)\}\downarrow0 .
\]
The detector parameters may be fixed or may vary with $t$. The underlying fixed
response profile is still the one coming from the same gap-regular absolutely
continuous stationary response input as in \Cref{cor:homogeneous-main}, with no
atom or singular part at $\pm\Omega(t)$ along the family. The hypothesis is
part of the statement, not a consequence of the exact ratio law: outside such a
family the horizon expansion below is not being invoked. This assumption by
itself gives only an exact Planck-ratio relation in a normal collar. The linear
proper-distance law below is an additional consequence obtained only after the
dimensionless small-gap condition
$\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$ is imposed.
\Cref{thm:response-locus-main,lem:abstract-homogeneous-specialization-main}
supply the response-level setting in which this family is interpreted.
\end{assumption}

\begin{lemma}[Near-horizon Planck denominator]\label{lem:varying-gap-denominator-main}
\ModelAClause assume the nonextremal horizon expansion
\[
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3),
\qquad
\beta_\infty=\frac{2\pi}{\kappa_{\mathrm H}}.
\]
Let $\Omega(t)>0$, let $0<\rho(t)\le\epsilon(t)$, and suppose
$\epsilon(t)\downarrow0$. Then, uniformly for such $\rho(t)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exact-near-horizon-denominator-main}
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t)N(\rho(t))}-1
=
e^{2\pi\Omega(t)\rho(t)(1+O(\epsilon(t)^2))}-1 .
\end{equation}
If, in addition, $\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, then the denominator linearizes
uniformly:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:varying-gap-denominator-main}
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t)N(\rho(t))}-1
=2\pi\Omega(t)\rho(t)
\Bigl(1+O(\Omega(t)\rho(t))+O(\rho(t)^2)\Bigr).
\end{equation}
In particular, if $\Omega(t)$ is bounded, the relative error is $O(\rho(t))$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The horizon expansion and the Hartle--Hawking relation give
\[
\beta_\infty\Omega(t)N(\rho)
=2\pi\Omega(t)\rho\bigl(1+O(\rho^2)\bigr),
\]
uniformly in a fixed normal collar. Since $\rho\le\epsilon(t)$ and
$\epsilon(t)\downarrow0$, this proves
\eqref{eq:exact-near-horizon-denominator-main}. If also
$\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, the exponent is $o(1)$ uniformly along the family.
Using $e^y-1=y(1+O(y))$ for $y=o(1)$ then gives
\[
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t)N(\rho)}-1
=2\pi\Omega(t)\rho
\bigl(1+O(\rho^2)\bigr)
\bigl(1+O(\Omega(t)\rho)\bigr),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:varying-gap-denominator-main}. For bounded $\Omega(t)$ the
term $O(\Omega(t)\rho(t))$ is $O(\rho(t))$, while
$O(\rho(t)^2)$ is smaller.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Exact nonlinear near-horizon collar ratio for one preselected homogeneous response-locus pair]\label{lem:exact-collar-ratio-main}
\ModelAClause the exact homogeneous
response-locus-pair law has the following near-horizon collar form.
Work under the standing convention for this section. Assume the small-locus
horizon-family hypothesis in \Cref{ass:small-shell-main} and the nonextremal
Hartle--Hawking expansion
\[
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3),
\qquad
\beta_\infty=\frac{2\pi}{\kappa_{\mathrm H}}.
\]
Then the exact homogeneous ratio law is equivalent to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:exact-collar-ratio-main}
C_D(t)=
\frac{
e^{2\pi\Omega(t)\rho_\varphi(t)(1+O(\epsilon(t)^2))}-1}
{
e^{2\pi\Omega(t)\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)(1+O(\epsilon(t)^2))}-1},
\end{equation}
with uniform $O(\epsilon(t)^2)$ terms along the family. This is the primary
near-horizon statement for small-locus families: it remains valid even when
$\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)$ does not tend to zero, and by itself it does not imply a
linear proper-distance or area-excess ratio.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Apply \Cref{lem:varying-gap-denominator-main} without the small-gap
linearization at $\rho_\varphi(t)$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)$, then substitute
the exact homogeneous ratio law in \Cref{ass:small-shell-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Conditional small-gap linearization of the exact collar ratio]\label{thm:horizon-main}
\ModelAClause assume in addition the homogeneous
small-locus horizon family and the dimensionless small-gap regime.
Work under the standing convention for this section. Assume the small-locus
horizon-family hypothesis in \Cref{ass:small-shell-main}, so that the
homogeneous response-locus pair comes from a
fixed calibrated response function $R_\Omega$, and the dimensionless
small-gap condition
\[
\eta(t):=\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\longrightarrow0 .
\]
Suppose that the static region ends at a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and, in proper distance $\rho$ from the horizon,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-main}
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)
\qquad(\rho\downarrow0).
\end{equation}
Then \Cref{lem:exact-collar-ratio-main} linearizes, and as
$\epsilon(t)\downarrow0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\varphi}(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}
=
C_D(t)\Bigl(1+O(\eta(t))+O(\epsilon(t)^2)\Bigr).
\end{equation}
If the detector gap is fixed, or more generally uniformly bounded, this
estimate reduces to the simpler $O(\epsilon(t))$ relative-error form.
This is the small-gap linearization of
\Cref{lem:exact-collar-ratio-main}; without $\eta(t)\to0$ only the exact
nonlinear collar identity remains. \Cref{lem:high-gap-obstruction-main} gives
an explicit regime where the linear ratio fails. Appendix
~\ref{app:schwarzschild-toy} records the corresponding Schwarzschild
area-excess specialization.
\end{corollary}

The preceding warning is part of the logical content of the subsection: the
proper-distance ratio \eqref{eq:rho-ratio-main} is not a consequence of the
shell law alone and not a consequence of the exact denominator ratio alone.
Without the extra condition $\eta(t)=\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, one has only
\[
C_D(t)=
\frac{
e^{2\pi\Omega(t)\rho_\varphi(t)(1+O(\epsilon(t)^2))}-1}
{
e^{2\pi\Omega(t)\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)(1+O(\epsilon(t)^2))}-1},
\]
which is precisely \Cref{lem:exact-collar-ratio-main}. That formula is already
the full near-horizon information available in the high-gap regime.

\begin{proof}
All estimates below are along the small-locus family, and the constants in the
$O(\cdot)$ terms are uniform after restricting to a sufficiently small normal
collar. Applying \Cref{lem:varying-gap-denominator-main} at
$\rho_\varphi(t)$ and $\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)$ gives
\[
\begin{aligned}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t) N(\rho_\varphi(t))}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t) N(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t))}-1}
&=
\frac{\rho_\varphi(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}
\frac{1+O(\Omega(t)\rho_\varphi(t))+O(\rho_\varphi(t)^2)}
     {1+O(\Omega(t)\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t))+O(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)^2)}  \\
&=
\frac{\rho_\varphi(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}
\Bigl(1+O(\eta(t))+O(\epsilon(t)^2)\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]
The exact homogeneous ratio law says that the left-hand side is $C_D(t)$.
Hence
\[
C_D(t)=
\frac{\rho_\varphi(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}
\bigl(1+\delta(t)\bigr),
\qquad
\delta(t)=O(\eta(t))+O(\epsilon(t)^2).
\]
After restricting to $\epsilon(t)$ so small that $|\delta(t)|<1/2$,
$(1+\delta(t))^{-1}=1+O(\eta(t))+O(\epsilon(t)^2)$, which proves
\eqref{eq:rho-ratio-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Asymptotically homogeneous small-gap linearization]\label{cor:asymptotic-horizon-main}
\ModelAClause the homogeneous near-horizon
linearization remains valid under asymptotically homogeneous spectral
transport as follows.
Work under the standing convention for this section. Assume the same
calibrated horizon hypotheses as in \Cref{thm:horizon-main}, but replace exact
homogeneity by the
spectral-transport setting of \Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}. Suppose the
shell points lie on the same static radial segment, define
\[
L_\epsilon(t)=\sup_{0\le \rho\le \epsilon(t)}
\left|\partial_\rho \log \mathcal S_\rho(\Omega(t))\right|,
\]
and assume in addition that
\[
L_\epsilon(t)\,\epsilon(t)\longrightarrow0 .
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:asymptotic-rho-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\varphi}(t)}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}
=
C_D(t)\Bigl(1+O(\eta(t))+O(\epsilon(t)^2)+O(L_\epsilon(t)\epsilon(t))\Bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus the homogeneous small-gap linearization remains the leading term whenever
the response-locus separation shrinks faster than the spatial variation scale of
$\log \mathcal S_\rho(\Omega)$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Because both shell points lie in $0\le\rho\le\epsilon(t)$ on the same radial segment, the one-dimensional mean-value estimate gives
\[
\left|
\log\frac{\mathcal S_{\rho_\varphi(t)}(\Omega(t))}
         {\mathcal S_{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}(\Omega(t))}
\right|
\le
L_\epsilon(t)\,|\rho_\varphi(t)-\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)|
\le
L_\epsilon(t)\,\epsilon(t).
\]
Hence
\[
\frac{\mathcal S_{\rho_\varphi(t)}(\Omega(t))}
     {\mathcal S_{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}(\Omega(t))}
=
1+O(L_\epsilon(t)\epsilon(t)).
\]
Combining this with the spectral-transport identity \eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} yields
\[
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t) N(\rho_\varphi(t))}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega(t) N(\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t))}-1}
=
C_D(t)\bigl(1+O(L_\epsilon(t)\epsilon(t))\bigr).
\]
Applying the proof of \Cref{thm:horizon-main} with this modified exact ratio replaces the left-hand side constant $C_D(t)$ by
$C_D(t)\bigl(1+O(L_\epsilon(t)\epsilon(t))\bigr)$, which gives
\eqref{eq:asymptotic-rho-ratio-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[High-gap obstruction to the linear ratio]\label{lem:high-gap-obstruction-main}
\ModelAClause work under the standing convention for this section. The condition
$\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$ in
\Cref{thm:horizon-main} cannot be dropped. Suppose, along a small-locus
family, that
\[
\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to\Lambda\in(0,\infty),\qquad
\frac{\rho_\varphi(t)}{\epsilon(t)}\to a,\qquad
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}(t)}{\epsilon(t)}\to b
\]
with $a,b\in(0,1]$. Then the exact collar ratio gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:high-gap-limit-main}
C_D(t)\longrightarrow
\frac{e^{2\pi\Lambda a}-1}{e^{2\pi\Lambda b}-1},
\end{equation}
whereas the proper-distance ratio tends to $a/b$. These two limits are not equal
in general.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Under the displayed hypotheses the correction
$O(\epsilon(t)^2)$ in \eqref{eq:exact-collar-ratio-main} contributes
$o(1)$ to the exponent, because
$\Omega(t)\rho_\star(t)O(\epsilon(t)^2)=O(\epsilon(t)^2)$ for
$\star\in\{\varphi,\mathrm{mem}\}$. Taking the limit in
\eqref{eq:exact-collar-ratio-main} gives \eqref{eq:high-gap-limit-main}. The
last assertion follows, for example, by taking $a\ne b$ and any fixed
$\Lambda>0$: the function $(e^{2\pi\Lambda x}-1)/x$ is not constant on
$(0,\infty)$.
\end{proof}

\Cref{lem:exact-collar-ratio-main} is the valid near-horizon statement before
linearization. \Cref{thm:horizon-main} is only the additional small-gap
linearization of that lemma, obtained in the dimensionless regime
$\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, and \Cref{lem:high-gap-obstruction-main} shows why
that hypothesis is essential. A fixed response-locus pair away from the horizon still
satisfies the exact ratio law proved in \Cref{thm:ratio-main}, but the
near-horizon expansion does not by itself supply proper-distance or area-excess
asymptotics; in the high-gap regime it supplies only the exact collar ratio.
Within the dimensionless small-gap small-locus regime, the leading ratio is
independent of the common response amplitude and depends on the detector only
through the known readout constant $C_D(t)$.

A particularly informative regime is the fast-sampling limit.
\begin{lemma}[Fast-sampling critical rate]\label{lem:fast-gammac-main}
\ModelAClause for fixed $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$ and $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$, equivalently $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\downarrow0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-main}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\qquad
L=\log\frac{1}{s}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $x=\Gamma_c/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. The critical equation~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main} is
\[
s=\frac{\log x}{x-1}.
\]
For $s\downarrow0$ the non-removable solution has $x\to\infty$. Put $x=(L+u)/s$, with $L=\log(1/s)$. Then
\[
\log x
=\log(L+u)-\log s
=L+\log(L+u),
\]
while
\[
s(x-1)=L+u-s.
\]
The equation $\log x=s(x-1)$ therefore becomes
\[
u=\log(L+u)+s .
\]
Since the right-hand side is $O(\log L)$, this identity first gives $u=O(\log L)$, and substituting back gives
$u=\log L+o(1)$. Hence
\[
\Gamma_c=\kappa_{\mathrm r}x
=\frac{s}{\Delta\tau}\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{s}
=\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau}.
\]
\end{proof}

In the calibrated homogeneous near-horizon setting with fixed detector gap and response amplitude, both target rates diverge as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$, so the corresponding lapse levels tend to zero. Thus the fast-sampling scaling produces a concrete algebraic small-locus family inside the homogeneous specialization of \Cref{thm:response-locus-main,lem:abstract-homogeneous-specialization-main}, rather than from a separately derived nonconstant-lapse UDW model, and it satisfies the dimensionless small-gap condition in Corollary~\ref{thm:horizon-main}. Combining that small-gap linearization with Lemma~\ref{lem:fast-gammac-main} gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\rho_{\varphi}}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus the memory-transition locus collapses toward the horizon parametrically faster than the ideal-benchmark locus. Whenever the regularity check is also verified, the corresponding shell pair therefore resolves a genuine hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales rather than two nearby realizations of the same thermal profile.

\subsection{Fixed-radius ratio-map corollaries inside the abstract calibrated specialization}

The response-locus pair also carries fixed-radius ratio-map information in
static spherical settings, but the conservative main-text object is the
abstract ratio map obtained after dividing within each mode. The common
response amplitude is absent from those two equations, but it remains an
admissibility variable that must be checked after a candidate background
parameter has been found. Appendix~\ref{app:schwarzschild-toy} records a
separate Schwarzschild toy specialization in which the absolute response scale
is kept.

Thus the main local fixed-radius corollary in this subsection uses the conservative inverse map
that the ratio equations actually justify: it fixes one radius record, solves from
the two ratio values, and keeps the full common-core admissibility conditions in
the same statement. If the four radii are also varied as algebraic
parameters, the resulting ambient residual graph is recorded separately in the
appendix; that enlarged bookkeeping is not an inverse theorem from nearby
response-locus records. Physical common-core shell data still form a generally smaller subset,
cut out by the undivided shell equations, positivity of one common hardware
prefactor, and the exterior range of the chosen background family.

\paragraph{Scope of the fixed-radius subsection.}
The logical input here is deliberately split into two parts. First, one exact
fixed radius record $\eta_*$ and one fixed pair of detector settings determine
the ratio target $C$ and the undivided shell target $b$. Second, any root-$N$
limit theory for nearby ratio data is supplied externally, either directly as
an asymptotic law for $\widehat y_N$ or indirectly through external
location or denominator estimators and a delta method. The results below then
package the exact fixed-target shell constraint, the local inverse/sensitivity
branch in ratio space, and the resulting covariance transport into one
conditional inverse proposition. No scan-to-shell-location-to-ratio CLT is
derived from the
single-setting click-record theorem of \Cref{thm:cycle-block-main}; the
one-record scope theorem \Cref{thm:one-record-estimates-main} stops at the
fixed-setting covariance $\gamma_2$ and does not produce ratio coordinates.
Accordingly, the fixed-radius statistical statements below are conditional
sensitivity formulas for externally supplied ratio asymptotics. They do not
turn the paper into a standalone derived-estimator theory for ratio data from
one fixed click record.
Relative to Oeckl's causal-measurement theorem
\cite{Oeckl2025CausalMeasurement}, the theorem object here is therefore much
narrower: one fixed-radius denominator-ratio map inside a calibrated response
model, together with one conditional proposition for the exact fixed-target
constraint and the local inverse/sensitivity algebra for nearby estimated ratio
data. The
paper does not construct a general causal measurement observable or protocol
for quantum fields.

The Schwarzschild-specific toy specialization of the homogeneous ratio
equations, including the absolute-scale algebra and explicit admissibility
checks, is recorded separately in Appendix~\ref{app:schwarzschild-toy}. The
main text keeps only the fixed-radius ratio map that is justified directly by
the quotient equations. A related construction extends beyond any one
background family in a local ratio form. Each
detector operating mode supplies one response-locus pair and hence one scalar ratio
equation. Two genuinely distinct modes can provide two equations for a
two-parameter background family. The data objects used in those equations are
defined explicitly as follows. There are two different maps. The first is the
abstract fixed-radius ratio map
\[
\mathcal R_\eta:\Theta\to(0,\infty)^2,
\]
obtained after dividing within each detector mode. The second is the full
fixed-setting data map
\[
\mathcal F_\eta:\Theta\times(0,\infty)\to(0,\infty)^4,
\]
which keeps the common unknown amplitude and the four undivided shell
equations. The inverse/sensitivity corollary below concerns only the abstract ratio map and
its local sensitivity to nearby ratio data in $(0,\infty)^2$; a physical shell
record at the same fixed radii is encoded by the separate four-equation map
$\mathcal F_\eta$, which for fixed detector settings forces the single target
vector selected by those settings.

For later reference, the three data objects are:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\eta &\quad \text{fixed radius record},\\
y &\in (0,\infty)^2 \quad \text{ratio datum at those fixed radii},\\
b &\in (0,\infty)^4 \quad \text{full fixed-setting shell target}.
\end{aligned}
\]
In the theorem below, the detector settings are held fixed, so $C$ and $b$ are
held fixed as well. Accordingly, a nearby point $y$ means nearby measured or
estimated denominator quotients for those same settings and that same radius
record $\eta_*$. It does not mean that the detector settings have been varied;
changing the settings would replace the constants $C_m$ and the target vector
$b$, hence would define a different inverse problem.

A two-mode radius record is written
\[
\eta=(\eta_{\varphi,1},\eta_{{\mathrm{mem}},1},
      \eta_{\varphi,2},\eta_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}).
\]
Here $J$ denotes the radial-coordinate interval for the chosen static spherical
coordinate convention. When radius records are varied only as ambient algebraic
variables, they are taken in an open set $\mathcal D\subset J^4$ on which the
response factors below are well defined, and the corresponding algebraic ratio
data lie in an open set $\mathcal E\subset\mathcal D\times(0,\infty)^2$.
For each $m$, the ideal-benchmark radius is obtained from ideal one-step
blanking with sampling interval $\Delta\tau_m$, while the memory radius is
obtained from the sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset mode and the
same sampling interval and recovery rate $\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m}$. The detector
settings supply the known physical ratio targets
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-mode-ratio-constants-main}
C_m=\frac{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}{\log\varphi},
\qquad m=1,2.
\end{equation}

For a candidate background parameter $\theta$, define the two radius-dependent ratio observables
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-mode-ratio-functions-main}
R_m(\theta;\eta)=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1},
\qquad
\mathcal R_\eta(\theta)=(R_1(\theta;\eta),R_2(\theta;\eta)).
\end{equation}
The physical residuals are
\[
H_m(\theta,\eta)=R_m(\theta;\eta)-C_m,\qquad
H(\theta,\eta)=\mathcal R_\eta(\theta)-C,
\]
where $C=(C_1,C_2)$.
If one physically estimates the two denominator quotients at the fixed radii
and detector settings, the resulting datum is a point
$\widehat y\in(0,\infty)^2$ approximating $\mathcal R_\eta(\theta)$. Thus a
nearby point of ratio space means a pair of nearby measured denominator
quotients for those same fixed radii and readout settings, not a perturbed
radius quadruple. Equivalently, the local inverse corollary below takes as input
estimated ratio data
\[
\widehat y\approx\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta_*)
\]
at one fixed radius record $\eta_*$ and one fixed pair of detector settings.
The paper does not derive such ratio data from the click-record diagnostics of
\Cref{sec:records} or from the shell-location equations alone. The covariance
statement later in this subsection is conditional on an external asymptotic law
for that ratio datum, and a subsequent remark records one standard delta-method
route by which shell-location or denominator estimators could supply it.
When those settings are kept fixed, exact full shell data are not a nearby
two-parameter family in ratio space: they still correspond only to the single
physical target $C$ in the ratio projection of the four-equation map
$\mathcal F_{\eta_*}$.

The full shell bookkeeping is separate from the two ratio equations. Let
\[
\mathcal A=\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S(\Omega)
\]
denote the unknown homogeneous hardware prefactor. For a candidate background parameter $\theta$, radius record $\eta$, and mode $m$, the undivided shell equations infer
\[
\mathcal A_{\varphi,m}(\theta;\eta)=
\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau_m}
\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1\bigr),
\]
and
\[
\mathcal A_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}(\theta;\eta)=
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)
\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1\bigr).
\]
Equivalently, for fixed $\eta$ the full undivided shell data are encoded by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:full-fixed-radius-map-main}
\mathcal F_\eta(\theta,\mathcal A)=
\begin{pmatrix}
\dfrac{\mathcal A\Delta\tau_1}
      {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{\varphi,1};\theta)}-1} \\
\dfrac{\mathcal A}
      {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{{\mathrm{mem}},1};\theta)}-1} \\
\dfrac{\mathcal A\Delta\tau_2}
      {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{\varphi,2};\theta)}-1} \\
\dfrac{\mathcal A}
      {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{{\mathrm{mem}},2};\theta)}-1}
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
with target vector
\begin{equation}\label{eq:full-fixed-radius-target-main}
b=
\bigl(
\log\varphi,\,
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},1},\Delta\tau_1),\,
\log\varphi,\,
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},2},\Delta\tau_2)
\bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus $\eta$ is a full common-core shell record exactly when there exist
$(\theta,\mathcal A)$ with $\mathcal A>0$ such that
$\mathcal F_\eta(\theta,\mathcal A)=b$. Dividing the first pair and the second
pair of entries in \eqref{eq:full-fixed-radius-map-main} eliminates
$\mathcal A$ and leaves the two ratio equations
$\mathcal R_\eta(\theta)=C$.

\begin{lemma}[Ratio residuals and full common-core data]\label{lem:ratio-admissibility-main}
\ModelAClause the ratio equations and the full
shell-data equations are related as follows at fixed detector settings.
Work under the standing convention for this section. For a fixed physical mode
$m$, candidate parameter $\theta$, and radius record
$\eta$, the equation $H_m(\theta,\eta)=0$ is equivalent to the within-mode
amplitude equality
\[
\mathcal A_{\varphi,m}(\theta;\eta)=\mathcal A_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}(\theta;\eta).
\]
The pair of ratio equations $H(\theta,\eta)=0$ contains no unknown
$\mathcal A$. A radius record is an exact full common-core shell record at the
same $\theta$ only if, in addition, the four estimates
\[
\mathcal A_{\varphi,1},\quad \mathcal A_{{\mathrm{mem}},1},\quad
\mathcal A_{\varphi,2},\quad \mathcal A_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}
\]
are all positive and equal to one common value $\mathcal A>0$, and the four
radii lie in the exterior coordinate ranges of the background family.
Equivalently, the abstract ratio condition $H(\theta,\eta)=0$ becomes a
physical fixed-radius shell solution only after the full map in
\eqref{eq:full-fixed-radius-map-main} hits the target vector
\eqref{eq:full-fixed-radius-target-main}. These extra conditions are not
consequences of the ratio equations. In particular, once the detector settings
and hence the target vector $b$ are fixed, every exact full common-core shell
solution satisfies
\[
\mathcal R_\eta(\theta)=C.
\]
Therefore a nearby point $y\neq C$ has no exact interpretation as full
fixed-setting shell data unless one explicitly changes the detector targets
themselves or treats $y$ only as an estimated ratio datum in the abstract ratio
space.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By the definitions above,
\[
\frac{\mathcal A_{\varphi,m}(\theta;\eta)}
{\mathcal A_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}(\theta;\eta)}
=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(\eta_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1}
\frac{\log\varphi}
{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}
=\frac{H_m(\theta,\eta)+C_m}{C_m}.
\]
Thus $H_m(\theta,\eta)=0$ is exactly the equality of the two within-mode
amplitude estimates. The formula for $H_m$ contains only the radius record, the
background family, the gap, and the readout constants; the common prefactor
$\mathcal A$ has cancelled. Equality across the two modes, positivity, and the
exterior-range restrictions are separate requirements. They cannot be inferred
from $H(\theta,\eta)=0$, because the ratio operation has deliberately discarded
the absolute scale and does not encode the allowed radial range. In
\eqref{eq:full-fixed-radius-map-main}, that discarded scale is exactly the
unknown common amplitude $\mathcal A$. Conversely, if
$\mathcal F_\eta(\theta,\mathcal A)=b$ for some $\mathcal A>0$, then dividing
the first and second entries and also the third and fourth entries recovers
$R_m(\theta;\eta)=C_m$ for $m=1,2$, hence $\mathcal R_\eta(\theta)=C$.
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}[External ratio-data CLT at fixed radii]\label{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}
\ModelAClause the statistical input for the fixed-radius
inverse statement is the following external ratio-data asymptotic law.
Assume the calibrated homogeneous fixed-radius setting inherited from
\Cref{thm:ratio-main}. Let $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ be open, let $J$ be the radial
interval used by a fixed static spherical coordinate chart, and suppose
\[
N:J\times\Theta\to(0,\infty),
\qquad
\beta_\infty:\Theta\to(0,\infty)
\]
are $C^1$. Choose a fixed radius record
\[
\eta_*=(r_{\varphi,1},r_{{\mathrm{mem}},1},
        r_{\varphi,2},r_{{\mathrm{mem}},2})\in J^4
\]
on which the ratio functions in \eqref{eq:two-mode-ratio-functions-main} are
defined, and keep the same detector settings and hence the same fixed targets
$C=(C_1,C_2)$ and $b$ from
\eqref{eq:two-mode-ratio-constants-main} and
\eqref{eq:full-fixed-radius-target-main}. Assume that this record comes from
admissible full common-core shell data at $\theta_*$ with one positive shared
amplitude $\mathcal A_*>0$, so that
\[
\mathcal F_{\eta_*}(\theta_*,\mathcal A_*)=b
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta_*)=C
\]
and
\[
\mathcal A_{\varphi,1}(\theta_*;\eta_*)
=\mathcal A_{{\mathrm{mem}},1}(\theta_*;\eta_*)
=\mathcal A_{\varphi,2}(\theta_*;\eta_*)
=\mathcal A_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}(\theta_*;\eta_*)
=\mathcal A_*>0,
\]
with all four radii lying in the exterior coordinate range at $\theta_*$. Let
$\widehat y_N\in(0,\infty)^2$ be externally supplied estimated ratio data for
those fixed detector settings and that fixed radius record. These data are not
claimed here to be produced by the click-record diagnostics or by the shell
equations alone; they are an additional input to the fixed-radius inverse
problem. Assume that
\[
\sqrt{N}\,(\widehat y_N-C)\Longrightarrow \mathcal N(0,\Sigma_y)
\]
for some positive semidefinite $2\times2$ matrix $\Sigma_y$. In particular,
\[
\widehat y_N=C+O_{\mathbb P}(N^{-1/2}),
\]
so any later inverse branch is applied only to estimated denominator quotients
centered at the exact fixed-setting target.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Conditional fixed-radius inverse/sensitivity and covariance transport at one fixed radius record]\label{thm:two-mode-main}
\ModelAClause this fixed-radius inverse statement is
purely conditional on the external ratio-data input in
\Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}. Set
\[
\mathcal R_*=\mathcal R_{\eta_*}.
\]
Assume, in addition, that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:two-mode-ratio-jacobian-main}
\det D_\theta\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta_*)\neq0.
\end{equation}
Then the following hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item Exact full common-core shell data at these same fixed settings remain pinned
to the single physical ratio target $C$. More precisely, for any
$\theta\in\Theta$ and any $\mathcal A>0$,
\[
\mathcal F_{\eta_*}(\theta,\mathcal A)=b
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta)=C.
\]
Consequently a nearby point $y\neq C$ in ratio space has no exact fixed-setting
shell interpretation at the same record $\eta_*$ and detector settings. Such a
nearby $y$ can only be treated as estimated denominator quotient data for the
same fixed-setting inverse problem, or as data for a retuned-target problem in
which the detector targets themselves change.
\item There are neighbourhoods $U\subset\Theta$ of $\theta_*$ and
$Y\subset(0,\infty)^2$ of $C$, together with a unique $C^1$ branch
\[
\mathcal I_{\eta_*}:Y\to U,\qquad \mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C)=\theta_*,
\]
such that
\[
\mathcal R_*(\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(y))=y\qquad(y\in Y).
\]
If $\theta\in U$ and $\mathcal R_*(\theta)=y\in Y$, then
$\theta=\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(y)$. By
\Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}, the only data to which this branch is applied
are estimated ratio values satisfying $\widehat y_N=C+O_{\mathbb P}(N^{-1/2})$.
\item With
\[
\widehat\theta_N=\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(\widehat y_N),
\]
one has
\[
\sqrt{N}\,(\widehat\theta_N-\theta_*)
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal N(0,\Sigma_\theta),
\]
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta-covariance-main}
\Sigma_\theta
=D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C)\,\Sigma_y\,D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C)^\top
=\bigl[D_\theta\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta_*)\bigr]^{-1}
\Sigma_y
\bigl[D_\theta\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta_*)\bigr]^{-{\top}}.
\end{equation}
\item If $\widehat\Sigma_{y,N}\to\Sigma_y$ in probability, then the plug-in
covariance transport formula
\[
\widehat\Sigma_{\theta,N}
=D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(\widehat y_N)\,
\widehat\Sigma_{y,N}\,
D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(\widehat y_N)^\top
\]
converges in probability to $\Sigma_\theta$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Part (i) is exactly the fixed-target consequence of
\Cref{lem:ratio-admissibility-main}: if
$\mathcal F_{\eta_*}(\theta,\mathcal A)=b$ for some $\mathcal A>0$, then
$\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta)=C$. The displayed exact shell identity at
$(\theta_*,\mathcal A_*)$ is already part of
\Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}, so exact full common-core shell data at the
fixed settings occur only at the single physical ratio target $C$.

For the inverse branch in part (ii), the stated regularity makes
$\mathcal R_*:\Theta\to\RR^2$ a $C^1$ map with exact base point
$\mathcal R_*(\theta_*)=C$. To make the Jacobian condition explicit, write
\[
B_{\varphi,m}(\theta)=\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{\varphi,m};\theta),
\qquad
B_{\mathrm{mem},m}(\theta)=\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta).
\]
Then
\[
d_\theta R_m=
\frac{
e^{B_{\varphi,m}}\,d_\theta B_{\varphi,m}\,(e^{B_{\mathrm{mem},m}}-1)
-(e^{B_{\varphi,m}}-1)e^{B_{\mathrm{mem},m}}\,d_\theta B_{\mathrm{mem},m}
}
{(e^{B_{\mathrm{mem},m}}-1)^2}.
\]
Thus \eqref{eq:two-mode-ratio-jacobian-main} is the concrete requirement that
the two row covectors supplied by the two fixed-radius ratio equations are not
collinear at $\theta_*$. Apply the ordinary inverse function theorem to
$\mathcal R_*$ at $\theta_*$. This yields neighbourhoods $U,Y$ and the unique
$C^1$ inverse branch $\mathcal I_{\eta_*}$. Exterior admissibility of the fixed
record is an open condition in $\theta$, so $U$ may be shrunk if necessary to
keep the fixed radii in the allowed coordinate range throughout $U$. Because
\Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main} gives
$\widehat y_N=C+O_{\mathbb P}(N^{-1/2})$, the estimators $\widehat y_N$ lie in
$Y$ with probability tending to one.

For part (iii), apply the multivariate delta method to the $C^1$ map
$\mathcal I_{\eta_*}$ at the point $C$ using the external CLT from
\Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}. This gives
\[
\sqrt{N}\,(\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(\widehat y_N)-\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C))
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal N\!\bigl(0,
D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C)\Sigma_y D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C)^\top\bigr).
\]
Since $\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C)=\theta_*$, this is the stated Gaussian limit.
The identity
\[
D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(C)=\bigl[D_\theta\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta_*)\bigr]^{-1}
\]
is the chain rule for the local inverse branch, yielding
\eqref{eq:delta-covariance-main}. Part (iv) follows from the continuity of
$D\mathcal I_{\eta_*}$ on $Y$ and Slutsky's theorem. No ratio-data asymptotic
is proved inside this proposition; all stochastic input comes from
\Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[One sufficient source of the ratio-data CLT]\label{rem:ratio-data-delta-source-main}
One standard route to \Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main} is to start from an
external estimator of the positive denominator vector
\[
D_*=(D_{\varphi,1}^*,D_{{\mathrm{mem}},1}^*,D_{\varphi,2}^*,D_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}^*)
\in(0,\infty)^4,
\]
for which
\[
C_1=\frac{D_{\varphi,1}^*}{D_{{\mathrm{mem}},1}^*},
\qquad
C_2=\frac{D_{\varphi,2}^*}{D_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}^*}.
\]
If an external procedure supplies estimators $\widehat D_N$ satisfying
\[
\sqrt{N}\,(\widehat D_N-D_*)\Longrightarrow\mathcal N(0,\Sigma_D),
\]
and one forms the ratio datum by
\[
\widehat y_N=G(\widehat D_N),\qquad
G(d_{\varphi,1},d_{{\mathrm{mem}},1},d_{\varphi,2},d_{{\mathrm{mem}},2})
=\left(\frac{d_{\varphi,1}}{d_{{\mathrm{mem}},1}},
\frac{d_{\varphi,2}}{d_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}}\right),
\]
then the multivariate delta method yields
\[
\sqrt{N}\,(\widehat y_N-C)\Longrightarrow \mathcal N(0,\Sigma_y),
\qquad
\Sigma_y=DG(D_*)\,\Sigma_D\,DG(D_*)^\top,
\]
with
\[
DG(D_*)=
\begin{pmatrix}
\dfrac{1}{D_{{\mathrm{mem}},1}^*}
&
-\dfrac{D_{\varphi,1}^*}{(D_{{\mathrm{mem}},1}^*)^2}
& 0 & 0\\[1.1ex]
0 & 0 &
\dfrac{1}{D_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}^*}
&
-\dfrac{D_{\varphi,2}^*}{(D_{{\mathrm{mem}},2}^*)^2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The same composition principle applies if one first proves a CLT for external
shell-location or lapse estimators and then passes to the denominators and
their quotients through the smooth maps in
\eqref{eq:two-mode-ratio-functions-main}. The present paper does not prove such
an external CLT; it records only the conditional inverse/sensitivity consequences once
that input is available. In particular,
\Cref{thm:one-record-estimates-main} already isolates why the single-record CLT
for $\gamma_2$ is insufficient for these ratio coordinates.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[One scan/localization route to the external ratio-data CLT]\label{prop:ratio-data-shell-source-main}
The same external input can arise from a scan or localization protocol, but
that derivation is separate from the one-record CLT proved in
\Cref{sec:records}. Keep the same fixed detector settings, the same exact
radius record $\eta_*\in J^4$, and the same background parameter
$\theta_*\in\Theta$ from \Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}. Define the
fixed-parameter ratio map in the radius variables by
\[
h:\mathcal D\to(0,\infty)^2,
\qquad
h(\eta)=\mathcal R_\eta(\theta_*),
\]
on an open neighborhood $\mathcal D\subset J^4$ of $\eta_*$. Suppose an
external scan or localization procedure supplies shell-location estimators
$\widehat\eta_N\in\mathcal D$ such that
\[
\sqrt N\,(\widehat\eta_N-\eta_*)
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal N(0,\Sigma_\eta)
\]
for some positive semidefinite $4\times4$ matrix $\Sigma_\eta$. Then the
derived ratio datum
\[
\widehat y_N=h(\widehat\eta_N)
\]
satisfies
\[
\sqrt N\,(\widehat y_N-C)
\Longrightarrow
\mathcal N(0,\Sigma_y),
\qquad
\Sigma_y=
D_\eta h(\eta_*)\,\Sigma_\eta\,D_\eta h(\eta_*)^\top.
\]
Because $h(\eta_*)=\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta_*)=C$, this is exactly one
sufficient protocol-level route to the external input assumed in
\Cref{ass:ratio-data-clt-main}. The present paper does not prove such a scan or
localization CLT; it records only that, once supplied, the inverse-function
algebra of \Cref{thm:two-mode-main} transports it to parameter space.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Inverse-branch meaning versus retuned exact targets]\label{rem:fixed-target-versus-retuned-main}
The interpretation of \Cref{thm:two-mode-main} is strictly
fixed-setting. Once the detector settings and the radius record $\eta_*$ are
frozen, the ratio target is the single point $C=(C_1,C_2)$ and the undivided
shell target is the single vector $b$. The inverse branch
$\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(y)$ should therefore be read only as an inverse or
sensitivity map for nearby observed denominator quotients $y$, while
\Cref{thm:two-mode-main} says that exact fixed-setting shell data at those same
settings still occur only at $y=C$.

If one instead wants nearby $y$ to represent exact shell data, the detector
targets must vary with $y$. In the present notation the corresponding
$y$-dependent target vector is
\[
b(y)=
\left(
\log\varphi,\,
\frac{y_1\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau_1},\,
\log\varphi,\,
\frac{y_2\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau_2}
\right),
\]
because the exact within-mode quotient relation is
$y_m=\Delta\tau_m\Gamma_{{\mathrm c},m}/\log\varphi$. The exact equation in
that retuned problem is then
\[
\mathcal F_{\eta_*}(\mathcal I_{\eta_*}(y),\mathcal A(y))=b(y),
\]
which is a different retuned-target calibration problem from the fixed-setting inverse problem
treated in \Cref{thm:two-mode-main}.
\end{remark}

If one instead varies the radius quadruple as an ambient algebraic parameter,
the corresponding residual graph and the restriction to full common-core shell
data are recorded separately in
\Cref{cor:ambient-ratio-graph-app-main,thm:full-shell-subset-main,cor:full-shell-restriction-main}. Those appendix statements describe the local geometry of the ratio residual equations in the enlarged data space; they are not local ratio-map inverse theorems from nearby shell records to background geometry.

Appendix~\ref{app:schwarzschild-toy} records a separate Schwarzschild toy
specialization of this fixed-radius algebra, including the one-parameter
Hartle--Hawking branch reduction and a worked table. It remains explicit
calibrated-model bookkeeping rather than an exact nonconstant-lapse
detector-field construction.

\begin{remark}\label{rem:jacobian-main}
The Jacobian condition~\eqref{eq:two-mode-ratio-jacobian-main} is the local regularity condition for the ratio-map inverse branch, evaluated at the exact radius record and physical ratio target. It can fail because the two modes produce redundant ratio functions, or because the chosen background parametrization affects the two recorded radii in linearly dependent ways. Equality $C_1=C_2$ is therefore not a complete degeneracy criterion; it is a warning sign that the two modes may not provide independent constraints. Choosing distinct sampling and recovery parameters is necessary in practice, but the determinant in~\eqref{eq:two-mode-ratio-jacobian-main} is the actual inverse-regularity test.
\end{remark}
