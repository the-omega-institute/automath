\section{Static KMS backgrounds and shell equations}\label{sec:kms-shells}

Having established the record-level thresholds, we now embed the detector in a static KMS background and derive the corresponding shell equations.

The construction has two logically distinct parts. First, the record law fixes target rates, namely $\log\varphi/\Delta\tau$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, before geometry is introduced. Second, a response model assigns a local excitation rate to each static worldline. Only after both steps have been made explicit can a target rate be interpreted as a geometric locus. This section therefore separates the response assumption, the rate-to-locus lemma, and the homogeneous Tolman specialization.

Let $N(x)$ be the lapse function of a static spacetime and assume the field is in a global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state for Killing time. Along a static worldline at position $x$, Tolman scaling gives \cite{Tolman1930,TolmanEhrenfest1930}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-main}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
This relation fixes the proper-time KMS parameter seen by a static detector. It does not say that every local response observable is a function of $N(x)$ alone; mode density, boundary conditions, detector profile, and greybody transmission can still enter the absolute transition rate.

For a static worldline, the weak-coupling transition rates are Fourier transforms of the pulled-back two-point function. The KMS condition fixes the detailed-balance ratio \cite{HaagHugenholtzWinnink1967,KayWald1991},
\[
\frac{\Gamma_-(x,\Omega)}{\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)}
=e^{\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)\Omega},
\]
with our convention that $\Gamma_+$ is excitation and $\Gamma_-$ is de-excitation. It does not, by itself, fix the absolute magnitude of the excitation rate. The remaining positive factor is a local response spectral weight. We therefore state the response input as a separate model assumption rather than treating it as a geometric identity.

\begin{assumption}[Static KMS response input]\label{ass:kms-response-main}
Fix a static region $U$ and a detector with positive gap $\Omega$ and fixed spatial profile. For each static worldline $x\in U$, the profile-smeared pulled-back two-point function is assumed to be stationary, to satisfy the KMS condition at inverse temperature $\beta_\infty N(x)$ with respect to proper time, and to have a finite nonzero positive-frequency response at $\Omega$. The weak-coupling stationary excitation attempts that drive the counter are postulated, as part of the response model class, to have the Planck-factor decomposition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-rate-main}
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ is the local response spectral weight \cite{KayWald1991}. In all shell-regularity and inverse statements, $x\mapsto\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ has the displayed $C^1$ regularity on the region under discussion. Equivalently, $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ may be read as the positive local response spectral density in the KMS representation of the profile-smeared two-point function restricted to the static worldline.
\end{assumption}

The companion de-excitation rate in this parametrization is
\[
\Gamma_-(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}
{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\]
so detailed balance is automatic while the excitation rate still depends on the spectral weight. Conversely, for any positive stationary excitation rate satisfying KMS detailed balance at the same local inverse temperature, equation~\eqref{eq:kms-rate-main} can be read as the definition of
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=
\lambda_c^{-2}\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1\bigr)\Gamma_+(x,\Omega).
\]
The content of \Cref{ass:kms-response-main} is therefore not that KMS thermality alone fixes the absolute rate, but that the remaining positive factor is treated as a controlled response input. Thus $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ is part of the response model, not an inferred geometric invariant. It absorbs the local mode density, detector profile, and any greybody or boundary contribution that remains after the KMS occupation factor has been separated. The homogeneous regime of \Cref{ass:homogeneous-spectral-main} is therefore an explicit calibration regime, not a consequence of KMS thermality. Until that specialization is invoked, all shell statements retain the position dependence of $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$.

Define the positive response function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:response-function-main}
R_\Omega(x)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}.
\end{equation}
The record analysis supplies two target rates,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:target-rates-main}
\gamma_\varphi=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau},
\qquad
\gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\end{equation}
The geometric problem is therefore not to derive new response rates, but to solve the two response-level equations
\[
R_\Omega(x)=\gamma_\varphi,
\qquad
R_\Omega(x)=\gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}.
\]
The following lemma records the geometric step used by both detectors. The word ``shell'' is used only after this regularity check; otherwise the same equation defines a response locus.

\begin{lemma}[Response loci and shell regularity]\label{lem:response-locus-main}
Let $U$ be the static region under consideration, and assume that $N$ and $x\mapsto\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ are $C^1$ on $U$, with $N>0$ and $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$. For any target rate $\gamma>0$, define
\[
L_\gamma=\{x\in U:R_\Omega(x)=\gamma\}.
\]
Then $L_\gamma$ is empty exactly when $\gamma$ is outside the range of $R_\Omega$ on $U$. If $x_0\in L_\gamma$ and $dR_\Omega(x_0)\neq0$, then $L_\gamma$ is, near $x_0$, a $C^1$ codimension-one hypersurface with normal covector $dR_\Omega(x_0)$.

In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime, $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$, the target equation is equivalent to
\[
\beta_\infty N(x)=\beta_\gamma,
\qquad
\beta_\gamma=\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}{\gamma}\right).
\]
In that regime $dR_\Omega(x_0)\neq0$ if and only if $dN(x_0)\neq0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The first assertion is the definition of the range. The local hypersurface statement is the regular level-set theorem applied to the $C^1$ function $R_\Omega$.

If $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ is constant, the equation
\[
\lambda_c^2\frac{\mathcal S(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\gamma
\]
is equivalent to
\[
e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}
=1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}{\gamma},
\]
which gives the displayed value of $\beta_\gamma$. Differentiating the homogeneous response function gives
\[
dR_\Omega
=
-\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)
\frac{\beta_\infty\Omega e^{\beta_\infty N\Omega}}
{(e^{\beta_\infty N\Omega}-1)^2}\,dN .
\]
The scalar prefactor is strictly negative on $U$, so $dR_\Omega(x_0)$ vanishes exactly when $dN(x_0)$ vanishes.
\end{proof}

The theorem below applies this lemma to the two record-selected target rates.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:shells-general-main}
Fix a detector design $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ in a static KMS background with lapse $N(x)>0$ everywhere in the region of interest, and assume the response input in \Cref{ass:kms-response-main}. Assume that $N$ and $x\mapsto\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ are $C^1$ on that region.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The ideal dead-time detector realizes exact symbolic thermality exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-phi-general-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\log\varphi
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the exact-clock locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface wherever the gradient of $x\mapsto\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau/(e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1)$ is nonvanishing; otherwise it may fail to be a manifold and we refer to it as the exact-clock locus.

\item The finite-recovery detector reaches the memory-transition point exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-mem-general-main}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the memory-transition locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface under the analogous nonvanishing-gradient condition. On $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For the ideal model, Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} says that exact symbolic thermality is equivalent to $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$, i.e. $R_\Omega(x)=\gamma_\varphi$ with the notation of~\eqref{eq:response-function-main}--\eqref{eq:target-rates-main}. Substituting the KMS response formula~\eqref{eq:kms-rate-main} gives~\eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main}. For finite recovery, Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery-main} and \Cref{lem:kernel-factorization-main} say that the memory transition is equivalent to $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, i.e. $R_\Omega(x)=\gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, and substitution gives~\eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main}. The regularity and possible nonexistence statements are then exactly \Cref{lem:response-locus-main} applied to these two target rates.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:shells-general-main} states the shell equations in their natural response-model form. The loci exist as level sets without any homogeneity assumption, but \Cref{lem:response-locus-main} shows exactly what must be checked before they are smooth hypersurfaces. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the defining functions reduce to pure functions of $N(x)$, and both loci are level sets of the lapse function.

Outside the homogeneous regime, the spectral-transport identity in \Cref{cor:spectral-transport-main} controls how the shell-ratio law is modified. The quantitative stability statement in Proposition~\ref{prop:spectral-stability-main} below gives the corresponding two-sided bound when the spectral inhomogeneity is uniformly small.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:homogeneous-main}
Assume that $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ on the region under study. Then both shells are pure lapse level sets. Their associated local inverse temperatures are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}
\beta_{\varphi}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-level-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\varphi}\},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
With $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$, the two shell equations contain $x$ only through $\beta_\infty N(x)$. Solving the exact-clock equation for $\beta_\infty N(x)$ gives $\beta_\varphi$, and solving the memory equation gives $\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Hence the loci are the lapse level sets in~\eqref{eq:lapse-level-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Homogeneous shell-equation ledger]\label{lem:homogeneous-denominators-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and let a spherical shell pair be
generated by the same field-facing detector core. Define
\[
\gamma_\varphi=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau},
\qquad
\gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\qquad
A=\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)>0,
\]
and introduce the Planck denominators at the two radii
\[
D_\varphi=e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi)}-1,
\qquad
D_{\mathrm{mem}}=e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1.
\]
At the exact-clock radius and memory-transition radius, the shell equations,
target-rate equations, and denominator identities are the following same two
statements:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.19\textwidth} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.25\textwidth} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.25\textwidth} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.19\textwidth}}
\toprule
Selector & Rate condition & Homogeneous shell equation & Denominator identity \\
\midrule
Exact clock & $\Gamma_+(r_\varphi)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ &
$A\Delta\tau/D_\varphi=\log\varphi$ &
$D_\varphi=A\Delta\tau/\log\varphi$ \\
Memory transition & $\Gamma_+(r_{\mathrm{mem}})=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ &
$A/D_{\mathrm{mem}}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ &
$D_{\mathrm{mem}}=A/\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Consequently, for the fixed denominator orientation
\[
Q_{\varphi/\mathrm{mem}}=\frac{D_\varphi}{D_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\]
one has
\begin{equation}\label{eq:homogeneous-denominator-ratio-main}
Q_{\varphi/\mathrm{mem}}
=\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
The reciprocal quotient
$\log\varphi/(\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau))$
corresponds to the different orientation $D_{\mathrm{mem}}/D_\varphi$.
Equivalently,
\[
\frac{\gamma_\varphi}{\gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\log\varphi}
{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
=
\frac{D_{\mathrm{mem}}}{D_\varphi}.
\]
Thus the factor $\Delta\tau$ belongs to the exact-clock denominator identity,
not to the memory-transition denominator identity; the ratio is obtained only
after those two identities are written in this orientation.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The two rate conditions are supplied by Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} and
\Cref{lem:one-dep-criterion-main}. Substituting the homogeneous KMS response
\[
\Gamma_+(r,\Omega)=\frac{A}{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r)}-1}
\]
gives the two homogeneous shell equations displayed in the ledger. Since
$A$, $\Delta\tau$, $\log\varphi$, $\Gamma_c$, $D_\varphi$, and
$D_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are positive, each shell equation can be multiplied through
without changing its content, giving the two denominator identities. Their
quotient in the stated orientation is
\[
\frac{D_\varphi}{D_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{A\Delta\tau/\log\varphi}
     {A/\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\]
The target-rate quotient has the opposite orientation,
\[
\frac{\gamma_\varphi}{\gamma_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\log\varphi/\Delta\tau}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}
=
\frac{D_{\mathrm{mem}}}{D_\varphi}.
\]
This is only algebraic division of the two shell equations. It uses no horizon
expansion, monotonicity of $N$, or inverse-problem argument.
\end{proof}

This elementary denominator lemma is the input to the amplitude-cancellation
identity in \Cref{thm:ratio-main}. It also fixes the orientation convention used
later: the main ratio is exact-clock denominator over memory-transition
denominator.

\begin{proposition}[Existence on monotone spherical regions]\label{prop:existence-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime in a static spherically symmetric region $r\in I$, and suppose $N:I\to N(I)$ is $C^1$ and strictly monotone. For either threshold $\star\in\{\varphi,\mathrm{mem}\}$, the corresponding shell exists in $I$ if and only if
\[
\frac{\beta_\star}{\beta_\infty}\in N(I),
\]
where $\beta_\varphi$ and $\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are defined in~\eqref{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}. When it exists, the shell radius is unique and equals
\[
r_\star=N^{-1}\!\left(\frac{\beta_\star}{\beta_\infty}\right).
\]
If $N'(r_\star)\neq0$, this radius defines a regular spherical hypersurface.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{cor:homogeneous-main}, the shell equation is exactly $\beta_\infty N(r)=\beta_\star$. Since $N$ is strictly monotone on $I$, it is one-to-one onto its range. Hence the equation has a solution precisely when $\beta_\star/\beta_\infty$ lies in $N(I)$, and the solution is then the displayed inverse image. The nonzero derivative condition is the one-dimensional form of the regular level-set condition.
\end{proof}

In this regime, the exact-clock shell is a Tolman isotherm selected by a grammar law, while the memory shell is a Tolman isotherm selected by a dependence law. The same local response scale therefore supports two inequivalent detector-selected hypersurfaces whenever the range condition in \Cref{prop:existence-main} is met. \Cref{fig:shell-radii} illustrates the two shell lapse values and their proper-distance ratio in Schwarzschild.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:order-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define
\[
\Gamma_{\varphi}=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}.
\]
Let $N_{\varphi}$ and $N_{\mathrm{mem}}$ denote the lapse values of $\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:order-main}
N_{\varphi}<N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_{\varphi}>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if $\Gamma_{\varphi}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. Thus the shell ordering is detector controlled.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Both shells are lapse level sets in the homogeneous spectral-weight regime. Writing $A=\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)>0$, the local excitation rate as a function of the lapse is
\[
\Gamma_+(N)=\frac{A}{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N}-1}.
\]
For $\beta_\infty,\Omega,N>0$,
\[
\frac{d\Gamma_+}{dN}
=
-A\,\frac{\beta_\infty\Omega e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N}}
{(e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N}-1)^2}<0 .
\]
Hence the higher threshold rate corresponds exactly to the smaller lapse value, giving~\eqref{eq:order-main} and the equality statement.
\end{proof}
