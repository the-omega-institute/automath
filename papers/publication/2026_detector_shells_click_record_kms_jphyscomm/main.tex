\begin{abstract}
We study a sampled two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector in a static KMS spacetime, keeping the full stationary binary click record rather than only the mean transition rate. The record carries observables that a rate summary discards: its allowed words, zero-run hazard, covariance profile, and counting noise. For ideal one-step dead time, the click process has the golden-mean grammar and realizes the Parry maximum-entropy law exactly at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$, defining an exact-clock shell. For exponential finite recovery, the visible process is instead a full-support renewal law with strictly increasing hazard; exact symbolic thermality is impossible, but a second shell appears where the hidden-state eigenvalue vanishes and the record is exact $1$-dependent. Embedding these two record thresholds in the KMS response formula gives detector-defined hypersurfaces. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime their shell pair obeys an exact common-amplitude cancellation law, which yields a near-horizon ratio rule and model-conditional inverse formulas in static spherical geometries. Thus full click records provide operational geometric selectors, through grammar and dependence thresholds, that are absent from rate-only detector thermometry.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} UDW detector, KMS state, static spacetime, symbolic dynamics, detector thermality

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates, detailed balance, and Tolman redshift are the standard observables used to characterize Unruh and Hawking thermality \cite{Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,Takagi1986,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. That summary is natural for thermality questions, but it is not what a sampled detector actually produces. Once the detector is read out at regular proper-time intervals, the experiment yields a binary click record with its own grammar, waiting-time structure, and lag correlations. Dead time and recovery therefore turn the detector into a stochastic-record probe rather than a mere rate meter. The basic physical question of this paper is whether those record-level observables can detect geometry in a static KMS background that the transition rate alone does not resolve.

This question matters for curved-spacetime detector theory because realistic counting devices are intrinsically temporal. A refractory interval or finite recovery law introduces serial dependence into the click record, and that dependence can survive even when the local mean rate is fixed. If such memory-sensitive observables lift cleanly to stationary KMS settings, then detector design ceases to be only a nuisance parameter and becomes part of the operational interface between click statistics and spacetime geometry.

This question is related to several active lines of investigation, but distinct from each of them. Repeated measurements of Unruh--DeWitt detectors and the reliability of master-equation descriptions have been revisited \cite{PranziniEtAl2024,HanEtAl2025}. Detector-based formulations of quantum measurements in QFT have sharpened the operational status of detector observables \cite{Fewster2020,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click,PercheLocalized2023}. The thermalization of particle detectors toward KMS states has been analysed in terms of asymptotic states and its reverse \cite{FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2022}. Detectors have been used to probe background geometry and topology, including hollow-shell detection and geon versus Schwarzschild discrimination \cite{NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020}. Metrological observables probe background structure via scalar functionals \cite{BraunsteinCaves1994,WangEtAl2014Metrology,HaoWu2016Unruh,DuMann2021AdS,FengZhang2022QFI,PercheMartinMartinez2022}. The methodology of waiting-time distributions, renewal statistics, spectral densities, Fano factors, and inverse counting statistics is well developed in mesoscopic transport \cite{Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014}. Our focus is more specific: we fix an explicit sampled UDW model, keep the full stationary click record, and ask which parts of its statistics become codimension-one geometric selectors. Relative to thermalization studies, we do not ask whether the detector state thermalizes, but which grammar and dependence thresholds pick out geometric loci. Relative to geometry probing, we exploit record statistics beyond rates. Relative to counting-statistics methodology, we embed the renewal framework in a curved-spacetime response model. To our knowledge, no previous work has used the full stationary click record of a sampled UDW detector to define detector-selected geometric loci in this way.

Dead time and finite recovery are not artificial mathematical constraints but intrinsic features of any real counting detector. In nuclear and photon counting, dead time has been a central design consideration since the earliest pulse detectors and remains part of modern single-photon detector modelling \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,Hadfield2009,EisamanEtAl2011}. Treating the Unruh--DeWitt detector as a dead-time counter therefore links the particle-detector formalism to a well-developed experimental framework. The observables exploited here---grammar, hazard, dependence class---are part of the distributional fingerprint of the sampled process. They are not, strictly speaking, independent of the local rate: once the full detector model is fixed, both the grammar threshold and the dependence threshold are functions of the same stationary excitation rate. What is new is that the full record selects special operational thresholds---the Parry maximum-entropy grammar level and the exact $1$-dependence transition---that are invisible to a rate-only summary of the same data. This places the present work in a different observable layer from the quantum-metrological studies cited above, which extract geometric information from scalar functionals of the detector state rather than from the full stochastic record.

The answer is not a mild refinement of rate thermometry. In the ideal one-step dead-time model, the sampled record has the golden-mean grammar: the word $11$ is forbidden. Exact symbolic thermality then means that the record realizes the Parry measure on that grammar, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy on the golden-mean shift \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. This selects an \emph{exact-clock shell}. For finite recovery the hidden detector remains two-state, but the visible record becomes a renewal process with infinite symbolic memory. There the relevant threshold is no longer entropic. At one distinguished rate the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent, and across that threshold the covariance sequence changes phase. This defines the \emph{memory-transition shell}. A central point of the paper is that finite recovery does not simply blur the ideal selector. It creates a second one.

Two further consequences sharpen the shell picture beyond a rephrased Tolman law. First, finite recovery approaches the ideal grammar only in a singular way. Consecutive-click violations are quadratically suppressed in the sampling interval, and fixed finite windows concentrate on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$. Yet exact symbolic thermality remains excluded at every positive sampling interval, because the hazard is strictly increasing. Second, the finite-recovery record is universally antibunched. Its low-frequency spectral density is suppressed, and the asymptotic counting Fano factor is strictly below one throughout the parameter range. These record-level signatures give the memory shell a direct operational meaning in finite counting data.

The geometric content emerges once the local detector rate is inserted. In a static KMS background, the ideal threshold and the memory threshold lift to two detector-defined hypersurfaces. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime, both become pure lapse level sets, and the shell pair satisfies an exact common-amplitude cancellation law. This yields an amplitude-light shell-pair observable, a universal near-horizon ratio law, and a model-conditional inverse principle in static spherical settings. Outside the homogeneous regime, the same shell pair obeys a spectral-transport identity, so that deviations from the homogeneous ratio law acquire a definite interpretation in terms of local spectral-weight variation.

The scope is deliberately controlled. We work with a single stationary worldline, a sampled two-level detector, weak coupling, discrete readout step $\Delta\tau$, and an exponential finite-recovery rule. The resulting shells are detector-defined hypersurfaces, not detector-independent invariants of the background. The inverse statements are likewise conditional on the response model and do not claim generic metric reconstruction. We also distinguish the present use of Tolman scaling in a fixed detector model from stronger notions of local thermal equilibrium in curved spacetime \cite{BuchholzSchlemmer2007,Solveen2012,BuchholzSolveen2013,GranseePinamontiVerch2017}. Finally, detailed balance is often used as a thermal diagnostic, but even this criterion can fail to characterize detector thermality once the detector structure is enlarged \cite{LimaEtAl2023Qudit}, which further motivates examining the full sampled record directly.

Detailed proofs are collected in the appendices, and short proof sketches are included in the main text alongside each major result. The logic of the main results is simple. The ideal threshold comes from matching the dead-time grammar to the entropy-maximizing Parry law on the golden-mean shift. The memory threshold comes from the vanishing of the hidden-state eigenvalue that transports serial dependence beyond one lag. The shell-pair inverse law comes from comparing two thresholds generated by the same hardware, so that the unknown common response amplitude cancels before any geometric inversion is attempted.

For ease of reference, the main claims may be summarized as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item Ideal dead time yields a golden-mean Markov law and a unique exact-clock threshold $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$.
\item Finite recovery yields a full-support renewal process with strictly increasing hazard, excludes exact symbolic thermality, and produces a second shell at the exact $1$-dependence threshold $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\item Finite recovery also has two robust operational signatures beyond the shell threshold itself: grammar violations are quadratically rare for small $\Delta\tau$, and the stationary record is strictly sub-Poissonian.
\item In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the shell pair obeys amplitude cancellation and detector-controlled ordering; outside that regime it obeys an exact spectral-transport identity.
\item Near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon the shell pair defines a universal hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales, and in static spherical settings it yields model-conditional inverse information.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is organized to keep that physics thread visible. \Cref{sec:operational,sec:records} define the record-level observables and derive the two detector thresholds together with their operational signatures. \Cref{sec:kms-shells,sec:inverse} then lift those thresholds to shell equations in static KMS backgrounds and extract the shell-pair ratio and inverse content. The appendices contain the complete derivations behind the closed-form formulas used in the main text.

\section{Operational setting and record-level observables}\label{sec:operational}

We consider a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector sampled at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. The visible output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ denotes a click in the $n$th sampling interval. Compressing this record to the empirical click frequency recovers only the one-point observable
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n.
\]
The full record, however, carries more. In particular, one may ask:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item what words belong to the support of the stationary law;
\item how the zero-run lengths between clicks are distributed;
\item whether the click hazard after a long run of zeros remembers the run length;
\item how the low-lag covariance profile behaves;
\item what counting noise remains after coarse time averaging.
\end{enumerate}
For later use, we fix some terminology. If a click is followed by $G$ consecutive zeros before the next click, then $G$ is the \emph{zero-run length}. Its hazard $h_k$ is the conditional probability that the next sample clicks after exactly $k$ zeros since the previous click. In the ideal model, \emph{exact symbolic thermality} means that the stationary record coincides with the Parry measure on the golden-mean grammar. In the finite-recovery model, \emph{exact $1$-dependence} means that blocks of samples separated by at least one intermediate time step are independent. These are the two record-level notions that later become shell conditions in a static KMS background.

These observables are invisible to a rate-only description, yet they are precisely the quantities that control the shell geometry developed below.

The two detector regimes studied in this work are illustrated schematically in \cref{fig:scheme}. The ideal model imposes a hard symbolic grammar, while finite recovery produces a full-support renewal law. The thresholds associated with the two regimes are therefore intrinsically different: one is entropic and grammar-based, the other is correlation-based.

\section{Sampled detector laws and operational signatures}\label{sec:records}

\subsection{Measurement protocol and click model}
We use an effective counter model driven by the UDW excitation rate. At the beginning of each proper-time interval $[n\Delta\tau,(n+1)\Delta\tau]$, the counter is in one of two hidden states: ready $R$ or dead/refractory $D$. If the counter is ready, a UDW excitation during the interval produces a click at the interval end and places the counter in $D$ for the next interval; if no excitation occurs, the output is $0$ and the counter remains ready. If the counter is refractory, an independent exponential recovery clock of rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ may return it to $R$ during the interval. A click is then registered only if recovery occurs and a UDW excitation follows before the interval end, in which case the next state is again $D$; if recovery occurs without a subsequent excitation, the next state is $R$, while no recovery leaves the counter in $D$. Integrating these alternatives over one sampling interval gives the output-labelled kernels $T_0$, $T_1$ below.

This protocol is phenomenological in the same sense as dead-time counter models \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975}: it captures the essential refractory structure of any counting device driven by the UDW excitation rate without deriving the measurement back-action from algebraic QFT first principles \cite{Fewster2020,PoloGomezEtAl2022}. The model is explicit: all subsequent grammar, hazard, covariance, and spectral-density results are derived from these kernels.

\subsection{Ideal one-step dead time and the exact-clock threshold}

In the ideal dead-time model, a click suppresses the next one-step interval. Writing
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-beta-main}
p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}, \qquad q_\beta=1-p_\beta,
\end{equation}
the sampled output is the stationary Markov chain on the golden-mean shift with transition matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-mean-main}
P_\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p_\beta & 1-p_\beta \\
 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The reference law on that shift is the Parry measure, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy compatible with the grammar $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ideal-main}
For the ideal dead-time detector, the sampled output realizes exact symbolic thermality if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-threshold-main}
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\end{equation}
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ is the golden ratio. This threshold defines the exact-clock selector.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
After a click the detector is refractory for the full next interval, so the block $11$ is forbidden: any consecutive-click sequence $(A_n,A_{n+1})=(1,1)$ has zero probability. After a zero, the detector is ready and clicks next with probability $q_\beta=1-p_\beta$. This yields transition matrix~\eqref{eq:golden-mean-main} with the single free parameter $p_\beta$. The Parry measure on the golden-mean shift is the unique stationary measure matching the Parry transition probabilities $P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1}$, $P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2}$, $P(1,0)=1$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. Matching gives $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$ and hence~\eqref{eq:parry-threshold-main}. Full derivations appear in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp-ideal}.
\end{proof}

The threshold in Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} should not be confused with a generic isotherm. It identifies the unique point at which a sampled detector record realizes the zero-jitter, maximum-entropy law compatible with the dead-time grammar. The resulting shell will therefore depend on the detector gap and on the sampling design even before curved-spacetime geometry is introduced.

\subsection{Three notions of detector thermality and the Parry benchmark}\label{subsec:thermality-notions}
Three distinct notions of thermality appear in UDW detector settings. \emph{Detailed-balance thermality}: the rate ratio satisfies $\Gamma_-/\Gamma_+=e^{-\beta\Omega}$, the standard criterion \cite{Unruh1976,BirDav1982}---a condition on the rate ratio alone. \emph{Gibbs thermalization}: the reduced detector state converges to $e^{-\beta H_{\mathrm{det}}}/Z$ \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2022}---a condition on the asymptotic state. \emph{Parry-law (maximum-entropy grammar) thermality}: the stationary click record realizes the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift, the unique measure of maximal entropy for the dead-time grammar $11\notin\mathrm{supp}\,\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}---a condition on the full word distribution. The third notion is what this paper studies. Neither the first nor the second implies the third or vice versa. We retain the name ``exact symbolic thermality'' because the Parry measure is the entropy-maximizing benchmark for its constraint class, just as the Gibbs measure is for the energy constraint; the physical content is that the detector click grammar is as disordered as the dead-time constraint permits. Readers may substitute ``Parry-law benchmark'' throughout without loss of meaning.

\subsection{Finite recovery, renewal structure, and exact \texorpdfstring{$1$}{1}-dependence}

A physically more realistic model allows the detector to recover over a finite time rather than instantaneously. Let $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$ be the recovery rate. Throughout this section and the remainder of the paper, we write $\Gamma\equiv\Gamma_+$ for the excitation rate when no confusion with other rates arises. Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-c-main}
p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}, \qquad c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-b-main}
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds,
\qquad
b=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\end{equation}
When $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, these integrals evaluate to
$a=[\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)]/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$
and
$b=\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$.
The visible click record is then a renewal process whose zero-run lengths are i.i.d.

The proof mechanism is physically simple and worth isolating. Every observed click resets the hidden detector state to the refractory state, so click times become regeneration times. That reset property is exactly what turns the visible law into a renewal process rather than a finite-order Markov chain. The threshold $\Gamma_c$ then appears when the nontrivial eigenvalue of the hidden two-state transition matrix vanishes, causing all dependence beyond one step to collapse at once.

\begin{unnumberedlemma}[Critical-rate uniqueness and covariance phase]
For fixed $\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau>0$, the equation $\lambda(\Gamma)=0$, with $\lambda$ defined by~\eqref{eq:lambda-main}, has exactly one positive solution. This solution is characterized by~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}. Moreover
\[
\lambda(\Gamma)>0\quad\text{for }0<\Gamma<\Gamma_c,
\qquad
\lambda(\Gamma)<0\quad\text{for }\Gamma>\Gamma_c .
\]
\end{unnumberedlemma}

\begin{proof}
Put $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $x=\Gamma/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. Away from $x=1$,
\[
\lambda(\Gamma)
=
\frac{e^{-s}-x e^{-sx}}{1-x}.
\]
Thus $\lambda=0$ is equivalent to
\[
H_s(x):=\log x-s(x-1)=0.
\]
The root $x=1$ is the removable root introduced by multiplying through the quotient; it corresponds to the equal-rate limit, where $\lambda=e^{-s}(1-s)$. Since $H_s''(x)=-x^{-2}<0$, the graph of $H_s$ is strictly concave. If $s<1$, then $H_s'(1)>0$ and the unique non-removable root lies in $(1,\infty)$; if $s>1$, then $H_s'(1)<0$ and the unique non-removable root lies in $(0,1)$; if $s=1$, the equal-rate value itself gives the unique critical point $\Gamma_c=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. Rewriting $H_s(x)=0$ in the original variables gives~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}. The sign statement follows from the same concavity picture together with the denominator $1-x$ in the displayed formula for $\lambda$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:recovery-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the zero-run length $G$ between successive clicks has law
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-main}
\PP(G=k)=g_k=e_D^{\top}T_0^kT_1\mathbf 1,
\qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t0-t1-main}
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 0 \\
 b & c
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
 0 & 1-p \\
 0 & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
If $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-closed-main}
g_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\end{equation}
and the click hazard after $k$ zeros is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard-main}
h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}
     {\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k},
\qquad k\ge0.
\end{equation}
The hazard is strictly increasing, so the visible process is not an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$.

Let $\rho=\PP(A_0=1)$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda-main}
\lambda=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}-\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}}
{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cov-main}
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},\qquad n\ge1.
\end{equation}
Consequently there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>0$ determined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-c-main}
\Delta\tau=
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma_c},
\end{equation}
such that the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$. Below $\Gamma_c$ all covariances are negative; above $\Gamma_c$ they alternate in sign.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Every click resets the hidden state to $D$ (refractory) deterministically, so click times are regeneration times and the zero-run lengths are i.i.d.; see Appendix~\ref{sec:supp-renewal}. Evaluating $e_D^\top T_0^k T_1\mathbf{1}$ using the lower-triangular structure of $T_0$ gives the closed form~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main}. The tail sum $\sum_{j\ge k}g_j=(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)/(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)$ is positive because it is a survival probability. Computing $h_{k+1}-h_k$ yields the positive quantity $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(p-c)^2p^kc^k/[(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^{k+1}-\Gamma c^{k+1})]$, confirming strict monotonicity. For the covariance: the nontrivial eigenvalue of the aggregate transition matrix $P=T_0+T_1$ is $\lambda=p-b$; two-state spectral decomposition gives~\eqref{eq:cov-main}. The critical-rate lemma gives the uniqueness of $\Gamma_c$ and the covariance sign change. The block factorization in \Cref{lem:kernel-factorization-main} then upgrades vanishing higher covariances to exact $1$-dependence.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Kernel factorization at the memory threshold]\label{lem:kernel-factorization-main}
At $\Gamma=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, let $\pi$ be the stationary row distribution of the aggregate hidden transition matrix $P=T_0+T_1$. Then
\[
P=\mathbf 1\,\pi .
\]
Consequently, for any two finite words $u$ and $v$ supported on time blocks separated by one unobserved sample,
\[
\PP(A_{-m}^{0}=u,\ A_2^{2+\ell}=v)
=
\PP(A_{-m}^{0}=u)\,\PP(A_2^{2+\ell}=v).
\]
Thus the visible output process is exact $1$-dependent at the memory threshold.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The condition $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$ is equivalent to $\lambda=p-b=0$. Since $P$ is a two-state stochastic matrix, its two eigenvalues are $1$ and $\lambda$; hence $\lambda=0$ makes $P$ rank one with every row equal to the stationary distribution $\pi$, i.e. $P=\mathbf 1\pi$. For output-labelled kernels, the probability of a past word $u$ and a future word $v$ separated by the unobserved sample at time $1$ has the form
\[
\pi T_u\,P\,T_v\mathbf 1
=
\pi T_u\,\mathbf 1\,\pi T_v\mathbf 1
=
\PP(A_{-m}^{0}=u)\,\PP(A_2^{2+\ell}=v),
\]
where $T_u$ and $T_v$ denote the ordered products of the labelled kernels for the two words. Cylinder events generate the corresponding past and future sigma algebras, so this factorization gives exact $1$-dependence.
\end{proof}

The memory-transition threshold is therefore not a softened version of the ideal one. It is a different record-level transition inside a full-support renewal class. Since the Bernoulli benchmark in that class would require constant hazard, exact symbolic thermality is excluded. \Cref{fig:hazard-cov} illustrates both the strictly increasing hazard and the covariance phase change across $\Gamma_c$.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:no-exact-main}
No finite-recovery detector with positive parameters $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ realizes exact symbolic thermality within the full-support renewal class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
In a full-support renewal process, the zero-jitter maximum-entropy benchmark has a geometric zero-run law, equivalently a constant click hazard. Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery-main} gives a strictly increasing hazard for every positive finite-recovery parameter choice, so the finite-recovery law cannot equal that benchmark.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Equal-rate limit and Lambert $W$ expression for $\Gamma_c$]\label{rem:edge-cases}
Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery-main} is stated for $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$. In the equal-rate limit $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\to\Gamma$, the formula~\eqref{eq:gap-closed-main} is $0/0$; l'H\^opital's rule in $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ yields
\[
g_k\big|_{\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma}
=
p^k\bigl[(1-p)+\Gamma\Delta\tau\bigl(k(1-p)-p\bigr)\bigr],
\]
which is a continuous function of $(\Gamma,\Delta\tau)$. The formulas for $a$, $b$, and $\lambda$ extend analogously to $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma$. The hazard remains strictly increasing in this limit by continuity of the positivity argument for $h_{k+1}-h_k$ in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp-renewal}.

The critical rate $\Gamma_c$ is defined implicitly by~\eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}. Setting $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $x=\Gamma_c/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, that equation is equivalent, for $x\neq1$, to
\[
\log x=s(x-1),
\qquad\text{or equivalently}\qquad
x e^{-sx}=e^{-s}.
\]
Thus
\[
(-sx)e^{-sx}=-s e^{-s},
\]
and the nontrivial solution is
\[
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
-\frac{1}{\Delta\tau}\,
W_k\!\left(-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\,e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}\right),
\]
where
\[
k=
\begin{cases}
-1, & 0<s<1,\\
0, & s>1.
\end{cases}
\]
The excluded branch gives $W(-s e^{-s})=-s$, hence the removable root $x=1$, which solves the multiplied equation but not the original quotient equation unless $s=1$. At $s=1$ both real branches meet at $W(-e^{-1})=-1$ and the unique solution is $\Gamma_c=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. The spectral weight $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ in the rate formula~\eqref{eq:kms-rate-main} is the even part of the commutator function, given by $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathrm{Im}\,\mathcal{W}_x(\Omega+i0^+)/\pi$ where $\mathcal{W}_x$ is the Fourier transform of the Wightman two-point function restricted to the static worldline at $x$; positivity $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ holds for $\Omega>0$ in any KMS state with a nontrivial spectrum \cite{KayWald1991}.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Soft approach to the ideal grammar}

The obstruction in Corollary~\ref{cor:no-exact-main} is exact, but the finite-recovery record still approaches the ideal grammar in a controlled small-$\Delta\tau$ regime. This is the sense in which finite recovery yields a \emph{soft} dead time.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:soft-main}
In the finite-recovery model,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soft-main}
\PP(A_{n+1}=1\mid A_n=1)=a
=\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2+O((\Delta\tau)^3)
\qquad (\Delta\tau\downarrow0).
\end{equation}
For a fixed window length $m\ge2$, let
\[
V_m=\{(a_0,\dots,a_{m-1})\in\{0,1\}^m:\exists\,k\le m-2,\ a_ka_{k+1}=11\}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:window-main}
\PP\!\bigl((A_0,\dots,A_{m-1})\in V_m\bigr)
\le
\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2\,(m-1)
+O\!\bigl(m(\Delta\tau)^3\bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus, for every fixed $m$, the finite-recovery record concentrates on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:soft-main} explains why the ideal grammar is a singular but physically meaningful limit. At fixed finite window length, inadmissible words become quadratically rare. Yet exact symbolic thermality is still excluded at any positive sampling interval because the hazard remains strictly increasing and hence genuinely remembers the zero-run length.

\subsection{Spectral density and universal antibunching}

The finite-recovery renewal law has a second operational consequence, independent of the shell geometry but directly relevant to finite counting data. Define the spectral density
\[
f(\theta)=\rho(1-\rho)+2\sum_{n\ge1}\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)\cos(n\theta),
\qquad \theta\in[-\pi,\pi].
\]
Since the covariance sequence is explicit, so are the low-frequency noise properties.

This antibunching statement is not automatic. Finite recovery suppresses immediate reclicks, but it could in principle still generate longer-range positive correlations and enhanced low-frequency noise. The explicit covariance formula shows that the opposite happens: the reset mechanism suppresses low-frequency fluctuations throughout the parameter range. Once the covariance tail is geometric, both the spectral density and the Fano factor follow from summing the resulting series.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectrum-main}
For the stationary finite-recovery record,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectrum-main}
f(\theta)=
\rho(1-\rho)
+
2\rho(a-\rho)\,
\frac{\cos\theta-\lambda}{1-2\lambda\cos\theta+\lambda^2},
\end{equation}
and $f$ is strictly increasing on $[0,\pi]$. If
\[
N_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n,
\]
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fano-main}
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{\Var(N_N)}{\EE N_N}
=
1-\rho+\frac{2(a-\rho)}{1-\lambda}
<1.
\end{equation}
Hence the finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range.
\end{proposition}

Operationally, the memory shell can therefore be located by more than the sign of one covariance. As one scans through stationary observers, the lag profile changes phase at exact $1$-dependence, while the whole renewal class remains antibunched and low-frequency suppressed. This is a robust signature in finite counting data and not merely a formal property of an idealized symbolic process. \Cref{fig:spectrum-fano} displays both the spectral density and the Fano factor across the parameter range.

\subsection{Finite-sample observability}\label{subsec:finite-sample}
The shell conditions are operationally meaningful only if the relevant observables can be estimated from finite data. Given a binary record $(A_0,\dots,A_{N-1})$, define the \emph{empirical hazard} $\widehat{h}_k=\#\{\text{runs of exactly }k\text{ zeros}\}/\#\{\text{runs of at least }k\text{ zeros}\}$, the \emph{empirical lag-$n$ covariance} $\widehat{\gamma}_n=(N-n)^{-1}\sum_{k}A_kA_{k+n}-\widehat\rho^2$, and the \emph{empirical Fano factor} $\widehat F=\widehat{\Var}(N_B)/\widehat{\mathbb E}N_B$ for block size $B$. By the renewal theorem and the ergodic theorem, all three converge a.s.\ to their population counterparts.

For the memory shell, the diagnostic is the sign of $\widehat\gamma_2$: it changes at $\Gamma_c$. Because the zero-run law has exponential tails, the finite-recovery record is a geometrically ergodic regenerative process. Standard regenerative central-limit theorems \cite{Asmussen2003} therefore give
\[
\sqrt N\,(\widehat\gamma_2-\gamma_2)
\Rightarrow
\mathcal N(0,\sigma_2^2),
\]
with a finite asymptotic variance $\sigma_2^2$ determined by the covariance series of the block observable entering $\widehat\gamma_2$. Thus the detectable separation from the memory shell scales as $N^{-1/2}$ once the local detector parameters are fixed. The often useful estimate $N\sim10^4$ clicks for a signal-to-noise ratio of order three should be read as a heuristic benchmark for typical parameters, not as a universal sample-size theorem; a design-specific confidence interval requires the corresponding regenerative variance or a Monte Carlo calibration.

For the exact-clock shell, estimate $\widehat p=\#\{(A_n,A_{n+1})=(0,0)\}/\#\{A_n=0\}$ and test $\widehat p=\varphi^{-1}$ by a chi-squared test on the $2\times2$ transition table; the required sample size at separation $\delta=|p-\varphi^{-1}|$ is $N\sim\delta^{-2}/\rho$.

\section{Static KMS backgrounds and shell equations}\label{sec:kms-shells}

Having established the record-level thresholds, we now embed the detector in a static KMS background and derive the corresponding shell equations.

The shell construction is conceptually straightforward but physically nontrivial. The constants $\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ are fixed by record statistics before geometry is introduced. Embedding the detector in a static KMS background then turns those record-level thresholds into geometric hypersurfaces by substituting the local excitation rate into the threshold equations. What results is not a rephrased Tolman law, but a detector-dependent lift of record statistics to geometry.

Let $N(x)$ be the lapse function of a static spacetime and assume the field is in a global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state for Killing time. Along a static worldline at position $x$, Tolman scaling gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-main}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
At weak coupling, the excitation rate of a detector with energy gap $\Omega$ takes the Planck form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-rate-main}
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ denotes the local commutator spectral weight \cite{KayWald1991}. This spectral weight is an input to the response model. The homogeneous regime considered below is an explicit idealization in which its spatial variation is negligible over the static region of interest.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:shells-general-main}
Fix a detector design $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ in a static KMS background with $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ and lapse $N(x)>0$ everywhere in the region of interest. Assume that $N$ and $x\mapsto\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ are $C^1$ on that region.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The ideal dead-time detector realizes exact symbolic thermality exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-phi-general-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\log\varphi
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the exact-clock locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface wherever the gradient of $x\mapsto\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau/(e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1)$ is nonvanishing; otherwise it may fail to be a manifold and we refer to it as the exact-clock locus.

\item The finite-recovery detector reaches the memory-transition point exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-mem-general-main}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
This is the memory-transition locus. It is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface under the analogous nonvanishing-gradient condition. On $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For the ideal model, Proposition~\ref{prop:ideal-main} says that exact symbolic thermality is equivalent to $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. Substituting the KMS response formula~\eqref{eq:kms-rate-main} gives~\eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main}. For finite recovery, Proposition~\ref{prop:recovery-main} and \Cref{lem:kernel-factorization-main} say that the memory transition is equivalent to $\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, and substitution gives~\eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main}. Since the defining functions are $C^1$ under the stated hypotheses, the regular level-set theorem gives a smooth codimension-one hypersurface at every point where the corresponding gradient is nonzero.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:shells-general-main} states the shell equations in their natural response-model form. The loci exist without any homogeneity assumption; regularity of their geometry depends on whether the defining function has nonvanishing gradient, which holds in generic static backgrounds. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the defining functions reduce to pure functions of $N(x)$, and both loci are level sets of the lapse function---smooth hypersurfaces under the standard condition that $dN\neq0$.

Outside the homogeneous regime, the spectral-transport identity~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main} controls how the shell-ratio law is modified. As a concrete error bound: if $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)=\mathcal S(\Omega)(1+\varepsilon(x))$ with $|\varepsilon|\le\varepsilon_0$, then the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main} receives a correction bounded by $O(\varepsilon_0)$ in the shell-ratio constant, and the shell positions shift by at most $O(\varepsilon_0)$ in lapse value relative to their homogeneous locations.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:homogeneous-main}
Assume that $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ on the region under study. Then both shells are pure lapse level sets. Their associated local inverse temperatures are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}
\beta_{\varphi}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-level-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\varphi}\},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
With $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$, the two shell equations contain $x$ only through $\beta_\infty N(x)$. Solving the exact-clock equation for $\beta_\infty N(x)$ gives $\beta_\varphi$, and solving the memory equation gives $\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Hence the loci are the lapse level sets in~\eqref{eq:lapse-level-main}.
\end{proof}

In this regime, the exact-clock shell is a Tolman isotherm selected by a grammar law, while the memory shell is a Tolman isotherm selected by a dependence law. The same local response scale therefore supports two inequivalent detector-selected hypersurfaces. \Cref{fig:shell-radii} illustrates the two shell lapse values and their proper-distance ratio in Schwarzschild.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:order-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define
\[
\Gamma_{\varphi}=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}.
\]
Let $N_{\varphi}$ and $N_{\mathrm{mem}}$ denote the lapse values of $\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:order-main}
N_{\varphi}<N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_{\varphi}>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if $\Gamma_{\varphi}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. Thus the shell ordering is detector controlled.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Both shells are lapse level sets in the homogeneous spectral-weight regime, and the Planck rate $\Gamma_+(N)$ is strictly decreasing in $N$; hence the higher threshold rate corresponds exactly to the smaller lapse value.
\end{proof}

\section{Shell-pair observables and inverse content}\label{sec:inverse}

We now examine the joint observables carried by the shell pair and their inverse content.

\subsection{Common-amplitude cancellation and spectral transport}

The most useful shell-pair observable arises when both shells are generated with the same hardware design.

The key point is that the same hardware also carries the same unknown response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ into both shell equations. Dividing the two equations therefore removes precisely the nuisance factor that is hardest to calibrate experimentally. This is why the shell pair contains more robust inverse information than either shell separately.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:ratio-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and let a fixed detector design generate an exact-clock shell at $r_{\varphi}$ and a memory-transition shell at $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in a static spherically symmetric background with lapse $N(r)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
The common multiplicative response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels exactly.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
At the exact-clock shell,
\[
\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)
=
\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}
\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_\varphi)}-1\bigr),
\]
while at the memory-transition shell,
\[
\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)
=
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\bigl(e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1\bigr).
\]
The left-hand sides are the same because the same detector design and the same homogeneous spectral weight are used at both shells. Equating them and rearranging gives~\eqref{eq:ratio-main}.
\end{proof}

The cancellation in Theorem~\ref{thm:ratio-main} is precise but not detector free. The remaining right-hand side still depends on the detector gap, the sampling step, and the recovery rate through $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. What cancels is the unknown common response amplitude---precisely the form of cancellation needed for inverse use with shared hardware.

The homogeneous ratio law is the simplest specialization of a more general identity.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:spectral-transport-main}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:shells-general-main}, let $x_{\varphi}$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$ be points on the exact-clock and memory-transition shells for the same detector design. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-transport-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_{\varphi}}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}
Hence deviations from the homogeneous ratio law directly measure spectral transport between the two shell points.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Use the general shell equations~\eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main} and~\eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main} at $x_\varphi$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$, respectively. Dividing the exact-clock equation by the memory-transition equation cancels $\lambda_c^2$ but not the position-dependent spectral weights, giving~\eqref{eq:spectral-transport-main}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Near-horizon universal hierarchy}

The shell-pair ratio becomes especially sharp near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:horizon-main}
Assume the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:ratio-main}. Suppose, in addition, that the static region ends at a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-main}
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)
\end{equation}
as the proper distance $\rho\downarrow0$. Then the shell distances satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\varphi}}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
In Schwarzschild, if $A_H=16\pi M^2$ is the horizon area and $A_{\varphi}$ and $A_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are the areas of the shell spheres, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:area-ratio-main}
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left[
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\right]^2
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
For a nonextremal horizon, the Gaussian normal coordinate $\rho$ satisfies $N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)$, so $e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(\rho)}-1=2\pi\Omega\rho(1+O(\rho))$. Inserting both shell points into the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main} and cancelling the common $2\pi\Omega$ factor gives~\eqref{eq:rho-ratio-main} to leading order. In Schwarzschild, $\rho$ is not the areal-radius increment: with $r_H=2M$,
\[
\rho=2\sqrt{r_H(r-r_H)}+O((r-r_H)^{3/2}),
\qquad
r-r_H=\frac{\rho^2}{4r_H}+O(\rho^4).
\]
Therefore
\[
A-A_H=4\pi(r^2-r_H^2)=2\pi\rho^2+O(\rho^4),
\]
and the area excess ratio equals the squared proper-distance ratio at leading order. Full details, including the error control, appear in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp-kms}.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:horizon-main} shows that the shell pair acts as a detector-dependent stretched-horizon ruler. One shell is defined by exact symbolic thermality, the other by exact $1$-dependence, yet their near-horizon separation is governed by a universal leading-order ratio that is independent of the common response amplitude.

A particularly informative regime is the fast-sampling limit. Let $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\downarrow0$ and $L=\log(1/s)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-main}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\rho_{\varphi}}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus the memory-transition shell collapses toward the horizon parametrically faster than the exact-clock shell. The shell pair therefore resolves a genuine hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales rather than two nearby realizations of the same thermal profile.

\subsection{Static spherical inversion}

The shell pair also carries direct inverse content in static spherical settings.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:schw-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define the detector constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-constants-main}
\beta_{\varphi}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right).
\end{equation}
If the two shells generated by the same detector design occur at distinct radii $r_{\varphi}\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in Schwarzschild, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-infty-main}
\beta_\infty^2=
\frac{\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_{\varphi}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mass-main}
M=
\frac{r_{\varphi}r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)}
{2\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In Schwarzschild,
\[
N(r)^2=1-\frac{2M}{r}.
\]
The two homogeneous shell equations are
\[
\frac{\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2}{\beta_\infty^2}=1-\frac{2M}{r_\varphi},
\qquad
\frac{\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2}{\beta_\infty^2}=1-\frac{2M}{r_{\mathrm{mem}}}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
\beta_\infty^2-\frac{2M\beta_\infty^2}{r_\varphi}=\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2,
\qquad
\beta_\infty^2-\frac{2M\beta_\infty^2}{r_{\mathrm{mem}}}=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2.
\]
This is a nonsingular linear system for $\beta_\infty^2$ and $2M\beta_\infty^2$ when $r_\varphi\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Solving it gives~\eqref{eq:beta-infty-main} and~\eqref{eq:mass-main}.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:schw-main} is exact algebra within the response model. Its significance is not that it bypasses detector design, but that two record-defined shell locations already determine the global redshift scale once the design is fixed.

The same logic extends beyond Schwarzschild in a local, self-calibrating form.

The proof idea is again operational. Each detector mode supplies one shell-ratio equation, and two genuinely distinct modes provide two constraints for the two background parameters. The Jacobian condition below is simply the statement that those two constraints are locally independent, so the shell data can be inverted without separately calibrating the common response amplitude.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:two-mode-main}
Let $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ parametrize a $C^1$ family of static spherically symmetric backgrounds, with parameter $\theta\in\Theta$ determining both the lapse $N(r;\theta)$ and the global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty(\theta)$. Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime at fixed gap $\Omega$ and fixed hardware prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.

Fix one detector hardware design $(\Omega,\lambda_c)$ and operate it in two modes
\[
D_m=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m}),
\qquad m=1,2,
\]
with observed shell radii $r_{\varphi,m}$ and $r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}$. Define the known constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-constants-main}
C_m=\frac{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}{\log\varphi},
\qquad m=1,2,
\end{equation}
and the shell-ratio functions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-functions-main}
F_m(\theta)=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1}
-C_m.
\end{equation}
If $\theta_*\in\Theta$ satisfies $F_1(\theta_*)=F_2(\theta_*)=0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main}
\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0,
\end{equation}
then the two shell pairs determine $\theta$ locally uniquely near $\theta_*$. In particular, the reconstruction is self calibrating: no prior knowledge of $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is required.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Each mode $D_m$ contributes one equation $F_m(\theta)=0$ by the ratio law~\eqref{eq:ratio-main}. Both functions are $C^1$ in $\theta$ under the stated regularity of $N$ and $\beta_\infty$, and the common amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels from each $F_m$ separately. Thus $F=(F_1,F_2)$ is a $C^1$ map from the two background parameters to two shell-ratio residuals. If $DF(\theta_*)$ is invertible, the inverse function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002} gives a neighbourhood $U$ of $\theta_*$ on which $F$ is one-to-one. Since $F(\theta_*)=0$, no other parameter in $U$ fits the same two shell pairs. The amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ does not appear in the $F_m$, confirming self-calibration. Full details appear in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp-kms}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:jacobian-main}
The Jacobian condition~\eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main} fails in particular when the two operating modes generate shell pairs with the same ratio $C_1=C_2$. Since $C_m$ depends on $(\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m})$, this is a codimension-one condition in the mode parameter space and is generically avoided by choosing modes with distinct sampling intervals.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The central result of this work is that stationary click records carry operational information beyond a rate-only summary. To be precise: the full record does not produce a geometric observable independent of the local excitation rate, because both thresholds are ultimately functions of the same rate. What the full record does is select two special operational benchmarks---the Parry maximum-entropy grammar level and the exact $1$-dependence transition---that are not visible from the rate alone. In the model studied here, those benchmarks organize themselves into two inequivalent codimension-one selectors in a static KMS background. The exact-clock shell is singled out by a grammar law, namely realization of the Parry measure in the ideal dead-time model. The memory shell is singled out by a dependence law, namely exact $1$-dependence in the finite-recovery renewal model. The distinction is not semantic. It marks two different statistical universality classes of sampled thermality.

Two observations sharpen this picture. The first is the singular soft-dead-time limit. Finite recovery approximates the ideal grammar on fixed finite windows with explicit $O((\Delta\tau)^2)$ control. However, exact symbolic thermality is never reached at any positive sampling interval, because the hazard retains monotone memory. The second is universal antibunching. The finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range, with suppressed low-frequency noise. The memory shell is therefore not merely a point where one covariance changes sign. It sits inside a broader renewal class with definite counting statistics.

On the geometric side, the most robust object is the shell pair rather than either shell in isolation. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime, the pair is governed by exact common-amplitude cancellation and therefore yields a detector-dependent but amplitude-light observable. Near a nonextremal horizon, the ratio law produces a universal hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales. In static spherical settings, it yields local inverse content. Outside the homogeneous regime, the same shell pair continues to be meaningful through the spectral-transport identity. Inhomogeneous response does not invalidate the construction; rather, it changes the interpretation of shell-ratio data from pure lapse information to mixed lapse and spectral-weight information.

Several structural features of the construction are expected to persist beyond the specific model studied here, though we emphasize that the following are conjectural extensions not proved in this paper. We conjecture that the renewal structure and the existence of a memory-transition threshold generalize to any recovery kernel for which a click deterministically resets the hidden state: under that condition click times are regeneration times and the covariance structure is again determined by the eigenvalue spectrum of the hidden-state matrix, suggesting that a memory-transition locus exists whenever that spectrum has a zero. What changes with the recovery kernel is the quantitative location of the threshold and the detailed hazard profile. We further conjecture that the qualitative obstruction to exact symbolic thermality---the strictly increasing hazard---holds whenever the recovery density has full support on $\mathbb R_+$, not only in the exponential case; the exponential model is the one for which we have a complete proof via the closed-form eigenvalue formula. These conjectural extensions require separate proofs and are left as directions for future work.

What is not conjectural is the spectral-transport identity (\cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}): it is an exact algebraic consequence of the shell equations and does not rely on the homogeneous spectral-weight assumption. In particular, greybody corrections in Schwarzschild enter through a calculable spectral-weight ratio $\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)/\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)$ rather than as an uncontrolled perturbation.

The relationship to prior literature can be stated precisely. Detector thermalization theory \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2022} asks whether the detector state converges to a Gibbs state; we ask instead which grammar and dependence thresholds the stationary record realizes. Operational detector-measurement theory \cite{PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click,PercheLocalized2023} establishes the physical basis for detector outputs; we take that framework as given and analyze the resulting stochastic process. Detector-based geometry probing \cite{NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020} extracts geometric information from rate observables; we additionally exploit the grammar and covariance structure of the full record. Counting-statistics methodology \cite{Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014} provides the renewal, spectral-density, and Fano-factor tools, but those works do not embed the framework in curved-spacetime response theory. The novelty here is in combining all four ingredients: a concrete sampled UDW model, record-level observables, static KMS response theory, and shell-pair inverse content.

The limitations are equally clear. We have fixed a weak-coupling response model, a two-level detector, a single stationary worldline, discrete readout, and exponential recovery. Switching effects, nonexponential recovery, paralyzable counting models, multi-level detectors, greybody corrections, backreaction, and full finite-sample inference are outside the present scope \cite{Carmichael1993Book,WisemanMilburn2009}. The shell equations should therefore be read as exact within the stated detector-response class and as a template for more elaborate detector models rather than as universal geometric invariants.

Within that scope, however, the construction is internally complete. The full stationary click record supplies two codimension-one shells, a soft but singular route from finite recovery to the ideal grammar, universal antibunching in the renewal regime, an amplitude-cancelling shell-pair ruler, a near-horizon hierarchy law, and a model-conditional inverse principle. Taken together, these results demonstrate a concrete gravity-facing application of full-record detector statistics in quantum field theory in curved spacetime.
