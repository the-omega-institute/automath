\begin{abstract}
We study a sampled two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector in a static KMS spacetime, keeping the stationary binary click record rather than only its mean transition rate. The record supplies two readout-selected benchmarks. With ideal one-step dead time, the output is the golden-mean Markov chain and realizes the Parry maximum-entropy law exactly when $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$, giving an exact-clock selector. With exponential finite recovery, the visible law has full support and strictly increasing zero-run hazard, so the Parry benchmark is excluded; instead the distinguished point is the unique rate at which the hidden-state eigenvalue vanishes and the visible record becomes exact $1$-dependent. Substituting a specified weak-coupling response model lifts these two rates to detector-defined response level sets; KMS detailed balance alone fixes only the thermal rate ratio and leaves the positive one-gap spectral weight as input. In the calibrated homogeneous spectral-weight regime their pair obeys an exact common-amplitude cancellation law, yielding a dimensionless small-gap near-horizon ratio rule and a local two-ratio inverse criterion in static spherical geometries. Thus the shells are conditional response-level loci after the readout and response models are fixed, but the selected constants come from full-record grammar and dependence laws rather than from rate thermometry alone.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} UDW detector, KMS state, static spacetime, symbolic dynamics, detector thermality

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates, detailed balance, and Tolman redshift are the standard observables used to characterize Unruh and Hawking thermality \cite{Tolman1930,TolmanEhrenfest1930,Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,Takagi1986,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. That summary is natural for thermality questions, but it is not what a sampled detector actually produces. Once the detector is read out at regular proper-time intervals, the experiment yields a binary click record with its own grammar, waiting-time structure, and lag correlations. Dead time and recovery therefore turn the detector into a stochastic-record probe rather than a mere rate meter. The basic physical question of this paper is whether those record-level laws, after the absolute response has been specified, select response-model loci in a static KMS background that would not be singled out by rate thermometry without the readout model.

This question matters for curved-spacetime detector theory because realistic counting devices are intrinsically temporal. A refractory interval or finite recovery law introduces serial dependence into the click record, and that dependence can survive even when the local mean rate is fixed. If such memory-sensitive observables lift cleanly after a stationary KMS response model has been specified, then detector design ceases to be only a nuisance parameter and becomes part of the operational interface between click statistics and spacetime geometry.

This question is related to several active lines of investigation, but distinct from each of them. Repeated measurements of Unruh--DeWitt detectors and the reliability of master-equation descriptions have been revisited \cite{PranziniEtAl2024,HanEtAl2025}. Detector-based formulations of quantum measurements in QFT have sharpened the operational status of detector observables \cite{Fewster2020,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click,PercheLocalized2023}. The thermalization of particle detectors toward KMS states has been analysed in terms of asymptotic states and its reverse \cite{FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2022}. Detectors have been used to probe background geometry and topology, including hollow-shell detection and geon versus Schwarzschild discrimination \cite{NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020}. Metrological observables probe background structure via scalar functionals \cite{BraunsteinCaves1994,WangEtAl2014Metrology,HaoWu2016Unruh,DuMann2021AdS,FengZhang2022QFI,PercheMartinMartinez2022}. The methodology of waiting-time distributions, renewal statistics, spectral densities, Fano factors, and inverse counting statistics is well developed in mesoscopic transport \cite{Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014}. Our focus is more specific: we fix an explicit sampled UDW model, keep the full stationary click record, and ask which parts of its statistics become codimension-one response selectors after a response model has been fixed. Relative to thermalization studies, we do not ask whether the detector state thermalizes, but which grammar and dependence thresholds pick out response loci. Relative to geometry probing, we exploit record statistics beyond rates. Relative to counting-statistics methodology, we embed the renewal framework in a curved-spacetime response model. To our knowledge, no previous work has used the full stationary click record of a sampled UDW detector to define detector-selected response loci in this way.

Dead time and finite recovery are not artificial mathematical constraints but intrinsic features of any real counting detector. In nuclear and photon counting, dead time has been a central design consideration since the earliest pulse detectors and remains part of modern single-photon detector modelling \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,Hadfield2009,EisamanEtAl2011}. Treating the Unruh--DeWitt detector as a dead-time counter therefore links the particle-detector formalism to a well-developed experimental framework. The observables exploited here---grammar, hazard, dependence class---are part of the distributional fingerprint of the sampled process. They are not, strictly speaking, independent of the local rate: once the full detector model is fixed, both the grammar threshold and the dependence threshold are functions of the same stationary excitation rate. The contribution is to keep these record observables rather than replacing them immediately by a mean rate, and to show how their selected rate values lift after a response model is specified. This places the present work in a different observable layer from the quantum-metrological studies cited above, which extract geometric information from scalar functionals of the detector state rather than from the full stochastic record.

At the record level, the two counter models lead to different benchmarks. In the ideal one-step dead-time model, the sampled record has the golden-mean grammar: the word $11$ is forbidden. The Parry benchmark then means that the record realizes the Parry measure on that grammar, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy on the golden-mean shift \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. The Perron--Frobenius matching step is the standard Parry calculation once this Markov chain has been identified; its role here is to fix the detector clock value $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. For finite recovery the hidden detector remains two-state, but the visible record becomes a renewal process with infinite symbolic memory. There the relevant threshold is no longer entropic. At one distinguished rate the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent, and across that threshold the covariance sequence changes phase. This defines the \emph{memory-transition shell}. The main stochastic distinction is that finite recovery does not merely perturb the ideal Parry selector; it produces a separate dependence threshold inside a full-support renewal law.

Two further consequences sharpen the shell picture beyond a rephrased Tolman law. First, finite recovery approaches the ideal grammar only in a singular way. Consecutive-click violations are quadratically suppressed in the sampling interval, and fixed finite windows concentrate on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$. Yet the Parry benchmark remains excluded at every positive sampling interval, because the hazard is strictly increasing. Second, the finite-recovery record is universally antibunched. Its low-frequency spectral density is suppressed, and the asymptotic counting Fano factor is strictly below one throughout the parameter range. These record-level signatures give the memory shell a direct operational meaning in finite counting data.

The response-model geometric content emerges only after the local detector rate has been specified. A static KMS condition gives the Tolman temperature and detailed balance, but it does not determine the absolute positive-frequency response at one fixed gap. With an independently specified response spectral weight, the ideal threshold and the memory threshold lift to detector-defined hypersurfaces wherever the resulting level sets are regular. In the calibrated homogeneous spectral-weight regime, both become pure lapse level sets, and the shell pair satisfies an exact common-amplitude cancellation law. This yields an amplitude-cancelling shell-pair observable, a near-horizon ratio law for small-shell families with $\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, and a local two-ratio inverse test in static spherical settings. Outside the homogeneous regime, the same shell pair obeys a spectral-transport identity, so that deviations from the homogeneous ratio law acquire a definite interpretation in terms of local spectral-weight variation.

The scope is deliberately controlled. We work with a single stationary worldline, a sampled two-level detector, weak coupling, discrete readout step $\Delta\tau$, and an exponential finite-recovery rule. The resulting shells are detector-defined hypersurfaces, not detector-independent invariants of the background. The ratio identifiability statements are likewise conditional on the response model and do not claim generic metric reconstruction or an inverse from arbitrary nearby four-radius records. We also distinguish the present use of Tolman scaling in a fixed detector model from stronger notions of local thermal equilibrium in curved spacetime \cite{BuchholzSchlemmer2007,Solveen2012,BuchholzSolveen2013,GranseePinamontiVerch2017}. Finally, detailed balance is often used as a thermal diagnostic, but even this criterion can fail to characterize detector thermality once the detector structure is enlarged \cite{LimaEtAl2023Qudit}, which further motivates examining the full sampled record directly.

The main text carries the complete theorem chain. The appendix is supplementary: it records edge-case formulas, finite-counting identities, and coordinate expansions that support the displayed formulas without repeating the main proofs. The logic of the main results is simple. The ideal threshold comes from matching the dead-time grammar to the entropy-maximizing Parry law on the golden-mean shift. The memory threshold comes from the vanishing of the hidden-state eigenvalue that transports serial dependence beyond one lag. The shell-pair ratio test comes from comparing two thresholds generated by a common detector core, so that the unknown common response amplitude cancels before any geometric inversion is attempted.

The novelty is therefore not uniform across the theorem chain. Once the ideal dead-time Markov chain has been written down, its Parry point is the standard Perron-vector calculation for the golden-mean shift; the detector-specific contribution is the identification of that point with the clock setting $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ and, after response calibration, with an exact-clock locus. The finite-recovery threshold is structurally different. It is not an entropy maximizer or a fit to one covariance: it is the unique zero of the hidden eigenmode, and the labelled-kernel factorization at that zero gives an exact if-and-only-if criterion for $1$-dependence. The later shell, ratio, horizon, and inverse statements are algebraic lifts of these record-selected constants under the response-model hypotheses stated with each result. Thus the paper's main new stochastic content lies in the finite-recovery renewal calculation and exact-dependence criterion; the ideal Parry result is a standard benchmark used to anchor the comparison.

For ease of reference, the main claims may be summarized as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item Ideal dead time yields a golden-mean Markov law and a unique exact-clock threshold $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$.
\item Finite recovery yields a full-support renewal process with strictly increasing hazard, excludes the Parry benchmark, and produces a second shell at the exact $1$-dependence threshold $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\item Finite recovery also has two robust operational signatures beyond the shell threshold itself: grammar violations are quadratically rare for small $\Delta\tau$, and the stationary record is strictly sub-Poissonian.
\item In the calibrated homogeneous spectral-weight regime the shell pair obeys amplitude cancellation and detector-controlled ordering; outside that regime it obeys an exact spectral-transport identity.
\item Under the small-shell response-model hypotheses near a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, with fixed gap or more generally $\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, the shell pair defines a leading hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales; in static spherical settings it yields model-conditional two-ratio inverse information subject to admissibility checks.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Operational setting and record-level observables}\label{sec:operational}

We consider a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector sampled at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. The visible output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ denotes a click in the $n$th sampling interval. Compressing this record to the empirical click frequency recovers only the one-point observable
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n.
\]
The full record, however, carries more. In particular, one may ask:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item what words belong to the support of the stationary law;
\item how the zero-run lengths between clicks are distributed;
\item whether the click hazard after a long run of zeros remembers the run length;
\item how the low-lag covariance profile behaves;
\item what counting noise remains after coarse time averaging.
\end{enumerate}
\begin{definition}[Record benchmarks]\label{def:record-benchmarks-main}
For a stationary binary record $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb Z}$, a \emph{zero-run length} is the number $G$ of consecutive zeros following a click and preceding the next click. If the zero-run law has full support, its hazard is
\[
h_k=\PP(G=k\mid G\ge k),\qquad k\ge0 .
\]
The ideal record is on the \emph{Parry benchmark} when its stationary law is the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift, i.e. the unique measure of maximal entropy subject to the grammar constraint $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$. A stationary record is \emph{exact $1$-dependent} when, for every $m$, the sigma algebras generated by $\{A_j:j\le m\}$ and $\{A_j:j\ge m+2\}$ are independent.
\end{definition}

\begin{assumption}[Homogeneous spectral-weight regime]\label{ass:homogeneous-spectral-main}
When the static response regularity and calibration of \Cref{ass:kms-response-main} are imposed at a fixed gap $\Omega$ on a region $U$, the homogeneous spectral-weight regime means
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)>0\qquad(x\in U).
\]
This is a calibration hypothesis on the response model, not a consequence of KMS detailed balance. In shell-pair statements it is used together with a common field-facing detector core, so that the same positive response amplitude $\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ enters the exact-clock and memory-transition source equations.
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}[Common-core shell data]\label{def:common-core-shell-data-main}
A shell pair is \emph{common-core} when the two records use the same field-facing detector core: gap, coupling scale, detector profile, and field state are fixed, while the counter mode is changed from ideal one-step blanking to exponential finite recovery. A two-mode radius record is a \emph{full common-core shell record} only when the undivided shell equations recover one positive common response amplitude for all exact-clock and memory-transition members and the four radii lie in the allowed exterior coordinate range of the background family. The weaker ratio equations alone give only ratio-admissible data.
\end{definition}

These observables are not themselves rate observables, yet in the explicit readout models below they reduce to sharp thresholds in the excitation rate. That reduction is important: the geometry enters only after the stochastic-record benchmark has already fixed the target rate.

We also separate the field-facing detector core from the classical counter mode. The core consists of the gap $\Omega$, coupling scale $\lambda_c$, detector profile, field state, and stationary excitation rate supplied by the UDW response. The counter mode is the readout layer: ideal one-step blanking for the Parry selector, or exponential recovery with rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ for the memory selector. The two selectors are therefore obtained from two operating modes of the same field-facing core, not from a single record that is simultaneously ideal and finite-recovery. The shell-pair cancellation results require this shared core and a common response amplitude at the two shell points; they do not identify the ideal and finite-recovery records as the same stochastic law.

It is useful to keep the construction in three logically separate steps.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item A counter model determines a stationary word law and a record-level benchmark: the Parry law in the ideal case, exact $1$-dependence in the finite-recovery case.
\item For the two models studied here, that benchmark is equivalent to a scalar rate condition, respectively $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\item Substitution of the specified static KMS response then turns those rate conditions into level-set equations for the detector response.
\end{enumerate}
Thus the shells are not claimed to be new detector-independent geometric invariants, nor are they derived from static KMS detailed balance alone. They are response-model level sets whose constants are selected by full-record statistics. This ordering of the argument is important: detailed balance supplies the thermal rate ratio, the readout law supplies the record benchmark, the positive spectral weight supplies the remaining one-gap response datum, and only their combination supplies the shell equation.

The two detector regimes studied in this work are illustrated schematically in \cref{fig:scheme}. The ideal model imposes a hard symbolic grammar, while finite recovery produces a full-support renewal law. The thresholds associated with the two regimes are therefore intrinsically different: one is entropic and grammar-based, the other is correlation-based.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The central result of this work is that stationary click records carry operational information beyond a rate-only summary. To be precise: the full record does not produce a geometric observable independent of the local excitation rate, because both thresholds are ultimately functions of the same rate. Nor does static KMS thermality by itself fix the absolute rate: the positive one-gap spectral weight must be supplied by the response model or calibration. What the full record does is select operational benchmarks---the Parry maximum-entropy grammar level and the exact $1$-dependence transition---that a rate-only summary would not single out without the readout law. These benchmarks do not have equal mathematical weight. The ideal benchmark is the standard Parry point of the golden-mean chain after ideal blanking has imposed the grammar; the finite-recovery benchmark is the stronger renewal-process result, where exact $1$-dependence is equivalent to the vanishing of the hidden eigenmode. Once the response function is calibrated, the two benchmarks become two response-level selectors: the exact-clock locus is tied to a grammar law, and the memory locus is tied to a dependence law.

Two observations sharpen this picture. The first is the singular soft-dead-time limit. Finite recovery approximates the ideal grammar on fixed finite windows with explicit $O((\Delta\tau)^2)$ control. However, the Parry benchmark is never reached at any positive sampling interval, because the hazard retains monotone memory. The second is universal antibunching. The finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range, with suppressed low-frequency noise. The memory shell is therefore not merely a point where one covariance changes sign. It sits inside a broader renewal class with definite counting statistics.

On the geometric side, the most robust object is the shell pair rather than either shell in isolation. In the calibrated homogeneous spectral-weight regime, the pair is governed by exact common-amplitude cancellation and therefore yields a detector-dependent but amplitude-cancelling observable. For shell pairs explicitly tending to a nonextremal horizon under the small-shell hypotheses, and with $\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$ when the gap varies, the ratio law produces a leading hierarchy of stretched-horizon scales. In static spherical settings, it yields local two-ratio inverse information; full shell-data compatibility still requires the undivided amplitude and exterior-range checks. Outside the homogeneous regime, the same shell pair continues to be meaningful through the spectral-transport identity. Inhomogeneous response does not invalidate the construction; rather, it changes the interpretation of shell-ratio data from pure lapse information to mixed lapse and spectral-weight information.

Several structural features of the construction are expected to persist beyond the specific model studied here, but the following are only future-work conjectures and are not used in any theorem above. We conjecture that the renewal structure and the existence of a memory-transition threshold generalize to any recovery kernel for which a click deterministically resets the hidden state: under that condition click times are regeneration times and the covariance structure is again determined by the eigenvalue spectrum of the hidden-state matrix, suggesting that a memory-transition locus exists whenever that spectrum has a zero. What changes with the recovery kernel is the quantitative location of the threshold and the detailed hazard profile. We further conjecture that the qualitative obstruction to the Parry benchmark---the strictly increasing hazard---holds whenever the recovery density has full support on $\mathbb R_+$, not only in the exponential case; the exponential model is the one for which we have a complete proof via the closed-form eigenvalue formula. These conjectural extensions require separate proofs and are left as directions for future work.

What is not conjectural is the spectral-transport identity (\cref{cor:spectral-transport-main}): it is an exact algebraic consequence of the shell equations and does not rely on the homogeneous spectral-weight assumption. In particular, greybody corrections in Schwarzschild enter through a calculable spectral-weight ratio $\mathcal S_{x_\varphi}(\Omega)/\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)$ rather than as an uncontrolled perturbation.

The relationship to prior literature can be stated precisely. Detector thermalization theory \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2022} asks whether the detector state converges to a Gibbs state; we ask instead which grammar and dependence thresholds the stationary record realizes. Operational detector-measurement theory \cite{PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click,PercheLocalized2023} establishes the physical basis for detector outputs; we take that framework as given and analyze the resulting stochastic process. Detector-based geometry probing \cite{NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020} extracts geometric information from rate observables; we additionally exploit the grammar and covariance structure of the full record. Counting-statistics methodology \cite{Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014} provides the renewal, spectral-density, and Fano-factor tools. The specific contribution here is the combination of a sampled UDW counter model with the exact finite-recovery dependence threshold, followed by a response-conditional lift to shell-pair ratio and identifiability statements. The ideal Parry threshold remains the standard symbolic-dynamics benchmark against which the finite-recovery result is compared.

The limitations are equally clear. We have fixed a weak-coupling response model, a two-level detector, a single stationary worldline, discrete readout, and exponential recovery. Switching effects, nonexponential recovery, paralyzable counting models, multi-level detectors, greybody corrections, backreaction, and full finite-sample inference are outside the present scope \cite{Carmichael1993Book,WisemanMilburn2009}. The shell equations should therefore be read as exact within the stated detector-response class and as a template for more elaborate detector models rather than as universal geometric invariants. The word ``shell'' always refers to a response-model level set; when the spectral weight is not homogeneous, that level set combines lapse and local spectral information rather than geometry alone.

Within that scope, however, the construction is internally complete. The full stationary click record supplies two conditional codimension-one response loci under regularity hypotheses, a soft but singular route from finite recovery to the ideal grammar, universal antibunching in the renewal regime, an amplitude-cancelling shell-pair ruler, a dimensionless small-gap near-horizon hierarchy law, and a model-conditional two-ratio inverse test with separate full-data admissibility checks. Taken together, these results demonstrate a concrete gravity-facing application of full-record detector statistics in quantum field theory in curved spacetime.
