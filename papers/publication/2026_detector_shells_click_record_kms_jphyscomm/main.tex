\begin{abstract}
We study a classical sampled counter whose binary law is fixed at the instrument level and only later calibrated by the stationary excitation rate of a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector core in a static KMS spacetime, keeping the stationary binary record rather than only the mean excitation rate. The main new content is the exact analysis of two record-level selectors for one explicit sampled counter model. With ideal one-step dead time, the counter output is the golden-mean Markov chain and realizes the Parry law exactly when $\Gamma\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$, giving an exact-clock selector. With the sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset model, the zero-run law has full support on $\mathbb N_0$, hence every finite binary word has positive probability, and the zero-run hazard is strictly increasing, so the Parry benchmark is excluded; instead the distinguished point is the unique attempt rate at which the hidden-state eigenvalue vanishes and the visible record becomes exact $1$-dependent. Section~3 writes the contrasting continuous intra-bin recovery kernel explicitly and shows that the theorem chain uses a different boundary-reset coarse graining. This stochastic law is not derived from UDW dynamics or from a repeated-measurement limit of the detector: the paper fixes a sampled classical interval instrument with independent Poisson attempts and fresh interval ancillas, and introduces the response-facing object only after the modelling closure $\Gamma=\Gamma_+$, calling the result a classical sampled counter with stationary UDW rate calibration. In Sections~4--5 we then fix a stationary weak-coupling response function
\[
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\widehat W_x(\Omega)
=
\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\]
and record the conditional consequences of substituting the selected rates into this specified response profile. KMS detailed balance fixes only the thermal ratio $\Gamma_-/\Gamma_+$; the positive one-gap spectral weight $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ remains calibration input, so the shell and inverse statements are conditional response-model level-set statements rather than new detector-theoretic consequences of KMS alone. In the calibrated homogeneous regime we obtain the common-amplitude cancellation law, an exact small-shell near-horizon collar ratio, and, only under the additional dimensionless small-gap condition $\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, its linear proper-distance hierarchy, together with a fixed-radius-ratio local inverse and sensitivity branch for the abstract map $\theta\mapsto\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta)$. Exact full fixed-setting shell data still correspond only to the physical target value $C$, and the full fixed-radius shell equations remain as separate amplitude-admissibility checks.
The homogeneous regime is exhibited exactly at the amplitude level in a translation-invariant thermal model with rigidly translated detector smearing; because that ultrastatic symmetry example has constant lapse, the later nonconstant-lapse homogeneous shell geometry is used only as an abstract calibrated response-level specialization and not as a further exact UDW construction proved here. The same amplitude regime is recovered asymptotically whenever $\sup|d\log \mathcal S_x(\Omega)|$ times the shell separation tends to zero.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} UDW detector, KMS state, static spacetime, symbolic dynamics, detector thermality

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates, detailed balance, and Tolman redshift are the standard observables used to characterize Unruh and Hawking thermality \cite{Tolman1930,TolmanEhrenfest1930,Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,Takagi1986,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. A sampled readout, however, produces a binary time series rather than a rate alone. Once a field-facing detector core is monitored at regular proper-time intervals by a classical counter, the experiment yields a record with its own grammar, waiting-time law, and lag correlations. The question of this paper is whether exact record-level thresholds of that sampled output, once a detector response profile has been fixed, select response-model loci that a rate-only summary would not single out. The response-facing construction is a UDW-calibrated classical sampled counter: a classical sampled interval instrument whose attempt rate is identified with the stationary UDW excitation rate only through the explicit closure $\Gamma=\Gamma_+$.

The answer has two layers. The main stochastic result is in one explicit sampled counter model. Ideal one-step blanking gives the golden-mean Markov chain, with Parry benchmark exactly at $\Gamma\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. By contrast, the sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset model gives a full-support renewal process whose zero-run hazard is strictly increasing and whose visible record becomes exact $1$-dependent exactly when the hidden eigenvalue vanishes, at the unique rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. This is the theorem-level contribution: the memory threshold is not an entropy benchmark but an exact dependence transition inside a renewal class that still supports every finite binary word.

After those record thresholds have been fixed, Sections~4--5 substitute them into a calibrated stationary UDW response function
\[
R_\Omega(x)=\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}.
\]
This second layer is conditional response-model algebra, not a derivation of shell geometry from one-gap KMS data alone. In the calibrated homogeneous regime the shell pair obeys exact common-amplitude cancellation, which yields an amplitude-free denominator ratio, an exact near-horizon collar ratio for small-shell families, and, only under the further small-gap condition $\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$, a linear proper-distance hierarchy and a fixed-radius ratio local inverse and sensitivity branch for the abstract ratio map. Exact full fixed-setting shell data still occur only at the detector-selected target ratio. Secondary modelling and calibration qualifications are consolidated in the hypothesis map of Table~\ref{tab:hypothesis-ledger}; the prose below keeps only the distinctions needed to identify where the new mathematics enters.

This problem sits near several active lines of work but is narrower than any of them. Repeated measurements of Unruh--DeWitt detectors and the reliability of master-equation descriptions have been revisited \cite{PranziniEtAl2024,HanEtAl2025}. Earlier detector-statistics work already studied click rates and detector-click observables \cite{LoukoSatz2006,TjoaEtAl2023click}, while higher-order detector correlations were analysed through coherence functions \cite{AnastopoulosSavvidou2012}. Detector-based QFT measurement theory has sharpened the operational meaning of detector observables \cite{Fewster2020,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,PoloGomezEtAl2022,PercheLocalized2023}, and detector thermalization toward KMS states has been analysed in terms of asymptotic detector states \cite{FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2022}. Detectors have also been used to probe geometry and topology \cite{NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020}, while metrological observables extract background information from scalar detector functionals \cite{BraunsteinCaves1994,WangEtAl2014Metrology,HaoWu2016Unruh,DuMann2021AdS,FengZhang2022QFI,PercheMartinMartinez2022}. Our focus is narrower: we fix one classical sampled interval instrument, pass to a UDW-calibrated classical sampled counter only through the explicit closure $\Gamma=\Gamma_+$, and ask which exact statistics of the resulting binary stationary record become codimension-one response selectors once a response model has been fixed.

Dead time and recovery are also physically natural. In nuclear and photon counting, refractory effects have been central since the earliest pulse detectors and remain standard in modern single-photon models \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,Hadfield2009,EisamanEtAl2011}. The observables used here---grammar, hazard, dependence class, low-frequency noise---therefore belong to the familiar counting-statistics fingerprint of a sampled detector output. Their role is not to replace the local rate, but to show that keeping the full record produces exact thresholds that can later be lifted to response-model level sets.

The two counter modes lead to genuinely different benchmarks. In the ideal model, the word $11$ is forbidden and the benchmark is the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. In the sampled boundary-reset model, the zero-run law has full support on $\mathbb N_0$, hence every finite binary word is allowed, and the relevant threshold is instead the exact-$1$-dependence point. Two further record-level consequences make this distinction operational: the sampled boundary-reset law approaches the ideal grammar only singularly, with consecutive-click violations quadratically suppressed as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$, and the stationary record is universally antibunched, with suppressed low-frequency spectral density and sub-Poisson asymptotic counting noise.

The logic of the theorem chain is therefore simple. The ideal threshold comes from matching the dead-time grammar to the Parry law on the golden-mean shift. The memory threshold comes from the vanishing of the hidden-state eigenvalue that transports serial dependence. The shell-pair ratio test comes from comparing two thresholds generated by a common detector core, so that the common response amplitude cancels before any inverse step is attempted. The appendix is supplementary: it records edge-case formulas, finite-counting identities, coordinate expansions, and the ambient radius-bookkeeping statements that are not inverse theorems from nearby shell records.

For ease of reference, the main claims may be summarized as follows.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item Ideal dead time yields a golden-mean Markov law and a unique exact-clock threshold $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$.
\item The sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset model yields a renewal process whose zero-run law has full support on $\mathbb N_0$ and hence supports every finite binary word, has strictly increasing hazard, excludes the Parry benchmark, and produces a second shell at the exact $1$-dependence threshold $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\item That sampled boundary-reset model also has two robust operational signatures beyond the shell threshold itself: grammar violations are quadratically rare for small $\Delta\tau$, and the stationary record is strictly sub-Poissonian.
\item After a response function has been fixed, the calibrated homogeneous spectral-weight regime gives amplitude cancellation and detector-controlled ordering; outside that regime the shell pair obeys an exact spectral-transport identity.
\item Under the additional small-shell and inverse-problem hypotheses, the same conditional shell pair yields an exact near-horizon collar ratio and, only in the dimensionless small-gap regime, a linear hierarchy together with a fixed-radius ratio local inverse and sensitivity branch for $\theta\mapsto\mathcal R_{\eta_*}(\theta)$. The exact fixed-setting shell interpretation remains at the detector-selected target ratio, with the common-amplitude checks kept in the main theorem and any ambient shell-record bookkeeping deferred to the appendix.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Operational setting and record-level observables}\label{sec:operational}

We consider a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector sampled at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. The visible output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ denotes a click in the $n$th sampling interval. Compressing this record to the empirical click frequency recovers only the one-point observable
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n.
\]
The full record, however, carries more. In particular, one may ask:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\alph*)},leftmargin=2.0em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item what words belong to the support of the stationary law;
\item how the zero-run lengths between clicks are distributed;
\item whether the click hazard after a long run of zeros remembers the run length;
\item how the low-lag covariance profile behaves;
\item what counting noise remains after coarse time averaging.
\end{enumerate}
\begin{definition}[Record benchmarks]\label{def:record-benchmarks-main}
For a stationary binary record $(A_n)_{n\in\mathbb Z}$, a \emph{zero-run length} is the number $G$ of consecutive zeros following a click and preceding the next click. If the zero-run law has full support, its hazard is
\[
h_k=\PP(G=k\mid G\ge k),\qquad k\ge0 .
\]
The ideal record is on the \emph{Parry benchmark} when its stationary law is the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift, i.e. the unique measure of maximal entropy subject to the grammar constraint $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$. A stationary record is \emph{exact $1$-dependent} when, for every $m$, the sigma algebras generated by $\{A_j:j\le m\}$ and $\{A_j:j\ge m+2\}$ are independent.
Equivalently, one leaves the intermediate sample at time $m+1$ unobserved: a block ending at time $m$ is tested for independence from a block beginning at time $m+2$, not conditioned on the middle sample.
\end{definition}

\begin{assumption}[Homogeneous spectral-weight regime]\label{ass:homogeneous-spectral-main}
When the static response regularity and calibration of \Cref{ass:kms-response-main} are imposed at a fixed gap $\Omega$ on a region $U$, the homogeneous spectral-weight regime means
\[
\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)>0\qquad(x\in U).
\]
This is a calibration hypothesis on the response model, not a consequence of KMS detailed balance. In shell-pair statements it is used together with a common field-facing detector core, so that the same positive response amplitude $\lambda_{\mathrm{sw}}^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ enters the ideal-benchmark and memory-transition source equations.
It holds exactly at the amplitude level in the symmetry model of \Cref{prop:symmetry-homogeneous-main}, although that ultrastatic example has constant lapse and hence no nontrivial shell geometry by itself. Accordingly, the later nonconstant-lapse homogeneous shell arguments are used only as calibrated level-set algebra for a fixed response function, while \Cref{prop:spectral-stability-main,cor:asymptotic-horizon-main} record the slow-variation substitute used when the shell pair is only asymptotically homogeneous.
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}[Common-core shell data]\label{def:common-core-shell-data-main}
A shell pair is \emph{common-core} when the two records use the same field-facing detector core: gap, coupling scale, detector profile, and field state are fixed, while the counter mode is changed from ideal one-step blanking to the sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset mode. A two-mode radius record is a \emph{full common-core shell record} only when the undivided shell equations recover one positive common response amplitude for all ideal-benchmark and memory-transition members and the four radii lie in the allowed exterior coordinate range of the background family. The weaker ratio equations alone give only ratio-admissible data.
\end{definition}

These observables are not themselves rate observables, yet in the explicit readout models below they reduce to sharp thresholds in the excitation rate. That reduction is important: the geometry enters only after the stochastic-record benchmark has already fixed the target rate.

We also separate the field-facing detector core from the classical counter mode. The core consists of the gap $\Omega$, coupling scale $\lambda_c$, detector profile, field state, and stationary excitation rate supplied by the UDW response. The counter mode is the readout layer: ideal one-step blanking for the Parry selector, or sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary reset at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ for the memory selector. The two selectors are therefore obtained from two operating modes of the same field-facing core, not from a single record that is simultaneously ideal and sampled-boundary-reset. The shell-pair cancellation results require this shared core and a common response amplitude at the two shell points; they do not identify the ideal and sampled boundary-reset records as the same stochastic law.

It is useful to keep the construction in three logically separate steps.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.2em]
\item A counter model determines a stationary word law and a record-level benchmark: the Parry law in the ideal case, exact $1$-dependence in the sampled boundary-reset case.
\item For the two models studied here, that benchmark is equivalent to a scalar rate condition, respectively $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ and $\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\item Substitution of the specified static KMS response then turns those rate conditions into level-set equations for the detector response.
\end{enumerate}
Thus the shells are not claimed to be new detector-independent geometric invariants, nor are they derived from static KMS detailed balance alone. They are response-model level sets whose constants are selected by full-record statistics. This ordering of the argument is important: detailed balance supplies the thermal rate ratio, the readout law supplies the record benchmark, the positive spectral weight supplies the remaining one-gap response datum, and only their combination supplies the shell equation.

The two detector regimes studied in this work are illustrated schematically in \cref{fig:scheme}. The ideal model imposes a hard symbolic grammar, while the sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset model produces a renewal law whose zero-run distribution has full support and hence allows every finite binary word. The thresholds associated with the two regimes are therefore intrinsically different: one is entropic and grammar-based, the other is correlation-based.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The main mathematical contribution is the sampled boundary-reset recovery theorem: one explicit sampled counter law produces a full-support renewal record with strictly increasing hazard, universal antibunching, and a unique exact-$1$-dependence threshold at the zero of the hidden eigenmode. The ideal Parry point remains the benchmark comparison. Table~\ref{tab:hypothesis-ledger} collects the modelling and calibration hypotheses that control how these record-selected constants are later lifted to response loci, so the discussion here does not repeat them.

Two operational consequences matter most. First, the sampled boundary-reset law approaches the ideal grammar only singularly: consecutive-click violations are $O((\Delta\tau)^2)$ on fixed windows, yet the Parry benchmark is never attained at positive sampling interval because the hazard remains strictly increasing. Second, the same renewal law is strictly sub-Poissonian across the parameter range, so the memory transition sits inside a broader class with definite low-frequency counting statistics and is not merely a sign change of one covariance.

On the response side, the robust object is the shell pair. In the calibrated homogeneous regime it obeys exact common-amplitude cancellation, giving a detector-dependent but amplitude-free denominator ratio. Near a nonextremal horizon, the primary statement is the exact collar ratio; the proper-distance and area hierarchies appear only after the additional small-gap linearization $\Omega(t)\epsilon(t)\to0$. In static spherical families, the same shell pair yields a fixed-radius ratio local inverse and sensitivity branch for the abstract ratio map, while exact full fixed-setting shell data remain tied to the detector-selected target value and the separate common-amplitude and exterior-admissibility checks kept in the main theorem. Outside the homogeneous regime, the exact spectral-transport identity remains available and interprets shell-ratio deviations as spectral-weight transport.

This places the paper relative to earlier work as follows. Thermalization studies \cite{GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2022} ask for Gibbs convergence of the detector state; here the observable is the stationary record. Earlier click and detector-statistics analyses \cite{LoukoSatz2006,TjoaEtAl2023click,AnastopoulosSavvidou2012} supply the direct output-statistics comparators for the finite-record claims, while detector-based measurement theory \cite{Fewster2020,PoloGomezEtAl2022,PercheLocalized2023,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,Oeckl2025CausalMeasurement} supplies the direct local-measurement comparators for the inverse and admissibility claims. The increment claimed here is therefore narrower than any general theory in those directions: it is the exact sampled-record selector together with its lift to calibrated-response shell equations, not a general theory of quantum trajectories, stationary motions, local temperature, or QFT measurement.

The scope remains specific: weak coupling, a two-level detector, one stationary worldline, discrete readout, and the sampled one-recovery-opportunity boundary-reset rule. Within that scope the construction is complete. The full stationary click record yields two response-model loci under explicit regularity hypotheses, a soft but singular route toward the ideal grammar, universal antibunching, an amplitude-cancelling shell-pair ruler, an exact small-shell near-horizon collar ratio with a further small-gap hierarchy, and a fixed-radius ratio inverse criterion with the amplitude-admissibility checks kept explicit.
