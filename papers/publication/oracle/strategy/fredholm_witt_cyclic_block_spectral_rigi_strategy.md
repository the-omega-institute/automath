## 1. Root cause of rejection
The referee is reacting to a structural lack of novelty: the paper openly frames itself as a “note” that simply realises prescribed Euler products via familiar normal trace-class blocks (main.tex:53) and concedes that symbolic dynamics appears only as motivation plus a classical appendix (main.tex:79). The introduction further labels both the determinant–Euler-product identity and the spectral rigidity inputs as classical and positions the work merely as a tidy packaging of those ingredients (sec_introduction.tex:55-57). With Theorem 3.3 amounting to a clean restatement of Simon + normal spectral theory, the manuscript never demonstrates an essential new mechanism, so the editor sees polish without substance.

## 2. Options
1. **Deep mathematical rewrite** – Prove a genuinely new rigidity theorem beyond the normal case (for example, classify all trace-class realizations sharing the same determinant or exhibit an obstruction in the non-normal category) so that Theorem 3.3 becomes one step inside a larger result. Subtasks: isolate a concrete gap (non-normal models, determinant-intrinsic invariants, or symbolic dynamical consequence), develop and vet the new theorem, rebuild Section 3 around it, and incorporate the notation/terminology fixes (M1–M4). Effort: 120–160 hours of focused work plus fresh computations. Probability of acceptance after change: ~55% at the current venue because a substantial new result would directly neutralize B1/B2. Downside risk: high — the needed breakthrough may simply not exist, and failure would waste months while still leaving the note underpowered.  
2. **Scope reduction (short note)** – Embrace the “tidy synthesis” label, compress the manuscript to the cyclic-block statement plus determinant consequences, and pitch it explicitly as a short technical note. Subtasks: drop most of Sections 2/appendices, merge Lemma 3.9/Theorem 3.11 into a single proposition, add a novelty map subsection, and fix the terminology inconsistencies (M1–M5). Effort: 35–45 hours (editing + rewriting the introduction/abstract). Probability of acceptance: ~30% because even as a short note the core result still looks derivative, but a lean presentation might convince a less demanding associate editor. Downside risk: if the journal insists on new theorems, you still receive a fast reject after burning revision time.  
3. **Venue change (expository/operator journal)** – Retarget the paper to outlets that welcome well-packaged operator-theoretic notes (e.g., *Integral Equations and Operator Theory* short notes section or *Operators and Matrices*). Subtasks: reframe the abstract/introduction to foreground expository value, highlight connections to symbolic dynamics audiences, trim redundant lemmas, and address the medium-severity terminology issues so reviewers see polish. Effort: 25–30 hours. Probability of acceptance at the new venue: ~60% provided the editors accept synthesis papers. Downside risk: lower prestige and possible desk rejection if the editors still expect a nontrivial new theorem despite the venue shift.  
4. **Scope split (two papers)** – Separate the operator classification from the symbolic-dynamics motivation: one concise operator note proving the cyclic-block realization, and a companion expository article on finite-state determinants. Subtasks: define clean boundaries between results, rewrite introductions so each paper has a distinct contribution, and ensure cross-referencing without redundancy. Effort: 70–90 hours. Probability of acceptance: ~35% overall (operator note might pass at a short-note venue; the symbolic piece could land in a dynamics-focused expository journal). Downside risk: doubles editorial overhead while neither part gains enough new mathematics, so both tracks might still be rejected.

## 3. Recommendation
Pursue **Option 1 (Deep mathematical rewrite)** despite the effort. The editor’s objection is fundamentally about missing substance, and even the manuscript’s own framing underlines that weakness; trimming or rebranding cannot change that perception. A bona fide extension—say, a determinant-intrinsic classification that works without normality or a symbolic-dynamical consequence that truly needs Theorem 3.3—directly resolves blocker B1 and lets us retain the current venue’s prestige. The other options merely repackage the same core and therefore keep the fatal novelty gap intact.

## 4. If chosen, the first 3 concrete actions
1. Decide on a specific strengthening target (e.g., extend the cyclic-block rigidity to non-normal trace-class operators or prove a symbolic-dynamics obstruction that fails without the block theorem) and write a one-page feasibility memo testing known counterexamples.  
2. Build a minimal working proof outline for the new statement, identifying which classical tools need enhancement (Simon’s theorem variants, perturbation theory, etc.) and what new lemmas must be developed.  
3. Prototype the revised Section 3 skeleton showing how the new theorem, its corollaries, and the existing cyclic-block result interlock, so later writing becomes filling in details rather than refactoring structure.

## 5. Red flags
- The sought-after stronger theorem may simply be false; without preliminary evidence, weeks of work could end with a counterexample and no publishable story.  
- Even with a new result, medium issues (terminology around reciprocal determinants, range invariants, redundancy) must be fixed in parallel; leaving them unfixed will give editors an easy reason to bounce the resubmission.  
- If we cannot articulate a crisp “what is classical vs new” subsection (sec_introduction.tex already admits almost everything is classical), reviewers will presume the new claims are cosmetic and reject again.
