But the draft still looks unfinished in ways that matter editorially. The introduction contains unresolved citation placeholders like [?, ?, ...]. Later sections contain unresolved cross-references such as ?? illustrates, Theorem ??, Section ??, and missing figure/equation references in exactly the parts that are supposed to sell the paper’s main results. That is a serious professionalism problem for submission.

The abstract is also overcompressed. It reads like a dense theorem bulletin rather than a physics communication. For this journal, the abstract needs to do less listing and more signaling: what observable is new, what physical question it answers, what assumptions are essential, and why a curved-spacetime detector theorist should care. Right now, even though the abstract is accurate, it is carrying too many claims at once.

5. Impact

Moderate and specialized. I do not see this becoming a widely cited paper across general physics. I do see it being cited by a focused set of communities: QFT in curved spacetime, Unruh–DeWitt detector theory, detector-based measurement in curved backgrounds, repeated-measurement and counting-detector models, and a smaller group interested in symbolic/stochastic descriptions of detector outputs. The paper’s strongest citation hooks are the move from transition-rate thermality to full-record thermality, the separation between the exact-clock shell and memory-transition shell, the universal antibunching statement, and the shell-pair inverse law.

The impact ceiling is limited by how model-conditional the results are. The paper itself is explicit that it fixes weak coupling, a two-level detector, a single stationary worldline, discrete readout, exponential recovery, and, for some of the sharpest geometric statements, a homogeneous spectral-weight regime. Those are reasonable modeling choices, but they constrain how broadly later authors will treat the results as foundational rather than model-specific.
