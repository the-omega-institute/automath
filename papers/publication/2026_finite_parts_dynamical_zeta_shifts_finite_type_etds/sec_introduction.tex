\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

The dynamical \(\zeta\)-function of a mixing subshift of finite type
\((X_A^+,\sigma)\) with primitive adjacency matrix \(A\) is the
rational function
\[
  \zeta_A(z)
  =\exp\!\left(\sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{\Tr(A^n)}{n}z^n\right)
  =\det(I-zA)^{-1}.
\]
Since Bowen--Lanford \cite{BowenLanford1970Zeta} and Manning
\cite{Manning1971Axiom}, the analytic properties of \(\zeta_A\) have
been central to the orbit-counting theory of symbolic dynamics, with
further development through the thermodynamic formalism of Ruelle
\cite{Ruelle1976ZetaExpanding,Ruelle1978Thermodynamic} and the
transfer-operator programme of Parry--Pollicott
\cite{ParryPollicott1990Zeta}. The spectral theory of the associated
Ruelle operator underlies both the prime orbit theorem and equidistribution
results for periodic orbits in hyperbolic dynamics
\cite{Baladi2000PositiveTransfer,ParryPollicott1990Zeta}.

The Perron root \(\lambda=\rho(A)\) gives \(\zeta_A\) a simple pole at
\(z_\ast=\lambda^{-1}\). The scalar finite part at this pole is one
input to the paper, but the main object is the primitive-orbit
determinant calculus for finite-group extensions. For a cocycle
\(\tau:E_A\to G\) into a finite group, periodic determinant data live in
the twisted transition matrices \(A_\varrho\). Passing from that periodic
data to primitive \(G\)-labels is the non-abelian step: repetitions of a
primitive orbit replace \(g\) by \(g^m\), so primitive extraction must
combine M\"obius inversion with the Adams operations on class functions.

We therefore state the finite-group results first. They are Theorem~C,
the primary theorem package of the paper. For clarity this package is
split into three main theorems: the Adams--M\"obius determinant
mechanism, its Perron-point Mertens consequence, and the quotient/shadow
structure. The scalar finite-part and cyclic statements following them
are auxiliary finite-matrix inputs and comparison cases.
The logical direction is: twisted determinants first give periodic
Peter--Weyl channels; Adams--M\"obius inversion extracts the primitive
Peter--Weyl channels; the explicit gap \(\eta<\lambda\) permits
evaluation at \(z=\lambda^{-1}\) and hence class Mertens constants; and
non-abelian Fourier inversion reconstructs the Perron-point primitive
channels from those constants.
The finite-part formula supplies the trivial-channel normalisation
\(\mathcal M(A)\), and the cyclic root-of-unity formulas supply the
abelian shadow against which the non-abelian defect is measured.

\subsection{Main finite-group results}

Let \(\tau:E_A\to G\) be a cocycle into a finite group, and let
\(A_\varrho\) be the twisted transition matrices. For a class function
\(\phi:G\to\CC\) write
\[
  T_{\phi,n}:=\sum_{x\in\operatorname{Fix}(\sigma^n)}
    \phi(\tau_n(x)),
  \qquad
  \mathcal L_\phi(z):=\sum_{n\ge 1}\frac{T_{\phi,n}}{n}z^n,
\]
and
\[
  \Pi_\phi(z):=\sum_{\gamma\in\cP}\phi(\tau(\gamma))z^{|\gamma|}.
\]

\begin{theorem}[Main Theorem I (Theorem C.I): Adams--M\"obius primitive
  determinant linearisation]
  \label{thm:intro-finite-group}
  \label{thm:intro-adams-mobius}
  For every class function \(\phi:G\to\CC\) and every
  \(|z|<\lambda^{-1}\),
  \[
    \mathcal L_\phi(z)
    =
    \sum_{\varrho\in\Irr(G)}
      \widehat\phi(\varrho)
      \log\det(I-zA_\varrho)^{-1},
  \]
  where \(\widehat\phi(\varrho)\) is the character coefficient of
  \(\phi\). Moreover,
  \[
    \Pi_\phi(z)
    =
    \sum_{m\ge 1}\frac{\mu(m)}{m}\,
      \mathcal L_{\psi^m\phi}(z^m).
  \]
  Equivalently, if
  \(\psi^m\chi_\varrho
    =\sum_{\sigma\in\Irr(G)}a_{\varrho,\sigma}^{(m)}\chi_\sigma\),
  then
  \[
    \Pi_\varrho(z)
    =
    \sum_{m\ge 1}\frac{\mu(m)}{m}
      \sum_{\sigma\in\Irr(G)}
        a_{\varrho,\sigma}^{(m)}
        \log\det(I-z^mA_\sigma)^{-1}.
  \]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Main Theorem II (Theorem C.II): class Mertens constants
  and non-abelian Fourier reconstruction]
  \label{thm:intro-class-mertens}
  Assume the explicit twisted spectral gap
  \[
    \eta:=\max_{\varrho\neq\mathbf 1}\rho(A_\varrho)<\lambda,
    \qquad
    \Lambda_*:=\max\{\Lambda(A),\eta,\lambda^{1/2}\}.
  \]
  Then for every conjugacy class \(C\subset G\),
  \[
    p_{n,C}
    =
    \frac{|C|}{|G|}\frac{\lambda^n}{n}
    +O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda_*^n}{n}\right),
  \]
  and
  \[
    \sum_{n\le N}\frac{p_{n,C}}{\lambda^n}
    =
    \frac{|C|}{|G|}\log N+\mathcal M_C(A)+O(N^{-1}).
  \]
  The deviations
  \[
    \Delta_C(A):=\mathcal M_C(A)-\frac{|C|}{|G|}\mathcal M(A)
  \]
  reconstruct the non-trivial Perron-point primitive channels by
  non-abelian Fourier inversion:
  \[
    \Pi_\sigma(\lambda^{-1})
    =
    \sum_{C\subset G}\Delta_C(A)\chi_\sigma(C)
    \qquad(\sigma\neq\mathbf 1).
  \]
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Main Theorem III (Theorem C.III): quotient
  functoriality and the abelian shadow]
  \label{thm:intro-quotient-shadow}
  Assume the hypotheses of Main Theorem~II, so the class deviations
  \(\Delta_C(A)\) are defined. Quotient maps \(q:G\twoheadrightarrow Q\)
  intertwine class-function logarithms and primitive series:
  \[
    \mathcal L_{q^\ast f}^{G}(z)=\mathcal L_f^{Q}(z),
    \qquad
    \Pi_{q^\ast f}^{G}(z)=\Pi_f^{Q}(z)
    \qquad(|z|<\lambda^{-1}).
  \]
  If
  \[
    \mathcal B_A(C):=\frac{|G|}{|C|}\Delta_C(A),
  \]
  then \(\mathcal B_A\) splits orthogonally into its one-dimensional
  Peter--Weyl part and its higher-dimensional Peter--Weyl part. Cyclic
  quotient observables span exactly the one-dimensional sector; hence
  cyclic quotient data exhaust the abelian shadow and are orthogonal to
  the genuine non-abelian defect. In particular, the full primitive
  class profile is generated by cyclic quotient shadows if and only if
  every higher-dimensional primitive channel
  \(\Pi_\varrho(\lambda^{-1})\) vanishes.
\leanverified{paper_etds_intro_finite_group}
\end{theorem}

An explicit \(S_3\)-extension shows that vanishing periodic traces do
not force vanishing primitive bias: the sign-character periodic channel
vanishes identically, yet the corresponding primitive channel is
non-zero.

The detailed statements underlying Main Theorems~I--III are proved in
\Cref{thm:class-function-linearisation,%
cor:class-function-adams-mobius,%
cor:primitive-peter-weyl-determinant,%
thm:class-mertens-explicit,%
thm:class-mertens-fourier}
and in
\Cref{thm:quotient-functoriality,%
thm:abelian-shadow-defect,%
thm:cyclic-detection-boundary,%
cor:s3-example-profile}.
The hypotheses used in these statements are as follows.

\subsection{Auxiliary scalar inputs}

We next record the scalar finite-matrix components used as inputs and
comparison cases for the main theorem package. After removing the
Perron factor one obtains the reduced determinant
\[
  C(A):=\det\nolimits'(I-A/\lambda)^{-1}
\]
and the reduced spectral polynomial
\[
  F_A(t):=\frac{\det(I-tA/\lambda)}{1-t}
  =\prod_{j=2}^d\left(1-\frac{\mu_j}{\lambda}t\right),
\]
where \(\mu_2,\dots,\mu_d\) are the non-Perron eigenvalues. The
Abel-regularised finite-part constant
\[
  \FP(A):=\lim_{r\uparrow 1}\bigl(P_A(r)+\log(1-r)\bigr),
  \qquad
  P_A(r):=\sum_{n\ge 1}p_n(A)\lambda^{-n}r^n,
\]
measures the deviation of the primitive orbit sum from the harmonic
series.

\begin{proposition}[Auxiliary Proposition A: finite-part formula at the
  Perron pole]
  \label{thm:intro-finite-part}
  Let \(A\) be a primitive non-negative matrix with Perron root
  \(\lambda=\rho(A)>1\), reduced determinant \(C(A)\), and primitive
  orbit counts \(p_n(A)\). Then
  \[
    \FP(A)
    =\log C(A)
      +\sum_{k\ge 2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}
        \log\det(I-\lambda^{-k}A)^{-1}.
  \]
  Equivalently,
  \[
    \FP(A)
    =\log C(A)
      +\sum_{n\ge 1}p_n(A)
        \bigl(\lambda^{-n}+\log(1-\lambda^{-n})\bigr).
  \]
  In particular, the orbit-Mertens constant
  \(\mathcal M(A)=\gamma+\FP(A)\) satisfies
  \[
    \sum_{n\le N}\frac{p_n(A)}{\lambda^n}
    =\log N+\mathcal M(A)+o(1).
  \]
\leanverified{paper_etds_intro_finite_part}
\end{proposition}

This is the SFT analogue of Mertens' theorem for primes and identifies
the scalar constant that appears in the classwise deviations above. Its
proof is a short Euler-product and M\"obius-inversion computation;
\Cref{thm:intro-finite-part} is proved in
\Cref{thm:finite-part-primitive-closed-form,cor:finite-part-mertens-asymptotic}.

The second scalar component addresses spectral reconstruction from
root-of-unity data, a theme connected to inverse spectral problems for
SFTs \cite{LindMarcus1995}. The genuinely useful part is the recovery
of the reduced spectrum from the cyclic-lift fingerprint; the
single-layer statement is separated as the elementary discrete Fourier
corollary that fixes the abelian comparison case for the later quotient
and shadow results.

\begin{proposition}[Auxiliary Proposition B: cyclic-lift reconstruction
  of the reduced spectrum]
  \label{thm:intro-cyclic-reconstruction}
  Let \(A\) be a primitive non-negative \(d\times d\) matrix, and set
  \[
    \Psi_q(A):=\log q+\log C_\lambda(A^{\langle q\rangle}),
  \]
  where \(A^{\langle q\rangle}=A\otimes P_q\) is the cyclic lift and
  \(C_\lambda\) denotes the reduced constant at the simple eigenvalue
  \(\lambda=\rho(A)\). Then the sequence
  \(\{\Psi_q(A)\}_{q\ge 1}\) determines \(F_A\), and hence the
  non-Perron spectrum of \(A\) with algebraic multiplicity. Moreover,
  \[
    \limsup_{q\to\infty}\abs{\Psi_q(A)}^{1/q}
    =
    \Lambda(A)/\lambda.
  \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Auxiliary DFT corollary: one root-of-unity layer]
  \label{cor:intro-one-layer-reconstruction}
  Let \(A\) be a primitive non-negative \(d\times d\) matrix. If
  \(q\ge d\), then the single root-of-unity layer
  \(\{F_A(\omega_q^j)\}_{0\le j\le q-1}\) determines every coefficient
  of the reduced spectral polynomial
  \[
    F_A(t)=\sum_{n=0}^{d-1}a_nt^n,
  \]
  by the finite Fourier formula
  \[
    a_n=\frac{1}{q}\sum_{j=0}^{q-1}
      F_A(\omega_q^j)\omega_q^{-jn}
    \qquad (0\le n\le d-1).
  \]
\leanverified{paper_etds_intro_cyclic_reconstruction_seeds}
\end{corollary}

The proof combines M\"obius inversion on multiples with Newton's
identities for Auxiliary Proposition~B, and finite Fourier inversion
for \Cref{cor:intro-one-layer-reconstruction}; see
\Cref{thm:finite-part-cyclic-lift-spectrum-identifiability,%
thm:finite-part-cyclic-lift-single-q-torsion-reconstruction,%
cor:finite-part-cyclic-lift-zeta-torsion-determines-spectrum}.
The terminology of cyclic, torsion, and exponential spectral
fingerprints is fixed in \Cref{def:spectral-fingerprints}.

\subsection{Relation to existing work}

The dynamical \(\zeta\)-function for SFTs goes back to
Artin--Mazur \cite{ArtinMazur1965PeriodicPoints} and Bowen--Lanford
\cite{BowenLanford1970Zeta,Manning1971Axiom}. The prime-orbit
asymptotic for shifts of finite type is classical: Parry
\cite{Parry1983PrimeOrbit} proved the analogue of the prime number
theorem for closed orbits of SFTs and their suspensions. The existence
of Mertens-type asymptotics for Axiom~A flows was established by Sharp
\cite{Sharp1991Mertens}. The present paper does not claim a new
prime-orbit theorem. Its primary contribution is the Adams--M\"obius
reconstruction of primitive Peter--Weyl channels from twisted determinant
data. The scalar finite-part identity at the Perron pole is included as
an explicit finite-matrix input for the classwise constants and as a
checkable comparison with the classical Mertens constant.

The transfer-operator approach is developed systematically in
\cite{Ruelle1976ZetaExpanding,Ruelle1978Thermodynamic,%
ParryPollicott1990Zeta,Baladi2000PositiveTransfer}.
Twisted determinant and \(L\)-function constructions for dynamical
systems and finite-group extensions go back at least to the twisted
Perron--Frobenius framework of Adachi--Sunada
\cite{AdachiSunada1987TwistedPF}. The leading Chebotarev asymptotics for
finite extensions are classical: Parry--Pollicott treat Axiom~A Galois
coverings \cite{ParryPollicott1986Chebotarev}, and Noorani--Parry prove
the corresponding theorem for finite homogeneous extensions of shifts
\cite{NooraniParry1992ChebotarevShifts}; see also
\cite{PollicottSharp2007Chebotarev,Parry1999LivsicNonAbelian}. The
present paper does not reprove those leading density theorems by a new
Tauberian method. Instead it isolates the finite determinant algebra
needed after the leading term: primitive class constants and their
Peter--Weyl coordinates are recovered from twisted determinant data by
Adams--M\"obius inversion.

Finite group actions on SFTs have a separate symbolic-dynamics
literature, and this is the closest context for the finite-group part of
the paper. Fiebig
\cite{Fiebig1993PeriodicFiniteGroupActions} studies periodic points for
finite group actions on shifts of finite type, including zeta functions
attached to fixed subsystems and quotient systems. Boyle--Schmieding
\cite{BoyleSchmieding2017FiniteGroupExtensions} analyse finite group
extensions of SFTs through \(K\)-theory, Parry obstructions, and
Liv\v{s}ic obstructions. Boyle--Carlsen--Eilers
\cite{BoyleCarlsenEilers2020FlowEquivalenceGSFT} develop the
classification and equivariant flow-equivalence theory of \(G\)-SFTs
using algebraic matrix structures over group rings. Those works address
existence, classification, and periodic-data obstructions for group
actions and extensions. Here the cocycle \(\tau:E_A\to G\) is fixed; the
question is inverse-determinantal and primitive: which primitive
conjugacy-class Mertens constants and non-abelian Fourier coefficients
are forced by the twisted matrices \(A_\varrho\)?

Tauberian refinements and closed-orbit asymptotics are treated in
\cite{Pollicott1992AgmonTauberian} and the monograph
\cite{ParryPollicott1990Zeta}. For the functorial orbit-counting
perspective underlying Section~\ref{sec:shadows} one should also compare
\cite{PakapongpunWard2009FunctorialOrbit}.
For graph coverings and graph \(L\)-functions one should compare the
Ihara--Bass and Hashimoto determinant formulas, the Stark--Terras theory
of graph coverings and Artin \(L\)-functions, and directed or weighted
variants
\cite{Hashimoto1989ZetaFiniteGraphs,%
StarkTerras1996FiniteGraphsCoverings,%
StarkTerras2000FiniteGraphsCoveringsII,%
StarkTerras2007FiniteGraphsCoveringsIII,%
Bass1992Ihara,%
MizunoSato2001ZetaDigraphs,%
Horton2007IharaDigraphs,%
ChoeKwakParkSato2007Bartholdi,%
Sato2008WeightedBartholdiCoverings}.
For more recent perspectives on dynamical zeta functions and dynamical
determinants beyond the classical symbolic-dynamics sources, compare
Baladi's modern monograph \cite{Baladi2018ZetaDeterminants}, the
parameter-regularity results of J\'ez\'equel
\cite{Jezequel2019ParameterRegularity}, and Shen's work on twisted
Ruelle zeta functions on locally symmetric spaces
\cite{Shen2023ComplexValuedAnalyticTorsion}. Our results stay in the
finite-state symbolic setting but fit naturally into this broader
determinant-based programme.

The relation between the present results and their closest antecedents
is therefore organised with the finite-group mechanism at the centre.
Classical \(\zeta\)-rationality supplies the finite-part input at the
Perron pole, and root-of-unity sampling for cyclic covers supplies the
abelian comparison case. The Chebotarev theory of finite-group
extensions is where the new ingredient appears: the Adams--M\"obius
passage from twisted determinant data to primitive Peter--Weyl series.
The appendices record secondary consequences of these inputs rather than
additional primary theorem chains.

\subsection{Plan of the paper}

\Cref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation for primitive orbit counts,
reduced determinants, cyclic lifts, and finite-group extensions.
\Cref{sec:chebotarev} develops the Adams--M\"obius mechanism for
finite-group extensions, derives the primitive Peter--Weyl determinant
series, and establishes the class Mertens theorem under a twisted
spectral gap. \Cref{sec:shadows} proves quotient functoriality,
identifies the abelian shadow and the exact boundary of cyclic
detection, and closes with the \(S_3\)-model demonstrating the Adams
obstruction. \Cref{sec:finite-part} then proves the auxiliary scalar
finite-part formula, the cyclic-lift reconstruction of the reduced
spectrum, and the one-layer discrete Fourier corollary used as the
abelian comparison case. The appendices collect secondary consequences:
holomorphic variation of the finite part (\Cref{app:variation}),
arithmetic root-of-unity corollaries including a Dirichlet-character
inversion (\Cref{app:arithmetic}), and rigidity statements for growing
families (\Cref{app:rigidity}).
