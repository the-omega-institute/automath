\begin{abstract}
We study the polynomial family $\{Z_m(y)\}_{m\ge 0}$ defined by the rational generating function $Z(t,y)=N(t,y)/D(t,y)$, where the associated companion matrix has characteristic polynomial
\[
\Pi(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4-\lambda^3-(2y+1)\lambda^2+\lambda+y(y+1).
\]
The paper gives an exact algebraic-spectral analysis of this quartic family: a balanced-family reduction to Weierstrass form, a projective normalization by an elliptic curve, a classification of the real branch values, a local square-root endpoint law at $y=0$, and explicit formulas for degree growth and a uniform zero of multiplicity at least two at $y=-1$.
More generally, this quartic belongs to the balanced family
\[
P_{a,c,d,g}(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4+a\lambda^3+(c-2y)\lambda^2+d\lambda+y(y-c)+g,
\]
for which the substitution \(\xi=y-\lambda^2\) converts the affine curve to a Weierstrass model.
For the present member \((a,c,d,g)=(-1,-1,1,0)\), the projective quartic curve attached to \(\Pi(\lambda,y)=0\) is normalized by the elliptic curve
\[
Y^2=4\lambda^3-4\lambda+1,
\]
so the four eigenvalue branches form a genus-one degree-$4$ cover of the \(y\)-line.
This reduction determines the finite branch values and yields the discriminant factorization
\[
\Disc_\lambda \Pi=-y(y-1)(256y^3+411y^2+165y+32).
\]
Among the three real branch values, we show that $y=0$ is the only dominant real EP2, whereas $y=1$ and the negative branch value $y_{\mathrm{sub}}$ are subdominant.
The real locus of the elliptic curve projects to $[y_{\mathrm{sub}},1]\cup[0,\infty)$, which gives the exact number of real roots of $\Pi(\cdot,y)$ for every real $y$.
We also prove a generic analytic EP2 branching lemma and verify, for this quartic family, the amplitude symmetry and complex-uniform remainder bounds needed for the endpoint cosine law. This yields the first negative zeros
\[
y_{m,k}
=
-\frac{\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{2m^2}
-\frac{3\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{4m^3}
+O_k(m^{-4}).
\]
Finally, we derive the quarter-power splitting on the positive large-$y$ branch, prove the degree law $\deg_y Z_m=\lfloor(m+3)/2\rfloor$ with explicit top-degree coefficients, and establish a uniform zero of multiplicity at least two at $y=-1$.
\end{abstract}
