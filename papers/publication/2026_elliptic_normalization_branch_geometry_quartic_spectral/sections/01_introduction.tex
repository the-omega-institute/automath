\section{Introduction}

We study the polynomial family $\{Z_m(y)\}_{m\ge 0}$ defined by the rational generating function
\[
Z(t,y)=\sum_{m\ge 0} Z_m(y)t^m
=
\frac{1+yt+(y^2-y-1)t^2+(y^3-2y)t^3}
{1-t-(2y+1)t^2+t^3+y(y+1)t^4}.
\]
Equivalently, $\{Z_m(y)\}$ is generated by an order-$4$ companion transfer matrix with characteristic polynomial
\[
\Pi(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4-\lambda^3-(2y+1)\lambda^2+\lambda+y(y+1).
\]
The paper is organized as a quartic case study.
We regard \(\Pi(\lambda,y)\) as a fixed algebraic-spectral object and ask for an exact description of its elliptic normalization, its real branch geometry, its dominant positive branch, and the zeros of \(Z_m\) nearest the dominant square-root endpoint at \(y=0\).

Section~\ref{sec:genus-one} places \(\Pi(\lambda,y)\) inside the balanced family
\[
P_{a,c,d,g}(\lambda,y)=\lambda^4+a\lambda^3+(c-2y)\lambda^2+d\lambda+y(y-c)+g,
\]
for which the substitution \(\xi=y-\lambda^2\) removes the quartic obstruction and produces a Weierstrass model.
Within that family we use only a normalized double-root statement: Theorem~\ref{thm:balanced-classification} shows that, once the colliding points are fixed at $(\lambda,y)=(1,0)$ and $(\lambda,y)=(-1,1)$, the balanced parameters are forced to be \((a,c,d,g)=(-1,-1,1,0)\).
Without the second prescription there remains a one-parameter family of balanced members; see Remark~\ref{rem:mu-parameter}.

For the specialization \((a,c,d,g)=(-1,-1,1,0)\), the projective closure of \(\Pi(\lambda,y)=0\) is normalized by the elliptic curve
\[
Y^2=4\lambda^3-4\lambda+1.
\]
This turns the problem into a degree-$4$ genus-one covering of the \(y\)-line.
As a result one can compute the finite branch values exactly, determine the real-spectral phase diagram, identify the dominant branch on \(y>0\), and derive explicit endpoint zero asymptotics near \(y=0\).

\subsection{Relation to existing literature}

The ingredients used in the paper are classical.
The Yang--Lee papers and Fisher's edge-singularity paper provide the historical background for zero distributions in parameter-dependent polynomial families \cite{YangLee1952I,LeeYang1952II,Fisher1978}, while modern Beraha--Kahane--Weiss formulations organize limiting zero sets through dominant equimodularity \cite{BerahaKahaneWeiss1975,BerahaKahaneWeiss1978,Sokal2004,BrownOtto2025}.
Companion matrices and cyclicity are standard \cite{HornJohnson2012}; rational generating functions, linear recurrences, and finite Hankel rank belong to the general theory of rational sequences \cite{FlajoletSedgewick2009,Stanley2012,Partington1989}; and the reduction to Weierstrass form, the Riemann--Hurwitz count, and local Puiseux expansions are standard algebraic-curve tools \cite{Walker1950,Silverman2009,Miranda1995,BrieskornKnorrer1986}.

Within the recurrence-generated zero literature, the closest references are Tran's discriminant approach \cite{Tran2014,Tran2015}, the four-term work of de Bruin, Forg\'acs--Tran, Tran--Zumba, Adams, and Botta--Fornazieri \cite{deBruin1984,ForgacsTran2016,ForgacsTran2017,ForgacsTran2020,TranZumba2018,TranZumba2021,Adams2020,BottaFornazieri2023}, and the exact-support and Tran-conjecture papers of Ndikubwayo and of B{\o}gvad--Ndikubwayo--Shapiro \cite{Ndikubwayo2020,BogvadNdikubwayoShapiro2020}.
Related geometric viewpoints also appear in Goh--He--Ricci and in Wang--Zhang \cite{GohHeRicci2009,WangZhang2021}, while Ndikubwayo's later Toeplitz-based work treats a different recurrence regime \cite{Ndikubwayo2022}.

\subsubsection*{Contribution and scope}

The contribution here is not a new general theory of short recurrences.
What the paper supplies is a complete analysis of one concrete quartic:
an explicit elliptic normalization of the projective spectral curve, the exact finite branch values, the componentwise real \(y\)-images and resulting real-root counts, a direct proof that the positive branch is globally dominant on \(y>0\), the endpoint cosine-law asymptotics at the dominant square-root branch point, and the degree-growth law for the polynomial sequence.
Section~\ref{sec:genus-one} also records three auxiliary balanced-family statements used to organize the specialization: the uniform Weierstrass reduction (Proposition~\ref{prop:balanced-family}), the interval-by-interval description of the real \(y\)-projection (Theorem~\ref{thm:balanced-real-projection}), and the normalized double-root determination (Theorem~\ref{thm:balanced-classification}).
The paper should therefore be read as a detailed quartic case study rather than as a classification theorem for general balanced quartics.

For real $y>0$ we write
\[
r(y):=\max_{1\le j\le 4}\abs{\lambda_j(y)},
\qquad
f_{\mathrm{dom}}(y):=\log r(y).
\]
The dominant branch over $y>0$ is the connected component of the smooth spectral curve on which $\abs{\lambda}=r(y)$.
The problem is thus to determine this branch and the corresponding zero geometry explicitly for the fixed quartic \(\Pi(\lambda,y)\).

\subsection{Summary of results}

For this quartic family we obtain the following.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Balanced-family reduction, real-projection description, and double-root normalization} (Section~\ref{sec:genus-one}).
We identify a four-parameter balanced family \(P_{a,c,d,g}\) for which the substitution \(\xi=y-\lambda^2\) converts the quartic curve to a Weierstrass model.
For real balanced parameters, Theorem~\ref{thm:balanced-real-projection} describes the componentwise real \(y\)-images in terms of the branch values and the real critical values coming from the ramification quintic.
We then prove the normalized double-root statement of Theorem~\ref{thm:balanced-classification}: if the balanced polynomial has double roots at $(\lambda,y)=(1,0)$ and $(\lambda,y)=(-1,1)$, then \((a,c,d,g)=(-1,-1,1,0)\).
For the present quartic this yields the elliptic model with invariants \(g_2=4\), \(g_3=-1\), and \(\Delta=37\), together with the finite branch values \(y=0\), \(y=1\), and the three roots of \(256y^3+411y^2+165y+32\).

\item \textbf{Real branch-point classification and real-root counts} (Section~\ref{sec:ep-classification}).
Among the three real branch values, $y=0$ is the only one whose outgoing pair contains the dominant root for $y>0$ small; $y=1$ and the negative branch value \(y_{\mathrm{sub}}\) are subdominant.
The real locus of the elliptic curve has component images
\[
[y_{\mathrm{sub}},1]
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
[0,\infty),
\]
so the total real image is \( [y_{\mathrm{sub}},\infty) \) with overlap on \([0,1]\).
This yields the exact count of real roots of \(\Pi(\cdot,y)\) for every real \(y\).

\item \textbf{Square-root branching and endpoint quantization} (Section~\ref{sec:physical-sheet}).
We verify the local square-root branching, amplitude symmetry, and complex-uniform remainder bounds needed for the localized cosine law at \(y=0\).
Consequently, for each fixed \(k\ge 1\), the \(k\)th negative zero nearest \(y=0\) satisfies
\[
y_{m,k}
=
-\frac{\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{2m^2}
-\frac{3\pi^2(2k-1)^2}{4m^3}
+O_k(m^{-4}).
\]

\item \textbf{Large-$y$ splitting and degree growth} (Sections~\ref{sec:physical-sheet} and \ref{sec:support-law}).
On the positive large-$y$ branch, the dominant root has a quarter-power splitting governed by a double tangency at projective infinity.
On the polynomial side, \(\deg_y Z_m=\lfloor(m+3)/2\rfloor\), and the top-degree coefficients are explicit and subexponential in \(m\).

\item \textbf{A rigid boundary value at \(y=-1\)} (Section~\ref{sec:boundary-zeros}).
For every \(m\ge 2\), the polynomial \(Z_m(y)\) has a zero of multiplicity at least two at \(y=-1\).
This gives a uniform algebraic boundary point for the whole sequence.
\end{enumerate}

Sections~\ref{sec:model}--\ref{sec:hankel} define the family and establish the generic order-$4$ minimality of the recurrence.
Sections~\ref{sec:genus-one}--\ref{sec:boundary-zeros} contain the explicit spectral and algebraic results for the quartic specialization.
Section~\ref{sec:verification} records reproducible numerical checks of the real-axis modulus ordering and the first edge zeros, and Section~\ref{sec:discussion-new} closes with the scope and limitations of the quartic analysis.
