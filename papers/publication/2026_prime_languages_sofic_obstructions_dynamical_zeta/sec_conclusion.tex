\section{Further remarks}\label{sec:conclusion}

The transfer-matrix dichotomy from \Cref{sec:automata} yields uniform
quantitative obstructions for approximating prime languages by any fixed
DFA, both on the full base-\(b\) shift and on trim deterministic
presentations of regular numeration systems. The main consequences are
twofold: (i) recall and precision cannot stay simultaneously bounded
below along infinitely many lengths, and (ii) the symmetric difference
from the prime slice is at least \(c_{\mathcal A,\bullet}\,\lambda^m/m\)
with \(\lambda\) equal to the relevant growth constant (so \(\lambda=b\)
for ordinary bases and \(\lambda=\varphi\) for Zeckendorf words). These
constants are uniform asymptotic lower bounds depending only on the DFA
and base; we do not claim an effective recipe for computing them.
\Cref{sec:zeckendorf} illustrates the extension quantitatively by combining
the Zeckendorf growth law with prime-counting estimates, thereby
quantifying Rigo's non-recognizability theorem for primes.

Several natural questions remain. The density dichotomy uses completeness
only as a normalization and uses determinism in an essential way, and
it would be interesting to know how far analogous lower bounds persist
for nondeterministic or weighted automata, where cancellation phenomena
become possible. It would also be natural to extend the fixed-DFA
obstruction further across Parry numeration systems, where the dominant
eigenvalue \(\lambda\) varies with the system, and to test other
arithmetic sets besides the primes, such as square-free numbers or
polynomial images. Finally, understanding whether bounded-ambiguity NFAs
or controlled families of automata can evade the dichotomy remains an
open direction.
