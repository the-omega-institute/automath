\section{Further remarks}\label{sec:conclusion}

The two new results of this note act at different levels. The density
dichotomy is probabilistic: a fixed complete DFA forces eventual
periodicity in normalized word counts, and the resulting
recall/precision analysis gives an explicit quantitative obstruction for
approximating the binary prime language. The sofic-slice obstruction is
combinatorial: exponential-polynomial growth forced by sofic word counts
is incompatible with the prime-number-theorem rate \(\varphi^m/m\). The
background analytic observations in \Cref{sec:analytic} complement these
by recording the classical natural-boundary and periodicity phenomena
for Euler products and finite determinant models.

Several natural questions remain. The density dichotomy in
\Cref{sec:automata} uses completeness and determinism in an essential
way, and it would be interesting to know how far analogous lower bounds
persist for nondeterministic or weighted automata, where cancellation
phenomena become possible. Likewise, the sofic obstruction in
\Cref{sec:zeckendorf} suggests asking for a sharp criterion on when an
arithmetically defined family of length slices can be realized by a
single sofic shift.

The Zeckendorf argument also points beyond the Fibonacci case. For
Parry numeration systems one expects the same regular power-law
constraint, and it is natural to test other arithmetic sets besides the
primes, such as square-free numbers or polynomial images. On the
analytic side, our natural-boundary result is qualitative. A more
quantitative analysis of how singularities accumulate at roots of unity,
or of how these Euler products compare with finite determinant models,
would further clarify the finite-state obstruction.
