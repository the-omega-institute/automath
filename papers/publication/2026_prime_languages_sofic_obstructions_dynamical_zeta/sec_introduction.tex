\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Finite-state symbolic models impose rigid constraints on counting
functions. In a deterministic automaton, the number of accepted words
of length \(m\) is governed by powers of a finite matrix. In a sofic
shift, the number of admissible words of a given length is likewise
controlled by a finite-state presentation. These mechanisms force
eventual periodicity or exponential-polynomial growth, and this is
often enough to rule out arithmetic languages whose size is governed by
the prime number theorem.

The purpose of this note is to isolate several elementary but useful
obstructions of this kind. The first concerns complete deterministic
automata over the binary alphabet. The second concerns regular
sublanguages of the canonical Zeckendorf syntax. The third concerns the
analytic behaviour of Euler products and finite determinants associated
with finite-state data.

Throughout, \(f(m)\asymp g(m)\) means that \(c_1g(m)\le f(m)\le c_2g(m)\)
for some positive constants \(c_1,c_2\) and all sufficiently large
\(m\). We write \(F_0=0\), \(F_1=1\) for the Fibonacci numbers,
\(\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2\), and for a Zeckendorf word
\(w=x_1\cdots x_m\) we set
\[
  \val(w):=\sum_{k=1}^m x_kF_{k+1}.
\]

\subsection{Main results}

Our first theorem is a density dichotomy for deterministic automata.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:intro-density}
Let \(\mathcal A\) be a complete deterministic finite automaton over
\(\Sigma=\{0,1\}\), and let
\[
  a_m(\mathcal A)
  :=\#\{w\in\Sigma^m:\mathcal A\text{ accepts }w\}.
\]
Then there exist an integer \(p\ge 1\), non-negative constants
\(c_0,\dots,c_{p-1}\), and \(\theta\in(0,1)\) such that
\[
  \frac{a_m(\mathcal A)}{2^m}=c_{m\bmod p}+O(\theta^m)
  \qquad (m\to\infty).
\]
Consequently exactly one of the following holds:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
  \item \(a_m(\mathcal A)/2^m\) decays exponentially.
  \item Along some residue class modulo \(p\), the ratio
  \(a_m(\mathcal A)/2^m\) is bounded below by a positive constant.
\end{enumerate}
In particular, no deterministic finite automaton can satisfy
\[
  a_m(\mathcal A)/2^m\asymp m^{-\alpha}
\]
for any \(\alpha>0\).
\end{theorem}

When this is applied to the binary prime language
\(\mathcal P_m^{(2)}\), whose size is \(\asymp 2^m/m\) by the prime
number theorem \cite{HardyWright2008}, one obtains a sharp
incompatibility with finite-state recognition.

\begin{corollary}[Symmetric-difference lower bound for binary primes]
  \label{cor:intro-binary-primes}
Let \(L(\mathcal A)\subseteq\{0,1\}^\ast\) be recognised by a fixed
deterministic finite automaton. Then there exists \(c>0\) such that
\[
  \abs{L(\mathcal A)\cap\{0,1\}^m\triangle\mathcal P_m^{(2)}}
  \ge c\,\frac{2^m}{m}
\]
for all sufficiently large \(m\). If \(L(\mathcal A)\) falls in the
positive-density alternative of \Cref{thm:intro-density}, then along an
infinite arithmetic progression of lengths one has the stronger bound
\[
  \abs{L(\mathcal A)\cap\{0,1\}^m\triangle\mathcal P_m^{(2)}}
  \gg 2^m.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[No simultaneous recall and precision for fixed DFA]
  \label{cor:intro-binary-precision}
  Let
  \[
    \mathrm{Rec}_m
    :=\frac{\abs{L(\mathcal A)\cap\mathcal P_m^{(2)}}}
            {\abs{\mathcal P_m^{(2)}}},
    \qquad
    \mathrm{Prec}_m
    :=\frac{\abs{L(\mathcal A)\cap\mathcal P_m^{(2)}}}
            {\abs{L(\mathcal A)\cap\{0,1\}^m}}
  \]
  whenever \(\abs{L(\mathcal A)\cap\{0,1\}^m}>0\). Then every fixed DFA
  satisfies one of the following:
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
    \item \(\mathrm{Rec}_m\to 0\) exponentially fast.
    \item \(\mathrm{Prec}_m\to 0\) along an infinite arithmetic
    progression of lengths.
  \end{enumerate}
  In particular, no fixed DFA has both recall and precision bounded
  away from zero for infinitely many lengths.
\end{corollary}

Our second theorem concerns the Zeckendorf language
\[
  \mathcal Z
  :=\{x_1\cdots x_m\in\{0,1\}^\ast:
      x_ix_{i+1}\neq 11,\ x_m=1\}.
\]

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:intro-zeckendorf}
Let \(L\subseteq\mathcal Z\) be regular, and define
\[
  N_L(T):=\#\{w\in L:\val(w)\le T\},
\]
where \(\val(w)\) is the Zeckendorf value of \(w\). Then there exists
\(\alpha\in[0,1]\) such that
\[
  N_L(T)=T^{\alpha+o(1)}
  \qquad (T\to\infty).
\]
In particular, the language of Zeckendorf representations of prime
numbers is not regular. Combined with the exponential-polynomial
description of sofic word counts proved later
(\Cref{prop:sofic-counts-exponential-polynomial}), the same comparison
also shows that there is no sofic shift \(X\) over \(\{0,1\}\) with
\(\mathcal B_m(X)=\mathcal P_m^{(\mathrm Z)}\) for all sufficiently
large \(m\), where \(\mathcal B_m(X)\) denotes the admissible words of
length \(m\) in \(X\).
\end{theorem}

Our final theorem records two analytic obstructions.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:intro-analytic}
The following statements hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
  \item If \((b_n)_{n\ge 1}\subseteq\ZZ_{\ge 0}\), if \(b_p>0\) for
  infinitely many primes \(p\), and if the Euler product
  \[
    Z_b(z):=\prod_{n\ge 1}(1-z^n)^{-b_n}
  \]
  converges to a holomorphic function on \(\abs{z}<1\), then it has the
  unit circle as a natural boundary.

  \item For every finite matrix \(M\), the function
  \[
    F_M(s):=\det(I-e^{-s}M)^{-1}
  \]
  is \(2\pi i\)-periodic. In particular, the Riemann
  \(\zeta\)-function cannot agree with \(F_M\) on any non-empty open
  subset of \(\Re(s)>1\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Previous work}

The general background on automatic and recognizable sequences is
classical; see Cobham's foundational papers
\cite{Cobham1969,Cobham1972} and the monograph
\cite{AlloucheShallit2003}. From that perspective our first section is
deliberately elementary: instead of invoking a base-dependence theorem,
we isolate a density dichotomy for a single fixed automaton, using only
the finite-state Markov-chain decomposition reviewed in
\cite[Chapter~4]{LevinPeresWilmer2009MarkovMixing}. The interaction
between primes and digital constraints has a much deeper literature. In
particular, the work of Mauduit and Rivat \cite{MauduitRivat2010} and
of Drmota, Mauduit and Rivat \cite{DrmotaMauduitRivat2009} shows that
prime inputs already display highly nontrivial equidistribution
phenomena inside automatic and digit-restricted settings.

For Zeckendorf and more general linear or Parry numeration systems, the
regularity of admissible expansions is standard; see
\cite{Frougny1992,BruyereHansel1997}. Our second section uses only this
regular syntax together with the transfer-matrix viewpoint on word
counts, familiar from symbolic dynamics and analytic combinatorics
\cite{LindMarcus1995,Kitchens1998,FlajoletSedgewick2009}. On the
analytic side, the natural-boundary statement belongs to the classical
circle of ideas initiated by Carlson and Estermann
\cite{Carlson1921,Estermann1928}. The value of the present note is
therefore not a single deep new theorem, but the fact that
probabilistic, combinatorial, and analytic obstructions can be
assembled into one short account of why prime-supported languages
resist finite-state models.

\subsection{Organisation}

\Cref{sec:automata} proves the density dichotomy for deterministic
automata and applies it to binary primes. \Cref{sec:zeckendorf}
establishes the growth law for regular Zeckendorf sublanguages and the
resulting regular and sofic obstructions. \Cref{sec:analytic} contains
the two analytic obstructions at the level of Euler products and
determinants.
