\section{Golden syntax and Zeckendorf normal forms}
\label{sec:golden-syntax}

Let \(F_1=1\), \(F_2=2\), and
\[
F_{k+1}=F_k+F_{k-1}\qquad(k\ge2).
\]
A binary word is Zeckendorf-legal if it has no adjacent ones.  We set
\[
X_m=\{x\in\{0,1\}^m:x_ix_{i+1}\ne 11\text{ for all }1\le i<m\}.
\]
The cardinality is
\[
|X_m|=F_{m+1},
\]
and therefore
\[
\lim_{m\to\infty}\frac1m\log |X_m|=\log\varphi,
\qquad
\varphi=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{2}.
\]

For \(\omega=(\omega_1,\ldots,\omega_m)\in\Om_m\), define its Fibonacci value by
\[
N_m(\omega)=\sum_{k=1}^m \omega_kF_k.
\]
Zeckendorf's theorem states that every nonnegative integer has a unique Fibonacci expansion with no adjacent ones \cite{Lekkerkerker1952,Zeckendorf1972,Fraenkel1985}.  We write \(Z(N)\) for this normalized digit sequence and \(\pi_mZ(N)\) for its visible length-\(m\) prefix.

\begin{definition}[Golden folding map]
\label{def:golden-folding-map}
The depth-\(m\) golden folding map is
\[
\Fold_m:\Om_m\to X_m,\qquad
\Fold_m(\omega)=\pi_mZ(N_m(\omega)).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Fixed points]
\label{prop:fold-fixed-points}
Every \(x\in X_m\) is fixed by \(\Fold_m\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(x\in X_m\), then \(x\) is already a legal Zeckendorf prefix for its Fibonacci value.  Normalizing and truncating returns the same visible digits.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Role of \(\varphi\)]
The role of \(\varphi\) in this paper is grammatical rather than physical: it determines the legal syntax of stable representatives and the local carry-elimination rules.  The golden mean is used as a canonical anti-resonant normalization baseline, not as a postulate about nature.
\end{remark}
