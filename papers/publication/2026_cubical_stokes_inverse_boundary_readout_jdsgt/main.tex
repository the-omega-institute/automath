\begin{abstract}
We study the equation $\dd\eta=\omega$ on the cube $I^k=[0,1]^k$ when
$(k-1)$-forms are measured in the coefficient-wise $L^\infty$ norm induced by
the standard coordinate basis. For coordinate-monomial $k$-forms
$\omega=f\,\dd x_I$ we prove that the midpoint radial homotopy operator is a
sharp right inverse with operator norm $1/(2k)$, and we identify the
incidence obstruction that prevents the same factor from extending to larger
coordinate classes. In top degree this yields the exact value $1/(2k)$ for
the extremal problem
$\inf\{\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}:\ \dd\eta=\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\dd x_k\}$.
Using the corresponding crystalline $\ell_\infty/\ell_1$ calibration, we show
that every smooth minimizer has the same codimension-one trace as the explicit
radial primitive. We also derive a sharp decomposition estimate under the
assumption that $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial and transfer the same
constant to the Whitney complex of a single cube.
\end{abstract}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} radial homotopy operator, coefficient-$L^\infty$
estimates, anisotropic calibration, Whitney forms, boundary trace rigidity.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

In this paper we study bounded primitives for differential forms on the cube
when the norm is taken coefficientwise with respect to the standard
coordinate basis. Our starting point is the extremal problem
\[
\inf\left\{\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}:\ \dd\eta=\omega_0
\text{ on } I^k\right\},
\qquad
\omega_0:=\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k,
\]
for $(k-1)$-forms on $I^k=[0,1]^k$. The explicit primitive
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:explicit-minimizer}
\eta_\ast
:=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(x_j-\frac12\right)\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
satisfies $\dd\eta_\ast=\omega_0$ and
$\norm{\eta_\ast}_{\coeff,\infty}=1/(2k)$. One of the main results of the
paper shows that this value is optimal. A second result shows that equality
forces a rigid boundary behavior: every smooth minimizer has the same trace on
each codimension-one face of the cube as \eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer}. Thus
the extremal problem is rigid at the boundary, although it remains nonunique
in the interior.

More generally, we consider the midpoint radial homotopy operator $K_k$.
Because the coefficient norm depends on the preferred basis, the decisive
issue is not decomposability in the usual exterior-algebraic sense, but the
incidence pattern created by contraction with the radial vector field. A
single $(k-1)$-coefficient of $\iota_X\omega$ may receive contributions from
several $k$-coefficients of $\omega$, and the sharp constant is governed
exactly by this combinatorial feature. On the coordinate-monomial class
$\omega=f\,\dd x_I$ no accumulation occurs, and we prove the estimate
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
We also identify the exact incidence obstruction to this bound. In
particular, the factor $1/(2k)$ already fails for the decomposable form
$(\dd x_1+\dd x_2)\wedge \dd x_3$.

This homotopy estimate has two consequences that will be developed in the
paper. First, it yields a decomposition
\[
\omega=\dd\eta+E,
\qquad
\norm{E}_{\coeff,\infty}\le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty},
\]
whenever $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial; in degree one this becomes a
potential reconstruction statement from restricted curl data. Second, in top
degree the same constant combines with the crystalline
$\ell_\infty/\ell_1$ calibration to determine both the extremal value and the
boundary trace of all smooth minimizers. Writing
$\eta=\iota_V(\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k)$ identifies the latter
problem with the divergence equation $\Div V=1$ on $I^k$ and the anisotropic
norm $\norm{V}_{L^\infty(I^k;\ell_\infty)}=\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}$.

The continuous part of the argument lies in the circle of ideas surrounding
radial homotopy formulas and bounded right inverses for the de~Rham complex;
see
\cite[Sec.~3]{BottTu1982},
\cite{Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Bogovskii1979,IwaniecLutoborski1993,CostabelMcIntosh2010,GuzmanSalgado2021,ChaumontFreletLichtVohralik2025}.
The lower bound is naturally related to crystalline calibrations and
anisotropic isoperimetric inequalities
\cite{Taylor1978,Fonseca1991,FonsecaMuller1991,DacorognaPfister1992,FigalliZhang2022,DeMason2024},
as well as to anisotropic Cheeger problems and continuum max-flow/min-cut
duality
\cite{CasellesChambolleNovaga2007,CasellesChambolleMollNovaga2008,CasellesFaccioloMeinhardt2009,CasellesChambolleNovaga2011,Leonardi2015,Strang1983,Nozawa1990,Grieser2006,Strang2010}.
For the cubical statement in the last section we use the standard Whitney map
and its finite-element counterparts
\cite{Whitney1957,Dodziuk1976,Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,ArnoldFalkWinther2010,ArnoldAwanou2014,Albert2020}.

The point of the paper is that the coordinate coefficient norm singles out a
natural class on which the midpoint homotopy becomes a sharp
coefficient-$L^\infty$ right inverse, and that the same constant controls the
associated extremal problem on the cube. The cubical one-cube consequence is
then obtained by combining this continuous estimate with the Whitney and
integration maps.

\medskip
\noindent
The paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:cubical} collects the
continuous and cubical preliminaries. In Section~\ref{sec:restricted-bounds}
we prove the sharp homotopy estimate and its first consequences. The extremal
problem on the cube is treated in Section~\ref{sec:anisotropic}. The final
section contains the corresponding one-cube cubical statement.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:cubical}

\subsection{The Continuous Setting}

Fix $n\ge 1$ and write
\[
I^n := [0,1]^n,
\qquad
c := \left(\frac12,\dots,\frac12\right).
\]
For $1\le k\le n$, let $\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ denote the smooth
$k$-forms on a neighborhood of $I^n$, restricted to the cube.  If
\[
\omega = \sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I,
\]
we use the coefficient sup norm
\[
\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} := \max_{\abs{I}=k}\ \sup_{x\in I^n} \abs{a_I(x)}.
\]
At a fixed point $x$, we abbreviate
\[
\norm{\omega(x)}_{\coeff}:=\max_{\abs{I}=k}\abs{a_I(x)},
\]
so that $\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=\sup_{x\in I^n}\norm{\omega(x)}_{\coeff}$.
All coefficient-$L^\infty$ estimates below are understood with respect to
this norm. Throughout, $\dd$ denotes the exterior derivative on smooth forms,
whereas $d$ denotes the cubical coboundary on cochains.

\begin{definition}[Coordinate-monomial]
\label{def:decomposable-form}
A smooth $k$-form on $I^n$ is called coordinate-monomial if
$\omega=f\,\dd x_I$ for some smooth scalar function $f$ and some
$k$-index $I\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
This class is stricter than the usual notion of decomposability in
$\Lambda^kT^\ast I^n$.  For example,
$(\dd x_1+\dd x_2)\wedge \dd x_3$ is classically decomposable, but it is not
coordinate-monomial.
\end{remark}

Let $X(x):=x-c$ be the radial vector field centered at $c$, and let
\[
H:[0,1]\times I^n \to I^n,
\qquad
H(t,x):=c+t(x-c).
\]
For each $t\in[0,1]$, write $H_t(x):=H(t,x)$.  Thus $H_1=\mathrm{Id}$ and
$H_0$ collapses the cube to its center.

\begin{definition}[Radial homotopy operator]
\label{def:homotopy-operator}
For $1\le k\le n$ define
\[
K_k:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-operator}
(K_k\omega)(x)
:=
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\,\bigl(\iota_{x-c}\,\omega\bigr)\!\bigl(c+t(x-c)\bigr)\,\dd t,
\end{equation}
where for a fixed tangent vector $v\in\RR^n$ and a $k$-form
$\omega=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}a_I(y)\,\dd y_I$, we write
$(\iota_v\omega)(y):=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}a_I(y)\,\iota_v(\dd y_I)$, i.e., the
coefficients of $\omega$ are evaluated at the point $y$ and then contracted with
the vector $v$.  In particular, the contraction vector in
\eqref{eq:homotopy-operator} is the \emph{fixed} vector $x-c\in\RR^n$
(depending on the external point $x$, but \emph{not} on the integration
variable $t$), while the coefficients of $\omega$ are evaluated along the
straight-line homotopy $H_t(x)=c+t(x-c)$.

Equivalently, if $\omega=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}a_I(y)\,\dd y_I$, then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-operator-explicit}
(K_k\omega)(x)
=
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}
\sum_{\abs{I}=k}\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1}(x_{i_r}-c_{i_r})\,
a_I\bigl(c+t(x-c)\bigr)\,
\dd x_{I\setminus\{i_r\}}\,\dd t.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

For later reference we note that $K_1(\dd x_i)=x_i-\tfrac12$ and that
\[
K_k(\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k)
=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k(-1)^{j-1}(x_j-\tfrac12)\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k.
\]
In particular, the latter form coincides with \eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer}.

The operator is the standard radial homotopy formula centered at the cube
midpoint; compare \cite[Sec.~3]{BottTu1982} and
\cite{Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,CostabelMcIntosh2010}.

We extend the operator family by setting $K_{n+1}:=0$ on
$\Omega^{n+1}(I^n)=\{0\}$.  Under this convention the homotopy identity
$\dd K_k\omega + K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)=\omega$ holds uniformly for every
$1\le k\le n$: at top degree $k=n$ the term $K_{n+1}(\dd\omega)$ vanishes
because every $(n+1)$-form on $I^n$ is zero.

\begin{lemma}[Uniform contraction estimate on coordinate-monomial forms]
\label{lem:contraction-estimate}
For every coordinate-monomial
\[
\omega = f(y)\,\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}
\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
and every $x,y\in I^n$ one has
\[
\norm{\iota_{x-c}\omega(y)}_{\coeff} \le \frac12 \norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
In particular,
\[
\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac12 \norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For such a form,
\[
\iota_{x-c}\omega(y)
=
\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1} f(y)\bigl(x_{i_r}-\tfrac12\bigr)\,
\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_{i_r}}\wedge\cdots\wedge
\dd x_{i_k}.
\]
Each component of $X(x)=x-c$ has absolute value at most $1/2$ on $I^n$, so
every coefficient in $\iota_{x-c}\omega(y)$ has absolute value at most
$\frac12\sup_{z\in I^n}\abs{f(z)}=\frac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$.  Taking $y=x$
gives the displayed bound for $\iota_X\omega$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Incidence criterion for the coefficient-sup contraction bound]
\label{prop:incidence-criterion}
Let
\[
\omega = \sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I
\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n).
\]
For each $(k-1)$-index $J$ define
\[
m_J(\omega):=
\#\{i\notin J:\ a_{J\cup\{i\}}\not\equiv 0\},
\qquad
m(\omega):=\max_{\abs{J}=k-1} m_J(\omega).
\]
Then
\[
\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{m(\omega)}{2}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Consequently, if $\mathcal M$ is any collection of coordinate $k$-monomials
and
\[
\mathcal A(\mathcal M)
:=
\left\{
\sum_{I\in\mathcal M} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I \in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\right\},
\]
then the universal bound
\[
\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}\le \frac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}
\qquad
\text{for every }\omega\in\mathcal A(\mathcal M)
\]
holds if and only if every coordinate $(k-1)$-monomial is contained in at most
one member of $\mathcal M$. Coordinate-monomial forms
($m(\omega)\le 1$) satisfy this condition, while any class that permits two or
more $k$-monomials sharing a common $(k-1)$-factor does not.  For instance,
the class of sums
$a\,\dd x_{1}\wedge\dd x_3+b\,\dd x_2\wedge\dd x_3$ has $m_{\{3\}}=2$, and the
$(k-1)$-coefficient of $\dd x_3$ in $\iota_X\omega$ can reach
$\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$ (not $\tfrac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$) at a corner of
$I^n$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a fixed $(k-1)$-index $J$, the coefficient of $\dd x_J$ in $\iota_X\omega$
has the form
\[
\sum_{i\notin J}\sigma(J,i)\bigl(x_i-\tfrac12\bigr)a_{J\cup\{i\}}(x),
\]
where $\sigma(J,i)\in\{\pm 1\}$ is the sign obtained by inserting $i$ into the
ordered set $J$.  At each point $x$, at most $m_J(\omega)$ summands are
present, and each has absolute value at most $\frac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$.
Hence the coefficient of $\dd x_J$ is bounded by
$\frac{m_J(\omega)}{2}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$, and taking the maximum over $J$
gives the displayed estimate.

If every coordinate $(k-1)$-monomial is contained in at most one element of
$\mathcal M$, then every $\omega\in\mathcal A(\mathcal M)$ satisfies
$m(\omega)\le 1$, so the universal factor $\frac12$ follows from the displayed
estimate. Conversely, if two allowed monomials
$\dd x_{J\cup\{i\}}$ and $\dd x_{J\cup\{\ell\}}$ share
the same $(k-1)$-factor $\dd x_J$, choose the sign in the constant form
\[
\omega_0:=\dd x_{J\cup\{i\}}\pm \dd x_{J\cup\{\ell\}}
\]
so that their contributions to the $\dd x_J$-coefficient in $\iota_X\omega_0$
add.  Then $\norm{\omega_0}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$, while at the point with
$x_i=x_\ell=1$ that coefficient has absolute value $1$.  Therefore no
universal factor $\frac12$ can hold on any class that allows such a pair.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:non-coord-monomial}
The restriction to coordinate-monomial forms is essential for the coefficient
sup norm.  The form $(\dd x_1+\dd x_2)\wedge \dd x_3$ is classically
decomposable in the sense of exterior algebra, but it is not
coordinate-monomial.  On $I^3$ let
\[
\omega := \dd x_1\wedge \dd x_3 + \dd x_2\wedge \dd x_3.
\]
Then $\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$, while
\[
\iota_X\omega
=
\bigl(x_1-\tfrac12+x_2-\tfrac12\bigr)\dd x_3
-\bigl(x_3-\tfrac12\bigr)(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
so $\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$.  A direct computation gives
\[
K_2\omega
=
\frac{x_1+x_2-1}{2}\,\dd x_3
-\frac{x_3-\tfrac12}{2}\,(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
hence $\norm{K_2\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=\frac12>\frac14=\frac{1}{2k}$ when $k=2$.
The estimate \eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} therefore fails outside the
coordinate-monomial class.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Cubical Setting}

We also use the standard cubical cell decomposition of $I^n$.  Let
$C^k(I^n;\RR)$ be the space of real-valued cubical $k$-cochains with
coboundary $d$.  For $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ we write
\[
\norm{\omega}_{\cell,\infty} := \max_{\sigma^{(k)}} \abs{\omega(\sigma)},
\]
where the maximum runs over oriented $k$-cells.

For $I=\{i_1<\cdots<i_k\}\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$ and
$\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}$, let
\[
\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}
:=
\{x\in I^n:\ x_j=\varepsilon_j \text{ for every } j\notin I\}
\]
be the oriented $k$-cell parallel to
$\dd x_I=\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}$, and let
$\delta_{I,\varepsilon}\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ denote the dual basis cochain.  Set
\[
\phi_0(t):=1-t,
\qquad
\phi_1(t):=t,
\qquad
\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x):=\prod_{j\notin I}\phi_{\varepsilon_j}(x_j).
\]
Following the standard cubical Whitney construction
\cite{Whitney1957,Dodziuk1976,Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020} and its
finite-element variants \cite{ArnoldFalkWinther2010,ArnoldAwanou2014}, define
\[
W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})
:=
\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)\,\dd x_I
\]
and extend linearly to $C^k(I^n;\RR)$.  For a smooth $k$-form $\alpha$ on a
neighborhood of $I^n$, define the integration map by
\[
\mathcal I(\alpha)(\sigma)
:=
\int_\sigma \iota_\sigma^\ast \alpha,
\]
where $\iota_\sigma:\sigma\hookrightarrow I^n$ is the inclusion of the cell.

\begin{proposition}[Cubical Whitney formulas and norm-one bounds]
\label{prop:cubical-whitney}
For every $k$ and every $\theta\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$, the maps $W$ and
$\mathcal I$ satisfy
\[
W\theta
=
\sum_{\abs{I}=k}
\left(
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
\right)\dd x_I,
\qquad
\theta=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}\sum_{\varepsilon}\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\delta_{I,\varepsilon},
\]
\[
\mathcal I\circ W=\mathrm{Id},
\qquad
\dd\circ W = W\circ d,
\qquad
\mathcal I\circ \dd = d\circ \mathcal I,
\]
and
\[
\norm{W}_{\infty\to\infty}=1,
\qquad
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}=1.
\]
In particular, $W(C^k(I^n;\RR))\subset \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ consists
of multilinear polynomial differential forms.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The formula for $W\theta$ follows immediately from linearity. To verify
$\mathcal I\circ W=\mathrm{Id}$, it is enough to test on the basis cochains.
If $\sigma_{J,\eta}$ is an oriented $k$-cell, then
\[
\mathcal I(W\delta_{I,\varepsilon})(\sigma_{J,\eta})
=
\int_{\sigma_{J,\eta}}
\iota_{\sigma_{J,\eta}}^\ast\!\bigl(\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}\,\dd x_I\bigr).
\]
If $J\neq I$, then the pullback of $\dd x_I$ to $\sigma_{J,\eta}$ vanishes.
If $J=I$ but $\eta\neq\varepsilon$, then at least one fixed complementary
coordinate has value opposite to $\varepsilon_j$, so
$\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}$ vanishes identically on $\sigma_{I,\eta}$.  Finally,
if $(J,\eta)=(I,\varepsilon)$, then $\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}\equiv 1$ on
$\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ and the cell has unit $k$-volume, so the integral is
$1$.  Thus $\mathcal I(W\delta_{I,\varepsilon})=\delta_{I,\varepsilon}$.

To check $\dd W = Wd$, again work on a basis cochain.  For
$\ell\notin I$, let
\[
p(I,\ell):=1+\#\{i\in I:\ i<\ell\},
\]
the position of $\ell$ in the ordered set $I\cup\{\ell\}$.  The only
$(k+1)$-cell containing $\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ as a face with free
coordinate $\ell$ is
$\sigma_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}$, and its boundary
orientation gives the incidence sign $(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}$.
Hence
\[
d\delta_{I,\varepsilon}
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}
\delta_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
\dd\bigl(W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})\bigr)
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
\phi_{\varepsilon_\ell}'(x_\ell)
\prod_{\substack{j\notin I\\ j\neq \ell}}
\phi_{\varepsilon_j}(x_j)\,
\dd x_\ell\wedge \dd x_I.
\]
Since $\phi_0'=-1$, $\phi_1'=1$, and
$\dd x_\ell\wedge \dd x_I=(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-1}\dd x_{I\cup\{\ell\}}$, the last
display becomes
\[
\dd\bigl(W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})\bigr)
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}
W\!\left(
\delta_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}
\right)
=
W(d\delta_{I,\varepsilon}).
\]
Thus $\dd W=Wd$ on basis cochains, hence on all cochains.

For $\mathcal I\dd = d\mathcal I$, let $\sigma$ be an oriented $k$-cell.
Stokes' theorem on the cube cell gives
\[
\mathcal I(\dd\alpha)(\sigma)
=
\int_\sigma \iota_\sigma^\ast(\dd\alpha)
=
\int_{\partial\sigma} \iota_{\partial\sigma}^\ast \alpha
=
d(\mathcal I\alpha)(\sigma).
\]

For the operator norm of $W$, write
\[
W\theta
=
\sum_{\abs{I}=k} b_I(x)\,\dd x_I,
\qquad
b_I(x)
=
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x).
\]
For each fixed $I$, the functions $\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}$ are nonnegative
and satisfy
\[
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
=
\prod_{j\notin I}\bigl(\phi_0(x_j)+\phi_1(x_j)\bigr)
=
1.
\]
Therefore $b_I(x)$ is a convex combination of the numbers
$\theta_{I,\varepsilon}$, so
$\abs{b_I(x)}\le \norm{\theta}_{\cell,\infty}$ for every $x$.  Hence
$\norm{W\theta}_{\coeff,\infty}\le \norm{\theta}_{\cell,\infty}$.  Equality is attained on any
basis cochain $\delta_{I,\varepsilon}$, because
$\norm{W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$.  Thus
$\norm{W}_{\infty\to\infty}=1$.

For the operator norm of $\mathcal I$, write
\[
\alpha=\sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I.
\]
If $\sigma=\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$, then $\iota_\sigma^\ast \alpha$ retains
only the coefficient $a_I$, so
\[
\abs{\mathcal I(\alpha)(\sigma)}
=
\left|
\int_{\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}
\iota_{\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}^\ast\alpha
\right|
\le
\sup_{x\in \sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}\abs{a_I(x)}
\le
\norm{\alpha}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\le 1$.  Equality holds for the form
$\alpha=\dd x_I$, whose integral over $\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ is $1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Whitney coordinate-monomial cochain]
\label{def:whitney-decomposable}
A cubical $k$-cochain $\theta$ is called Whitney coordinate-monomial if
$W\theta$ is coordinate-monomial in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:decomposable-form}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Characterization of the Whitney coordinate-monomial class]
\label{prop:whitney-characterization}
A cubical $k$-cochain $\theta$ on $I^n$ is Whitney coordinate-monomial if and
only if there exists a single coordinate set
$I\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$ with $\abs{I}=k$ such that $\theta$ vanishes on every
$k$-cell not parallel to $\dd x_I$.  Equivalently,
\[
W\theta = f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I
\]
for a multilinear polynomial $f$ in the complementary variables.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $\theta$ vanishes on every $k$-cell outside one orientation class $I$, then
Proposition~\ref{prop:cubical-whitney} gives
\[
W\theta
=
\left(
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
\right)\dd x_I,
\]
which is of the required form, and the coefficient is multilinear in
$x_{I^c}$.

Conversely, suppose $W\theta=f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I$ for some $I$.  For each
$J\neq I$ with $\abs{J}=k$, the coefficient of $\dd x_J$ in $W\theta$ is
\[
g_J(x)
:=
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{J^c}}
\theta_{J,\varepsilon}\lambda_{J,\varepsilon}(x),
\]
and this coefficient vanishes identically.  Fix
$\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{J^c}$ and evaluate at any point satisfying
$x_j=\varepsilon_j$ for all $j\notin J$.  Then
$\lambda_{J,\varepsilon}(x)=1$ and every other
$\lambda_{J,\varepsilon'}(x)=0$, so $g_J(x)=\theta_{J,\varepsilon}=0$.  Hence
all coefficients on cells outside the orientation class $I$ vanish.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
In degree $2$, the Whitney coordinate-monomial condition means that there is a
single coordinate pair $(i,j)$ such that the cochain may be nonzero only on
squares parallel to the $(i,j)$-plane. This is precisely the restriction used
in the hypercube corollary of Section~\ref{sec:cubical-transfer}.
\end{remark}

\section{\texorpdfstring{The Sharp Coefficient-$L^\infty$ Estimate}{The Sharp Coefficient-L-infinity Estimate}}
\label{sec:restricted-bounds}

\subsection{The Main Estimate}

\begin{theorem}[Sharp homotopy estimate]
\label{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse}
Let $1\le k\le n$. In the setting of
Definition~\ref{def:homotopy-operator}, with the convention $K_{n+1}:=0$, one
has
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-identity}
\dd(K_k\omega) + K_{k+1}(\dd\omega) = \omega.
\end{equation}
In particular, every closed $k$-form on $I^n$ is exact. If, moreover,
$\omega$ is coordinate-monomial in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:decomposable-form}, then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound}
\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The constant is uniform in the ambient dimension. Moreover, if
$R:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ is a
linear map satisfying $\dd(R\omega)=\omega$ for every closed
coordinate-monomial $k$-form $\omega$, then
\[
\sup\Bigl\{\frac{\norm{R\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}}{\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}}:\
\omega\text{ closed coordinate-monomial}, \omega\neq 0\Bigr\}
\ge \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
Hence $1/(2k)$ is the optimal constant on this class.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We first establish \eqref{eq:homotopy-identity}. The argument is standard, but
we record it because the same coordinate representation will be used in the
proof of \eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound}.
Write $\omega=\sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(y)\,\dd y_I$ in a neighborhood of $I^n$
and set $H(t,x)=c+t(x-c)$.  Since
\[
H^\ast(\dd y_j)=t\,\dd x_j+(x_j-c_j)\,\dd t,
\]
each wedge product contains at most one $\dd t$ factor.  Expanding
$H^\ast\omega$ therefore gives a unique decomposition
\[
H^\ast\omega=A+ \dd t\wedge B,
\]
where the terms contributing to $A$ pick up $k$ tangential factors $t\,\dd x_i$
and the terms contributing to $B$ pick up exactly one $\dd t$ and therefore
only $k-1$ such tangential factors. Accordingly,
\[
A(t,x)=
t^k\sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(H(t,x))\,\dd x_I
\]
and
\[
B(t,x)=
t^{k-1}\sum_{\abs{I}=k}\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1}
\bigl(x_{i_r}-c_{i_r}\bigr)a_I(H(t,x))\,
\dd x_{I\setminus\{i_r\}}.
\]
Comparing with \eqref{eq:homotopy-operator-explicit}, one sees that
$B(t,x)=t^{k-1}(\iota_{x-c}\omega)(H_t(x))$, so
\[
K_k\omega(x)=\int_0^1 B(t,x)\,\dd t.
\]

Applying the same expansion to $\dd\omega$ yields
\[
H^\ast(\dd\omega)=C+\dd t\wedge D
\]
for some forms $C$ and $D$, and the $\dd t$-component is exactly
\[
D(t,x)=
t^k\sum_{\abs{J}=k+1}\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} (-1)^{r-1}
\bigl(x_{j_r}-c_{j_r}\bigr)b_J(H(t,x))\,
\dd x_{J\setminus\{j_r\}},
\]
where $\dd\omega=\sum_{\abs{J}=k+1} b_J(y)\,\dd y_J$.  Hence
\[
K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)(x)=\int_0^1 D(t,x)\,\dd t.
\]

Now use $\dd(H^\ast\omega)=H^\ast(\dd\omega)$.  Since
$\dd=\dd_x+\dd t\wedge \partial_t$ on $[0,1]\times I^n$, one has
\[
\dd(A+\dd t\wedge B)=\dd_x A + \dd t\wedge(\partial_t A-\dd_x B).
\]
Comparing the $\dd t$-components with $H^\ast(\dd\omega)=C+\dd t\wedge D$
gives
\[
\partial_t A-\dd_x B=D.
\]
Integrating in $t$ from $0$ to $1$ yields
\[
A(1,x)-A(0,x)=
\dd_x\!\left(\int_0^1 B(t,x)\,\dd t\right)
+ \int_0^1 D(t,x)\,\dd t.
\]
Because $A(1,x)=\omega(x)$ and $A(0,x)=0$ for $k\ge 1$, this is exactly
\[
\omega=\dd(K_k\omega)+K_{k+1}(\dd\omega),
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:homotopy-identity}.

For the norm bound, assume now that $\omega=f(y)\,\dd x_I$ is
coordinate-monomial.  By \eqref{eq:homotopy-operator}, each coefficient of
$K_k\omega$ at a point $x$ is bounded by
$\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\cdot\frac12\abs{f(c+t(x-c))}\,\dd t$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:contraction-estimate} with the contraction vector $x-c$ and
the coefficient $f$ evaluated at $y=c+t(x-c)\in I^n$).  Since
$\abs{f(y)}\le\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$ for all $y\in I^n$,
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}
\le
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\,\dd t \cdot \frac12 \norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}
=
\frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]

For sharpness, consider the constant form
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
and the $k$-face
\[
F := [0,1]^k \times \{0\}^{n-k}\subset I^n.
\]
Let $\iota_F:F\hookrightarrow I^n$ denote the inclusion.  If
$\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ satisfies $\dd\eta=\omega_0$, then
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1
=
\int_F \iota_F^\ast\omega_0
=
\int_{\partial F} \iota_{\partial F}^\ast\eta.
\]
For each codimension-one face $G\subset \partial F$, the pullback
$\iota_G^\ast\eta$ is a scalar multiple of the oriented $(k-1)$-volume form on
$G$, and that scalar coefficient is bounded in absolute value by
$\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}$. Since $\partial F$ has $2k$ faces, each with
$(k-1)$-dimensional volume $1$, it follows that
\[
1
=
\abs{\int_{\partial F}\iota_{\partial F}^\ast\eta}
\le
\sum_{G\subset\partial F} \abs{\int_G \iota_G^\ast \eta}
\le
2k\,\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}\ge 1/(2k)$.  Taking $\eta=R\omega_0$ yields the lower
bound for every right inverse $R$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Consequences}

\begin{proposition}[Decomposition with controlled defect]
\label{thm:homotopy-decomposition}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and $\omega\in\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$.  Assume that
$\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.  Define
\[
\eta := K_k\omega \in \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n),
\qquad
E := K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact-plus-defect}
\omega = \dd\eta + E,
\qquad
\dd E = \dd\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:optimal-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is sharp.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Identity \eqref{eq:exact-plus-defect} is simply
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $\eta=K_k\omega$ and
$E=K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)$. Since $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial, applying
\eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to $K_{k+1}$ gives
\eqref{eq:optimal-defect-bound}.

To prove sharpness, consider the constant $(k+1)$-form
\[
\nu_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}
\]
and set $\omega_0 := K_{k+1}\nu_0$.  Then $\dd\omega_0=\nu_0$ and
$\norm{\dd\omega_0}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$. Assume that a constant $C$ satisfies
the conclusion of the proposition for every $\omega$ with
coordinate-monomial $\dd\omega$. Applying that assertion to $\omega_0$ yields
a decomposition
\[
\omega_0 = \dd\phi + \widetilde E,
\qquad
\dd\widetilde E = \nu_0,
\qquad
\norm{\widetilde E}_{\coeff,\infty} \le C.
\]
\[
1
=
\int_{I^{k+1}} \nu_0
=
\int_{I^{k+1}} \dd\widetilde E
=
\int_{\partial I^{k+1}} \iota_{\partial I^{k+1}}^\ast \widetilde E.
\]
For each codimension-one face $G\subset\partial I^{k+1}$, the pullback
$\iota_G^\ast\widetilde E$ is a scalar multiple of the oriented $k$-volume form
on $G$, and that scalar coefficient is bounded by
$\norm{\widetilde E}_{\coeff,\infty}$. Since $\partial I^{k+1}$ has $2(k+1)$
faces of unit $k$-volume,
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial I^{k+1}} \iota_{\partial I^{k+1}}^\ast \widetilde E}
\le
\sum_{G\subset\partial I^{k+1}} \abs{\int_G \iota_G^\ast \widetilde E}
\le
2(k+1)\norm{\widetilde E}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\widetilde E}_{\coeff,\infty} \ge 1/(2(k+1))$.
No smaller uniform constant can hold even on this restricted class.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Potential reconstruction from restricted curl]
\label{cor:curl-reconstruction}
Let $v$ be a smooth vector field on a neighborhood of $I^n$ and set
\[
\omega := \sum_{i=1}^n v_i\,\dd x_i.
\]
Assume that the curl form $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.
Then $U:=K_1\omega$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:curl-potential-bound}
\max_{1\le i\le n}\sup_{x\in I^n}\abs{v_i(x)-\partial_i U(x)}
\le \frac14 \norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/4$ is sharp.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Set $U:=K_1\omega$. By \eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $k=1$,
\[
\omega = \dd U + K_2(\dd\omega).
\]
The coefficient vector of the $1$-form $\omega-\dd U$ is exactly
$v-\nabla U$, so its coefficient sup norm is the left-hand side of
\eqref{eq:curl-potential-bound}. Applying \eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound}
to $K_2$ yields
\eqref{eq:curl-potential-bound}.

For sharpness, let $\nu_0:=\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2$ and $\alpha:=K_2\nu_0$. Then
$\dd\alpha=\nu_0$ and $\norm{\nu_0}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$.  For every $U$,
\[
\dd(\alpha-\dd U)=\nu_0.
\]
Let
\[
Q:=[0,1]^2\times\{0\}^{n-2}\subset I^n
\]
and let $\iota_Q:Q\hookrightarrow I^n$ be the inclusion.  Stokes' theorem on
$Q$ gives
\[
1
=
\int_Q \iota_Q^\ast\nu_0
=
\int_Q \iota_Q^\ast\dd(\alpha-\dd U)
=
\int_{\partial Q} \iota_{\partial Q}^\ast(\alpha-\dd U).
\]
The boundary of $Q$ has four edges of unit length, so
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial Q} \iota_{\partial Q}^\ast(\alpha-\dd U)}
\le
4\norm{\alpha-\dd U}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\alpha-\dd U}_{\coeff,\infty}\ge 1/4$.
Therefore no constant smaller than $1/4$ can work uniformly.
\end{proof}

\section{The Extremal Problem on the Cube}
\label{sec:anisotropic}

We now turn to the top-degree extremal problem. For the coefficient
$\ell_\infty$ norm used throughout the paper, the dual boundary functional is
the crystalline $\ell_1$ perimeter
\[
P_1(E)
:=
\int_{\partial E} \abs{\nu_E}_1\,\dd\Haus^{n-1},
\qquad
\abs{\nu_E}_1 := \sum_{i=1}^n \abs{(\nu_E)_i}.
\]
The cube is the corresponding Wulff shape. Accordingly, the lower bound is a
direct instance of the usual crystalline calibration argument, which may also
be viewed as the continuum form of $\ell_\infty/\ell_1$ max-flow/min-cut
duality; see \cite{Strang1983,Nozawa1990,Grieser2006,Strang2010}.

\subsection{Calibration Lower Bound}

Throughout this section vectors are measured in the coordinate
$\ell_\infty$ norm
\[
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}
:=
\operatorname*{ess\,sup}_{x\in\Omega} \max_{1\le i\le n} \abs{V_i(x)},
\]
which matches $\norm{\iota_V\mathrm{vol}}_{\coeff,\infty}$.  We keep this norm,
rather than the Euclidean/comass norm, so that the dual boundary term is
exactly the crystalline perimeter $P_1$ appearing in the references above.

\begin{lemma}[Calibration lower bound]
\label{lem:boundary-calibration}
Let $\Omega\subset\RR^n$ be a bounded Lipschitz domain, and let
\[
\mathrm{vol} := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n.
\]
Define
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)
:=
\inf\Bigl\{
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}:
V\in C^\infty(U;\RR^n),\ U\supset \overline{\Omega}\ \text{open},
\ \Div V = 1 \text{ on }\Omega
\Bigr\},
\]
or, equivalently, the infimum of $\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}$ over smooth
$(n-1)$-forms $\eta$ on a neighborhood of $\overline\Omega$ satisfying
$\dd\eta=\mathrm{vol}$ on $\Omega$.  Then every admissible vector field $V$
and every Lipschitz subdomain $E\subset \Omega$ satisfy
\[
\Vol(E)\le \norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}\, P_1(E).
\]
Consequently,
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)\ge
\sup\left\{
\frac{\Vol(E)}{P_1(E)}:
E\subset \Omega,\ E \text{ Lipschitz}
\right\}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The two formulations of $\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)$ agree because, for a
smooth vector field $V$, the form $\eta:=\iota_V\mathrm{vol}$ satisfies
$\dd\eta=(\Div V)\mathrm{vol}$ and
$\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}=\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}$. The
divergence theorem on Lipschitz domains used below is standard; see
\cite{Federer1969,EvansGariepy2015,Maggi2012}.

Now let $E\subset\Omega$ be a Lipschitz subdomain and let $V$ be admissible.
The divergence theorem gives
\[
\Vol(E)
=
\int_E \Div V\,\dd x
=
\int_{\partial E} V\cdot \nu_E\,\dd\Haus^{n-1}
\le
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}
\int_{\partial E} \abs{\nu_E}_1\,\dd\Haus^{n-1}
=
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}\,P_1(E).
\]
Since $|V\cdot \nu_E|\le \norm{V}_{\ell_\infty}|\nu_E|_1$ pointwise, the desired
inequality follows.  Taking the supremum over $E$ and then the infimum over
admissible $V$ yields the stated lower bound.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Boundary Trace Rigidity on the Cube}

\begin{definition}[Face trace on the cube]
\label{def:face-trace}
For $\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^k)$ define
\[
\Tr_{\partial I^k}(\eta)
:=
\left\{\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta\right\}_{1\le j\le k,\ \varepsilon\in\{0,1\}},
\]
where
\[
F_{j,\varepsilon}:=\{x_j=\varepsilon\},
\qquad
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}:F_{j,\varepsilon}\hookrightarrow I^k.
\]
Because all members of this family come from one smooth form, their pullbacks to
codimension-two intersections agree automatically.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Extremal value on the cube]
\label{lem:cube-extremal-value}
Let
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
on $I^k=[0,1]^k$.  Then
\[
\inf\left\{
\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}:
\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^k),\ \dd\eta=\omega_0
\right\}
=
\frac{1}{2k}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Lemma~\ref{lem:boundary-calibration} applied with $E=I^k$ gives
\[
\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}\ge
\frac{\Vol(I^k)}{P_1(I^k)}
=
\frac{1}{2k}
\]
for every admissible $\eta$, because $\Vol(I^k)=1$ and $P_1(I^k)=2k$.

For the reverse inequality, consider the form $\eta_\ast$ from
\eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer}. Differentiating term by term gives
\[
\dd\eta_\ast
=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}\dd x_j\wedge
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
=
\omega_0.
\]
Since $\abs{x_j-\frac12}\le \frac12$ on $I^k$, one has
$\norm{\eta_\ast}_{\coeff,\infty}=1/(2k)$.  Hence the infimum is exactly
$1/(2k)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Boundary trace rigidity of minimizers]
\label{thm:boundary-rigidity}
Let $\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^k)$ be a minimizer for the extremal
problem in Lemma~\ref{lem:cube-extremal-value}; that is,
\[
\dd\eta = \omega_0,
\qquad
\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty} = \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
Then
\[
\Tr_{\partial I^k}(\eta)=\Tr_{\partial I^k}(\eta_\ast).
\]
Equivalently, for each $j\in\{1,\dots,k\}$ and $\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast \eta
=
(-1)^{j-1}\frac{\varepsilon-\tfrac12}{k}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
on the face $F_{j,\varepsilon}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1
=
\int_{I^k}\omega_0
=
\int_{\partial I^k}\iota_{\partial I^k}^\ast\eta
=
\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(
\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta
-
\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta
\right).
\]
For notational convenience set
\[
A_{j,1}
:=
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta,
\qquad
A_{j,0}
:=
-(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta.
\]
Then
\[
1=\sum_{j=1}^k (A_{j,1}+A_{j,0}).
\]
Each face has $(k-1)$-dimensional volume $1$, and on each face the pullback of
$\eta$ is a scalar multiple of the induced orientation form. Since
$\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}=1/(2k)$,
\[
\abs{A_{j,\varepsilon}}
\le
\frac{1}{2k}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
1
=
\sum_{j=1}^k (A_{j,1}+A_{j,0})
\le
\sum_{j=1}^k\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}}
\abs{A_{j,\varepsilon}}
\le
2k\cdot \frac{1}{2k}
=
1.
\]
Equality therefore holds throughout. Since each $A_{j,\varepsilon}$ is bounded
above by $1/(2k)$ and the sum of the $2k$ terms already attains the maximal
possible value $1$, one must have
\[
A_{j,1}=A_{j,0}=\frac{1}{2k}
\qquad
\text{for every }j\in\{1,\dots,k\}.
\]
Equivalently,
\[
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta = \frac{1}{2k},
\qquad
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta = -\frac{1}{2k}.
\]

Now each pullback has the form
\[
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta
=
f_{j,\varepsilon}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
for a continuous scalar function $f_{j,\varepsilon}$ on $F_{j,\varepsilon}$;
this is because, after pullback to the $(k-1)$-dimensional face
$F_{j,\varepsilon}$, only the tangential monomial
$\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k$ can
remain.  Moreover $\abs{f_{j,\varepsilon}}\le 1/(2k)$ everywhere on
$F_{j,\varepsilon}$, hence
\[
(-1)^{j-1}(2\varepsilon-1)f_{j,\varepsilon}\le \frac{1}{2k}
\qquad
\text{on }F_{j,\varepsilon}.
\]
Assume that this inequality is strict at some point
$p\in F_{j,\varepsilon}$.  By continuity, there exist a neighborhood
$U\subset F_{j,\varepsilon}$ of $p$ and a number $\delta>0$ such that
\[
(-1)^{j-1}(2\varepsilon-1)f_{j,\varepsilon}\le \frac{1}{2k}-\delta
\qquad
\text{on }U.
\]
Since $\Haus^{k-1}(U)>0$ and $\Haus^{k-1}(F_{j,\varepsilon})=1$, it follows
that
\[
A_{j,\varepsilon}
=
\int_{F_{j,\varepsilon}}
(-1)^{j-1}(2\varepsilon-1)f_{j,\varepsilon}
<
\frac{1}{2k},
\]
contrary to the preceding equality $A_{j,\varepsilon}=1/(2k)$. Therefore
\[
f_{j,\varepsilon}\equiv
(-1)^{j-1}\frac{\varepsilon-\tfrac12}{k}
\qquad
\text{on }F_{j,\varepsilon}.
\]
This yields
\eqref{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}, hence
$\Tr_{\partial I^k}(\eta)=\Tr_{\partial I^k}(\eta_\ast)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[The canonical minimizer]
\label{cor:explicit-minimizer}
The form $\eta_\ast$ in \eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer} is a minimizer of
$\dd\eta=\omega_0$ on $I^k$, and its face trace is the canonical one from
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Lemma~\ref{lem:cube-extremal-value} shows that $\eta_\ast$ attains the minimum
$1/(2k)$. Restricting \eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer} to the faces
$x_j=0$ and $x_j=1$ gives \eqref{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula} directly.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Nonuniqueness in the interior]
Assume $k\ge 2$. Lemma~\ref{lem:cube-extremal-value} and
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity} show that $\mathcal C_\infty(I^k)=1/(2k)$
and that all smooth minimizers share the same codimension-one trace, but they
do not imply interior uniqueness. Indeed, if $\psi$ is a smooth $(k-2)$-form
compactly supported in the interior of $I^k$ and
$\eta_\varepsilon:=\eta_\ast+\varepsilon\,\dd\psi$, then
$\dd\eta_\varepsilon=\omega_0$ for every $\varepsilon$,
$\Tr_{\partial I^k}(\eta_\varepsilon)=\Tr_{\partial I^k}(\eta_\ast)$, and
$\norm{\eta_\varepsilon}_{\coeff,\infty}=1/(2k)$ for all sufficiently small
$|\varepsilon|$. Indeed, on the compact support of $\dd\psi$ each coefficient
of $\eta_\ast$ stays a positive distance below $1/(2k)$, so a sufficiently
small perturbation preserves the same coefficient-$L^\infty$ norm. The
multiplicity of minimizers is therefore an interior phenomenon. By contrast,
for $k=1$ the minimizer is unique and equals $x_1-\tfrac12$ on $I$.
\end{remark}

\section{Cubical Consequences on a Single Cube}
\label{sec:cubical-transfer}

We conclude with the one-cube cubical counterpart of the preceding estimate.
On a single cube, the same sharp constant is recovered through the Whitney and
integration maps, without introducing an additional cochain homotopy beyond
the continuous operator already constructed above; compare
\cite{Dodziuk1976,Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020}. Since Whitney forms on
$I^n$ are global multilinear polynomials, they belong to
$\Omega^\bullet_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$, and the smooth homotopy identity from
Section~\ref{sec:restricted-bounds} applies directly.

\begin{proposition}[Cubical Stokes--Poincar\'e decomposition on one cube]
\label{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and let $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ be a cubical $k$-cochain
on the single standard cube $I^n$.  Assume that $d\omega$
is Whitney coordinate-monomial, i.e., $d\omega$ vanishes on every
$(k+1)$-cell outside a single cubical orientation class.  Then there exist
\[
\eta\in C^{k-1}(I^n;\RR),
\qquad
E\in C^k(I^n;\RR)
\]
such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-decomposition}
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_{\cell,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_{\cell,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is sharp.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each $1\le j\le n$ define
\[
\mathcal H_j := \mathcal I\circ K_j\circ W:
C^j(I^n;\RR)\to C^{j-1}(I^n;\RR).
\]
In particular,
\[
\mathcal H_{k+1}:C^{k+1}(I^n;\RR)\to C^k(I^n;\RR).
\]
Using the identities from Section~\ref{sec:cubical}, we obtain
\[
d\mathcal H_j + \mathcal H_{j+1}d = \mathrm{Id}
\qquad
\text{on } C^j(I^n;\RR)
\]
for every $1\le j\le n-1$. Indeed, for $\theta\in C^j(I^n;\RR)$,
\[
\begin{aligned}
d\mathcal H_j\theta + \mathcal H_{j+1}d\theta
&=
d(\mathcal I K_jW\theta)+\mathcal I K_{j+1}W(d\theta) \\
&=
\mathcal I(\dd K_jW\theta)+\mathcal I K_{j+1}(\dd W\theta) \\
&=
\mathcal I(\dd K_j + K_{j+1}\dd)W\theta \\
&=
\mathcal I W\theta
=
\theta,
\end{aligned}
\]
where we used $\mathcal I\dd=d\mathcal I$, $\dd W = Wd$, and
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} on the smooth form $W\theta$.
Now set
\[
\eta := \mathcal H_k\omega,
\qquad
E := \mathcal H_{k+1}(d\omega).
\]
Then
\[
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\norm{E}_{\cell,\infty}
\le
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\,
\norm{K_{k+1}(W(d\omega))}_{\coeff,\infty}
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{W(d\omega)}_{\coeff,\infty}
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_{\cell,\infty},
\]
where the second inequality uses that $W(d\omega)$ is coordinate-monomial and
the last inequality uses Proposition~\ref{prop:cubical-whitney}.  This proves
\eqref{eq:discrete-defect-bound}.

For sharpness it is enough to treat the case $n=k+1$.  Let
$\theta_{\mathrm{top}}\in C^{k+1}(I^{k+1};\RR)$ be the cochain taking the value
$1$ on the unique oriented top cell.  Its Whitney form is
$\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}$, so $\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$ is
Whitney coordinate-monomial.  Since $I^{k+1}$ is contractible and there are no
$(k+2)$-cells, there exists $\omega_0\in C^k(I^{k+1};\RR)$ with
$d\omega_0=\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$.  If
\[
\omega_0 = d\phi + \widetilde E,
\qquad
d\widetilde E = d\omega_0 = \theta_{\mathrm{top}},
\]
then
\[
1
=
\theta_{\mathrm{top}}(I^{k+1})
=
d\widetilde E(I^{k+1})
=
\sum_{F\subset\partial I^{k+1}} \pm \widetilde E(F).
\]
There are $2(k+1)$ codimension-one faces, hence
\[
1 \le 2(k+1)\norm{\widetilde E}_{\cell,\infty}.
\]
Therefore every such defect term satisfies
$\norm{\widetilde E}_{\cell,\infty}\ge 1/(2(k+1))$, so the constant cannot be
improved uniformly on the restricted class.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The operator $\mathcal H_j=\mathcal I K_jW$ is defined and used here only for a
single cube $I^n$. On a general cubical complex, the same formula can be
applied cellwise on any star identified with a cube, but the resulting
cochains need not be globally compatible across neighboring cubes.
Constructing a global bounded cochain homotopy on an arbitrary cubical complex
requires either partition-of-unity techniques or an algebraic subdivision
argument (e.g., the Dupont--Albert framework
\cite{Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020}); such constructions lie beyond the
scope of the present paper.
\end{remark}

Let $\{0,1\}^m$ denote the $m$-dimensional hypercube graph, identified with the
1-skeleton of $I^m$.  Squares correspond to $2$-faces, so $d\omega$ records the
square defect of an edge cochain $\omega$.

\begin{corollary}[Hypercube corollary]
\label{cor:hypercube-gradient}
Let $\omega\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ with Whitney coordinate-monomial square defect, and
set $\varepsilon:=\norm{d\omega}_{\cell,\infty}$.  Then:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item There exist $f\in C^0(I^m;\RR)$ and $r\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ such that
  $\omega=df+r$, $dr=d\omega$, and $\norm{r}_{\cell,\infty}\le \varepsilon/4$.
  \item If $\omega(u,v)=\log\frac{q(u,v)}{q(v,u)}$ for positive jump rates
  $q(u,v)$ on oriented edges, then the same potential $f$ satisfies
  \[
  e^{-\varepsilon/4}
  \le
  \frac{\pi_f(u)\,q(u,v)}{\pi_f(v)\,q(v,u)}
  \le
  e^{\varepsilon/4},
  \qquad
  \pi_f(u):=e^{f(u)},\ u\sim v.
  \]
\end{itemize}
No smaller constant works uniformly within this Whitney coordinate-monomial
class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition~\ref{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare} with $k=1$ to obtain
$(f,r)=(\eta,E)$. The second item is simply the logarithmic reformulation of the
same bound, and sharpness reduces to the proposition.
\end{proof}
