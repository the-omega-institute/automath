\title{A Sharp Coefficient-Sup Estimate for the Radial Homotopy on Coordinate-Monomial Forms, with Cubical and Dyadic Corollaries}
\author{Haobo Ma\\CHRONOAI PTE. LTD.\\Singapore}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study an explicit bounded inverse problem on the unit cube for the
coefficient $L^\infty$ norm.  For smooth closed coordinate-monomial $k$-forms
$f\,\dd x_I$, the standard radial homotopy operator $K_k$ satisfies the sharp
ambient-dimension-free bound
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty
\]
and this yields an optimal decomposition when $\dd\omega$ is
coordinate-monomial.  In degree one this gives an optimal coefficient-$L^\infty$
reconstruction bound for vector fields whose curl $2$-form is
coordinate-monomial.  We then transfer the same constant to cubical cochains
whose coboundaries lie in a single cubical orientation class by writing the
cubical Whitney forms explicitly and proving that the Whitney and integration
maps have operator norm one for the relevant sup norms.  In this restricted
cubical class we obtain sharp hypercube correction and near-detailed-balance
statements.  The distinctive rigidity statement is that every
coefficient-$L^\infty$ minimizer for
$\dd\eta=\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k$ on $I^k$ has the same boundary
trace.  Separately, for dyadic outer approximations of compact sets, a fixed
probe form reads off the volume exactly and hence recovers upper Minkowski
dimension from a boundary-flux sequence.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The point of this paper is narrow.  We record a sharp coefficient-$L^\infty$
estimate for the standard radial homotopy operator on a restricted class of
forms, transfer the same estimate to the corresponding cubical Whitney class on
a single cube, and isolate one equality-case rigidity statement for boundary
traces.  Let $I^n=[0,1]^n$.  Given a closed coordinate-monomial $k$-form
$\omega$ on $I^n$, can one choose a primitive whose coefficient $L^\infty$
size is controlled by an explicit universal constant?  If $\omega$ has
coordinate-monomial exterior derivative, can one still separate it into an
exact part plus an error term that depends only on $\dd\omega$?

The answer is especially clean for the radial contraction from the cube center.
Because the coefficient sup norm is taken componentwise, the sharp contraction
estimate behind the inverse bound is valid only for forms consisting of one
fixed coordinate wedge monomial.  On that restricted class the associated
homotopy operator $K_k$ satisfies the sharp ambient-dimension-free estimate
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty,
\]
and the constant $1/(2k)$ cannot be improved.  Applied to forms with
coordinate-monomial exterior derivative, this yields an optimal
decomposition
\[
\omega = \dd\eta + E,
\qquad
\norm{E}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_\infty.
\]
The degree-one case recovers an optimal sup-norm estimate for approximating a
vector field by a gradient from its coordinate-monomial curl tensor.

The admissible cubical class is equally narrow.  A cubical $k$-cochain is
Whitney coordinate-monomial precisely when it is supported on one orientation
class of $k$-cells, or equivalently when its Whitney form is
$f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I$ for one fixed $I$.  In degree two on a hypercube, this
means that square defects may be nonzero only on squares parallel to a single
coordinate $2$-plane.  For this class the same sharp constant survives
discretization.  Using explicit cubical Whitney forms and the integration map,
the continuous homotopy operator transfers to the standard cubical cochain
complex.  This gives a sharp cubical Stokes--Poincar\'e lemma, an optimal
square-defect correction statement on the hypercube, and a logarithmic
near-detailed-balance corollary for edge jump asymmetries in that same
restricted class.

The boundary side of the paper is more rigid than the inverse bound alone.  We
record a boundary calibration inequality for primitives of the volume form and
then show that on the cube every minimizer has the same boundary trace.  Thus
the optimal primitive is not only norm-sharp but boundary-rigid.

The dyadic outer-approximation theorem in Section~6 is logically independent
of the sharp inverse theory in Sections~3--5.  It uses only a primitive of the
volume form together with polyhedral Stokes.  What Section~5 contributes is
canonicality of a centered probe form on the cube, not the flux identity
itself.  In particular, for a compact set $A\subset I^n$, the boundary of the
dyadic outer approximation $U_m(A)$ can be tested by one universal
$(n-1)$-form $\omega_0$, and the flux identity
\[
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0 = \Vol(U_m(A))
\]
turns boundary data into a direct Minkowski-dimension readout.

The de Rham homotopy formula, bounded right inverses on star-shaped domains,
cubical Whitney transfer, polyhedral Stokes theorem, and dyadic cube-count
characterization of Minkowski dimension are standard; see
\cite{BottTu1982,Bogovskii1979,IwaniecLutoborski1993,CostabelMcIntosh2010,Whitney1957,Dupont1976,ArnoldFalkWinther2010,ArnoldAwanou2014,Federer1969,EvansGariepy2015,Maggi2012,Falconer2014,Mattila1995}.
The contribution here is the sharp coefficient-$L^\infty$ constant for the
standard radial homotopy operator on the restricted class $f\,\dd x_I$, its
transfer to the corresponding cubical Whitney class on the single cube, and
the equality-case boundary trace theorem for cube minimizers.  The theorem
chain used in Sections~3--5 is summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:constants-overview}.

\section{Cubical Forms and Homotopy Operators}

\subsection{Continuous Cubical Setup}

Fix $n\ge 1$ and write
\[
I^n := [0,1]^n,
\qquad
c := \left(\frac12,\dots,\frac12\right).
\]
For $1\le k\le n$, let $\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ denote the smooth
$k$-forms on a neighborhood of $I^n$, restricted to the cube.  If
\[
\omega = \sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I,
\]
we use the coefficient sup norm
\[
\norm{\omega}_\infty := \max_{\abs{I}=k}\ \sup_{x\in I^n} \abs{a_I(x)}.
\]
All $L^\infty$ statements below refer to this coordinate norm.

\begin{definition}[Coordinate-monomial form]
\label{def:decomposable-form}
A smooth $k$-form on $I^n$ is called coordinate-monomial if it has the
form
\[
\omega = f(x)\,\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}
\]
for some smooth scalar function $f$ and some ordered index set
$1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
This class is stricter than the usual notion of decomposability in
$\Lambda^kT^\ast I^n$.  For example,
$(\dd x_1+\dd x_2)\wedge \dd x_3$ is classically decomposable, but it is not
coordinate-monomial.
\end{remark}

Let $X(x):=x-c$ be the radial vector field centered at $c$, and let
\[
H:[0,1]\times I^n \to I^n,
\qquad
H(t,x):=c+t(x-c).
\]
For each $t\in[0,1]$, write $H_t(x):=H(t,x)$.  Thus $H_1=\mathrm{Id}$ and
$H_0$ collapses the cube to its center.

\begin{definition}[Cubical homotopy operator]
\label{def:homotopy-operator}
For $1\le k\le n$ define
\[
K_k:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-operator}
(K_k\omega)(x)
:=
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\bigl(\iota_X\omega\bigr)\bigl(H_t(x)\bigr)\,\dd t.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The operator is the standard radial homotopy formula centered at the cube
midpoint; compare \cite[Sec.~3]{BottTu1982} and the bounded right-inverse
literature \cite{Bogovskii1979,IwaniecLutoborski1993,CostabelMcIntosh2010}.

We extend the operator family by setting $K_{n+1}:=0$ on
$\Omega^{n+1}(I^n)=\{0\}$.  Under this convention the homotopy identity
$\dd K_k\omega + K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)=\omega$ holds uniformly for every
$1\le k\le n$: at top degree $k=n$ the term $K_{n+1}(\dd\omega)$ vanishes
because every $(n+1)$-form on $I^n$ is zero.

\begin{lemma}[Contraction estimate on coordinate-monomial forms]
\label{lem:contraction-estimate}
For every coordinate-monomial
\[
\omega = f(x)\,\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}
\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
one has
\[
\norm{\iota_X\omega}_\infty \le \frac12 \norm{\omega}_\infty.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For such a form,
\[
\iota_X\omega
=
\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1} f(x)\bigl(x_{i_r}-\tfrac12\bigr)\,
\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_{i_r}}\wedge\cdots\wedge
\dd x_{i_k}.
\]
Each component of $X(x)=x-c$ has absolute value at most $1/2$ on $I^n$, so
every coefficient in $\iota_X\omega$ has absolute value at most
$\frac12\sup_{x\in I^n}\abs{f(x)}=\frac12\norm{\omega}_\infty$.  This is
exactly the desired estimate.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:non-coord-monomial}
The restriction to coordinate-monomial forms is essential for the coefficient
sup norm.  The form $(\dd x_1+\dd x_2)\wedge \dd x_3$ is classically
decomposable in the sense of exterior algebra, but it is not
coordinate-monomial.  On $I^3$ let
\[
\omega := \dd x_1\wedge \dd x_3 + \dd x_2\wedge \dd x_3.
\]
Then $\norm{\omega}_\infty=1$, while
\[
\iota_X\omega
=
\bigl(x_1-\tfrac12+x_2-\tfrac12\bigr)\dd x_3
-\bigl(x_3-\tfrac12\bigr)(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
so $\norm{\iota_X\omega}_\infty=1$.  A direct computation gives
\[
K_2\omega
=
\frac{x_1+x_2-1}{3}\,\dd x_3
-\frac{x_3-\tfrac12}{3}\,(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
hence $\norm{K_2\omega}_\infty=\frac13>\frac14=\frac{1}{2k}$ when $k=2$.
The estimate \eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} therefore fails outside the
coordinate-monomial class.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Cubical Cochains}

We also use the standard cubical cell decomposition of $I^n$.  Let
$C^k(I^n;\RR)$ be the space of real-valued cubical $k$-cochains with
coboundary $d$.  For $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ we write
\[
\norm{\omega}_\infty := \max_{\sigma^{(k)}} \abs{\omega(\sigma)},
\]
where the maximum runs over oriented $k$-cells.

For $I=\{i_1<\cdots<i_k\}\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$ and
$\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}$, let
\[
\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}
:=
\{x\in I^n:\ x_j=\varepsilon_j \text{ for every } j\notin I\}
\]
be the oriented $k$-cell parallel to
$\dd x_I=\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}$, and let
$\delta_{I,\varepsilon}\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ denote the dual basis cochain.  Set
\[
\phi_0(t):=1-t,
\qquad
\phi_1(t):=t,
\qquad
\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x):=\prod_{j\notin I}\phi_{\varepsilon_j}(x_j).
\]
Following the standard cubical Whitney construction
\cite{Whitney1957,Dupont1976,ArnoldFalkWinther2010,ArnoldAwanou2014}, define
\[
W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})
:=
\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)\,\dd x_I
\]
and extend linearly to $C^k(I^n;\RR)$.  For a smooth $k$-form $\alpha$ on a
neighborhood of $I^n$, define the integration map by
\[
\mathcal I(\alpha)(\sigma)
:=
\int_\sigma \iota_\sigma^\ast \alpha,
\]
where $\iota_\sigma:\sigma\hookrightarrow I^n$ is the inclusion of the cell.

\begin{proposition}[Cubical Whitney formulas and norm-one bounds]
\label{prop:cubical-whitney}
For every $k$ and every $\theta\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$, the maps $W$ and
$\mathcal I$ satisfy
\[
W\theta
=
\sum_{\abs{I}=k}
\left(
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
\right)\dd x_I,
\qquad
\theta=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}\sum_{\varepsilon}\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\delta_{I,\varepsilon},
\]
\[
\mathcal I\circ W=\mathrm{Id},
\qquad
\dd\circ W = W\circ d,
\qquad
\mathcal I\circ \dd = d\circ \mathcal I,
\]
and
\[
\norm{W}_{\infty\to\infty}=1,
\qquad
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}=1.
\]
In particular, $W(C^k(I^n;\RR))\subset \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ consists
of multilinear polynomial differential forms.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The displayed formula for $W\theta$ is just linearity.  To verify
$\mathcal I\circ W=\mathrm{Id}$, it is enough to test on the basis cochains.
If $\sigma_{J,\eta}$ is an oriented $k$-cell, then
\[
\mathcal I(W\delta_{I,\varepsilon})(\sigma_{J,\eta})
=
\int_{\sigma_{J,\eta}}
\iota_{\sigma_{J,\eta}}^\ast\!\bigl(\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}\,\dd x_I\bigr).
\]
If $J\neq I$, then the pullback of $\dd x_I$ to $\sigma_{J,\eta}$ vanishes.
If $J=I$ but $\eta\neq\varepsilon$, then at least one fixed complementary
coordinate has value opposite to $\varepsilon_j$, so
$\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}$ vanishes identically on $\sigma_{I,\eta}$.  Finally,
if $(J,\eta)=(I,\varepsilon)$, then $\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}\equiv 1$ on
$\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ and the cell has unit $k$-volume, so the integral is
$1$.  Thus $\mathcal I(W\delta_{I,\varepsilon})=\delta_{I,\varepsilon}$.

To check $\dd W = Wd$, again work on a basis cochain.  For
$\ell\notin I$, let
\[
p(I,\ell):=1+\#\{i\in I:\ i<\ell\},
\]
the position of $\ell$ in the ordered set $I\cup\{\ell\}$.  The only
$(k+1)$-cell containing $\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ as a face with free
coordinate $\ell$ is
$\sigma_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}$, and its boundary
orientation gives the incidence sign $(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}$.
Hence
\[
d\delta_{I,\varepsilon}
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}
\delta_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
\dd\bigl(W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})\bigr)
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
\phi_{\varepsilon_\ell}'(x_\ell)
\prod_{\substack{j\notin I\\ j\neq \ell}}
\phi_{\varepsilon_j}(x_j)\,
\dd x_\ell\wedge \dd x_I.
\]
Since $\phi_0'=-1$, $\phi_1'=1$, and
$\dd x_\ell\wedge \dd x_I=(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-1}\dd x_{I\cup\{\ell\}}$, the last
display becomes
\[
\dd\bigl(W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})\bigr)
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}
W\!\left(
\delta_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}
\right)
=
W(d\delta_{I,\varepsilon}).
\]
Thus $\dd W=Wd$ on basis cochains, hence on all cochains.

For $\mathcal I\dd = d\mathcal I$, let $\sigma$ be an oriented $k$-cell.
Stokes' theorem on the cube cell gives
\[
\mathcal I(\dd\alpha)(\sigma)
=
\int_\sigma \iota_\sigma^\ast(\dd\alpha)
=
\int_{\partial\sigma} \iota_{\partial\sigma}^\ast \alpha
=
d(\mathcal I\alpha)(\sigma).
\]

For the operator norm of $W$, write
\[
W\theta
=
\sum_{\abs{I}=k} b_I(x)\,\dd x_I,
\qquad
b_I(x)
=
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x).
\]
For each fixed $I$, the functions $\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}$ are nonnegative
and satisfy
\[
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
=
\prod_{j\notin I}\bigl(\phi_0(x_j)+\phi_1(x_j)\bigr)
=
1.
\]
Therefore $b_I(x)$ is a convex combination of the numbers
$\theta_{I,\varepsilon}$, so
$\abs{b_I(x)}\le \norm{\theta}_\infty$ for every $x$.  Hence
$\norm{W\theta}_\infty\le \norm{\theta}_\infty$.  Equality is attained on any
basis cochain $\delta_{I,\varepsilon}$, because
$\norm{W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})}_\infty=1$.  Thus
$\norm{W}_{\infty\to\infty}=1$.

For the operator norm of $\mathcal I$, write
\[
\alpha=\sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I.
\]
If $\sigma=\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$, then $\iota_\sigma^\ast \alpha$ retains
only the coefficient $a_I$, so
\[
\abs{\mathcal I(\alpha)(\sigma)}
=
\left|
\int_{\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}
\iota_{\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}^\ast\alpha
\right|
\le
\sup_{x\in \sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}\abs{a_I(x)}
\le
\norm{\alpha}_\infty.
\]
Hence $\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\le 1$.  Equality holds for the form
$\alpha=\dd x_I$, whose integral over $\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ is $1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Whitney coordinate-monomial cochain]
\label{def:whitney-decomposable}
A cubical $k$-cochain $\theta$ is called Whitney coordinate-monomial if
$W\theta$ is coordinate-monomial in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:decomposable-form}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Characterization of the Whitney coordinate-monomial class]
\label{prop:whitney-characterization}
A cubical $k$-cochain $\theta$ on $I^n$ is Whitney coordinate-monomial if and
only if there exists a single coordinate set
$I\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$ with $\abs{I}=k$ such that $\theta$ vanishes on every
$k$-cell not parallel to $\dd x_I$.  Equivalently,
\[
W\theta = f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I
\]
for a multilinear polynomial $f$ in the complementary variables.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $\theta$ vanishes on every $k$-cell outside one orientation class $I$, then
Proposition~\ref{prop:cubical-whitney} gives
\[
W\theta
=
\left(
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
\right)\dd x_I,
\]
which is of the required form, and the coefficient is multilinear in
$x_{I^c}$.

Conversely, suppose $W\theta=f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I$ for some $I$.  For each
$J\neq I$ with $\abs{J}=k$, the coefficient of $\dd x_J$ in $W\theta$ is
\[
g_J(x)
:=
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{J^c}}
\theta_{J,\varepsilon}\lambda_{J,\varepsilon}(x),
\]
and this coefficient vanishes identically.  Fix
$\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{J^c}$ and evaluate at any point satisfying
$x_j=\varepsilon_j$ for all $j\notin J$.  Then
$\lambda_{J,\varepsilon}(x)=1$ and every other
$\lambda_{J,\varepsilon'}(x)=0$, so $g_J(x)=\theta_{J,\varepsilon}=0$.  Hence
all coefficients on cells outside the orientation class $I$ vanish.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
In degree $2$, the Whitney coordinate-monomial condition means that there is a
single coordinate pair $(i,j)$ such that the cochain may be nonzero only on
squares parallel to the $(i,j)$-plane.  This is the precise restriction used
in the hypercube corollaries of Section~4.2.
\end{remark}

\section{Optimal Bounded Inverses on Cubes}

\subsection{Homotopy Identity and Sharp Constant}

\begin{theorem}[Optimal coefficient-$L^\infty$ right inverse on closed coordinate-monomial cube forms]
\label{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse}
Let $1\le k\le n$.  In the setting of
Definition~\ref{def:homotopy-operator}, adopting the convention
$K_{n+1}:=0$, the following hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item For every $\omega\in\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-identity}
\dd(K_k\omega) + K_{k+1}(\dd\omega) = \omega.
\end{equation}
At $k=n$ the identity reads $\dd(K_n\omega)=\omega$ because $K_{n+1}=0$
and every $(n+1)$-form on $I^n$ vanishes.  In particular, if $\dd\omega=0$,
then $\omega=\dd(K_k\omega)$.
\item If $\omega$ is coordinate-monomial, then the operator satisfies the
ambient-dimension-free bound
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound}
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
\item The constant $1/(2k)$ is sharp even under this restriction: if
$R:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ is linear
and $\dd(R\omega)=\omega$ for every closed coordinate-monomial $k$-form
$\omega$,
then
\[
\norm{R}_{\infty\to\infty}\ge \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate the pullback along the contraction:
\[
\frac{\dd}{\dd t}(H_t^\ast\omega)
=
H_t^\ast\bigl(\mathcal L_X\omega\bigr)
=
H_t^\ast\bigl(\dd\,\iota_X\omega + \iota_X\dd\omega\bigr),
\]
where $\mathcal L_X$ is the Lie derivative.  Integrating from $0$ to $1$
gives
\[
H_1^\ast\omega - H_0^\ast\omega
=
\dd\int_0^1 H_t^\ast(\iota_X\omega)\,\dd t
+ \int_0^1 H_t^\ast(\iota_X\dd\omega)\,\dd t.
\]
Because $H_1=\mathrm{Id}$ and $H_0$ is constant, $H_0^\ast\omega=0$ for
$k\ge 1$.  If
\[
\alpha = g(x)\,\dd x_{j_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{j_{k-1}},
\]
then
\[
H_t^\ast\alpha
=
g\bigl(c+t(x-c)\bigr)\,t^{k-1}\,
\dd x_{j_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{j_{k-1}}.
\]
Therefore
\[
H_t^\ast(\iota_X\omega)(x)=t^{k-1}(\iota_X\omega)\bigl(H_t(x)\bigr),
\qquad
H_t^\ast(\iota_X\dd\omega)(x)=t^k(\iota_X\dd\omega)\bigl(H_t(x)\bigr),
\]
so the two integrals are exactly
$\dd(K_k\omega)$ and $K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)$.  This proves
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity}.

For the norm bound, assume now that $\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.  Then
Lemma~\ref{lem:contraction-estimate} and \eqref{eq:homotopy-operator} give
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty
\le
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\norm{\iota_X\omega}_\infty\,\dd t
\le
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\,\dd t \cdot \frac12 \norm{\omega}_\infty
=
\frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty.
\]

For sharpness, consider the constant form
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
and the $k$-face
\[
F := [0,1]^k \times \{0\}^{n-k}\subset I^n.
\]
Let $\iota_F:F\hookrightarrow I^n$ denote the inclusion.  If
$\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ satisfies $\dd\eta=\omega_0$, then
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1
=
\int_F \iota_F^\ast\omega_0
=
\int_{\partial F} \iota_{\partial F}^\ast\eta.
\]
The boundary $\partial F$ has $2k$ faces, each with $(k-1)$-dimensional volume
$1$, so
\[
1
=
\abs{\int_{\partial F}\iota_{\partial F}^\ast\eta}
\le
\sum_{G\subset\partial F} \abs{\int_G \iota_G^\ast \eta}
\le
2k\,\norm{\eta}_\infty.
\]
Hence $\norm{\eta}_\infty\ge 1/(2k)$.  Taking $\eta=R\omega_0$ yields the lower
bound for every right inverse $R$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Homotopy Decomposition and Curl Reconstruction}

\begin{theorem}[Optimal decomposition with coordinate-monomial Stokes defect]
\label{thm:homotopy-decomposition}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and $\omega\in\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$.  Assume that
$\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.  Define
\[
\eta := K_k\omega \in \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n),
\qquad
E := K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact-plus-defect}
\omega = \dd\eta + E,
\qquad
\dd E = \dd\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:optimal-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is optimal even under the restriction that
$\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:exact-plus-defect} is just
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $\eta=K_k\omega$ and
$E=K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)$.  Since $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial, applying
\eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to $K_{k+1}$ gives
\eqref{eq:optimal-defect-bound}.

To see sharpness, take the constant $(k+1)$-form
\[
\nu_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}
\]
and set $\omega_0 := K_{k+1}\nu_0$.  Then $\dd\omega_0=\nu_0$ and
$\norm{\dd\omega_0}_\infty=1$.  Suppose a constant $C$ satisfied the conclusion
of the theorem for every $\omega$ with coordinate-monomial $\dd\omega$.
Applying
that assertion to $\omega_0$ yields a decomposition
\[
\omega_0 = \dd\phi + \widetilde E,
\qquad
\dd\widetilde E = \nu_0,
\qquad
\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty \le C.
\]
\[
1
=
\int_{I^{k+1}} \nu_0
=
\int_{I^{k+1}} \dd\widetilde E
=
\int_{\partial I^{k+1}} \iota_{\partial I^{k+1}}^\ast \widetilde E.
\]
Since $\partial I^{k+1}$ has $2(k+1)$ faces of unit $(k)$-volume,
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial I^{k+1}} \iota_{\partial I^{k+1}}^\ast \widetilde E}
\le
\sum_{G\subset\partial I^{k+1}} \abs{\int_G \iota_G^\ast \widetilde E}
\le
2(k+1)\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty.
\]
Hence $\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty \ge 1/(2(k+1))$.
No smaller uniform constant can hold even on this restricted class.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Potential reconstruction from coordinate-monomial curl]
\label{cor:curl-reconstruction}
Let $v$ be a smooth vector field on a neighborhood of $I^n$ and set
\[
\omega := \sum_{i=1}^n v_i\,\dd x_i.
\]
Assume that the curl form $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.
Then there exists a smooth function $U$ on $I^n$ such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:curl-potential-bound}
\norm{v-\nabla U}_\infty \le \frac14 \norm{\dd\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/4$ is optimal within this coordinate-monomial curl class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Take $U:=K_1\omega$.  By \eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $k=1$,
\[
\omega = \dd U + K_2(\dd\omega).
\]
The coefficient vector of $\omega-\dd U$ is exactly $v-\nabla U$.  Applying
\eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to $K_2$ yields
\eqref{eq:curl-potential-bound}.

For sharpness, let $\nu_0:=\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2$ and $\alpha:=K_2\nu_0$.  Then
$\dd\alpha=\nu_0$ and $\norm{\nu_0}_\infty=1$.  For every $U$,
\[
\dd(\alpha-\dd U)=\nu_0.
\]
Let
\[
Q:=[0,1]^2\times\{0\}^{n-2}\subset I^n
\]
and let $\iota_Q:Q\hookrightarrow I^n$ be the inclusion.  Stokes' theorem on
$Q$ gives
\[
1
=
\int_Q \iota_Q^\ast\nu_0
=
\int_Q \iota_Q^\ast\dd(\alpha-\dd U)
=
\int_{\partial Q} \iota_{\partial Q}^\ast(\alpha-\dd U).
\]
The boundary of $Q$ has four edges of unit length, so
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial Q} \iota_{\partial Q}^\ast(\alpha-\dd U)}
\le
4\norm{\alpha-\dd U}_\infty.
\]
Hence $\norm{\alpha-\dd U}_\infty\ge 1/4$.
Therefore no constant smaller than $1/4$ can work uniformly.
\end{proof}

\section{Cubical Cochains and Hypercube Consequences}

\subsection{A Sharp Cubical Stokes--Poincar\'e Lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Cubical Stokes--Poincar\'e lemma with Whitney coordinate-monomial coboundary]
\label{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and let $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$.  Assume that $d\omega$
is Whitney coordinate-monomial.  Equivalently, $d\omega$ vanishes on every
$(k+1)$-cell outside a single cubical orientation class.  Then there exist
\[
\eta\in C^{k-1}(I^n;\RR),
\qquad
E\in C^k(I^n;\RR)
\]
such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-decomposition}
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is sharp in the uniform ambient-dimension-free sense
on this restricted class: no smaller constant can hold simultaneously for all
cubes $I^n$ with $n\ge k+1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each $1\le j\le n$ define
\[
\mathcal H_j := \mathcal I\circ K_j\circ W:
C^j(I^n;\RR)\to C^{j-1}(I^n;\RR).
\]
In particular,
\[
\mathcal H_{k+1}:C^{k+1}(I^n;\RR)\to C^k(I^n;\RR).
\]
Using the identities from Section~2, we obtain
\[
d\mathcal H_j + \mathcal H_{j+1}d = \mathrm{Id}
\qquad
\text{on } C^j(I^n;\RR)
\]
for every $1\le j\le n-1$.
Indeed, for $\theta\in C^j(I^n;\RR)$ one has
\[
\begin{aligned}
d\mathcal H_j\theta + \mathcal H_{j+1}d\theta
&=
d(\mathcal I K_jW\theta)+\mathcal I K_{j+1}W(d\theta) \\
&=
\mathcal I(\dd K_jW\theta)+\mathcal I K_{j+1}(\dd W\theta) \\
&=
\mathcal I(\dd K_j + K_{j+1}\dd)W\theta \\
&=
\mathcal I W\theta
=
\theta,
\end{aligned}
\]
where we used $\mathcal I\dd=d\mathcal I$, $\dd W = Wd$, and
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} on the smooth form $W\theta$.
Now set
\[
\eta := \mathcal H_k\omega,
\qquad
E := \mathcal H_{k+1}(d\omega).
\]
Then
\[
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\norm{E}_\infty
\le
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\,
\norm{K_{k+1}(W(d\omega))}_\infty
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{W(d\omega)}_\infty
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_\infty,
\]
where the second inequality uses that $W(d\omega)$ is coordinate-monomial and
the last inequality uses Proposition~\ref{prop:cubical-whitney}.  This proves
\eqref{eq:discrete-defect-bound}.

For sharpness it is enough to treat the case $n=k+1$.  Let
$\theta_{\mathrm{top}}\in C^{k+1}(I^{k+1};\RR)$ be the cochain taking the value
$1$ on the unique oriented top cell.  Its Whitney form is
$\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}$, so $\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$ is
Whitney coordinate-monomial.  Since $I^{k+1}$ is contractible and there are no
$(k+2)$-cells, there exists $\omega_0\in C^k(I^{k+1};\RR)$ with
$d\omega_0=\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$.  If
\[
\omega_0 = d\phi + \widetilde E,
\qquad
d\widetilde E = d\omega_0 = \theta_{\mathrm{top}},
\]
then
\[
1
=
\theta_{\mathrm{top}}(I^{k+1})
=
d\widetilde E(I^{k+1})
=
\sum_{F\subset\partial I^{k+1}} \pm \widetilde E(F).
\]
There are $2(k+1)$ codimension-one faces, hence
\[
1 \le 2(k+1)\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty.
\]
Therefore every such defect term satisfies
$\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty\ge 1/(2(k+1))$, so the constant cannot be improved
uniformly on the restricted class.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Hypercube Corollaries}

Let $\{0,1\}^m$ be the vertex set of the $m$-dimensional hypercube, with edges
joining words that differ in exactly one coordinate.  Identifying this graph
with the $1$-skeleton of the cubical complex $I^m$, an edge function is simply
a cubical $1$-cochain and the square defect is its coboundary on $2$-faces.

\begin{corollary}[Gradient consistency from square defects]
\label{cor:hypercube-gradient}
Let $\omega\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ and write
\[
\varepsilon := \norm{d\omega}_\infty.
\]
Assume that $d\omega$ is Whitney coordinate-monomial; equivalently, there is a
single coordinate pair $(i,j)$ such that the square defect vanishes on every
square not parallel to the $(i,j)$-plane.
Then there exist a vertex potential $f\in C^0(I^m;\RR)$ and a residual
$r\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ such that
\[
\omega = df + r,
\qquad
\norm{r}_\infty \le \frac{\varepsilon}{4},
\qquad
dr = d\omega.
\]
The constant $1/4$ is optimal within this Whitney coordinate-monomial class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare} with $k=1$ and set
$f:=\eta$, $r:=E$.  The optimality is the $k=1$ case of the theorem.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Near detailed balance as a logarithmic corollary of square-defect control]
\label{thm:near-detailed-balance}
Let $q(u,v)>0$ be jump rates on the hypercube graph, defined for adjacent
vertices $u\sim v$.  Define the antisymmetric edge cochain
\[
\omega(u,v) := \log \frac{q(u,v)}{q(v,u)}.
\]
Let
\[
\varepsilon := \norm{d\omega}_\infty,
\]
where $d\omega$ records the signed cycle defect on each square face.  Assume
that $d\omega$ is Whitney coordinate-monomial.  Then
there exists a potential $f:\{0,1\}^m\to \RR$ such that for every edge
$u\sim v$,
\[
\abs{\omega(u,v) - (f(v)-f(u))} \le \frac{\varepsilon}{4}.
\]
Consequently, with $\pi_f(u):=e^{f(u)}$ one has the edgewise multiplicative
bound
\[
e^{-\varepsilon/4}
\le
\frac{\pi_f(u)\,q(u,v)}{\pi_f(v)\,q(v,u)}
\le
e^{\varepsilon/4},
\qquad
u\sim v.
\]
The constant $1/4$ is optimal within this Whitney coordinate-monomial class.
\leanverified{paper_cubical_stokes_near_detailed_balance}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
This is just the logarithmic reformulation of
Corollary~\ref{cor:hypercube-gradient}.  That corollary gives a potential $f$
with
\[
\norm{\omega-df}_\infty \le \frac{\varepsilon}{4}.
\]
For an oriented edge $(u,v)$,
\[
\log \frac{\pi_f(u)\,q(u,v)}{\pi_f(v)\,q(v,u)}
=
f(u)-f(v)+\log q(u,v)-\log q(v,u)
=
\omega(u,v)-(f(v)-f(u)).
\]
Taking absolute values and exponentiating yields the two-sided estimate.  The
sharpness is inherited from Corollary~\ref{cor:hypercube-gradient}.
\end{proof}

\section{Boundary Calibration and Boundary Rigidity}

\subsection{Calibration Lower Bound}

\begin{proposition}[Boundary calibration inequality]
\label{prop:boundary-calibration}
Let $\Omega\subset\RR^n$ be a bounded Lipschitz domain, and let
\[
\mathrm{vol} := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n.
\]
Define
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)
:=
\inf\Bigl\{
\norm{V}_\infty:
V\in C^\infty(U;\RR^n),\ U\supset \overline{\Omega}\ \text{open},
\ \Div V = 1 \text{ on }\Omega
\Bigr\},
\]
or, equivalently, the infimum of $\norm{\eta}_\infty$ over smooth
$(n-1)$-forms $\eta$ on a neighborhood of $\overline\Omega$ satisfying
$\dd\eta=\mathrm{vol}$ on $\Omega$.  Then every admissible vector field $V$
and every Lipschitz subdomain $E\subset \Omega$ satisfy
\[
\Vol(E)\le \norm{V}_\infty \Haus^{n-1}(\partial E).
\]
Consequently,
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)\ge
\sup\left\{
\frac{\Vol(E)}{\Haus^{n-1}(\partial E)}:
E\subset \Omega,\ E \text{ Lipschitz}
\right\}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a smooth vector field $V$, the form $\eta:=\iota_V\mathrm{vol}$ satisfies
$\dd\eta=(\Div V)\mathrm{vol}$, and under the coefficient sup norm used in this
paper one has $\norm{\eta}_\infty=\norm{V}_\infty$.  This identifies the two
definitions of $\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)$.  The divergence theorem on
Lipschitz domains used below is standard; see
\cite{Federer1969,EvansGariepy2015,Maggi2012}.

Now let $E\subset\Omega$ be a Lipschitz subdomain and let $V$ be admissible.
The divergence theorem gives
\[
\Vol(E)
=
\int_E \Div V\,\dd x
=
\int_{\partial E} V\cdot \nu_E\,\dd\Haus^{n-1}
\le
\norm{V}_\infty \Haus^{n-1}(\partial E).
\]
Taking the supremum over $E$ and then the infimum over admissible $V$ yields
the stated lower bound.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Boundary Rigidity on the Cube}

\begin{theorem}[Boundary rigidity of cube minimizers]
\label{thm:boundary-rigidity}
Let $k\ge 1$ and define
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
on $I^k=[0,1]^k$.  Suppose
$\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^k)$ satisfies
\[
\dd\eta = \omega_0,
\qquad
\norm{\eta}_\infty = \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
For each $j\in\{1,\dots,k\}$ and $\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}$ let
\[
F_{j,\varepsilon}:=\{x_j=\varepsilon\},
\qquad
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}:F_{j,\varepsilon}\hookrightarrow I^k.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast \eta
=
(-1)^{j-1}\frac{\varepsilon-\tfrac12}{k}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
on every face $F_{j,\varepsilon}$.  In particular, all minimizers of
$\dd\eta=\omega_0$ have the same boundary trace.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1
=
\int_{I^k}\omega_0
=
\int_{\partial I^k}\iota_{\partial I^k}^\ast\eta
=
\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(
\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta
-
\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta
\right).
\]
Each face has $(k-1)$-dimensional volume $1$, so by the norm bound
\[
\abs{\int_{F_{j,\varepsilon}} \iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta}
\le
\frac{1}{2k}.
\]
There are $2k$ face integrals.  Since their signed sum equals $1$, the triangle
inequality can be sharp only if every face integral has maximal absolute value
and the signs align with the boundary orientation.  Therefore
\[
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta = \frac{1}{2k},
\qquad
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta = -\frac{1}{2k}.
\]

Now each pullback has the form
\[
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta
=
f_{j,\varepsilon}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
for a continuous scalar function $f_{j,\varepsilon}$ on $F_{j,\varepsilon}$,
with $\abs{f_{j,\varepsilon}}\le 1/(2k)$.  Because the face volume is $1$ and
the integral attains the extremal value $\pm 1/(2k)$, continuity forces
$f_{j,\varepsilon}$ to be constant on the face with the corresponding sign.
This is exactly \eqref{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Explicit minimizer]
\label{cor:explicit-minimizer}
The form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:explicit-minimizer}
\eta_\ast
:=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(x_j-\frac12\right)\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
satisfies
\[
\dd\eta_\ast = \omega_0,
\qquad
\norm{\eta_\ast}_\infty = \frac{1}{2k},
\]
and its boundary trace is the canonical one from
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate term by term:
\[
\dd\eta_\ast
=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}\dd x_j\wedge
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
=
\omega_0.
\]
Since $\abs{x_j-1/2}\le 1/2$ on $I^k$, one has
$\norm{\eta_\ast}_\infty=1/(2k)$.  Restricting \eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer} to
$x_j=0$ and $x_j=1$ gives the stated boundary traces immediately.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse} and
Corollary~\ref{cor:explicit-minimizer} together show that
$\mathcal C_\infty(I^k)=1/(2k)$.  Proposition~\ref{prop:boundary-calibration}
gives the matching lower bound already from the test domain $E=I^k$, since
$\Vol(I^k)=1$ and $\Haus^{k-1}(\partial I^k)=2k$.  The point of
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity} is stronger: the minimizer is not unique as
an interior primitive, but its boundary trace is completely rigid.
\end{remark}

\section{Dyadic Outer Approximations and Boundary Readout}

This section is logically independent of Sections~3--5.  It uses only a
primitive of the volume form and polyhedral Stokes on the dyadic outer
approximations.  If one wants to connect it back to Section~5, the centered
minimizer from Corollary~\ref{cor:explicit-minimizer} also satisfies
$\dd\eta_\ast=\mathrm{vol}$ when $k=n$, but the flux identity below does not
depend on the minimizing property.

For a nonempty compact set $A\subset I^n$ and an integer $m\ge 1$, let
$U_m(A)$ be the union of all dyadic cubes of side length $2^{-m}$ that
intersect $A$:
\[
U_m(A)
:=
\bigcup\Bigl\{
Q:\ Q \text{ dyadic } n\text{-cube of side }2^{-m},\ Q\cap A\neq\emptyset
\Bigr\}.
\]
This is a polyhedral neighborhood of $A$ with naturally oriented boundary
$\partial U_m(A)$.

\begin{proposition}[Stokes bound via tube volume]
\label{prop:dyadic-stokes-bound}
Let $A\subset I^n$ be nonempty and compact.  For every smooth $(n-1)$-form
$\omega$ on a neighborhood of $I^n$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dyadic-stokes-bound}
\abs{\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega}
\le
\norm{\dd\omega}_\infty \Vol(U_m(A)).
\end{equation}
If additionally $\Vol(A_\varepsilon)\le C_A\,\varepsilon^{n-d}$ for some
$d\in[0,n)$, $C_A<\infty$, and all $0<\varepsilon\le 1$, where
$A_\varepsilon:=\{x\in I^n:\dist(x,A)\le\varepsilon\}$, then setting
$C_A':=C_A n^{(n-d)/2}$ one has
\[
\Vol(U_m(A))\le C_A' 2^{-m(n-d)},
\]
and therefore
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega}
\le
C_A' 2^{-m(n-d)} \norm{\dd\omega}_\infty
\qquad
\text{for all }m\ge 1.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The set $U_m(A)$ is a finite union of dyadic cubes, so Stokes' theorem on
polyhedral domains gives
\[
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \iota_{\partial U_m(A)}^\ast\omega
=
\int_{U_m(A)} \dd\omega.
\]
Therefore $\abs{\int_{\partial U_m(A)}\omega}\le \norm{\dd\omega}_\infty\Vol(U_m(A))$,
proving \eqref{eq:dyadic-stokes-bound}.
Every dyadic cube of side $2^{-m}$ has diameter $\sqrt{n}\,2^{-m}$, so any
cube intersecting $A$ lies inside $A_{\sqrt{n}\,2^{-m}}$.  Hence
\[
\Vol(U_m(A))
\le
\Vol(A_{\sqrt{n}\,2^{-m}})
\le
C_A(\sqrt{n}\,2^{-m})^{n-d}
=
C_A' 2^{-m(n-d)},
\]
and combining with \eqref{eq:dyadic-stokes-bound} gives the decay estimate.
The underlying Stokes input is standard for polyhedral domains;
see \cite{Federer1969,EvansGariepy2015,Maggi2012}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Volume-flux identity]
\label{prop:volume-flux}
Define the standard volume primitive
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:probe-form}
\omega_0
:=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (-1)^{i-1}x_i\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_i}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n.
\end{equation}
Then $\dd\omega_0 = \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n$, and for every
nonempty compact $A\subset I^n$ and every $m\ge 1$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:volume-probe-identity}
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0 = \Vol(U_m(A))>0.
\end{equation}
If $N_m(A)$ denotes the number of side-$2^{-m}$ dyadic cubes intersecting $A$,
then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:volume-count}
\Vol(U_m(A)) = N_m(A)\,2^{-mn}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A direct exterior derivative computation gives
$\dd\omega_0 = \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n$.
Applying Stokes' theorem to $\omega_0$ on the polyhedral domain $U_m(A)$,
\[
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0
=
\int_{U_m(A)} \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n
=
\Vol(U_m(A)).
\]
Since $U_m(A)$ is a nonempty finite union of $n$-cubes, $\Vol(U_m(A))>0$.
Equation~\eqref{eq:volume-count} holds because each dyadic cube of side
$2^{-m}$ has volume $2^{-mn}$ and the cubes comprising $U_m(A)$ are pairwise
disjoint up to boundaries.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Upper Minkowski dimension readout]
\label{cor:minkowski-readout}
In the notation of Proposition~\ref{prop:volume-flux}, writing
$\overline{\dim}_{\mathrm M}(A)$ for the upper Minkowski (box-counting)
dimension of $A$ \cite{Falconer2014,Mattila1995},
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:minkowski-readout}
\overline{\dim}_{\mathrm M}(A)
=
\limsup_{m\to\infty}\frac{\log N_m(A)}{m\log 2}
=
n + \limsup_{m\to\infty}
\frac{\log \Vol(U_m(A))}{m\log 2}
=
n + \limsup_{m\to\infty}
\frac{\log\!\left(\displaystyle\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0\right)}{m\log 2}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The first equality is the standard dyadic characterization of upper Minkowski
dimension; see \cite{Falconer2014,Mattila1995} (and note that ball and cube
definitions give the same value in $\RR^n$).  Substituting
$N_m(A)=2^{mn}\Vol(U_m(A))$ from \eqref{eq:volume-count} gives the second
equality, and \eqref{eq:volume-probe-identity} gives the third.  The logarithm
is well-defined because $\Vol(U_m(A))>0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The gain in Proposition~\ref{prop:dyadic-stokes-bound} is codimension-sensitive:
the decay exponent is $n-d$, reflecting the fractal dimension of $A$.  The
single fixed probe form $\omega_0$ turns a family of boundary integrals
into a direct measurement of the outer-approximation volume and hence of the
upper Minkowski dimension.  Neither the sharp constant $1/(2k)$ from
Sections~3--4 nor the boundary rigidity of Section~5 is used here; the only
inputs are a primitive of the volume form and polyhedral Stokes.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}

Several ingredients used here are standard: the radial homotopy formula,
bounded right inverses on star-shaped sets, cubical Whitney transfer,
polyhedral Stokes, and dyadic cube-count formulas for Minkowski dimension.
Within that framework, the paper isolates three contributions: the sharp
coefficient-$L^\infty$ constant $1/(2k)$ for the standard radial homotopy
operator on closed coordinate-monomial forms, the transfer of the same
constant to the corresponding Whitney coordinate-monomial cubical class on the
single cube, and the equality-case boundary-trace rigidity theorem for cube
minimizers.

Section~6 should be read separately from that sharp-inverse theory.  Its flux
identity uses only $\dd\omega=\mathrm{vol}$ and Stokes on dyadic polyhedra,
while Corollary~\ref{cor:explicit-minimizer} supplies a canonical centered
probe if one wants to tie the readout back to the minimization problem.
