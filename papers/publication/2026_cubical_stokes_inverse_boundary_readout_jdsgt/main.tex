\begin{abstract}
The centerpiece of this note is a boundary-trace rigidity theorem for
coefficient-$L^\infty$ primitives of $\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k$ on the
cube $I^k$: every minimizer has the same canonical face trace, so the optimal
primitive is simultaneously norm-sharp and boundary-rigid inside the anisotropic
$\ell_\infty/\ell_1$ duality that governs the $P_1$ perimeter.  The supporting
ingredients are kept explicit: (i) a sharp restricted sup-ratio for the radial
homotopy operator on closed coordinate-monomial $k$-forms,
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty},
\]
uniform in the ambient dimension; (ii) the corresponding Whitney/integration
transfer to a single cube with exactly the same constant and no new cubical
machinery; and (iii) an anisotropic boundary calibration inequality phrased in
terms of the $\ell_1$ perimeter.  Sections~2--4 therefore serve only to isolate
these explicit computations, while Section~5 contains the genuinely new
boundary-rigidity statement and its consequences.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

This note is written as a short, cube-scale study centered on the boundary
rigidity of coefficient-$L^\infty$ primitives of the volume form.  The main
theorem, proved in Section~5, shows that every minimizer of
$\dd\eta=\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k$ on $I^k$ has the canonical face
trace coming from the explicit primitive $\eta_\ast$; hence optimal primitives
are boundary-rigid as well as norm-sharp.  Sections~2--4 contain only the
explicit computations needed for this boundary argument:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item Section~3 evaluates the restricted sup-ratio of the classical radial
  homotopy $K_k$ on the incidence-one coordinate-monomial class and records the
  sharp defect decomposition for forms with coordinate-monomial exterior
  derivative.
  \item Section~4 compresses the one-cube Whitney/integration transfer to a
  single proposition and corollary, observing that the same constant carries
  over to cubical cochains without introducing new homotopy operators.
  \item Section~5 supplies an anisotropic boundary calibration inequality and
  the boundary-rigidity theorem itself, formulated directly in the
  $\ell_\infty/\ell_1$ duality of the $P_1$ perimeter.
\end{itemize}

We do not treat general domains, Sobolev norms, or patching across multiple
cubes; the scope is intentionally limited to what can be proved exactly on a
single cube, with the novelty concentrated entirely in the Section~5 boundary
result.

Related explicit-constant work places the present note in a narrow corner of
the literature.  Chaumont--Frelet--Licht--Vohral\'ik~\cite{ChaumontFreletLichtVohralik2025}
and the references therein (including the spectral bounds of
Guerini--Savo~\cite{GueriniSavo2004}) study computable de~Rham constants on
much broader geometries and norms, while Costabel--McIntosh, Guzm\'an--Salgado,
and others give regularized operators on Lipschitz domains.
On the boundary side we work next to the continuum max-flow/min-cut and Cheeger
literature: compare Strang's continuum calibration~\cite{Strang1983}, the
anisotropic network result of Nozawa~\cite{Nozawa1990}, Strang's planar flows
\cite{Strang2010}, the Euclidean spectral comparison of
Grieser~\cite{Grieser2006}, the boundary Laplacian analysis of
Guerini--Savo~\cite{GueriniSavo2004}, Leonardi's survey of the Cheeger
problem~\cite{Leonardi2015}, and the calibrability works of
Caselles--Facciolo--Meinhardt and
Caselles--Chambolle--Moll--Novaga~\cite{CasellesFaccioloMeinhardt2009,CasellesChambolleMollNovaga2008},
supplemented by the uniqueness/rigidity papers of
Caselles--Chambolle--Novaga~\cite{CasellesChambolleNovaga2007,CasellesChambolleNovaga2011}.
We keep the coefficient $\ell_\infty$ norm throughout, so the dual boundary
term is the anisotropic $\ell_1$ perimeter emphasized in these references, and
the entire manuscript is deliberately framed as a short note isolating the
Section~5 boundary-rigidity phenomenon on a single cube.

Let $c=(1/2,\dots,1/2)$ and $X(x)=x-c$.  Because the coefficient sup norm is
taken coordinatewise, the relevant issue is not merely decomposability but
incidence: how many $k$-coefficients can contribute to the same
$(k-1)$-coefficient after contraction with $X$.  Proposition~\ref{prop:incidence-criterion}
shows that the universal factor $1/2$ is available exactly on the
``incidence-one'' patterns.  We work with the single-orientation
coordinate-monomial class $f\,\dd x_I$, which is the cleanest incidence-one
family and the one preserved by the hypotheses used later.  On this class the
radial homotopy operator $K_k$ satisfies the sharp ambient-dimension-free
estimate
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty},
\]
and the constant $1/(2k)$ already equals $\norm{K_k}_{\coord}$, so no linear right inverse can achieve a smaller sup-ratio on closed coordinate-monomial forms.  Applied to forms with
coordinate-monomial exterior derivative, this yields the sharp defect
decomposition
\[
\omega = \dd\eta + E,
\qquad
\norm{E}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
The degree-one case gives the corresponding sharp gradient-reconstruction
estimate from coordinate-monomial curl data.

The cubical statement is equally local.  A cubical $k$-cochain is Whitney
coordinate-monomial precisely when it is supported on one orientation class of
$k$-cells, or equivalently when its Whitney form is
$f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I$ for one fixed $I$.  In degree two on a hypercube, this
means that square defects may be nonzero only on squares parallel to a single
coordinate $2$-plane.  Section~4 does not construct a new cubical homotopy;
it only records that on one cube the classical Whitney/integration transfer
preserves the same sharp constant on this restricted class.

The boundary side of the paper is more rigid than the inverse bound alone.  We
record a boundary calibration inequality for primitives of the volume form and
then show that on the cube every coefficient-$L^\infty$ minimizer has the same
boundary trace.  Thus the optimal primitive is not only norm-sharp but also
boundary-rigid.

Compared with Costabel--McIntosh and Guzm\'an--Salgado
\cite{CostabelMcIntosh2010,GuzmanSalgado2021}, we do not treat general domains,
Sobolev norms, or regularized Poincar\'e/Bogovski\u{\i} operators; the point
here is an exact coefficient-sup constant on the cube.  Compared with
Dodziuk, Dupont, and Albert
\cite{Dodziuk1976,Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020}, we do not construct a
new Whitney or cubical Dupont homotopy; the cubical section is only a sharp
one-cube corollary of the standard transfer formalism.  Recent explicit-constant
work such as Chaumont--Frelet, Licht, and Vohral\'ik
\cite{ChaumontFreletLichtVohralik2025} concerns computable de Rham constants on
broader geometric classes, but in different norms and with different goals.
The theorem chain used in Sections~3--5 is summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:constants-overview}.

Whenever we restrict to the coordinate-monomial class, we abbreviate
\[
\norm{T}_{\coord}
:=
\sup\left\{
\frac{\norm{T\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}}{\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}} :
0\neq \omega \text{ closed coordinate-monomial}
\right\},
\]
a sharp sup-ratio over the admissible family rather than a Banach-space
operator norm.

\section{Cubical Forms and Homotopy Operators}

\subsection{Continuous Cubical Setup}

Fix $n\ge 1$ and write
\[
I^n := [0,1]^n,
\qquad
c := \left(\frac12,\dots,\frac12\right).
\]
For $1\le k\le n$, let $\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ denote the smooth
$k$-forms on a neighborhood of $I^n$, restricted to the cube.  If
\[
\omega = \sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I,
\]
we use the coefficient sup norm
\[
\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} := \max_{\abs{I}=k}\ \sup_{x\in I^n} \abs{a_I(x)}.
\]
All $L^\infty$ statements below refer to this coordinate norm.

\begin{remark}[Notation]
We write $\dd$ for the smooth exterior derivative on differential forms and
$d$ for the cubical coboundary on cochains.  Coefficient norms for forms are
always written $\norm{\cdot}_{\coeff,\infty}$, while cubical cochains use the
cellwise norm $\norm{\cdot}_{\cell,\infty}$ introduced below.  The ambient
category will always be clear from the argument.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Coordinate-monomial form]
\label{def:decomposable-form}
A smooth $k$-form on $I^n$ is called coordinate-monomial if it has the
form
\[
\omega = f(x)\,\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}
\]
for some smooth scalar function $f$ and some ordered index set
$1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
This class is stricter than the usual notion of decomposability in
$\Lambda^kT^\ast I^n$.  For example,
$(\dd x_1+\dd x_2)\wedge \dd x_3$ is classically decomposable, but it is not
coordinate-monomial.
\end{remark}

Let $X(x):=x-c$ be the radial vector field centered at $c$, and let
\[
H:[0,1]\times I^n \to I^n,
\qquad
H(t,x):=c+t(x-c).
\]
For each $t\in[0,1]$, write $H_t(x):=H(t,x)$.  Thus $H_1=\mathrm{Id}$ and
$H_0$ collapses the cube to its center.

\begin{definition}[Radial homotopy operator]
\label{def:homotopy-operator}
For $1\le k\le n$ define
\[
K_k:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-operator}
(K_k\omega)(x)
:=
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\,\bigl(\iota_{x-c}\,\omega\bigr)\!\bigl(c+t(x-c)\bigr)\,\dd t,
\end{equation}
where for a fixed tangent vector $v\in\RR^n$ and a $k$-form
$\omega=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}a_I(y)\,\dd y_I$, we write
$(\iota_v\omega)(y):=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}a_I(y)\,\iota_v(\dd y_I)$, i.e., the
coefficients of $\omega$ are evaluated at the point $y$ and then contracted with
the vector $v$.  In particular, the contraction vector in
\eqref{eq:homotopy-operator} is the \emph{fixed} vector $x-c\in\RR^n$
(depending on the external point $x$, but \emph{not} on the integration
variable $t$), while the coefficients of $\omega$ are evaluated along the
straight-line homotopy $H_t(x)=c+t(x-c)$.

Equivalently, if $\omega=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}a_I(y)\,\dd y_I$, then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-operator-explicit}
(K_k\omega)(x)
=
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}
\sum_{\abs{I}=k}\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1}(x_{i_r}-c_{i_r})\,
a_I\bigl(c+t(x-c)\bigr)\,
\dd x_{I\setminus\{i_r\}}\,\dd t.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Sanity checks]
\label{ex:sanity-checks}
(i) For $\omega=\dd x_i$ ($k=1$), one has
$\iota_{x-c}\,\dd x_i = x_i - \tfrac12$ (independent of the evaluation
point since $\dd x_i$ has constant coefficients), so
\[
K_1(\dd x_i)(x) = \int_0^1 t^0\cdot(x_i-\tfrac12)\,\dd t = x_i - \tfrac12,
\]
and $\dd K_1(\dd x_i) = \dd x_i = \omega$.  Note that
$\norm{K_1(\dd x_i)}_{\coeff,\infty}=\tfrac12=\tfrac{1}{2\cdot 1}\norm{\dd x_i}_{\coeff,\infty}$.

(ii) For $\omega=\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2$ ($k=2$), one has
$\iota_{x-c}\omega=(x_1-\tfrac12)\,\dd x_2-(x_2-\tfrac12)\,\dd x_1$, so
\[
K_2\omega
=
\int_0^1 t\bigl[(x_1-\tfrac12)\,\dd x_2-(x_2-\tfrac12)\,\dd x_1\bigr]\dd t
=
\tfrac12\bigl[(x_1-\tfrac12)\,\dd x_2-(x_2-\tfrac12)\,\dd x_1\bigr],
\]
and $\dd K_2\omega=\tfrac12(\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2+\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2)=\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2=\omega$.  Here
$\norm{K_2\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=\tfrac14=\tfrac{1}{2\cdot 2}$.

(iii) For $\omega=\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k$, the same calculation
yields the form $\eta_\ast$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:explicit-minimizer}:
\[
K_k\omega
=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k(-1)^{j-1}(x_j-\tfrac12)\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k,
\]
with $\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=\tfrac{1}{2k}$ and $\dd K_k\omega=\omega$.
\end{example}

The operator is the standard radial homotopy formula centered at the cube
midpoint; compare \cite[Sec.~3]{BottTu1982},
\cite{Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020}, and the bounded right-inverse
literature
\cite{Bogovskii1979,IwaniecLutoborski1993,CostabelMcIntosh2010,GuzmanSalgado2021}.

We extend the operator family by setting $K_{n+1}:=0$ on
$\Omega^{n+1}(I^n)=\{0\}$.  Under this convention the homotopy identity
$\dd K_k\omega + K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)=\omega$ holds uniformly for every
$1\le k\le n$: at top degree $k=n$ the term $K_{n+1}(\dd\omega)$ vanishes
because every $(n+1)$-form on $I^n$ is zero.

\begin{lemma}[Uniform contraction estimate on coordinate-monomial forms]
\label{lem:contraction-estimate}
For every coordinate-monomial
\[
\omega = f(y)\,\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}
\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
and every $x,y\in I^n$ one has
\[
\norm{\iota_{x-c}\omega(y)}_\infty \le \frac12 \norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
In particular,
\[
\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac12 \norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For such a form,
\[
\iota_{x-c}\omega(y)
=
\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1} f(y)\bigl(x_{i_r}-\tfrac12\bigr)\,
\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_{i_r}}\wedge\cdots\wedge
\dd x_{i_k}.
\]
Each component of $X(x)=x-c$ has absolute value at most $1/2$ on $I^n$, so
every coefficient in $\iota_{x-c}\omega(y)$ has absolute value at most
$\frac12\sup_{z\in I^n}\abs{f(z)}=\frac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$.  Taking $y=x$
gives the displayed bound for $\iota_X\omega$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Incidence criterion for the coefficient-sup contraction bound]
\label{prop:incidence-criterion}
Let
\[
\omega = \sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I
\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n).
\]
For each $(k-1)$-index $J$ define
\[
m_J(\omega):=
\#\{i\notin J:\ a_{J\cup\{i\}}\not\equiv 0\},
\qquad
m(\omega):=\max_{\abs{J}=k-1} m_J(\omega).
\]
Then
\[
\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{m(\omega)}{2}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Consequently, a class of forms described only by which coordinate wedge
monomials may occur admits the universal bound
$\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}\le \frac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$ for all of its
members if and only if every
active $(k-1)$-monomial is incident to at most one allowed $k$-monomial.
Coordinate-monomial forms ($m(\omega)\le 1$) satisfy this condition, while any
class that permits two or more $k$-monomials sharing a common $(k-1)$-factor
does not.  For instance, the class of sums $a\,\dd x_{1}\wedge\dd x_3+b\,\dd x_2\wedge\dd x_3$ has $m_{\{3\}}=2$, and the
$(k-1)$-coefficient of $\dd x_3$ in $\iota_X\omega$ can reach
$\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$ (not $\tfrac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$) at a corner of
$I^n$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a fixed $(k-1)$-index $J$, the coefficient of $\dd x_J$ in $\iota_X\omega$
has the form
\[
\sum_{i\notin J}\sigma(J,i)\bigl(x_i-\tfrac12\bigr)a_{J\cup\{i\}}(x),
\]
where $\sigma(J,i)\in\{\pm 1\}$ is the sign obtained by inserting $i$ into the
ordered set $J$.  At each point $x$, at most $m_J(\omega)$ summands are
present, and each has absolute value at most $\frac12\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$.
Hence the coefficient of $\dd x_J$ is bounded by
$\frac{m_J(\omega)}{2}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$, and taking the maximum over $J$
gives the displayed estimate.

If two allowed monomials $\dd x_{J\cup\{i\}}$ and $\dd x_{J\cup\{\ell\}}$ share
the same $(k-1)$-factor $\dd x_J$, choose the sign in the constant form
\[
\omega_0:=\dd x_{J\cup\{i\}}\pm \dd x_{J\cup\{\ell\}}
\]
so that their contributions to the $\dd x_J$-coefficient in $\iota_X\omega_0$
add.  Then $\norm{\omega_0}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$, while at the point with
$x_i=x_\ell=1$ that coefficient has absolute value $1$.  Therefore no
universal factor $\frac12$ can hold on any class that allows such a pair.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:non-coord-monomial}
The restriction to coordinate-monomial forms is essential for the coefficient
sup norm.  The form $(\dd x_1+\dd x_2)\wedge \dd x_3$ is classically
decomposable in the sense of exterior algebra, but it is not
coordinate-monomial.  On $I^3$ let
\[
\omega := \dd x_1\wedge \dd x_3 + \dd x_2\wedge \dd x_3.
\]
Then $\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$, while
\[
\iota_X\omega
=
\bigl(x_1-\tfrac12+x_2-\tfrac12\bigr)\dd x_3
-\bigl(x_3-\tfrac12\bigr)(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
so $\norm{\iota_X\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$.  A direct computation gives
\[
K_2\omega
=
\frac{x_1+x_2-1}{2}\,\dd x_3
-\frac{x_3-\tfrac12}{2}\,(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
hence $\norm{K_2\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}=\frac12>\frac14=\frac{1}{2k}$ when $k=2$.
The estimate \eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} therefore fails outside the
coordinate-monomial class.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Cubical Cochains}

We also use the standard cubical cell decomposition of $I^n$.  Let
$C^k(I^n;\RR)$ be the space of real-valued cubical $k$-cochains with
coboundary $d$.  For $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ we write
\[
\norm{\omega}_{\cell,\infty} := \max_{\sigma^{(k)}} \abs{\omega(\sigma)},
\]
where the maximum runs over oriented $k$-cells.

For $I=\{i_1<\cdots<i_k\}\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$ and
$\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}$, let
\[
\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}
:=
\{x\in I^n:\ x_j=\varepsilon_j \text{ for every } j\notin I\}
\]
be the oriented $k$-cell parallel to
$\dd x_I=\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}$, and let
$\delta_{I,\varepsilon}\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ denote the dual basis cochain.  Set
\[
\phi_0(t):=1-t,
\qquad
\phi_1(t):=t,
\qquad
\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x):=\prod_{j\notin I}\phi_{\varepsilon_j}(x_j).
\]
Following the standard cubical Whitney construction
\cite{Whitney1957,Dodziuk1976,Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020,ArnoldFalkWinther2010,ArnoldAwanou2014}, define
\[
W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})
:=
\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)\,\dd x_I
\]
and extend linearly to $C^k(I^n;\RR)$.  For a smooth $k$-form $\alpha$ on a
neighborhood of $I^n$, define the integration map by
\[
\mathcal I(\alpha)(\sigma)
:=
\int_\sigma \iota_\sigma^\ast \alpha,
\]
where $\iota_\sigma:\sigma\hookrightarrow I^n$ is the inclusion of the cell.

\begin{proposition}[Cubical Whitney formulas and norm-one bounds]
\label{prop:cubical-whitney}
For every $k$ and every $\theta\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$, the maps $W$ and
$\mathcal I$ satisfy
\[
W\theta
=
\sum_{\abs{I}=k}
\left(
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
\right)\dd x_I,
\qquad
\theta=\sum_{\abs{I}=k}\sum_{\varepsilon}\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\delta_{I,\varepsilon},
\]
\[
\mathcal I\circ W=\mathrm{Id},
\qquad
\dd\circ W = W\circ d,
\qquad
\mathcal I\circ \dd = d\circ \mathcal I,
\]
and
\[
\norm{W}_{\infty\to\infty}=1,
\qquad
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}=1.
\]
In particular, $W(C^k(I^n;\RR))\subset \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ consists
of multilinear polynomial differential forms.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The displayed formula for $W\theta$ is just linearity.  To verify
$\mathcal I\circ W=\mathrm{Id}$, it is enough to test on the basis cochains.
If $\sigma_{J,\eta}$ is an oriented $k$-cell, then
\[
\mathcal I(W\delta_{I,\varepsilon})(\sigma_{J,\eta})
=
\int_{\sigma_{J,\eta}}
\iota_{\sigma_{J,\eta}}^\ast\!\bigl(\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}\,\dd x_I\bigr).
\]
If $J\neq I$, then the pullback of $\dd x_I$ to $\sigma_{J,\eta}$ vanishes.
If $J=I$ but $\eta\neq\varepsilon$, then at least one fixed complementary
coordinate has value opposite to $\varepsilon_j$, so
$\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}$ vanishes identically on $\sigma_{I,\eta}$.  Finally,
if $(J,\eta)=(I,\varepsilon)$, then $\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}\equiv 1$ on
$\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ and the cell has unit $k$-volume, so the integral is
$1$.  Thus $\mathcal I(W\delta_{I,\varepsilon})=\delta_{I,\varepsilon}$.

To check $\dd W = Wd$, again work on a basis cochain.  For
$\ell\notin I$, let
\[
p(I,\ell):=1+\#\{i\in I:\ i<\ell\},
\]
the position of $\ell$ in the ordered set $I\cup\{\ell\}$.  The only
$(k+1)$-cell containing $\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ as a face with free
coordinate $\ell$ is
$\sigma_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}$, and its boundary
orientation gives the incidence sign $(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}$.
Hence
\[
d\delta_{I,\varepsilon}
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}
\delta_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
\dd\bigl(W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})\bigr)
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
\phi_{\varepsilon_\ell}'(x_\ell)
\prod_{\substack{j\notin I\\ j\neq \ell}}
\phi_{\varepsilon_j}(x_j)\,
\dd x_\ell\wedge \dd x_I.
\]
Since $\phi_0'=-1$, $\phi_1'=1$, and
$\dd x_\ell\wedge \dd x_I=(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-1}\dd x_{I\cup\{\ell\}}$, the last
display becomes
\[
\dd\bigl(W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})\bigr)
=
\sum_{\ell\notin I}
(-1)^{p(I,\ell)-\varepsilon_\ell}
W\!\left(
\delta_{I\cup\{\ell\},\,\varepsilon|_{(I\cup\{\ell\})^c}}
\right)
=
W(d\delta_{I,\varepsilon}).
\]
Thus $\dd W=Wd$ on basis cochains, hence on all cochains.

For $\mathcal I\dd = d\mathcal I$, let $\sigma$ be an oriented $k$-cell.
Stokes' theorem on the cube cell gives
\[
\mathcal I(\dd\alpha)(\sigma)
=
\int_\sigma \iota_\sigma^\ast(\dd\alpha)
=
\int_{\partial\sigma} \iota_{\partial\sigma}^\ast \alpha
=
d(\mathcal I\alpha)(\sigma).
\]

For the operator norm of $W$, write
\[
W\theta
=
\sum_{\abs{I}=k} b_I(x)\,\dd x_I,
\qquad
b_I(x)
=
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x).
\]
For each fixed $I$, the functions $\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}$ are nonnegative
and satisfy
\[
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
=
\prod_{j\notin I}\bigl(\phi_0(x_j)+\phi_1(x_j)\bigr)
=
1.
\]
Therefore $b_I(x)$ is a convex combination of the numbers
$\theta_{I,\varepsilon}$, so
$\abs{b_I(x)}\le \norm{\theta}_{\cell,\infty}$ for every $x$.  Hence
$\norm{W\theta}_{\coeff,\infty}\le \norm{\theta}_{\cell,\infty}$.  Equality is attained on any
basis cochain $\delta_{I,\varepsilon}$, because
$\norm{W(\delta_{I,\varepsilon})}_\infty=1$.  Thus
$\norm{W}_{\infty\to\infty}=1$.

For the operator norm of $\mathcal I$, write
\[
\alpha=\sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I.
\]
If $\sigma=\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$, then $\iota_\sigma^\ast \alpha$ retains
only the coefficient $a_I$, so
\[
\abs{\mathcal I(\alpha)(\sigma)}
=
\left|
\int_{\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}
\iota_{\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}^\ast\alpha
\right|
\le
\sup_{x\in \sigma_{I,\varepsilon}}\abs{a_I(x)}
\le
\norm{\alpha}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\le 1$.  Equality holds for the form
$\alpha=\dd x_I$, whose integral over $\sigma_{I,\varepsilon}$ is $1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Whitney coordinate-monomial cochain]
\label{def:whitney-decomposable}
A cubical $k$-cochain $\theta$ is called Whitney coordinate-monomial if
$W\theta$ is coordinate-monomial in the sense of
Definition~\ref{def:decomposable-form}.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Characterization of the Whitney coordinate-monomial class]
\label{prop:whitney-characterization}
A cubical $k$-cochain $\theta$ on $I^n$ is Whitney coordinate-monomial if and
only if there exists a single coordinate set
$I\subset\{1,\dots,n\}$ with $\abs{I}=k$ such that $\theta$ vanishes on every
$k$-cell not parallel to $\dd x_I$.  Equivalently,
\[
W\theta = f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I
\]
for a multilinear polynomial $f$ in the complementary variables.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $\theta$ vanishes on every $k$-cell outside one orientation class $I$, then
Proposition~\ref{prop:cubical-whitney} gives
\[
W\theta
=
\left(
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{I^c}}
\theta_{I,\varepsilon}\lambda_{I,\varepsilon}(x)
\right)\dd x_I,
\]
which is of the required form, and the coefficient is multilinear in
$x_{I^c}$.

Conversely, suppose $W\theta=f(x_{I^c})\,\dd x_I$ for some $I$.  For each
$J\neq I$ with $\abs{J}=k$, the coefficient of $\dd x_J$ in $W\theta$ is
\[
g_J(x)
:=
\sum_{\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{J^c}}
\theta_{J,\varepsilon}\lambda_{J,\varepsilon}(x),
\]
and this coefficient vanishes identically.  Fix
$\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}^{J^c}$ and evaluate at any point satisfying
$x_j=\varepsilon_j$ for all $j\notin J$.  Then
$\lambda_{J,\varepsilon}(x)=1$ and every other
$\lambda_{J,\varepsilon'}(x)=0$, so $g_J(x)=\theta_{J,\varepsilon}=0$.  Hence
all coefficients on cells outside the orientation class $I$ vanish.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
In degree $2$, the Whitney coordinate-monomial condition means that there is a
single coordinate pair $(i,j)$ such that the cochain may be nonzero only on
squares parallel to the $(i,j)$-plane.  This is the precise restriction used
in the hypercube corollaries of Section~4.2.
\end{remark}

\section{Optimal Bounded Inverses on Cubes}

\subsection{Homotopy Identity and Sharp Constant}

\begin{theorem}[Coefficient-$L^\infty$ right inverse on closed coordinate-monomial cube forms]
\label{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse}
Let $1\le k\le n$.  In the setting of
Definition~\ref{def:homotopy-operator}, adopting the convention
$K_{n+1}:=0$, the following hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item \textbf{Homotopy identity.}  For every
$\omega\in\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ (not necessarily coordinate-monomial),
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-identity}
\dd(K_k\omega) + K_{k+1}(\dd\omega) = \omega.
\end{equation}
At $k=n$ the identity reads $\dd(K_n\omega)=\omega$ because $K_{n+1}=0$
and every $(n+1)$-form on $I^n$ vanishes.  In particular, if $\dd\omega=0$,
then $\omega=\dd(K_k\omega)$.
\item \textbf{Upper bound (pointwise-in-$n$, restricted sup-ratio on coordinate-monomial $k$-forms).}
If $\omega$ is coordinate-monomial
(Definition~\ref{def:decomposable-form}), then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound}
\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The admissible class is all smooth coordinate-monomial $k$-forms on $I^n$;
the bound is uniform in $n$ and sharp as a restricted sup-ratio over this
admissible family.
\item \textbf{Sharpness (restricted sup-ratio lower bound over all linear right inverses).}
If $R:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ is any
linear map satisfying $\dd(R\omega)=\omega$ for every closed
coordinate-monomial $k$-form $\omega$, then
\[
\sup\Bigl\{\frac{\norm{R\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}}{\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}}:\
\omega\text{ closed coordinate-monomial}, \omega\neq 0\Bigr\}
\ge \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
Hence $1/(2k)$ is the best constant available to any right inverse on
this class.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write $\omega=\sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(y)\,\dd y_I$ in a neighborhood of $I^n$
and set $H(t,x)=c+t(x-c)$.  Since
\[
H^\ast(\dd y_j)=t\,\dd x_j+(x_j-c_j)\,\dd t,
\]
each wedge product contains at most one $\dd t$ factor.  Expanding
$H^\ast\omega$ therefore gives a unique decomposition
\[
H^\ast\omega=A+ \dd t\wedge B,
\]
where
\[
A(t,x)=
t^k\sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(H(t,x))\,\dd x_I
\]
and
\[
B(t,x)=
t^{k-1}\sum_{\abs{I}=k}\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1}
\bigl(x_{i_r}-c_{i_r}\bigr)a_I(H(t,x))\,
\dd x_{I\setminus\{i_r\}}.
\]
Comparing with \eqref{eq:homotopy-operator-explicit}, one sees that
$B(t,x)=t^{k-1}(\iota_{x-c}\omega)(H_t(x))$, so
\[
K_k\omega(x)=\int_0^1 B(t,x)\,\dd t.
\]

Applying the same expansion to $\dd\omega$ yields
\[
H^\ast(\dd\omega)=C+\dd t\wedge D
\]
for some forms $C$ and $D$, and the $\dd t$-component is exactly
\[
D(t,x)=
t^k\sum_{\abs{J}=k+1}\sum_{r=1}^{k+1} (-1)^{r-1}
\bigl(x_{j_r}-c_{j_r}\bigr)b_J(H(t,x))\,
\dd x_{J\setminus\{j_r\}},
\]
where $\dd\omega=\sum_{\abs{J}=k+1} b_J(y)\,\dd y_J$.  Hence
\[
K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)(x)=\int_0^1 D(t,x)\,\dd t.
\]

Now use $\dd(H^\ast\omega)=H^\ast(\dd\omega)$.  Since
$\dd=\dd_x+\dd t\wedge \partial_t$ on $[0,1]\times I^n$, one has
\[
\dd(A+\dd t\wedge B)=\dd_x A + \dd t\wedge(\partial_t A-\dd_x B).
\]
Comparing the $\dd t$-components with $H^\ast(\dd\omega)=C+\dd t\wedge D$
gives
\[
\partial_t A-\dd_x B=D.
\]
Integrating in $t$ from $0$ to $1$ yields
\[
A(1,x)-A(0,x)=
\dd_x\!\left(\int_0^1 B(t,x)\,\dd t\right)
+ \int_0^1 D(t,x)\,\dd t.
\]
Because $A(1,x)=\omega(x)$ and $A(0,x)=0$ for $k\ge 1$, this is exactly
\[
\omega=\dd(K_k\omega)+K_{k+1}(\dd\omega),
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:homotopy-identity}.

For the norm bound, assume now that $\omega=f(y)\,\dd x_I$ is
coordinate-monomial.  By \eqref{eq:homotopy-operator}, each coefficient of
$K_k\omega$ at a point $x$ is bounded by
$\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\cdot\frac12\abs{f(c+t(x-c))}\,\dd t$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:contraction-estimate} with the contraction vector $x-c$ and
the coefficient $f$ evaluated at $y=c+t(x-c)\in I^n$).  Since
$\abs{f(y)}\le\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$ for all $y\in I^n$,
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}
\le
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\,\dd t \cdot \frac12 \norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}
=
\frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]

For sharpness, consider the constant form
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
and the $k$-face
\[
F := [0,1]^k \times \{0\}^{n-k}\subset I^n.
\]
Let $\iota_F:F\hookrightarrow I^n$ denote the inclusion.  If
$\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ satisfies $\dd\eta=\omega_0$, then
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1
=
\int_F \iota_F^\ast\omega_0
=
\int_{\partial F} \iota_{\partial F}^\ast\eta.
\]
The boundary $\partial F$ has $2k$ faces, each with $(k-1)$-dimensional volume
$1$, so
\[
1
=
\abs{\int_{\partial F}\iota_{\partial F}^\ast\eta}
\le
\sum_{G\subset\partial F} \abs{\int_G \iota_G^\ast \eta}
\le
2k\,\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}\ge 1/(2k)$.  Taking $\eta=R\omega_0$ yields the lower
bound for every right inverse $R$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Consistency checks for downstream results]
\label{rem:downstream-consistency}
The homotopy identity (1) and the norm bound (2) are the two inputs to all
subsequent results.  As a cross-check:
\begin{itemize}
\item Proposition~\ref{thm:homotopy-decomposition} sets
  $E=K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)$; applying \eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to the
  coordinate-monomial form $\dd\omega$ gives
  $\norm{E}_{\coeff,\infty}\le\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$.  On the test
  form $\nu_0=\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\dd x_{k+1}$ one computes
  $K_{k+1}\nu_0=\eta_\ast$ from Example~\ref{ex:sanity-checks}(iii) with
  $k$ replaced by $k+1$, confirming
  $\norm{K_{k+1}\nu_0}_\infty=\frac{1}{2(k+1)}$.
\item Corollary~\ref{cor:curl-reconstruction} uses $K_2(\dd x_1\wedge\dd x_2)
  =\frac12[(x_1-\frac12)\dd x_2-(x_2-\frac12)\dd x_1]$ from
  Example~\ref{ex:sanity-checks}(ii), with norm $\frac14$ matching the claimed
  constant.
\item The cubical transfer
  (Proposition~\ref{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare}) composes
  $\mathcal I\circ K_j\circ W$ with operator norms $1$, $1/(2j)$, and $1$
  respectively, giving $1/(2j)$ overall.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Homotopy Decomposition and Curl Reconstruction}

\begin{proposition}[Defect decomposition with coordinate-monomial Stokes defect]
\label{thm:homotopy-decomposition}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and $\omega\in\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$.  Assume that
$\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.  Define
\[
\eta := K_k\omega \in \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n),
\qquad
E := K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact-plus-defect}
\omega = \dd\eta + E,
\qquad
\dd E = \dd\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:optimal-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The admissible class is all smooth $k$-forms on $I^n$ whose exterior derivative
is coordinate-monomial.  The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is the best uniform
constant $C$ such that $\norm{E}_{\coeff,\infty}\le C\norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}$ holds for
every member of this class; the lower bound is already witnessed on $I^{k+1}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:exact-plus-defect} is just
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $\eta=K_k\omega$ and
$E=K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)$.  Since $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial, applying
\eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to $K_{k+1}$ gives
\eqref{eq:optimal-defect-bound}.

To see sharpness, take the constant $(k+1)$-form
\[
\nu_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}
\]
and set $\omega_0 := K_{k+1}\nu_0$.  Then $\dd\omega_0=\nu_0$ and
$\norm{\dd\omega_0}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$.  Suppose a constant $C$ satisfied the conclusion
of the proposition for every $\omega$ with coordinate-monomial $\dd\omega$.
Applying
that assertion to $\omega_0$ yields a decomposition
\[
\omega_0 = \dd\phi + \widetilde E,
\qquad
\dd\widetilde E = \nu_0,
\qquad
\norm{\widetilde E}_{\coeff,\infty} \le C.
\]
\[
1
=
\int_{I^{k+1}} \nu_0
=
\int_{I^{k+1}} \dd\widetilde E
=
\int_{\partial I^{k+1}} \iota_{\partial I^{k+1}}^\ast \widetilde E.
\]
Since $\partial I^{k+1}$ has $2(k+1)$ faces of unit $(k)$-volume,
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial I^{k+1}} \iota_{\partial I^{k+1}}^\ast \widetilde E}
\le
\sum_{G\subset\partial I^{k+1}} \abs{\int_G \iota_G^\ast \widetilde E}
\le
2(k+1)\norm{\widetilde E}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\widetilde E}_{\coeff,\infty} \ge 1/(2(k+1))$.
No smaller uniform constant can hold even on this restricted class.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Potential reconstruction from coordinate-monomial curl]
\label{cor:curl-reconstruction}
Let $v$ be a smooth vector field on a neighborhood of $I^n$ and set
\[
\omega := \sum_{i=1}^n v_i\,\dd x_i.
\]
Assume that the curl form $\dd\omega$ is coordinate-monomial.
Then $U:=K_1\omega$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:curl-potential-bound}
\norm{v-\nabla U}_{\coeff,\infty} \le \frac14 \norm{\dd\omega}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The admissible class is all smooth $1$-forms on $I^n$ with
coordinate-monomial exterior derivative.  The constant $1/4$ is the best
(smallest) uniform constant for this class, and the lower bound is attained
on $I^2$ (see proof).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Take $U:=K_1\omega$.  By \eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $k=1$,
\[
\omega = \dd U + K_2(\dd\omega).
\]
The coefficient vector of $\omega-\dd U$ is exactly $v-\nabla U$.  Applying
\eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to $K_2$ yields
\eqref{eq:curl-potential-bound}.

For sharpness, let $\nu_0:=\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2$ and $\alpha:=K_2\nu_0$.  Then
$\dd\alpha=\nu_0$ and $\norm{\nu_0}_{\coeff,\infty}=1$.  For every $U$,
\[
\dd(\alpha-\dd U)=\nu_0.
\]
Let
\[
Q:=[0,1]^2\times\{0\}^{n-2}\subset I^n
\]
and let $\iota_Q:Q\hookrightarrow I^n$ be the inclusion.  Stokes' theorem on
$Q$ gives
\[
1
=
\int_Q \iota_Q^\ast\nu_0
=
\int_Q \iota_Q^\ast\dd(\alpha-\dd U)
=
\int_{\partial Q} \iota_{\partial Q}^\ast(\alpha-\dd U).
\]
The boundary of $Q$ has four edges of unit length, so
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial Q} \iota_{\partial Q}^\ast(\alpha-\dd U)}
\le
4\norm{\alpha-\dd U}_{\coeff,\infty}.
\]
Hence $\norm{\alpha-\dd U}_{\coeff,\infty}\ge 1/4$.
Therefore no constant smaller than $1/4$ can work uniformly.
\end{proof}

\section{One-Cube Cubical Corollaries}

This section merely records the one-cube consequence of the classical
Whitney/integration transfer: on a single cube (or hypercube graph) the same
restricted constant appears without any new homotopy operators; compare
\cite{Dodziuk1976,Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020}.

\begin{proposition}[One-cube cubical Stokes--Poincar\'e decomposition]
\label{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and let $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ be a cubical $k$-cochain
on the single standard cube $I^n$.  Assume that $d\omega$
is Whitney coordinate-monomial, i.e., $d\omega$ vanishes on every
$(k+1)$-cell outside a single cubical orientation class.  Then there exist
\[
\eta\in C^{k-1}(I^n;\RR),
\qquad
E\in C^k(I^n;\RR)
\]
such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-decomposition}
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_{\cell,\infty} \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_{\cell,\infty}.
\end{equation}
The admissible class is all cubical $k$-cochains on $I^n$ whose coboundary is
Whitney coordinate-monomial.  The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is the best uniform
constant for this class; the lower bound is witnessed on $I^{k+1}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each $1\le j\le n$ define
\[
\mathcal H_j := \mathcal I\circ K_j\circ W:
C^j(I^n;\RR)\to C^{j-1}(I^n;\RR).
\]
In particular,
\[
\mathcal H_{k+1}:C^{k+1}(I^n;\RR)\to C^k(I^n;\RR).
\]
Using the identities from Section~2, we obtain
\[
d\mathcal H_j + \mathcal H_{j+1}d = \mathrm{Id}
\qquad
\text{on } C^j(I^n;\RR)
\]
for every $1\le j\le n-1$.
Indeed, for $\theta\in C^j(I^n;\RR)$ one has
\[
\begin{aligned}
d\mathcal H_j\theta + \mathcal H_{j+1}d\theta
&=
d(\mathcal I K_jW\theta)+\mathcal I K_{j+1}W(d\theta) \\
&=
\mathcal I(\dd K_jW\theta)+\mathcal I K_{j+1}(\dd W\theta) \\
&=
\mathcal I(\dd K_j + K_{j+1}\dd)W\theta \\
&=
\mathcal I W\theta
=
\theta,
\end{aligned}
\]
where we used $\mathcal I\dd=d\mathcal I$, $\dd W = Wd$, and
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} on the smooth form $W\theta$.
Now set
\[
\eta := \mathcal H_k\omega,
\qquad
E := \mathcal H_{k+1}(d\omega).
\]
Then
\[
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\norm{E}_{\cell,\infty}
\le
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\,
\norm{K_{k+1}(W(d\omega))}_\infty
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{W(d\omega)}_{\coeff,\infty}
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_{\cell,\infty},
\]
where the second inequality uses that $W(d\omega)$ is coordinate-monomial and
the last inequality uses Proposition~\ref{prop:cubical-whitney}.  This proves
\eqref{eq:discrete-defect-bound}.

For sharpness it is enough to treat the case $n=k+1$.  Let
$\theta_{\mathrm{top}}\in C^{k+1}(I^{k+1};\RR)$ be the cochain taking the value
$1$ on the unique oriented top cell.  Its Whitney form is
$\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}$, so $\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$ is
Whitney coordinate-monomial.  Since $I^{k+1}$ is contractible and there are no
$(k+2)$-cells, there exists $\omega_0\in C^k(I^{k+1};\RR)$ with
$d\omega_0=\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$.  If
\[
\omega_0 = d\phi + \widetilde E,
\qquad
d\widetilde E = d\omega_0 = \theta_{\mathrm{top}},
\]
then
\[
1
=
\theta_{\mathrm{top}}(I^{k+1})
=
d\widetilde E(I^{k+1})
=
\sum_{F\subset\partial I^{k+1}} \pm \widetilde E(F).
\]
There are $2(k+1)$ codimension-one faces, hence
\[
1 \le 2(k+1)\norm{\widetilde E}_{\cell,\infty}.
\]
Therefore every such defect term satisfies
$\norm{\widetilde E}_{\cell,\infty}\ge 1/(2(k+1))$, so the constant cannot be improved
uniformly on the restricted class.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Scope limitation]
The operator $\mathcal H_j=\mathcal I K_jW$ is defined and used here only on a
single cube $I^n$.  On a general cubical complex, the same formula can be
applied cellwise on any star identified with a cube, but the resulting
cochains need not be globally compatible across neighboring cubes.
Constructing a global bounded cochain homotopy on an arbitrary cubical complex
requires either partition-of-unity techniques or an algebraic subdivision
argument (e.g., the Dupont--Albert framework
\cite{Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020}); such constructions are outside the
scope of this note.
\end{remark}

Let $\{0,1\}^m$ denote the $m$-dimensional hypercube graph, identified with the
1-skeleton of $I^m$.  Squares correspond to $2$-faces, so $d\omega$ records the
square defect of an edge cochain $\omega$.

\begin{corollary}[Hypercube consequences]\label{cor:hypercube-gradient}
Let $\omega\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ with Whitney coordinate-monomial square defect, and
set $\varepsilon:=\norm{d\omega}_{\cell,\infty}$.  Then:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.2em]
  \item There exist $f\in C^0(I^m;\RR)$ and $r\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ such that
  $\omega=df+r$, $dr=d\omega$, and $\norm{r}_{\cell,\infty}\le \varepsilon/4$.
  \item If $\omega(u,v)=\log\frac{q(u,v)}{q(v,u)}$ for positive jump rates
  $q(u,v)$ on oriented edges, then the same potential $f$ satisfies
  \[
  e^{-\varepsilon/4}
  \le
  \frac{\pi_f(u)\,q(u,v)}{\pi_f(v)\,q(v,u)}
  \le
  e^{\varepsilon/4},
  \qquad
  \pi_f(u):=e^{f(u)},\ u\sim v.
  \]
\end{itemize}
No smaller constant works uniformly within this Whitney coordinate-monomial
class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition~\ref{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare} with $k=1$ to obtain
$(f,r)=(\eta,E)$.  The second item records the logarithmic reformulation used
in detailed-balance estimates; the edgewise inequality follows from the bound
on $\norm{r}_{\cell,\infty}$ and sharpness reduces to the proposition.
\end{proof}

\section{Boundary Calibration and Boundary Rigidity}

\subsection{Calibration Lower Bound}

Throughout this section vectors are measured in the coordinate
$\ell_\infty$ norm
\[
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}
:=
\operatorname*{ess\,sup}_{x\in\Omega} \max_{1\le i\le n} \abs{V_i(x)},
\]
which matches $\norm{\iota_V\mathrm{vol}}_{\coeff,\infty}$.  The dual boundary
functional is the anisotropic perimeter
\[
P_1(E)
:=
\int_{\partial E} \abs{\nu_E}_1\,\dd\Haus^{n-1},
\qquad
\abs{\nu_E}_1 := \sum_{i=1}^n \abs{(\nu_E)_i},
\]
as in the anisotropic max-flow/min-cut and calibrable-set frameworks
\cite{Strang1983,Grieser2006,GueriniSavo2004,Nozawa1990,Strang2010,Leonardi2015,CasellesFaccioloMeinhardt2009,CasellesChambolleMollNovaga2008,CasellesChambolleNovaga2007,CasellesChambolleNovaga2011}.

\begin{proposition}[Boundary calibration inequality]
\label{prop:boundary-calibration}
Let $\Omega\subset\RR^n$ be a bounded Lipschitz domain, and let
\[
\mathrm{vol} := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n.
\]
Define
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)
:=
\inf\Bigl\{
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}:
V\in C^\infty(U;\RR^n),\ U\supset \overline{\Omega}\ \text{open},
\ \Div V = 1 \text{ on }\Omega
\Bigr\},
\]
or, equivalently, the infimum of $\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}$ over smooth
$(n-1)$-forms $\eta$ on a neighborhood of $\overline\Omega$ satisfying
$\dd\eta=\mathrm{vol}$ on $\Omega$.  Then every admissible vector field $V$
and every Lipschitz subdomain $E\subset \Omega$ satisfy
\[
\Vol(E)\le \norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}\, P_1(E),
\qquad
P_1(E)=\int_{\partial E} \abs{\nu_E}_1\,\dd\Haus^{n-1}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)\ge
\sup\left\{
\frac{\Vol(E)}{P_1(E)}:
E\subset \Omega,\ E \text{ Lipschitz}
\right\}.
\]
For the cube $I^k$ we have $P_1(I^k)=2k$, so $1/(2k)$ is the natural lower
bound dictated by Proposition~\ref{prop:boundary-calibration}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
Working instead with the Euclidean/comass norm in Section~5 would require an
explicit statement that the norm changes, as in the Euclidean max-flow/min-cut
estimate of Grieser~\cite{Grieser2006}.  We keep the coefficient
$\ell_\infty$ norm throughout so that the dual is automatically the anisotropic
$P_1$ perimeter.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
For a smooth vector field $V$, the form $\eta:=\iota_V\mathrm{vol}$ satisfies
$\dd\eta=(\Div V)\mathrm{vol}$, and under the coefficient sup norm used in this
paper one has $\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}=\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}$.  This identifies the two
definitions of $\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)$.  The divergence theorem on
Lipschitz domains used below is standard; see
\cite{Federer1969,EvansGariepy2015,Maggi2012}.

Now let $E\subset\Omega$ be a Lipschitz subdomain and let $V$ be admissible.
The divergence theorem gives
\[
\Vol(E)
=
\int_E \Div V\,\dd x
=
\int_{\partial E} V\cdot \nu_E\,\dd\Haus^{n-1}
\le
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)} \int_{\partial E} |\nu_E|_1\,\dd\Haus^{n-1}
=
\norm{V}_{L^\infty(\Omega;\ell_\infty)}\,P_1(E).
\]
Since $|V\cdot \nu_E|\le \norm{V}_{\ell_\infty}|\nu_E|_1$ pointwise, the desired
inequality follows.  Taking the supremum over $E$ and then the infimum over
admissible $V$ yields the stated lower bound.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Boundary Rigidity on the Cube}

\begin{theorem}[Boundary rigidity of coefficient-$L^\infty$ minimizers on the cube]
\label{thm:boundary-rigidity}
Let $k\ge 1$ and define
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
on $I^k=[0,1]^k$.  The admissible class is all smooth $(k-1)$-forms $\eta$
on $I^k$ satisfying $\dd\eta=\omega_0$.
Theorem~\ref{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse} gives the minimum
$\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty}=1/(2k)$.  Suppose
$\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^k)$ attains this minimum:
\[
\dd\eta = \omega_0,
\qquad
\norm{\eta}_{\coeff,\infty} = \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
For each $j\in\{1,\dots,k\}$ and $\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}$ let
\[
F_{j,\varepsilon}:=\{x_j=\varepsilon\},
\qquad
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}:F_{j,\varepsilon}\hookrightarrow I^k.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast \eta
=
(-1)^{j-1}\frac{\varepsilon-\tfrac12}{k}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
on every face $F_{j,\varepsilon}$.  In particular, all minimizers of
$\dd\eta=\omega_0$ have the same boundary trace.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1
=
\int_{I^k}\omega_0
=
\int_{\partial I^k}\iota_{\partial I^k}^\ast\eta
=
\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(
\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta
-
\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta
\right).
\]
Each face has $(k-1)$-dimensional volume $1$, so by the norm bound
\[
\abs{\int_{F_{j,\varepsilon}} \iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta}
\le
\frac{1}{2k}.
\]
There are $2k$ face integrals.  Since their signed sum equals $1$, the triangle
inequality can be sharp only if every face integral has maximal absolute value
and the signs align with the boundary orientation.  Therefore
\[
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta = \frac{1}{2k},
\qquad
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta = -\frac{1}{2k}.
\]

Now each pullback has the form
\[
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta
=
f_{j,\varepsilon}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
for a continuous scalar function $f_{j,\varepsilon}$ on $F_{j,\varepsilon}$,
with $\abs{f_{j,\varepsilon}}\le 1/(2k)$.  Because the face volume is $1$ and
the integral attains the extremal value $\pm 1/(2k)$, continuity forces
$f_{j,\varepsilon}$ to be constant on the face with the corresponding sign.
This is exactly \eqref{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Explicit minimizer]
\label{cor:explicit-minimizer}
The form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:explicit-minimizer}
\eta_\ast
:=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(x_j-\frac12\right)\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
satisfies
\[
\dd\eta_\ast = \omega_0,
\qquad
\norm{\eta_\ast}_{\coeff,\infty} = \frac{1}{2k},
\]
and its boundary trace is the canonical one from
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate term by term:
\[
\dd\eta_\ast
=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}\dd x_j\wedge
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
=
\omega_0.
\]
Since $\abs{x_j-1/2}\le 1/2$ on $I^k$, one has
$\norm{\eta_\ast}_{\coeff,\infty}=1/(2k)$.  Restricting \eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer} to
$x_j=0$ and $x_j=1$ gives the stated boundary traces immediately.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse} and
Corollary~\ref{cor:explicit-minimizer} together show that
$\mathcal C_\infty(I^k)=1/(2k)$.  Proposition~\ref{prop:boundary-calibration}
gives the matching lower bound already from the test domain $E=I^k$, since
$\Vol(I^k)=1$ and $P_1(I^k)=2k$.  The point of
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity} is stronger: the minimizer is not unique as
an interior primitive, but its boundary trace is completely rigid.  Indeed,
take a smooth $(k-2)$-form $\psi$ compactly supported in the interior of $I^k$
and set $\eta_\varepsilon:=\eta_\ast+\varepsilon\,\dd\psi$.  Then
$\dd\eta_\varepsilon=\omega_0$ for every $\varepsilon$,
$\iota_{\partial I^k}^\ast\eta_\varepsilon=\iota_{\partial I^k}^\ast\eta_\ast$,
and $\norm{\eta_\varepsilon}_{\coeff,\infty}=1/(2k)$ for all sufficiently small
$|\varepsilon|$.  The multiplicity of minimizers is therefore purely an
interior phenomenon.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Scope and limitations]
This note isolates a local cube phenomenon.  The sharp constant $1/(2k)$ comes
from the interaction between the radial homotopy and the coefficient sup norm
on the incidence-one class (Proposition~\ref{prop:incidence-criterion}); the
cubical section records only the corresponding one-cube Whitney transfer
(Proposition~\ref{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare}); and the boundary theorem
identifies the common trace of all coefficient-$L^\infty$ minimizers
(Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity}).  What is not attempted is equally
important: no general-domain theory, no Sobolev-space estimate, and no global
patching result on arbitrary cubical complexes.  The natural next step would
be a sharp-constant analysis on a full cubical complex via the Dupont--Albert
subdivision framework \cite{Dupont1976,DupontBook1978,Albert2020}, but the
compatibility conditions across cube boundaries introduce combinatorial
complications that fall outside the present scope.
\end{remark}
