\begin{abstract}
We isolate the geometric-analysis core of the scan-projection manuscript.  On
the unit cube we write down an explicit homotopy operator $K_k$ and prove the
dimension-free sharp bound
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty
\]
for smooth coordinate-decomposable $k$-forms.  This yields an optimal
Stokes-defect decomposition for forms whose exterior derivative is
coordinate-decomposable and, in degree one, an optimal $L^\infty$
reconstruction bound for vector fields whose curl $2$-form is
coordinate-decomposable.  We then transfer the same constant to cubical
cochains whose Whitney coboundaries are decomposable and obtain two discrete
consequences: gradient consistency on the hypercube and an optimal
near-detailed-balance statement for edge-based jump asymmetries in the same
restricted class.  A second part of the paper interprets the sharp inverse
through boundary geometry: we record a Cheeger--Stokes duality principle,
prove that every cube minimizer has the same boundary trace, and identify an
explicit extremal primitive.  Finally, for dyadic outer approximations of
compact sets we show that one universal probe form reads the volume of the
approximation exactly and therefore recovers upper Minkowski dimension from a
boundary-flux sequence.  The paper deliberately stops at this deterministic
geometric layer and does not import the probabilistic scan-error machinery of
the companion manuscript.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The point of this paper is narrow.  We study one explicit inverse problem on
cubes and follow it to its geometric endpoint.  Let $I^n=[0,1]^n$.  Given a
closed decomposable $k$-form $\omega$ on $I^n$, can one choose a primitive
whose $L^\infty$ size is controlled by an explicit universal constant?  If
$\omega$ has decomposable exterior derivative, can one still separate it into
an exact part plus an error term that depends only on $\dd\omega$?  And if
such a bounded inverse exists, what does its extremal behavior say about
boundary probes and dyadic outer approximations?

The answer is especially clean for the radial contraction from the cube center.
Because the coefficient sup norm is taken componentwise, the sharp contraction
estimate behind the inverse bound is valid only for forms consisting of a
single coordinate wedge monomial.  The associated homotopy operator $K_k$ is
still explicit and dimension-free, and on this decomposable class it is sharp:
for every smooth decomposable $k$-form one has
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty,
\]
and the constant $1/(2k)$ cannot be improved.  Applied to forms with
decomposable exterior derivative, this immediately yields an optimal
decomposition
\[
\omega = \dd\eta + E,
\qquad
\norm{E}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_\infty.
\]
The degree-one case recovers an optimal sup-norm estimate for approximating a
vector field by a gradient from its decomposable curl tensor.

The same constant survives discretization for cochains whose Whitney
coboundaries are decomposable.  Using a cubical Whitney map and the integration
map, the continuous homotopy operator transfers to the standard cubical
cochain complex.  This gives a sharp cubical Stokes--Poincare lemma, an
optimal square-defect correction statement on the hypercube, and a compact
near-detailed-balance theorem for edge jump asymmetries in that same
restricted class.  These discrete results are included because they are
geometric consequences of the same inverse estimate, not because we want to
start a separate stochastic theory.

The boundary side of the paper is equally rigid.  We formulate a
Cheeger--Stokes duality principle: minimizing the coefficient sup norm of a
primitive of the volume form is dual to an isoperimetric ratio.  On the cube,
every minimizer has the same boundary trace, so the optimal primitive is not
only norm-sharp but boundary-rigid.  This rigidity then feeds directly into the
final dyadic result: for a compact set $A\subset I^n$, the boundary of the
dyadic outer approximation $U_m(A)$ can be tested by one universal
$(n-1)$-form $\omega_0$, and the flux identity
\[
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0 = \Vol(U_m(A))
\]
turns boundary data into a direct Minkowski-dimension readout.

This manuscript is deliberately separate from the scan-error companion.  The
companion paper starts from prefix filtrations, Bayes risk, and rate laws for
classification error.  Here we stop earlier.  Boundary flux is treated as a
deterministic geometric observable, not as a posterior statistic, and no
probabilistic coding or complexity material is imported.

Standard background on de Rham homotopy formulas, Whitney forms, finite
perimeter, and Minkowski dimension may be found in
\cite{BottTu1982,Whitney1957,IwaniecLutoborski1993,ArnoldFalkWinther2010,Federer1969,EvansGariepy2015,Falconer2014}.

\section{Cubical Forms and Homotopy Operators}

\subsection{Continuous Cubical Setup}

Fix $n\ge 1$ and write
\[
I^n := [0,1]^n,
\qquad
c := \left(\frac12,\dots,\frac12\right).
\]
For $1\le k\le n$, let $\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ denote the smooth
$k$-forms on a neighborhood of $I^n$, restricted to the cube.  If
\[
\omega = \sum_{\abs{I}=k} a_I(x)\,\dd x_I,
\]
we use the coefficient sup norm
\[
\norm{\omega}_\infty := \max_{\abs{I}=k}\ \sup_{x\in I^n} \abs{a_I(x)}.
\]
All $L^\infty$ statements below refer to this coordinate norm.

\begin{definition}[Coordinate-decomposable form]
\label{def:decomposable-form}
A smooth $k$-form on $I^n$ is called coordinate-decomposable if it has the
form
\[
\omega = f(x)\,\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}
\]
for some smooth scalar function $f$ and some ordered index set
$1\le i_1<\cdots<i_k\le n$.  For brevity we say \emph{decomposable} below.
\end{definition}

Let $X(x):=x-c$ be the radial vector field centered at $c$, and let
\[
H:[0,1]\times I^n \to I^n,
\qquad
H(t,x):=c+t(x-c).
\]
For each $t\in[0,1]$, write $H_t(x):=H(t,x)$.  Thus $H_1=\mathrm{Id}$ and
$H_0$ collapses the cube to its center.

\begin{definition}[Cubical homotopy operator]
\label{def:homotopy-operator}
For $1\le k\le n$ define
\[
K_k:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-operator}
(K_k\omega)(x)
:=
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\bigl(\iota_X\omega\bigr)\bigl(H_t(x)\bigr)\,\dd t.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The operator is the standard radial homotopy formula centered at the cube
midpoint; compare \cite[Sec.~3]{BottTu1982}.

\begin{lemma}[Contraction estimate on decomposable forms]
\label{lem:contraction-estimate}
For every decomposable
\[
\omega = f(x)\,\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{i_k}
\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)
\]
one has
\[
\norm{\iota_X\omega}_\infty \le \frac12 \norm{\omega}_\infty.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For such a form,
\[
\iota_X\omega
=
\sum_{r=1}^k (-1)^{r-1} f(x)\bigl(x_{i_r}-\tfrac12\bigr)\,
\dd x_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_{i_r}}\wedge\cdots\wedge
\dd x_{i_k}.
\]
Each component of $X(x)=x-c$ has absolute value at most $1/2$ on $I^n$, so
every coefficient in $\iota_X\omega$ has absolute value at most
$\frac12\sup_{x\in I^n}\abs{f(x)}=\frac12\norm{\omega}_\infty$.  This is
exactly the desired estimate.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The restriction to decomposable forms is essential for the coefficient sup
norm.  On $I^3$ let
\[
\omega := \dd x_1\wedge \dd x_3 + \dd x_2\wedge \dd x_3.
\]
Then $\norm{\omega}_\infty=1$, while
\[
\iota_X\omega
=
\bigl(x_1-\tfrac12+x_2-\tfrac12\bigr)\dd x_3
-\bigl(x_3-\tfrac12\bigr)(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
so $\norm{\iota_X\omega}_\infty=1$.  A direct computation gives
\[
K_2\omega
=
\frac{x_1+x_2-1}{3}\,\dd x_3
-\frac{x_3-\tfrac12}{3}\,(\dd x_1+\dd x_2),
\]
hence $\norm{K_2\omega}_\infty=\frac13>\frac14=\frac{1}{2k}$ when $k=2$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Cubical Cochains}

We also use the standard cubical cell decomposition of $I^n$.  Let
$C^k(I^n;\RR)$ be the space of real-valued cubical $k$-cochains with
coboundary $d$.  For $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$ we write
\[
\norm{\omega}_\infty := \max_{\sigma^{(k)}} \abs{\omega(\sigma)},
\]
where the maximum runs over oriented $k$-cells.

The transfer from forms to cochains uses the standard Whitney--integration
pair.  We shall use only the following properties, which are standard in the
finite-element exterior calculus viewpoint
\cite{Whitney1957,ArnoldFalkWinther2010}: there exist linear maps
\[
W:C^k(I^n;\RR)\to \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n),
\qquad
\mathcal I:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to C^k(I^n;\RR)
\]
such that
\[
\mathcal I\circ W = \mathrm{Id},
\qquad
\dd\circ W = W\circ d,
\qquad
\mathcal I\circ \dd = d\circ \mathcal I,
\]
and
\[
\norm{W}_{\infty\to\infty}\le 1,
\qquad
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\le 1.
\]
These identities are all that will be needed in the discrete section.

\begin{definition}[Whitney-decomposable cochain]
\label{def:whitney-decomposable}
A cubical $k$-cochain $\theta$ is called Whitney-decomposable if $W\theta$ is
decomposable in the sense of Definition~\ref{def:decomposable-form}.
\end{definition}

\section{Optimal Bounded Inverses on Cubes}

\subsection{Homotopy Identity and Sharp Constant}

\begin{theorem}[Optimal bounded Poincare--Stokes inverse on decomposable cube forms]
\label{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse}
Let $1\le k\le n$.  In the setting of
Definition~\ref{def:homotopy-operator}, the following hold.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item For every $\omega\in\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:homotopy-identity}
\dd(K_k\omega) + K_{k+1}(\dd\omega) = \omega.
\end{equation}
In particular, if $\dd\omega=0$, then $\omega=\dd(K_k\omega)$.
\item If $\omega$ is decomposable, then the operator satisfies the
dimension-free bound
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound}
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
\item The constant $1/(2k)$ is sharp even under this restriction: if
$R:\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)\to \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ is linear
and $\dd(R\omega)=\omega$ for every closed decomposable $k$-form $\omega$,
then
\[
\norm{R}_{\infty\to\infty}\ge \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate the pullback along the contraction:
\[
\frac{\dd}{\dd t}(H_t^\ast\omega)
=
H_t^\ast\bigl(\mathcal L_X\omega\bigr)
=
H_t^\ast\bigl(\dd\,\iota_X\omega + \iota_X\dd\omega\bigr),
\]
where $\mathcal L_X$ is the Lie derivative.  Integrating from $0$ to $1$
gives
\[
H_1^\ast\omega - H_0^\ast\omega
=
\dd\int_0^1 H_t^\ast(\iota_X\omega)\,\dd t
+ \int_0^1 H_t^\ast(\iota_X\dd\omega)\,\dd t.
\]
Because $H_1=\mathrm{Id}$ and $H_0$ is constant, $H_0^\ast\omega=0$ for
$k\ge 1$.  Pullback by $H_t$ scales the $k-1$ tangential directions in
$\iota_X\omega$ by $t^{k-1}$, so the two integrals are exactly
$\dd(K_k\omega)$ and $K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)$.  This proves
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity}.

For the norm bound, assume now that $\omega$ is decomposable.  Then
Lemma~\ref{lem:contraction-estimate} and \eqref{eq:homotopy-operator} give
\[
\norm{K_k\omega}_\infty
\le
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\norm{\iota_X\omega}_\infty\,\dd t
\le
\int_0^1 t^{k-1}\,\dd t \cdot \frac12 \norm{\omega}_\infty
=
\frac{1}{2k}\norm{\omega}_\infty.
\]

For sharpness, consider the constant form
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
and the $k$-face
\[
F := [0,1]^k \times \{0\}^{n-k}\subset I^n.
\]
If $\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$ satisfies $\dd\eta=\omega_0$, then
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1 = \int_F \omega_0 = \int_{\partial F}\eta.
\]
The boundary $\partial F$ has $2k$ faces, each with $(k-1)$-dimensional volume
$1$, so
\[
1
=
\abs{\int_{\partial F}\eta}
\le
\sum_{G\subset\partial F} \abs{\int_G \eta}
\le
2k\,\norm{\eta}_\infty.
\]
Hence $\norm{\eta}_\infty\ge 1/(2k)$.  Taking $\eta=R\omega_0$ yields the lower
bound for every right inverse $R$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Homotopy Decomposition and Curl Reconstruction}

\begin{theorem}[Optimal decomposition with decomposable Stokes defect]
\label{thm:homotopy-decomposition}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and $\omega\in\Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n)$.  Assume that
$\dd\omega$ is decomposable.  Define
\[
\eta := K_k\omega \in \Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n),
\qquad
E := K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)\in \Omega^k_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^n).
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact-plus-defect}
\omega = \dd\eta + E,
\qquad
\dd E = \dd\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:optimal-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{\dd\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is optimal even under the restriction that
$\dd\omega$ is decomposable.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:exact-plus-defect} is just
\eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $\eta=K_k\omega$ and
$E=K_{k+1}(\dd\omega)$.  Since $\dd\omega$ is decomposable, applying
\eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to $K_{k+1}$ gives
\eqref{eq:optimal-defect-bound}.

To see sharpness, take the constant $(k+1)$-form
\[
\nu_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}
\]
and set $\omega_0 := K_{k+1}\nu_0$.  Then $\dd\omega_0=\nu_0$ and
$\norm{\dd\omega_0}_\infty=1$.  For any decomposition
\[
\omega_0 = \dd\phi + \widetilde E
\]
with $\widetilde E$ determined only by $\dd\omega_0=\nu_0$, we have
$\dd\widetilde E = \nu_0$.  The sharp part of
Theorem~\ref{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse} in degree $k+1$ therefore implies
\[
\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty \ge \frac{1}{2(k+1)}.
\]
No smaller uniform constant can hold even on this restricted class.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Potential reconstruction from decomposable curl]
\label{cor:curl-reconstruction}
Let $v\in C^1(I^n;\RR^n)$ and set
\[
\omega := \sum_{i=1}^n v_i\,\dd x_i.
\]
Assume that the curl form $\dd\omega$ is decomposable.
Then there exists $U\in C^2(I^n)$ such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:curl-potential-bound}
\norm{v-\nabla U}_\infty \le \frac14 \norm{\dd\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/4$ is optimal within this decomposable-curl class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Take $U:=K_1\omega$.  By \eqref{eq:homotopy-identity} with $k=1$,
\[
\omega = \dd U + K_2(\dd\omega).
\]
The coefficient vector of $\omega-\dd U$ is exactly $v-\nabla U$.  Applying
\eqref{eq:continuous-supnorm-bound} to $K_2$ yields
\eqref{eq:curl-potential-bound}.

For sharpness, let $\nu_0:=\dd x_1\wedge \dd x_2$ and $\alpha:=K_2\nu_0$.  Then
$\dd\alpha=\nu_0$ and $\norm{\nu_0}_\infty=1$.  For every $U$,
\[
\dd(\alpha-\dd U)=\nu_0.
\]
By the sharpness statement in degree two from
Theorem~\ref{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse},
\[
\norm{\alpha-\dd U}_\infty \ge \frac14.
\]
Therefore no constant smaller than $1/4$ can work uniformly.
\end{proof}

\section{Cubical Cochains and Hypercube Consequences}

\subsection{A Sharp Cubical Stokes--Poincare Lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Cubical Stokes--Poincare lemma with Whitney-decomposable coboundary]
\label{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare}
Let $1\le k\le n-1$ and let $\omega\in C^k(I^n;\RR)$.  Assume that $d\omega$
is Whitney-decomposable.  Then there exist
\[
\eta\in C^{k-1}(I^n;\RR),
\qquad
E\in C^k(I^n;\RR)
\]
such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-decomposition}
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega,
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-defect-bound}
\norm{E}_\infty \le \frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_\infty.
\end{equation}
The constant $1/(2(k+1))$ is sharp in the uniform dimension-free sense on this
restricted class: no smaller constant can hold simultaneously for all cubes
$I^n$ with $n\ge k+1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Define
\[
\mathcal H_{k+1} := \mathcal I\circ K_{k+1}\circ W:
C^{k+1}(I^n;\RR)\to C^k(I^n;\RR).
\]
Using the identities from Section~2, we obtain
\[
d\mathcal H_j + \mathcal H_{j+1}d = \mathrm{Id}
\qquad
\text{on } C^j(I^n;\RR)
\]
for every $1\le j\le n-1$.
Now set
\[
\eta := \mathcal H_k\omega,
\qquad
E := \mathcal H_{k+1}(d\omega).
\]
Then
\[
\omega = d\eta + E,
\qquad
dE = d\omega.
\]
Moreover,
\[
\norm{E}_\infty
\le
\norm{\mathcal I}_{\infty\to\infty}\,
\norm{K_{k+1}(W(d\omega))}_\infty
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{W(d\omega)}_\infty
\le
\frac{1}{2(k+1)}\norm{d\omega}_\infty,
\]
where the second inequality uses that $W(d\omega)$ is decomposable.  This
proves \eqref{eq:discrete-defect-bound}.

For sharpness it is enough to treat the case $n=k+1$.  Let
$\theta_{\mathrm{top}}\in C^{k+1}(I^{k+1};\RR)$ be the cochain taking the value
$1$ on the unique oriented top cell.  Its Whitney form is
$\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_{k+1}$, so $\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$ is
Whitney-decomposable.  Since $I^{k+1}$ is contractible and there are no
$(k+2)$-cells, there exists $\omega_0\in C^k(I^{k+1};\RR)$ with
$d\omega_0=\theta_{\mathrm{top}}$.  If
\[
\omega_0 = d\phi + \widetilde E,
\qquad
d\widetilde E = d\omega_0 = \theta_{\mathrm{top}},
\]
then
\[
1
=
\theta_{\mathrm{top}}(I^{k+1})
=
d\widetilde E(I^{k+1})
=
\sum_{F\subset\partial I^{k+1}} \pm \widetilde E(F).
\]
There are $2(k+1)$ codimension-one faces, hence
\[
1 \le 2(k+1)\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty.
\]
Therefore every such defect term satisfies
$\norm{\widetilde E}_\infty\ge 1/(2(k+1))$, so the constant cannot be improved
uniformly on the restricted class.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Hypercube Corollaries}

Let $\{0,1\}^m$ be the vertex set of the $m$-dimensional hypercube, with edges
joining words that differ in exactly one coordinate.  Identifying this graph
with the $1$-skeleton of the cubical complex $I^m$, an edge function is simply
a cubical $1$-cochain and the square defect is its coboundary on $2$-faces.

\begin{corollary}[Gradient consistency from square defects]
\label{cor:hypercube-gradient}
Let $\omega\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ and write
\[
\varepsilon := \norm{d\omega}_\infty.
\]
Assume that $d\omega$ is Whitney-decomposable.
Then there exist a vertex potential $f\in C^0(I^m;\RR)$ and a residual
$r\in C^1(I^m;\RR)$ such that
\[
\omega = df + r,
\qquad
\norm{r}_\infty \le \frac{\varepsilon}{4},
\qquad
dr = d\omega.
\]
The constant $1/4$ is optimal within this Whitney-decomposable class.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Theorem~\ref{thm:discrete-stokes-poincare} with $k=1$ and set
$f:=\eta$, $r:=E$.  The optimality is the $k=1$ case of the theorem.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Near detailed balance from square curvature]
\label{thm:near-detailed-balance}
Let $q(u,v)>0$ be jump rates on the hypercube graph, defined for adjacent
vertices $u\sim v$.  Define the antisymmetric edge cochain
\[
\omega(u,v) := \log \frac{q(u,v)}{q(v,u)}.
\]
Let
\[
\varepsilon := \norm{d\omega}_\infty,
\]
where $d\omega$ records the signed cycle defect on each square face.  Assume
that $d\omega$ is Whitney-decomposable.  Then
there exists a potential $f:\{0,1\}^m\to \RR$ such that for every edge
$u\sim v$,
\[
\abs{\omega(u,v) - (f(v)-f(u))} \le \frac{\varepsilon}{4}.
\]
Consequently, with $\pi_f(u):=e^{f(u)}$ one has the edgewise multiplicative
bound
\[
e^{-\varepsilon/4}
\le
\frac{\pi_f(u)\,q(u,v)}{\pi_f(v)\,q(v,u)}
\le
e^{\varepsilon/4},
\qquad
u\sim v.
\]
The constant $1/4$ is optimal within this Whitney-decomposable class.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Corollary~\ref{cor:hypercube-gradient} gives a potential $f$ with
\[
\norm{\omega-df}_\infty \le \frac{\varepsilon}{4}.
\]
For an oriented edge $(u,v)$,
\[
\log \frac{\pi_f(u)\,q(u,v)}{\pi_f(v)\,q(v,u)}
=
f(u)-f(v)+\log q(u,v)-\log q(v,u)
=
\omega(u,v)-(f(v)-f(u)).
\]
Taking absolute values and exponentiating yields the two-sided estimate.  The
sharpness is inherited from Corollary~\ref{cor:hypercube-gradient}.
\end{proof}

\section{Cheeger--Stokes Duality and Boundary Rigidity}

\subsection{Duality}

\begin{proposition}[Cheeger--Stokes duality]
\label{prop:cheeger-stokes}
Let $\Omega\subset\RR^n$ be a bounded Lipschitz domain, and let
\[
\mathrm{vol} := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n.
\]
Define
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)
:=
\inf\Bigl\{
\norm{\eta}_\infty:
\eta\in \Omega^{n-1}(\Omega),\ \dd\eta = \mathrm{vol}
\Bigr\},
\]
and
\[
h(\Omega)
:=
\inf\left\{
\frac{\Haus^{n-1}(\partial^\ast E\cap \Omega)}{\Vol(E)}:
E\subset\Omega,\ 0<\Vol(E)<\infty,\ E \text{ of finite perimeter}
\right\}.
\]
Assume there exists a Cheeger minimizer $E_\ast$ and a calibrating vector field
$V_\ast\in L^\infty(\Omega;\RR^n)$ such that
\[
\Div V_\ast = 1 \text{ in distributions},
\qquad
\norm{V_\ast}_\infty \le h(\Omega)^{-1},
\qquad
V_\ast\cdot \nu_{E_\ast} = h(\Omega)^{-1}
\ \text{a.e. on }\partial^\ast E_\ast\cap\Omega.
\]
Then
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega) = h(\Omega)^{-1}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\eta = \iota_V \mathrm{vol}$.  Then $\dd\eta=(\Div V)\mathrm{vol}$, so
the constraint $\dd\eta=\mathrm{vol}$ is equivalent to $\Div V=1$.  Under the
coefficient sup norm used in this paper, $\norm{\eta}_\infty=\norm{V}_\infty$.

Let $E\subset\Omega$ be a set of finite perimeter.  The divergence theorem for
finite-perimeter sets \cite[Sec.~4.5.6]{Federer1969}; see also
\cite{EvansGariepy2015} yields
\[
\Vol(E)
=
\int_E \Div V\,\dd x
=
\int_{\partial^\ast E\cap\Omega} V\cdot \nu_E\,\dd\Haus^{n-1}
\le
\norm{V}_\infty \Haus^{n-1}(\partial^\ast E\cap\Omega).
\]
Hence
\[
\norm{V}_\infty \ge
\frac{\Vol(E)}{\Haus^{n-1}(\partial^\ast E\cap\Omega)}.
\]
Taking the supremum over $E$ and then the infimum over admissible $V$ gives
\[
\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)\ge h(\Omega)^{-1}.
\]

If $V_\ast$ is a calibrating field, then
$\eta_\ast:=\iota_{V_\ast}\mathrm{vol}$ satisfies $\dd\eta_\ast=\mathrm{vol}$
and $\norm{\eta_\ast}_\infty\le h(\Omega)^{-1}$.  Therefore
$\mathcal C_\infty(\Omega)\le h(\Omega)^{-1}$, and equality follows.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Boundary Rigidity on the Cube}

\begin{theorem}[Boundary rigidity of cube minimizers]
\label{thm:boundary-rigidity}
Let $k\ge 1$ and define
\[
\omega_0 := \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
on $I^k=[0,1]^k$.  Suppose
$\eta\in\Omega^{k-1}_{\mathrm{sm}}(I^k)$ satisfies
\[
\dd\eta = \omega_0,
\qquad
\norm{\eta}_\infty = \frac{1}{2k}.
\]
For each $j\in\{1,\dots,k\}$ and $\varepsilon\in\{0,1\}$ let
\[
F_{j,\varepsilon}:=\{x_j=\varepsilon\},
\qquad
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}:F_{j,\varepsilon}\hookrightarrow I^k.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast \eta
=
(-1)^{j-1}\frac{\varepsilon-\tfrac12}{k}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
on every face $F_{j,\varepsilon}$.  In particular, all minimizers of
$\dd\eta=\omega_0$ have the same boundary trace.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Stokes' theorem gives
\[
1
=
\int_{I^k}\omega_0
=
\int_{\partial I^k}\eta
=
\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(
\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta
-
\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta
\right).
\]
Each face has $(k-1)$-dimensional volume $1$, so by the norm bound
\[
\abs{\int_{F_{j,\varepsilon}} \iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta}
\le
\frac{1}{2k}.
\]
There are $2k$ face integrals.  Since their signed sum equals $1$, the triangle
inequality can be sharp only if every face integral has maximal absolute value
and the signs align with the boundary orientation.  Therefore
\[
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,1}} \iota_{j,1}^\ast\eta = \frac{1}{2k},
\qquad
(-1)^{j-1}\int_{F_{j,0}} \iota_{j,0}^\ast\eta = -\frac{1}{2k}.
\]

Now each pullback has the form
\[
\iota_{j,\varepsilon}^\ast\eta
=
f_{j,\varepsilon}\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\]
for a continuous scalar function $f_{j,\varepsilon}$ on $F_{j,\varepsilon}$,
with $\abs{f_{j,\varepsilon}}\le 1/(2k)$.  Because the face volume is $1$ and
the integral attains the extremal value $\pm 1/(2k)$, continuity forces
$f_{j,\varepsilon}$ to be constant on the face with the corresponding sign.
This is exactly \eqref{eq:boundary-rigidity-formula}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Explicit minimizer]
\label{cor:explicit-minimizer}
The form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:explicit-minimizer}
\eta_\ast
:=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}
\left(x_j-\frac12\right)\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
\end{equation}
satisfies
\[
\dd\eta_\ast = \omega_0,
\qquad
\norm{\eta_\ast}_\infty = \frac{1}{2k},
\]
and its boundary trace is the canonical one from
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate term by term:
\[
\dd\eta_\ast
=
\frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^k (-1)^{j-1}\dd x_j\wedge
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_k
=
\omega_0.
\]
Since $\abs{x_j-1/2}\le 1/2$ on $I^k$, one has
$\norm{\eta_\ast}_\infty=1/(2k)$.  Restricting \eqref{eq:explicit-minimizer} to
$x_j=0$ and $x_j=1$ gives the stated boundary traces immediately.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:optimal-continuous-inverse} and
Corollary~\ref{cor:explicit-minimizer} together show that
$\mathcal C_\infty(I^k)=1/(2k)$.  The point of
Theorem~\ref{thm:boundary-rigidity} is stronger: the minimizer is not unique as
an interior primitive, but its boundary trace is completely rigid.
\end{remark}

\section{Dyadic Outer Approximations and Boundary Readout}

For a nonempty compact set $A\subset I^n$ and an integer $m\ge 1$, let
$U_m(A)$ be the union of all dyadic cubes of side length $2^{-m}$ that
intersect $A$:
\[
U_m(A)
:=
\bigcup\Bigl\{
Q:\ Q \text{ dyadic } n\text{-cube of side }2^{-m},\ Q\cap A\neq\emptyset
\Bigr\}.
\]
This is a polyhedral neighborhood of $A$ with naturally oriented boundary
$\partial U_m(A)$.

\begin{theorem}[Dyadic Stokes gain and Minkowski readout]
\label{thm:dyadic-readout}
Let $A\subset I^n$ be nonempty and compact.  Assume that for some
$d\in[0,n)$ and some constant $C_A<\infty$ one has
\[
\Vol(A_\varepsilon)\le C_A\,\varepsilon^{n-d}
\qquad
(0<\varepsilon\le 1),
\]
where
\[
A_\varepsilon:=\{x\in I^n:\dist(x,A)\le \varepsilon\}.
\]
Then every smooth $(n-1)$-form $\omega$ on a neighborhood of $I^n$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dyadic-stokes-bound}
\abs{\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega}
\le
C_A' 2^{-m(n-d)} \norm{\dd\omega}_\infty
\end{equation}
for all $m\ge 1$, where $C_A':=C_A n^{(n-d)/2}$.

Moreover, the universal probe form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:probe-form}
\omega_0
:=
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (-1)^{i-1}x_i\,
\dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{\dd x_i}\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n
\end{equation}
satisfies
\[
\dd\omega_0 = \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n
\]
and therefore
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:volume-probe-identity}
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0 = \Vol(U_m(A)).
\end{equation}
If $N_m(A)$ denotes the number of side-$2^{-m}$ dyadic cubes intersecting $A$,
then
\[
\Vol(U_m(A)) = N_m(A)\,2^{-mn},
\]
and hence
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:minkowski-readout}
\overline{\dim}_{\mathrm M}(A)
=
n + \limsup_{m\to\infty}
\frac{\log\!\left(\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0\right)}{m\log 2}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The set $U_m(A)$ is a finite union of dyadic cubes, so Stokes' theorem on
polyhedral domains gives
\[
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega = \int_{U_m(A)} \dd\omega.
\]
Therefore
\[
\abs{\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega}
\le
\norm{\dd\omega}_\infty \Vol(U_m(A)).
\]
Every dyadic cube of side $2^{-m}$ has diameter $\sqrt n\,2^{-m}$, so any cube
intersecting $A$ is contained in $A_{\sqrt n\,2^{-m}}$.  Hence
\[
\Vol(U_m(A))
\le
\Vol(A_{\sqrt n\,2^{-m}})
\le
C_A(\sqrt n\,2^{-m})^{n-d}
=
C_A' 2^{-m(n-d)}.
\]
Combining the last two estimates proves \eqref{eq:dyadic-stokes-bound}.

For the probe form \eqref{eq:probe-form}, a direct calculation gives
$\dd\omega_0 = \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n$.  Applying Stokes again,
\[
\int_{\partial U_m(A)} \omega_0
=
\int_{U_m(A)} \dd x_1\wedge\cdots\wedge \dd x_n
=
\Vol(U_m(A)),
\]
which proves \eqref{eq:volume-probe-identity}.

Finally, $\Vol(U_m(A)) = N_m(A)2^{-mn}$ by definition.  The upper Minkowski
dimension can equivalently be computed from dyadic cube counts,
\[
\overline{\dim}_{\mathrm M}(A)
=
\limsup_{m\to\infty}\frac{\log N_m(A)}{m\log 2},
\]
see for instance \cite[Ch.~3]{Falconer2014}.  Substituting
$N_m(A)=2^{mn}\Vol(U_m(A))$ yields
\[
\overline{\dim}_{\mathrm M}(A)
=
n+\limsup_{m\to\infty}\frac{\log \Vol(U_m(A))}{m\log 2},
\]
and \eqref{eq:minkowski-readout} follows from
\eqref{eq:volume-probe-identity}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The gain in \eqref{eq:dyadic-stokes-bound} is genuinely codimension-sensitive:
the decay exponent is $n-d$, not merely the surface-growth exponent of the
polyhedral boundary.  The single probe $\omega_0$ therefore turns a family of
boundary integrals into a direct geometric size measurement of the outer
approximation.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}

The paper establishes one compact chain:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{cubical homotopy operator}
&\Longrightarrow \text{sharp bounded inverse on decomposable forms} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{boundary-rigid minimizer} \\
&\Longrightarrow \text{dyadic boundary readout}.
\end{aligned}
\]
The continuous and discrete theorems use the same constant and the same
underlying mechanism.  That is the real reason the cochain section belongs in
the paper: it is not an application layer added afterwards, but a faithful
transfer of the same inverse estimate within the Whitney-decomposable class.

This is also the correct stopping point.  We do not discuss prefix
filtrations, Bayes classifiers, scan-error rates, coding semantics, or
complexity barriers.  Those topics belong to separate manuscripts with
different journal boundaries.  The contribution here is the geometric-analysis
core that can stand on its own: one optimal inverse on the decomposable class,
one rigid boundary theory, and one dyadic flux readout principle.
