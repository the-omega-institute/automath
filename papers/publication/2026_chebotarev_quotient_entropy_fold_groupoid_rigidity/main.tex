\begin{abstract}
We study two coarse-graining procedures arising from standard inputs in Chebotarev counting and in finite-dimensional fold groupoids. On the Chebotarev side, starting from primitive counts on a finite abelian label group and a quotient map, we show that pushforward to the quotient retains exactly the Fourier modes that are trivial on the kernel. Combined with the classical entropy chain rule and Parseval on finite abelian groups, this gives an exact decomposition of the relative entropy and of the \(L^2\)-defect into visible and hidden character contributions. Under a spectrally isolated dominant nontrivial channel, the same channel controls the secondary term in subset counts, the first pole of the nontrivial Artin factor, and the rate at which the induced label measures converge to the uniform law; witness sets show that square-root decay is impossible once the nontrivial spectral radius exceeds \(\lambda_1(u)^{1/2}\). On the fold-groupoid side, using only the finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure and the stated fingerprint assumptions, we prove that scan-reversal compatible automorphisms preserve the \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\)-fingerprint, recover the associated sector decomposition from canonical central involutions, and characterize exactly what is detected by the central observables \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\), \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{scan}}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rev}}\). In the enumerated window-\(6\) model, these observables leave a center of codimension at least \(9\) unresolved. The paper isolates these consequences from the model-dependent spectral expansions and enumerations, which are recorded as standing hypotheses.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
The Chebotarev density theorem and its dynamical analogues provide a standard source of equidistribution problems with finite labels \cite{Tschebotareff1926,NeukirchSchmidtWingberg2008,IwaniecKowalski2004,ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,PollicottSharp2007}. In many situations the full label is not observed directly; instead one sees only its image in a quotient group. The first part of the paper asks for an exact description of the information lost under this passage to a coarser label space.

The second part concerns an apparently different question in finite-dimensional operator algebra. Fold groupoids give rise to semisimple groupoid algebras equipped with a canonical scan-reversal fingerprint in \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\). Here the natural observables are central ones: traces, involutions, and signed moments. We ask how much of the block decomposition these canonical central observables detect and where distinct blocks necessarily become indistinguishable.

The common theme is loss of information under coarse graining. In the Chebotarev setting the relevant bookkeeping is Fourier-theoretic; in the fold-groupoid setting it is encoded by the center of a finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra. The novelty of the paper lies in making the resulting exact coarse-graining identities explicit and in showing that several apparently different secondary quantities are governed by the same surviving spectral or central data. We do not reprove the model-dependent transfer-operator expansions or the fold enumerations; those inputs are stated as standing hypotheses and all subsequent arguments are formal once they are granted.

Let \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) be a surjective homomorphism of finite abelian groups. Primitive Chebotarev counts on \(G_2\) push forward to coarse counts on \(G_1\). The first basic observation is that the quotient retains exactly those characters of \(G_2\) that are trivial on \(\ker \pi\). Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-quotient-relative-entropy-chain} specializes the classical entropy chain rule to this pushforward situation, while Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-visible-hidden-character-energy} combines Parseval with the quotient map to identify the \(L^2\)-loss exactly with the energy carried by the non-descending characters. What is specific to the present setting is therefore not the underlying information-theoretic or harmonic-analytic identities themselves, but the exact arithmetic interpretation of the discarded terms.

The Chebotarev part also uses the familiar channel expansion coming from the twisted transfer matrices \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\). Once one assumes that a nontrivial spectral value is isolated at the edge of the spectrum, several secondary phenomena are controlled by the same datum. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-second-main-term-witness} isolates the corresponding second term in subset counts, Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-artin-dominant-channel-pole-order} identifies the pole order forced by that channel in the nontrivial Artin factor, and Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-statistical-convergence} transfers the same input to quantitative convergence of the induced label measures, together with witness sets showing precisely when square-root decay cannot occur.

For fold groupoids, the starting point is the finite-dimensional algebra
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC),
\]
whose simple blocks are indexed by stable fold types. Each block carries canonical scan and reversal symmetries, and their signs define a fingerprint \(\chi(x)\in\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\). Theorem \ref{thm:fold-groupoid-chi-rigidity} shows that any \(\ast\)-automorphism compatible with these symmetries must preserve \(\chi\). Section \ref{sec:central-idempotents} then reconstructs the sector decomposition from canonical central involutions, identifies the associated joint spectral measure, and records two consequences that are decisive for the center: the canonical signed moments determine the sectorwise fiber polynomial by Newton identities, and the three central observables \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\), \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{scan}}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rev}}\) generate the whole center exactly when the fiber multiplicity separates points inside each \(\chi\)-sector.

The explicit window-\(6\) calculation is kept separate. Using the enumerated fiber histogram from Assumption \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}, Appendix \ref{app:window6} turns the general moment identities into concrete reconstruction formulas and shows that the three basic central observables cannot distinguish all \(21\) stable types.

The paper is therefore conditional on a short list of standing inputs. On the Chebotarev side we use only the spectral expansion and Artin factorization recorded in Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}; compare \cite{ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,Bass1992,StarkTerras1996,StarkTerras2000}. On the fold-groupoid side we assume the maximal-fiber fingerprint and the window-\(6\) multiplicity data from Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}. Once these hypotheses are fixed, the remaining arguments use only classical information theory, Fourier analysis on finite abelian groups, spectral projections in finite dimension, and the standard structure theory of finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebras \cite{CoverThomas2006,CsiszarKorner2011,Diaconis1988,Terras1999,Renault1980,Paterson1999,Watatani1990,KadisonRingrose1983,BrownOzawa2008,Jones1983}.

Section \ref{sec:preliminaries} sets notation and states the standing hypotheses. Sections \ref{sec:entropy-chain} and \ref{sec:artin-pole} treat the Chebotarev results. Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents} are devoted to the fold-groupoid side, and Appendix \ref{app:window6} contains the explicit window-\(6\) specialization.

\section{Notation and standing hypotheses}\label{sec:preliminaries}
\subsection{Finite groups, characters, and divergences}
If \(G\) is a finite group, we write \(\widehat G\) for a complete set of irreducible complex representations of \(G\); when \(G\) is abelian, this is identified with its Pontryagin dual of characters. We write \(\mathrm{unif}_G\) for the uniform probability measure on \(G\). For a surjective homomorphism \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) with kernel \(N\), we set
\[
N^\perp:=\{\chi\in\widehat G_2:\chi|_N\equiv 1\}\cong \widehat G_1.
\]
When \(G\) is abelian and \(f:G\to \CC\), its Fourier transform is
\[
\widehat f(\chi):=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G} f(c)\,\overline{\chi(c)}.
\]
With respect to the normalized inner product
\[
\langle f,g\rangle_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}:=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G}f(c)\,\overline{g(c)},
\]
Parseval's identity takes the form
\[
\|f\|_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}^2=\sum_{\chi\in \widehat G}\abs{\widehat f(\chi)}^2.
\]
We use these facts in the standard normalization of finite-group harmonic analysis; see \cite{Diaconis1988,Terras1999}.

For probability measures \(\mu,\nu\) on a finite set \(X\) with \(\mu\ll\nu\), we use
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\mu(x)\log\frac{\mu(x)}{\nu(x)},
\]
\[
\chi^2(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\frac{(\mu(x)-\nu(x))^2}{\nu(x)},
\qquad
\|\mu-\nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}:=\frac12\sum_{x\in X}\abs{\mu(x)-\nu(x)}.
\]
When \(X\) is partitioned into fibers of a map \(\pi:X\to Y\), the relative-entropy chain rule reads
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu)=D(\pi_\ast\mu\Vert \pi_\ast\nu)+\sum_{y\in Y}(\pi_\ast\mu)(y)\,D(\mu(\cdot\mid y)\Vert \nu(\cdot\mid y)),
\]
whenever the conditional distributions are defined.
This form of the chain rule is classical; see \cite[Section 2.5]{CoverThomas2006} or \cite[Chapter 2]{CsiszarKorner2011}.

\subsection{Standing spectral input}
Fix a finite group \(G\), a temperature parameter \(u\in(0,1]\), and primitive Chebotarev counts \(B_{n,c}(u)\) indexed by \(c\in G\). We write
\[
B_n(\ind;u):=\sum_{c\in G}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
and, when \(G\) is abelian,
\[
B_n(\chi;u):=\sum_{c\in G}\chi(c)\,B_{n,c}(u)
\quad(\chi\in \widehat G),
\]
and for each irreducible channel \(\rho\) we let \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\) denote the corresponding transfer matrix. The trivial-channel Perron root is
\[
\lambda_1(u):=\spr(M_{K,\ind}(u)),
\]
the nontrivial spectral radius is
\[
\eta(u):=\max_{\rho\neq \ind}\spr(M_{K,\rho}(u)),
\]
and we set
\[
\Lambda(u):=\max\{|\lambda_2(u)|,\eta(u)\},
\]
where \(\lambda_2(u)\) denotes the second-largest eigenvalue modulus in the trivial channel.

The Chebotarev part of the paper uses only the following standard consequences of the transfer formalism.

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp}
Assume \(\Lambda(u)<\lambda_1(u)\). Then, uniformly in \(c\in G\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
B_{n,c}(u)
&=
\frac{1}{|G|}\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right),\\
B_n(\ind;u)
&=
\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
Moreover, for every conjugacy-invariant subset \(A\subseteq G\), if we set
\[
B_{n,A}(u):=\sum_{c\in A}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
then there exist linear functionals \(L_{A,\rho}\) on the corresponding channel spaces such that
\[
B_{n,A}(u)
=
\mathrm{unif}_G(A)\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{A,\rho}\!\bigl(M_{K,\rho}(u)^n\bigr).
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}
For a finite group \(G\), define
\[
L_{K,\rho}(z,u):=\det(I-zM_{K,\rho}(u))^{-1}.
\]
Then the associated zeta function satisfies
\[
\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)=\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho},
\]
and hence
\[
\frac{\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)}{L_{K,\ind}(z,u)}
=
\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho}.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}\label{def:kernel-rh}
We write
\[
\textsf{GRH}_{K,G}(u):\qquad \Lambda(u)\le \lambda_1(u)^{1/2}
\]
for the square-root spectral gap condition that plays the role of a generalized Riemann-hypothesis bound in the present transfer-matrix setting.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization} are the only Chebotarev inputs used below. They collect the model-dependent analytic content into two statements that, in concrete transfer settings, are verified by the usual spectral and determinant-factorization arguments; compare \cite{ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,Bass1992,StarkTerras1996,StarkTerras2000}. Every later argument depends only on the asymptotic expansion, the channel decomposition of subset counts, and the Artin factorization recorded here. In the counting situations that motivate the notation, one typically has \(\lambda_1(u)>1\), so the condition \(\textsf{GRH}_{K,G}(u)\) yields a genuine square-root decay statement.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Fold groupoids and central observables}
We next record the finite-dimensional groupoid-algebraic framework used in Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:fold-equivalence-groupoid-algebra}
For each resolution \(m\), let \(\Fold_m:\Omega_m\to X_m\) be the fold map from microstates to stable macrostates, where \(\Omega_m\) is a binary microstate set of cardinality \(2^m\). The associated equivalence-relation groupoid is
\[
\mathcal R_m:=\Omega_m\times_{X_m}\Omega_m
=
\{(\omega,\omega')\in \Omega_m\times\Omega_m:\Fold_m(\omega)=\Fold_m(\omega')\},
\]
and we write
\[
A_{m,1}:=\CC[\mathcal R_m]
\]
for its complex groupoid algebra. The fiber multiplicity at \(x\in X_m\) is
\[
d_m(x):=\abs{\Fold_m^{-1}(x)},
\qquad
D_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}d_m(x),
\qquad
\mathcal M_m:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=D_m\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-wedderburn}
Fix orderings of the fibers \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Then there is a \(\ast\)-isomorphism
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC).
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(\widetilde P_x\in Z(A_{m,1})\) denote the unit of the simple ideal \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\subseteq A_{m,1}\); under any such block identification it is the minimal central idempotent projecting onto the \(x\)-block.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since
\[
\mathcal R_m=\bigsqcup_{x\in X_m}\mathcal R_m(x),
\]
the groupoid algebra decomposes canonically as
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)].
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), the subgroupoid
\[
\mathcal R_m(x):=\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\times \Fold_m^{-1}(x)
\]
is a pair groupoid on \(d_m(x)\) objects. After choosing an ordering
\[
\Fold_m^{-1}(x)=\{\omega_1,\dots,\omega_{d_m(x)}\},
\]
the basis element \(\delta_{(\omega_i,\omega_j)}\in \CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\) is sent to the matrix unit \(E_{ij}\in M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\). Under this identification, convolution on \(\mathcal R_m(x)\) becomes matrix multiplication and the involution becomes conjugate transpose. Since
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)],
\]
this gives the stated block decomposition. The element \(\widetilde P_x\) is simply the identity of the direct summand \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-z2x2-sector-by-orientation}
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(c_x\) and \(\iota_x\) be the scan and reversal permutations of the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Define the fingerprint
\[
\chi(x):=\bigl(\operatorname{sgn}(c_x),\operatorname{sgn}(\iota_x)\bigr)\in\{\pm1\}^2.
\]
For \((\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2\), set
\[
X_m^{\alpha,\beta}:=\{x\in X_m:\chi(x)=(\alpha,\beta)\},
\qquad
\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}:=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m^{\alpha,\beta}}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)].
\]
Then
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{(\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2}\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}
\]
is the sector decomposition determined by the scan-reversal fingerprint.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The sets \(X_m^{\alpha,\beta}\) form a partition of \(X_m\) by definition of \(\chi\). Since
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)],
\]
regrouping the simple ideals according to the value of \(\chi(x)\) gives the asserted sector decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:fold-groupoid-aut-semidirect-pi0}
Let
\[
\Pi_0:=\{\sigma\in \mathrm{Sym}(X_m):d_m(\sigma(x))=d_m(x)\ \forall x\in X_m\}.
\]
After choosing identifications \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\cong M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\), the \(\ast\)-automorphism group of \(A_{m,1}\) is isomorphic to
\[
\Aut^\ast(A_{m,1})
\cong
\Bigl(\prod_{x\in X_m}\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\Bigr)\rtimes \Pi_0.
\]
Equivalently, a \(\ast\)-automorphism acts by blockwise projective-unitary conjugation together with a permutation of blocks of equal size.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each \(d\ge 1\), let
\[
X_{m,d}:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=d\}.
\]
By finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure theory, every \(\ast\)-automorphism of
\[
\bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)
\]
permutes the simple summands and preserves their matrix size \cite{KadisonRingrose1983,BrownOzawa2008}. Thus it induces a permutation of \(X_{m,d}\) for each \(d\), equivalently an element of \(\Pi_0\). Once the block permutation is fixed, the restriction to each simple summand \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\) is a \(\ast\)-automorphism of a full matrix algebra, hence inner and determined up to scalars by an element of \(\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\). Collecting these blockwise projective-unitary factors yields the claimed semidirect-product decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic}
For every resolution \(m\), there exists a fingerprint
\[
\chi_m=(\alpha_m,\beta_m)\in\{\pm1\}^2
\]
such that
\[
\chi(x)=\chi_m
\qquad\text{for all }x\in \mathcal M_m.
\]
In the odd-\(m\) stable periodic regime, one furthermore has \(\beta_m=+1\).
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-condexp-index-maxfiber}
Let \(E_m\) be the conditional expectation induced by \(\Fold_m\), that is, averaging uniformly on the fibers of \(\Fold_m\). For a probability distribution \(\rho\) on \(\Omega_m\), write \(\rho^\flat:=\rho\circ E_m\); equivalently, \(\rho^\flat\) has the same pushforward to \(X_m\) as \(\rho\) and is uniform on each fiber of \(\Fold_m\). Then
\[
\sup_{\rho}D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m,
\]
where \(\rho\) ranges over probability distributions on \(\Omega_m\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\rho_X:=(\Fold_m)_\ast\rho\), and for each \(x\in X_m\) let \(u_x\) denote the uniform distribution on the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Since \(\rho^\flat\) has the same macroscopic marginal as \(\rho\) and fiberwise conditionals \(u_x\), the relative-entropy chain rule gives
\[
D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)
=
\sum_{x\in X_m}\rho_X(x)\,
D\!\bigl(\rho(\cdot\mid x)\Vert u_x\bigr).
\]
For each \(x\), the divergence to the uniform measure on a fiber of size \(d_m(x)\) is at most \(\log d_m(x)\le \log D_m\). Hence \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)\le \log D_m\) for every \(\rho\). Conversely, if \(x_\star\in X_m\) satisfies \(d_m(x_\star)=D_m\), take \(\rho\) to be a Dirac mass on a point of \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x_\star)\). Then \(\rho^\flat\) is the uniform distribution on that same fiber, so \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m\).
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}
For \(m=6\), direct enumeration yields
\[
\begin{aligned}
d_6(x)&\in\{2,3,4\},\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=2\}&=8,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=3\}&=4,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=4\}&=9.
\end{aligned}
\]
and therefore
\[
\abs{X_6}=21.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist} are the only fold-model inputs used below. Everything that follows depends only on the constancy of the maximal-fiber fingerprint and the stated window-\(6\) multiplicity data.
\end{remark}
