\begin{abstract}
We consider two instances of coarse graining. For primitive Chebotarev counts on a finite abelian label group and a quotient map, we identify the information lost when one passes to the quotient. The relative-entropy identity is the chain rule written in this setting, while the accompanying \(L^2\)-identity shows that the hidden part is exactly the Fourier energy carried by characters that do not descend. Under a spectrally isolated dominant nontrivial channel, the same datum governs the secondary term in subset counts, the first pole of the nontrivial Artin factor, and the rate of convergence of the induced label measures to uniformity; in particular, witness sets rule out square-root decay once the nontrivial spectral radius exceeds \(\lambda_1(u)^{1/2}\). We then turn to finite-dimensional fold groupoids. Using only the semisimple \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure and the stated fingerprint hypotheses, we show that scan-reversal compatible automorphisms preserve the \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\)-fingerprint, construct the associated central sector projections, and determine what is seen by the canonical observables \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\), \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{scan}}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rev}}\). In the enumerated window-\(6\) model these observables leave a central quotient of dimension at least \(9\).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
The purpose of this paper is to isolate two consequences of passing from a fine description to a coarser one. The first arises in Chebotarev-type counting problems with finite labels, where one starts from primitive counts on a finite group and observes only their image in a quotient. The second arises in finite-dimensional fold groupoids, where the natural observables are central and therefore retain only part of the block structure of the associated groupoid algebra. The settings are rather different, but in both cases the basic question is the same: which part of the original data survives the coarse graining, and how can the discarded part be described exactly?

\subsection*{Chebotarev quotients}
The Chebotarev density theorem and its dynamical analogues provide a familiar source of equidistribution problems with finite labels \cite{Tschebotareff1926,NeukirchSchmidtWingberg2008,IwaniecKowalski2004,ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,PollicottSharp2007}. Let \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) be a surjective homomorphism of finite abelian groups. Primitive counts on \(G_2\) push forward to coarse counts on \(G_1\), and the first issue is to measure the loss of information in this passage. The answer is particularly transparent on the Fourier side: the quotient retains precisely those characters of \(G_2\) that are trivial on \(\ker \pi\). Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-quotient-relative-entropy-chain} writes the relative entropy of the fine distribution as the entropy of its pushforward plus the average entropy along the fibers, while Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-visible-hidden-character-energy} identifies the corresponding \(L^2\)-defect with the Fourier energy carried by the non-descending characters. The ingredients are classical, but in the present setting the discarded term has a direct arithmetic interpretation because the coarse data are obtained by exact pushforward from primitive Chebotarev counts.

The same point of view becomes more rigid once one inserts the channel expansion coming from the twisted transfer matrices \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\). Under a spectral isolation hypothesis on a dominant nontrivial channel, a single spectral value controls several secondary quantities at once. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-second-main-term-witness} extracts the second term in subset counts, Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-artin-dominant-channel-pole-order} identifies the corresponding pole of the nontrivial Artin factor, and Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-statistical-convergence} translates the same input into quantitative convergence of the induced label measures to the uniform law. The witness part of Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-second-main-term-witness} shows, in particular, that square-root decay cannot persist once the nontrivial spectral radius exceeds \(\lambda_1(u)^{1/2}\).

\subsection*{Fold groupoids}
The second part of the paper concerns the finite-dimensional algebra
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC),
\]
attached to a fold groupoid. Its simple blocks are indexed by stable fold types, and each block carries canonical scan and reversal symmetries. Their signs define a fingerprint \(\chi(x)\in\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\). The first issue is rigidity. Theorem \ref{thm:fold-groupoid-chi-rigidity} shows that any \(\ast\)-automorphism compatible with the canonical scan and reversal symmetries must preserve \(\chi\). We then turn to the center. Theorem \ref{thm:fold-groupoid-z2x2-central-idempotents} constructs the central sector projections from the canonical involutions, identifies the corresponding joint spectral measure, and records the maximal-fiber consequences needed later. Proposition \ref{prop:fold-chi-layered-fiber-spectrum-certified-by-signed-powersums} and Proposition \ref{prop:fold-center-resolution-criterion-indw-eps} show that the signed moments determine the sectorwise fiber polynomial and that the three observables \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\), \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{scan}}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rev}}\) generate the whole center precisely when the fiber multiplicity separates points inside each \(\chi\)-sector.

The only genuinely model-specific calculation is the window-\(6\) specialization, and we therefore keep it in Appendix \ref{app:window6}. There the multiplicity histogram from Assumption \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist} turns the general moment identities into explicit reconstruction formulas and shows that the three basic observables leave at least a \(9\)-dimensional quotient of the center unresolved.

\subsection*{Standing input and organization}
We do not revisit the analytic or enumerative arguments that produce the spectral expansions and multiplicity data. On the Chebotarev side the only external input is the expansion and Artin factorization recorded in Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}; compare \cite{ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,Bass1992,StarkTerras1996,StarkTerras2000}. On the fold-groupoid side we use only the maximal-fiber fingerprint and the window-\(6\) multiplicity data in Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}. Once these hypotheses are in place, the remaining arguments are formal. They use only relative entropy on finite sets, Fourier analysis on finite abelian groups, spectral projections in finite dimension, and the structure theory of finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebras \cite{CoverThomas2006,CsiszarKorner2011,Diaconis1988,Terras1999,Renault1980,Paterson1999,Watatani1990,KadisonRingrose1983,BrownOzawa2008,Jones1983}.

The paper therefore makes a deliberately circumscribed claim. It does not propose new transfer-operator asymptotics or new fold-enumeration data. Rather, it identifies the exact formal consequences of those inputs for coarse graining: on the Chebotarev side, the quotient defect is described simultaneously by fiber entropy, hidden Fourier energy, and the dominant nontrivial spectral channel; on the fold-groupoid side, the canonical scan and reversal involutions produce intrinsic sector projections and a sharp criterion for what the basic central observables fail to resolve. Stating these consequences cleanly, and separating them from the external analytic and enumerative input, is part of the mathematical content.

Section \ref{sec:preliminaries} collects notation and standing hypotheses. Sections \ref{sec:entropy-chain} and \ref{sec:artin-pole} contain the Chebotarev results. Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents} treat the fold-groupoid side, and Appendix \ref{app:window6} contains the explicit window-\(6\) specialization.

\section{Preliminaries and standing hypotheses}\label{sec:preliminaries}
We collect the notation and the model-dependent inputs used throughout the paper.
\subsection{Finite groups, characters, and divergences}
If \(G\) is a finite group, we write \(\widehat G\) for a complete set of irreducible complex representations of \(G\); when \(G\) is abelian, this is identified with its Pontryagin dual of characters. We write \(\mathrm{unif}_G\) for the uniform probability measure on \(G\). For a surjective homomorphism \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) with kernel \(N\), we set
\[
N^\perp:=\{\chi\in\widehat G_2:\chi|_N\equiv 1\}\cong \widehat G_1.
\]
When \(G\) is abelian and \(f:G\to \CC\), its Fourier transform is
\[
\widehat f(\chi):=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G} f(c)\,\overline{\chi(c)}.
\]
With respect to the normalized inner product
\[
\langle f,g\rangle_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}:=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G}f(c)\,\overline{g(c)},
\]
Parseval's identity takes the form
\[
\|f\|_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}^2=\sum_{\chi\in \widehat G}\abs{\widehat f(\chi)}^2.
\]
We use these facts in the standard normalization of finite-group harmonic analysis; see \cite{Diaconis1988,Terras1999}.

For probability measures \(\mu,\nu\) on a finite set \(X\) with \(\mu\ll\nu\), we use
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\mu(x)\log\frac{\mu(x)}{\nu(x)},
\]
\[
\chi^2(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\frac{(\mu(x)-\nu(x))^2}{\nu(x)},
\qquad
\|\mu-\nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}:=\frac12\sum_{x\in X}\abs{\mu(x)-\nu(x)}.
\]
When \(X\) is partitioned into fibers of a map \(\pi:X\to Y\), the relative-entropy chain rule reads
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu)=D(\pi_\ast\mu\Vert \pi_\ast\nu)+\sum_{y\in Y}(\pi_\ast\mu)(y)\,D(\mu(\cdot\mid y)\Vert \nu(\cdot\mid y)),
\]
whenever the conditional distributions are defined.
This form of the chain rule is classical; see \cite[Section 2.5]{CoverThomas2006} or \cite[Chapter 2]{CsiszarKorner2011}.

\subsection{Standing spectral input}
Fix a finite group \(G\), a temperature parameter \(u\in(0,1]\), and primitive Chebotarev counts \(B_{n,c}(u)\) indexed by \(c\in G\). We write
\[
B_n(\ind;u):=\sum_{c\in G}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
and, when \(G\) is abelian,
\[
B_n(\chi;u):=\sum_{c\in G}\chi(c)\,B_{n,c}(u)
\quad(\chi\in \widehat G),
\]
and for each irreducible channel \(\rho\) we let \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\) denote the corresponding transfer matrix. The trivial-channel Perron root is
\[
\lambda_1(u):=\spr(M_{K,\ind}(u)),
\]
the nontrivial spectral radius is
\[
\eta(u):=\max_{\rho\neq \ind}\spr(M_{K,\rho}(u)),
\]
and we set
\[
\Lambda(u):=\max\{|\lambda_2(u)|,\eta(u)\},
\]
where \(\lambda_2(u)\) denotes the second-largest eigenvalue modulus in the trivial channel.

The Chebotarev part of the paper uses only the following standard consequences of the transfer formalism.

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp}
Assume \(\Lambda(u)<\lambda_1(u)\). Then, uniformly in \(c\in G\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
B_{n,c}(u)
&=
\frac{1}{|G|}\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right),\\
B_n(\ind;u)
&=
\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
Moreover, for every conjugacy-invariant subset \(A\subseteq G\), if we set
\[
B_{n,A}(u):=\sum_{c\in A}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
then there exist linear functionals \(L_{A,\rho}\) on the corresponding channel spaces such that
\[
B_{n,A}(u)
=
\mathrm{unif}_G(A)\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{A,\rho}\!\bigl(M_{K,\rho}(u)^n\bigr).
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}
For a finite group \(G\), define
\[
L_{K,\rho}(z,u):=\det(I-zM_{K,\rho}(u))^{-1}.
\]
Then the associated zeta function satisfies
\[
\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)=\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho},
\]
and hence
\[
\frac{\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)}{L_{K,\ind}(z,u)}
=
\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho}.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}\label{def:kernel-rh}
We write
\[
\textsf{GRH}_{K,G}(u):\qquad \Lambda(u)\le \lambda_1(u)^{1/2}
\]
for the square-root spectral gap condition that plays the role of a generalized Riemann-hypothesis bound in the present transfer-matrix setting.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization} are the only Chebotarev inputs used below. In concrete transfer settings they are verified by the standard spectral and determinant arguments; compare \cite{ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,Bass1992,StarkTerras1996,StarkTerras2000}. Every later argument depends only on the asymptotic expansion, the channel decomposition of subset counts, and the Artin factorization recorded here. In the counting situations that motivate the notation one typically has \(\lambda_1(u)>1\), so the condition \(\textsf{GRH}_{K,G}(u)\) gives a genuine square-root decay statement.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Fold groupoids and central observables}
We next record the finite-dimensional groupoid-algebraic framework used in Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:fold-equivalence-groupoid-algebra}
For each resolution \(m\), let \(\Fold_m:\Omega_m\to X_m\) be the fold map from microstates to stable macrostates, where \(\Omega_m\) is a binary microstate set of cardinality \(2^m\). The associated equivalence-relation groupoid is
\[
\mathcal R_m:=\Omega_m\times_{X_m}\Omega_m
=
\{(\omega,\omega')\in \Omega_m\times\Omega_m:\Fold_m(\omega)=\Fold_m(\omega')\},
\]
and we write
\[
A_{m,1}:=\CC[\mathcal R_m]
\]
for its complex groupoid algebra. The fiber multiplicity at \(x\in X_m\) is
\[
d_m(x):=\abs{\Fold_m^{-1}(x)},
\qquad
D_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}d_m(x),
\qquad
\mathcal M_m:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=D_m\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-wedderburn}
Fix orderings of the fibers \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Then there is a \(\ast\)-isomorphism
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC).
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(\widetilde P_x\in Z(A_{m,1})\) denote the unit of the simple ideal \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\subseteq A_{m,1}\); under any such block identification it is the minimal central idempotent projecting onto the \(x\)-block.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since
\[
\mathcal R_m=\bigsqcup_{x\in X_m}\mathcal R_m(x),
\]
the groupoid algebra decomposes canonically as
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)].
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), the subgroupoid
\[
\mathcal R_m(x):=\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\times \Fold_m^{-1}(x)
\]
is a pair groupoid on \(d_m(x)\) objects. After choosing an ordering
\[
\Fold_m^{-1}(x)=\{\omega_1,\dots,\omega_{d_m(x)}\},
\]
the basis element \(\delta_{(\omega_i,\omega_j)}\in \CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\) is sent to the matrix unit \(E_{ij}\in M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\). Under this identification, convolution on \(\mathcal R_m(x)\) becomes matrix multiplication and the involution becomes conjugate transpose. Since
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)],
\]
this yields the stated block decomposition. The element \(\widetilde P_x\) is simply the identity of the direct summand \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-z2x2-sector-by-orientation}
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(c_x\) and \(\iota_x\) be the scan and reversal permutations of the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Define the fingerprint
\[
\chi(x):=\bigl(\operatorname{sgn}(c_x),\operatorname{sgn}(\iota_x)\bigr)\in\{\pm1\}^2.
\]
For \((\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2\), set
\[
X_m^{\alpha,\beta}:=\{x\in X_m:\chi(x)=(\alpha,\beta)\},
\qquad
\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}:=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m^{\alpha,\beta}}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)].
\]
Then
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{(\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2}\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}
\]
is the sector decomposition determined by the scan-reversal fingerprint.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The sets \(X_m^{\alpha,\beta}\) form a partition of \(X_m\) by definition of \(\chi\). Since
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)],
\]
regrouping the simple ideals according to the value of \(\chi(x)\) gives the asserted sector decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The fingerprint \(\chi(x)\) is intrinsic. Indeed, changing the ordering of the finite fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\) conjugates the associated scan and reversal permutations inside the symmetric group of that fiber, and the sign of a permutation is invariant under conjugation.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:fold-groupoid-aut-semidirect-pi0}
Let
\[
\Pi_0:=\{\sigma\in \mathrm{Sym}(X_m):d_m(\sigma(x))=d_m(x)\ \forall x\in X_m\}.
\]
After choosing identifications \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\cong M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\), the \(\ast\)-automorphism group of \(A_{m,1}\) is isomorphic to
\[
\Aut^\ast(A_{m,1})
\cong
\Bigl(\prod_{x\in X_m}\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\Bigr)\rtimes \Pi_0.
\]
Equivalently, a \(\ast\)-automorphism acts by blockwise projective-unitary conjugation together with a permutation of blocks of equal size.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each \(d\ge 1\), let
\[
X_{m,d}:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=d\}.
\]
By finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure theory, every \(\ast\)-automorphism of
\[
\bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)
\]
permutes the simple summands and preserves their matrix size \cite{KadisonRingrose1983,BrownOzawa2008}. Thus it induces a permutation of \(X_{m,d}\) for each \(d\), equivalently an element of \(\Pi_0\). Once the block permutation is fixed, the restriction to each simple summand \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\) is a \(\ast\)-automorphism of a full matrix algebra, hence inner and determined up to scalars by an element of \(\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\). Collecting these blockwise projective-unitary factors gives the claimed semidirect-product decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic}
For every resolution \(m\), there exists a fingerprint
\[
\chi_m=(\alpha_m,\beta_m)\in\{\pm1\}^2
\]
such that
\[
\chi(x)=\chi_m
\qquad\text{for all }x\in \mathcal M_m.
\]
In the odd-\(m\) stable periodic regime, one furthermore has \(\beta_m=+1\).
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-condexp-index-maxfiber}
Let \(E_m\) be the conditional expectation induced by \(\Fold_m\), that is, averaging uniformly on the fibers of \(\Fold_m\). For a probability distribution \(\rho\) on \(\Omega_m\), write \(\rho^\flat:=\rho\circ E_m\); equivalently, \(\rho^\flat\) has the same pushforward to \(X_m\) as \(\rho\) and is uniform on each fiber of \(\Fold_m\). Then
\[
\sup_{\rho}D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m,
\]
where \(\rho\) ranges over probability distributions on \(\Omega_m\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\rho_X:=(\Fold_m)_\ast\rho\), and for each \(x\in X_m\) let \(u_x\) denote the uniform distribution on the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Since \(\rho^\flat\) has the same macroscopic marginal as \(\rho\) and fiberwise conditionals \(u_x\), the relative-entropy chain rule gives
\[
D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)
=
\sum_{x\in X_m}\rho_X(x)\,
D\!\bigl(\rho(\cdot\mid x)\Vert u_x\bigr).
\]
For each \(x\), the divergence to the uniform measure on a fiber of size \(d_m(x)\) is at most \(\log d_m(x)\le \log D_m\). Hence \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)\le \log D_m\) for every \(\rho\). Conversely, if \(x_\star\in X_m\) satisfies \(d_m(x_\star)=D_m\), take \(\rho\) to be a Dirac mass at a point of \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x_\star)\). Then \(\rho^\flat\) is the uniform distribution on that fiber, and \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m\).
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}
For \(m=6\), direct enumeration yields
\[
\begin{aligned}
d_6(x)&\in\{2,3,4\},\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=2\}&=8,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=3\}&=4,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=4\}&=9.
\end{aligned}
\]
and therefore
\[
\abs{X_6}=21.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist} are the only fold-model inputs used below. Everything that follows depends only on the constancy of the maximal-fiber fingerprint and on the stated window-\(6\) multiplicity data.
\end{remark}
