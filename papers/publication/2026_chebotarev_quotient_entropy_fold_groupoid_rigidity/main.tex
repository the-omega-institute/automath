\begin{abstract}
We study two coarse-observation problems, one arithmetic and one operator-algebraic, both governed by the question of which invariants survive after passage to a quotient or to a restricted commutative algebra of observables. On the arithmetic side we assume an abstract Chebotarev spectral expansion for primitive counts together with the associated Artin factorization. For quotients of finite abelian groups we prove an exact chain rule for relative entropy with respect to the uniform distribution and a corresponding orthogonal decomposition of the \(L^2\)-defect into quotient-visible and quotient-invisible Fourier modes. Under an isolated dominant nontrivial spectral pair we obtain the secondary term in subset counts; under a one-channel multiplicity-one hypothesis we determine the pole order of the corresponding Artin factor; and we deduce exponential convergence of primitive label distributions to uniformity, together with an obstruction to square-root total-variation decay when the detected modulus exceeds \(\lambda_1(u)^{1/2}\). On the operator-algebraic side we consider the fold-groupoid algebra attached to a fold map. We prove rigidity of the scan-reversal fingerprint under the compatible \(\ast\)-automorphisms considered here, realize the resulting \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\)-sector decomposition by explicit central idempotents, and show that signed Watatani-index moments recover maximal-fiber data in general and the full sectorwise multiplicity data in window \(6\).
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Chebotarev distribution; relative entropy; Artin factor; fold groupoid; central idempotents; Watatani index

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
Chebotarev-type equidistribution theorems and finite-dimensional rigidity statements are both affected by the same basic phenomenon: in many problems one sees only a coarse shadow of the underlying object. On the arithmetic side the available data may record only the image of a Frobenius class in a quotient, or only a restricted family of subset counts. On the operator-algebraic side one may have access only to a small commutative algebra of observables inside a much larger finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra. The purpose of this paper is to study, in these two settings, what survives this loss of resolution.

\subsection{Arithmetic side}
The arithmetic part of the paper is conditional rather than foundational. We do not reprove a Chebotarev theorem. Instead we assume an abstract spectral expansion for primitive counts together with the corresponding Artin factorization and ask which exact identities can be extracted from this input alone. This viewpoint isolates the formal consequences of the spectral expansion that are relevant for coarse observables. The information-theoretic and harmonic-analytic ingredients are classical \cite{CoverThomas2006,CsiszarKorner2011,DemboZeitouni1998,Diaconis1988,Terras1999}; the point here is that, in the Chebotarev setting, they remain tied to the same spectral data that govern secondary terms and Artin factors.

Our first result concerns quotients of finite abelian groups. If \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) is such a quotient, then Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-quotient-relative-entropy-chain} gives an exact decomposition of the relative entropy of the fine distribution into the entropy of the pushed-forward distribution and the average fiber entropy. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-visible-hidden-character-energy} proves the corresponding orthogonal decomposition of the \(L^2\)-defect: the visible part is carried precisely by the characters factoring through \(\pi\), while the complementary Fourier mass is invisible on the quotient.

The second group of results isolates the effect of a dominant nontrivial spectral channel. Under an isolated conjugate pair at the top of the nontrivial spectrum, Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-second-main-term-witness} extracts the secondary oscillatory term in subset counts. Under the stronger hypothesis that one dominant eigenvalue occurs in a single nontrivial channel with algebraic multiplicity one, Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-artin-dominant-channel-pole-order} identifies the exact pole order of the corresponding nontrivial Artin factor. Finally, Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-statistical-convergence} translates the spectral gap into exponential convergence of primitive label distributions to the uniform law and shows, when \(\lambda_1(u)>1\), that square-root total-variation decay is impossible once a detected dominant modulus exceeds \(\lambda_1(u)^{1/2}\).

\subsection{Fold-groupoid side}
The second part of the paper is likewise concerned with the passage from fine data to coarse observables, now in a finite-dimensional operator-algebraic model attached to a fold map. Starting from the associated equivalence-relation groupoid, we study the extent to which the scan-reversal fingerprint can be recovered from intrinsic central observables. The necessary \(C^\ast\)-algebraic input is standard \cite{Renault1980,Paterson1999,Watatani1990,BrownOzawa2008,Jones1983}; what seems to be specific to the present setting is the explicit internal realization of the fingerprint sectors and the corresponding reconstruction statements.

The first step is rigidity. Theorem \ref{thm:fold-groupoid-chi-rigidity} shows that the compatible \(\ast\)-automorphisms considered here preserve the scan-reversal fingerprint, and Theorem \ref{thm:fold-groupoid-z2x2-central-idempotents} realizes the resulting \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\)-sector decomposition by explicit central idempotents. The Watatani index element then yields a natural family of signed moments. These recover maximal-fiber data in general, and in window \(6\) they determine the full sectorwise multiplicity data; see Theorem \ref{thm:fold-window6-chi-layered-spectrum-reconstruction-by-12-moments}. This leads to a quantitative lower bound for the central quotient not detected by the three basic central observables.

\subsection{Structure of the paper}
Section \ref{sec:entropy-chain} fixes the arithmetic framework and proves the exact quotient identities. Section \ref{sec:artin-pole} treats dominant channels, pole orders, and convergence to uniformity. Section \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} introduces the fold-groupoid algebra and establishes fingerprint rigidity. Section \ref{sec:central-idempotents} studies the central observables and the resulting reconstruction problem, with particular attention to window \(6\).

\section{Quotients, Entropy and Hidden Spectrum}\label{sec:entropy-chain}
We fix the notation used in the arithmetic part of the paper and record the abstract Chebotarev input on which all subsequent arguments rest.

\subsection{Finite Groups, Characters and Divergences}
If \(G\) is a finite group, we write \(\widehat G\) for a complete set of irreducible complex representations of \(G\); when \(G\) is abelian, this is identified with its Pontryagin dual of characters. We write \(\mathrm{unif}_G\) for the uniform probability measure on \(G\). For a surjective homomorphism \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) with kernel \(K\), we set
\[
K^\perp:=\{\chi\in\widehat G_2:\chi|_K\equiv 1\}\cong \widehat G_1.
\]
When \(G\) is abelian and \(f:G\to \CC\), its Fourier transform is
\[
\widehat f(\chi):=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G} f(c)\,\overline{\chi(c)}.
\]
With respect to the normalized inner product
\[
\langle f,g\rangle_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}:=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G}f(c)\,\overline{g(c)},
\]
Parseval's identity takes the form
\[
\|f\|_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}^2=\sum_{\chi\in \widehat G}\abs{\widehat f(\chi)}^2.
\]

For probability measures \(\mu,\nu\) on a finite set \(X\) with \(\mu\ll\nu\), we use
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\mu(x)\log\frac{\mu(x)}{\nu(x)},
\]
\[
\chi^2(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\frac{(\mu(x)-\nu(x))^2}{\nu(x)},
\qquad
\|\mu-\nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}:=\frac12\sum_{x\in X}\abs{\mu(x)-\nu(x)}.
\]
When \(\nu=\mathrm{unif}_G\) on a finite group \(G\) and \(r:=|G|\mu\) denotes the density of \(\mu\) relative to \(\mathrm{unif}_G\), one has
\[
\chi^2(\mu\Vert \mathrm{unif}_G)=\|r-1\|_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}^2.
\]
When \(X\) is partitioned into fibers of a map \(\pi:X\to Y\), the relative-entropy chain rule reads
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu)=D(\pi_\ast\mu\Vert \pi_\ast\nu)+\sum_{y\in Y}(\pi_\ast\mu)(y)\,D(\mu(\cdot\mid y)\Vert \nu(\cdot\mid y)),
\]
whenever the conditional distributions are defined.

\subsection{Abstract Chebotarev Input}
Fix a finite group \(G\), a temperature parameter \(u\in(0,1]\), and nonnegative primitive Chebotarev counts \(B_{n,c}(u)\) indexed by \(c\in G\). We write
\[
B_n(\ind;u):=\sum_{c\in G}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
and, when \(G\) is abelian,
\[
B_n(\chi;u):=\sum_{c\in G}\chi(c)\,B_{n,c}(u)
\quad(\chi\in \widehat G),
\]
and for each irreducible channel \(\rho\) we let \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\) denote the corresponding transfer matrix. The trivial-channel Perron root is
\[
\lambda_1(u):=\spr(M_{K,\ind}(u)),
\]
the nontrivial spectral radius is
\[
\eta(u):=\max_{\rho\neq \ind}\spr(M_{K,\rho}(u)),
\]
and we set
\[
\Lambda(u):=\max\{|\lambda_2(u)|,\eta(u)\},
\]
where \(\lambda_2(u)\) denotes the second-largest eigenvalue modulus in the trivial channel.

The results of Sections \ref{sec:entropy-chain} and \ref{sec:artin-pole} depend only on the following hypotheses.

\begin{assumption}[Chebotarev Spectral Expansion]\label{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp}
Assume \(\Lambda(u)<\lambda_1(u)\). Then, uniformly in \(c\in G\),
\[
B_{n,c}(u)
=
\frac{1}{|G|}\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right),
\qquad
B_n(\ind;u)
=
\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right).
\]
Moreover, for every conjugacy-invariant subset \(A\subseteq G\), if we set
\[
B_{n,A}(u):=\sum_{c\in A}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
then there exist linear functionals \(L_{A,\rho}\) on the corresponding channel spaces such that
\[
B_{n,A}(u)
=
\mathrm{unif}_G(A)\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{A,\rho}\!\bigl(M_{K,\rho}(u)^n\bigr).
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Artin Factorization]\label{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}
For a finite group \(G\), define
\[
L_{K,\rho}(z,u):=\det(I-zM_{K,\rho}(u))^{-1}.
\]
Then the associated zeta function satisfies
\[
\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)=\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho},
\]
and hence
\[
\frac{\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)}{L_{K,\ind}(z,u)}
=
\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho}.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}[Square-Root Spectral Bound]\label{def:kernel-rh}
We write
\[
\textsf{SR}_{K,G}(u):\qquad \Lambda(u)\le \lambda_1(u)^{1/2}
\]
for the square-root spectral bound used below.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
In Sections \ref{sec:entropy-chain} and \ref{sec:artin-pole} we use Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization} only through the statements displayed above. In particular, the arguments there require no additional arithmetic input.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
The condition in Definition \ref{def:kernel-rh} yields a genuine square-root \emph{decay} statement only when \(\lambda_1(u)>1\). This is the regime relevant for Section \ref{sec:artin-pole}. If \(\lambda_1(u)\le 1\), the same inequalities remain formally valid but should not be interpreted as decay estimates.
\end{remark}

\section{Fold-Groupoid Algebras and Fingerprint Rigidity}\label{sec:groupoid-rigidity}
We now turn to the finite-dimensional operator-algebraic model attached to the fold map and identify the automorphisms under which the scan-reversal fingerprint is rigid.

\subsection{The Fold-Groupoid Algebra}
\begin{definition}[Fold Groupoid Algebra]\label{def:fold-equivalence-groupoid-algebra}
For each resolution \(m\), let \(\Fold_m:\Omega_m\to X_m\) be the fold map from microstates to stable macrostates, where \(\Omega_m\) is a binary microstate set of cardinality \(2^m\). The associated equivalence-relation groupoid is
\[
\mathcal R_m:=\Omega_m\times_{X_m}\Omega_m
=
\{(\omega,\omega')\in \Omega_m\times\Omega_m:\Fold_m(\omega)=\Fold_m(\omega')\},
\]
and we write
\[
A_{m,1}:=\CC[\mathcal R_m]
\]
for its complex groupoid algebra. Concretely, \(A_{m,1}\) is the complex vector space with basis \(\{[\omega,\omega']:(\omega,\omega')\in\mathcal R_m\}\), equipped with convolution product and involution
\[
[\omega,\omega']\,[\eta,\eta']
:=
\delta_{\omega',\eta}\,[\omega,\eta'],
\qquad
[\omega,\omega']^\ast:=[\omega',\omega].
\]
The fiber multiplicity at \(x\in X_m\) is
\[
d_m(x):=\abs{\Fold_m^{-1}(x)},
\qquad
D_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}d_m(x),
\qquad
\mathcal M_m:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=D_m\}.
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), we further assume that the fold model provides distinguished permutations
\[
c_x,\iota_x\in \mathrm{Sym}\bigl(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\bigr),
\qquad
\iota_x^2=\mathrm{id},
\]
called respectively the scan permutation and the reversal permutation of the fiber. Only their signatures and their behavior under the compatible automorphisms introduced below will be used in the sequel.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-wedderburn}
There is a canonical \(\ast\)-isomorphism
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\End\!\bigl(\CC[\Fold_m^{-1}(x)]\bigr),
\]
and hence, after choosing bases on the fiber spaces, a \(\ast\)-isomorphism
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC).
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), we denote by \(\widetilde P_x\in Z(A_{m,1})\) the minimal central idempotent projecting onto the block \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(H_x:=\CC[\Fold_m^{-1}(x)]\) with basis \(\{\delta_\omega:\omega\in\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\}\). The subgroupoid
\[
\mathcal R_m(x):=\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\times\Fold_m^{-1}(x)
\]
is the full pair groupoid on the fiber, and the assignment
\[
[\omega,\omega']\longmapsto \bigl(\delta_\eta\mapsto \delta_{\omega',\eta}\,\delta_\omega\bigr)
\]
identifies \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\) with \(\End(H_x)\): on the distinguished basis, the image of \([\omega,\omega']\) is the rank-one operator \(E_{\omega,\omega'}\), and the formulas for multiplication and involution match the matrix-unit relations. Since
\[
\mathcal R_m=\bigsqcup_{x\in X_m}\mathcal R_m(x),
\]
the groupoid algebra is the direct sum of the fiber algebras, which gives the canonical decomposition into endomorphism algebras. Choosing bases on the \(H_x\) then identifies \(\End(H_x)\) with \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\); see also \cite[Chapter 1]{Renault1980} or \cite[Section 4.1]{Paterson1999}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-z2x2-sector-by-orientation}
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(c_x\) and \(\iota_x\) be the scan and reversal permutations of the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Define the fingerprint
\[
\chi(x):=\bigl(\operatorname{sgn}(c_x),\operatorname{sgn}(\iota_x)\bigr)\in\{\pm1\}^2.
\]
For \((\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2\), set
\[
X_m^{\alpha,\beta}:=\{x\in X_m:\chi(x)=(\alpha,\beta)\},
\qquad
\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}:=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m^{\alpha,\beta}}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC).
\]
Then
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{(\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2}\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}
\]
is the sector decomposition determined by the scan-reversal fingerprint.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
Only two features of the distinguished permutations \(c_x\) and \(\iota_x\) will be used below: their signatures and their behavior under the compatible \(\ast\)-automorphisms considered in this section. No additional combinatorics of the fold map enters the proofs.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Compatible Automorphisms}
\begin{theorem}\label{thm:fold-groupoid-aut-semidirect-pi0}
Let
\[
\Pi_0:=\{\sigma\in \mathrm{Sym}(X_m):d_m(\sigma(x))=d_m(x)\ \forall x\in X_m\}.
\]
Then the \(\ast\)-automorphism group of \(A_{m,1}\) is naturally isomorphic to
\[
\Aut^\ast(A_{m,1})
\cong
\Bigl(\prod_{x\in X_m}\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\Bigr)\rtimes \Pi_0.
\]
Equivalently, a \(\ast\)-automorphism acts by blockwise projective-unitary conjugation together with a permutation of blocks of equal size.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure theory, every \(\ast\)-automorphism of \(\bigoplus_x M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\) permutes the minimal central projections; see \cite[Section 2.1]{BrownOzawa2008}. Compatibility with matrix size forces the induced permutation of blocks to lie in \(\Pi_0\). After that permutation is fixed, the restriction to each simple summand \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\) is a \(\ast\)-automorphism of a full matrix algebra, hence inner, and is therefore represented by conjugation with a unitary unique modulo scalars. This yields the factor \(\prod_x\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\), while \(\Pi_0\) acts by reindexing the blocks.
\end{proof}

\section{Central Observables and Reconstruction}\label{sec:central-idempotents}
Having established rigidity of the fingerprint, we study the center of the fold-groupoid algebra and the information carried by the natural central observables. The arguments in this section depend only on the fingerprint partition and on the fiber-multiplicity data recorded below.

\subsection{Auxiliary Input}
\begin{assumption}[Maximal-Fiber Fingerprint]\label{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic}
For every resolution \(m\), there exists a fingerprint
\[
\chi_m=(\alpha_m,\beta_m)\in\{\pm1\}^2
\]
such that
\[
\chi(x)=\chi_m
\qquad\text{for all }x\in \mathcal M_m.
\]
In the odd-\(m\) stable periodic regime, one furthermore has \(\beta_m=+1\).
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-condexp-index-maxfiber}
Let \(E_m\) be the conditional expectation induced by \(\Fold_m\), that is, averaging uniformly on the fibers of \(\Fold_m\). For a probability distribution \(\rho\) on \(\Omega_m\), write \(\rho_X:=(\Fold_m)_\ast\rho\) and define the fiberwise-uniform distribution
\[
\rho^\flat(\omega):=\frac{\rho_X(\Fold_m(\omega))}{d_m(\Fold_m(\omega))}
\qquad(\omega\in \Omega_m).
\]
Equivalently, \(\rho^\flat\) has the same pushforward to \(X_m\) as \(\rho\) and is uniform on each fiber of \(\Fold_m\). Then
\[
\sup_{\rho}D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m,
\]
where \(\rho\) ranges over probability distributions on \(\Omega_m\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(u_x\) denote the uniform distribution on the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Since \(\rho^\flat\) has the same macroscopic marginal as \(\rho\) and fiberwise conditionals \(u_x\), the relative-entropy chain rule gives
\[
D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)
=
\sum_{x\in X_m}\rho_X(x)\,
D\!\bigl(\rho(\cdot\mid x)\Vert u_x\bigr).
\]
For each \(x\) and each probability distribution \(\sigma\) on \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
D(\sigma\Vert u_x)
&=
\sum_{\omega\in\Fold_m^{-1}(x)}\sigma(\omega)\log\!\bigl(\sigma(\omega)d_m(x)\bigr)\\
&=
\log d_m(x)+\sum_{\omega\in\Fold_m^{-1}(x)}\sigma(\omega)\log \sigma(\omega)\\
&\le
\log d_m(x),
\end{aligned}
\]
because \(\sum_\omega \sigma(\omega)\log \sigma(\omega)\le 0\). Hence
\[
D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)\le \log D_m
\]
for every \(\rho\). Conversely, choose \(x_\star\in X_m\) with \(d_m(x_\star)=D_m\), and let \(\rho\) be a Dirac mass on a point of \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x_\star)\). Then \(\rho^\flat\) is the uniform distribution on that same fiber, so
\[
D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-block-expectation}
Under the Wedderburn identification
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC),
\]
define
\[
E_{m,1}\bigl((A_x)_{x\in X_m}\bigr)
:=
\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\frac{\Tr(A_x)}{d_m(x)}\,I_{d_m(x)}.
\]
Then \(E_{m,1}:A_{m,1}\to Z(A_{m,1})\) is a faithful conditional expectation. In the sense of \cite{Watatani1990}, its Watatani index element is
\[
\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})=\sum_{x\in X_m}d_m(x)^2\,\widetilde P_x.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each block \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\), fix matrix units \(\{e_{ij}^{(x)}\}_{1\le i,j\le d_m(x)}\). The displayed formula is the normalized trace on each simple summand, so it defines a faithful conditional expectation onto the direct sum of scalar matrices, which is exactly \(Z(A_{m,1})\).

Moreover,
\[
E_{m,1}(e_{ij}^{(x)})=\delta_{ij}\,d_m(x)^{-1}\widetilde P_x.
\]
A direct computation shows that
\[
\Bigl\{\bigl(\sqrt{d_m(x)}\,e_{ij}^{(x)},\sqrt{d_m(x)}\,e_{ji}^{(x)}\bigr): x\in X_m,\ 1\le i,j\le d_m(x)\Bigr\}
\]
is a quasi-basis for \(E_{m,1}\): on the \(x\)-block, if \(A_x=\sum_{k,\ell}a_{k\ell}^{(x)}e_{k\ell}^{(x)}\), then
\[
\sum_{i,j=1}^{d_m(x)}\sqrt{d_m(x)}\,e_{ij}^{(x)}
\,E_{m,1}\!\bigl(\sqrt{d_m(x)}\,e_{ji}^{(x)}A_x\bigr)
=
\sum_{i,j=1}^{d_m(x)}a_{ij}^{(x)}e_{ij}^{(x)}
=
A_x,
\]
and similarly on the right. Hence \(E_{m,1}\) has finite Watatani index and
\[
\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})
=
\sum_{x\in X_m}\sum_{i,j=1}^{d_m(x)}d_m(x)\,e_{ij}^{(x)}e_{ji}^{(x)}
=
\sum_{x\in X_m}d_m(x)^2\,\widetilde P_x.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}[Window-\texorpdfstring{$6$}{6} Data]\label{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}
For the window-\(6\) results, we assume the following fiber-multiplicity data:
\[
\begin{aligned}
d_6(x)&\in\{2,3,4\},\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=2\}&=8,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=3\}&=4,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=4\}&=9.
\end{aligned}
\]
These values are consistent with the total microstate count, since
\[
2\cdot 8+3\cdot 4+4\cdot 9=64=2^6.
\]
In particular,
\[
\sum_{x\in X_6}d_6(x)=|\Omega_6|
\]
and
\[
\abs{X_6}=8+4+9=21.
\]
\end{assumption}
