\begin{abstract}
We study two coarse-graining procedures under explicit standing spectral and combinatorial inputs. On the Chebotarev side, let primitive counts be indexed by a finite abelian label group and let the labels be pushed forward along a quotient. We show that the quotient retains exactly the Fourier modes that are trivial on the kernel. This gives an exact chain rule for the relative entropy and an orthogonal decomposition of the \(L^2\)-defect into visible and hidden character energies. Under an isolated dominant nontrivial channel, the same spectral datum governs the second main term in subset counts, the leading pole of the nontrivial Artin factor, and the convergence rate of the induced label distributions; moreover, explicit witness sets show that square-root decay cannot hold when the spectral gap is too weak.

On the fold-groupoid side, we prove that scan-reversal compatible automorphisms preserve the \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\)-fingerprint, realize the corresponding sector decomposition by canonical central involutions, and determine the amount of block data detected by the central observables \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\), \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{scan}}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rev}}\). For the enumerated window-\(6\) model, these observables do not separate the full stable-type space. Apart from the stated inputs, the arguments use only entropy identities, Fourier analysis on finite abelian groups, and finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra theory.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
The Chebotarev density theorem is one of the standard templates for equidistribution in arithmetic: it describes the asymptotic distribution of Frobenius classes in finite Galois extensions \cite{Tschebotareff1926,NeukirchSchmidtWingberg2008,IwaniecKowalski2004}. In dynamical settings, prime-orbit counting and transfer operators lead to closely related questions for closed-orbit labels in finite covers and homology classes \cite{ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,PollicottSharp2007}. In both situations one often has access only to a coarse observable, for instance because the label group is viewed through a quotient. The first part of this paper isolates what remains visible after such a passage to a smaller label space.

The second part concerns a parallel problem in finite-dimensional operator algebra. Fold groupoids give rise to semisimple groupoid algebras equipped with a canonical scan-reversal fingerprint in \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\). Here the natural coarse observables are central ones: traces, involutions, and moment data. We ask how much of the block structure they detect and, conversely, where distinct blocks become indistinguishable. The window-\(6\) model provides a concrete test case.

The two settings are formally different, but the underlying question is the same: how should one measure the loss of information under coarse graining? On the Chebotarev side the answer is Fourier-theoretic; on the fold-groupoid side it is encoded in the center of a finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra. Our aim is to separate the model-dependent input from the formal consequences and to record the latter in a form that can be reused in other instances.

The contribution is therefore structural rather than foundational. We do not establish new transfer-operator expansions or new combinatorial enumerations here. Instead, once the standard analytic and finite-dimensional inputs are isolated, we show that several consequences that are usually treated separately become exact and mutually compatible: entropy loss, hidden Fourier energy, second terms in subset counts, Artin singularities, convergence rates, intrinsic sector projections, and the precise defect of central observables. This synthesis is what gives the paper its novelty.

\subsection{Quotients of primitive Chebotarev data}
Let \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) be a surjective homomorphism of finite abelian groups. Primitive Chebotarev counts on \(G_2\) push forward to coarse counts on \(G_1\). At the level of formal identities, one has the chain rule for relative entropy and Parseval's identity on finite abelian groups \cite{CoverThomas2006,CsiszarKorner2011,DemboZeitouni1998,Diaconis1988,Terras1999}. In the present setting these identities fit together especially well: the quotient sees exactly the characters that are trivial on \(\ker \pi\), and the missing Fourier mass is measured by a canonical nonnegative remainder.

This is made precise in Theorems \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-quotient-relative-entropy-chain} and \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-visible-hidden-character-energy}. The first theorem gives an exact entropy decomposition into a visible term on the quotient and a fiberwise contribution. The second theorem identifies the corresponding \(L^2\)-loss as the character energy carried by the non-descending modes. In particular, the passage to the quotient comes with an exact bookkeeping identity and not merely with an asymptotic estimate.

\subsection{Dominant channels}
The analytic input on the Chebotarev side is the usual channel decomposition attached to the twisted transfer matrices \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\). Chebotarev-type orbit-counting expansions of this kind are standard in hyperbolic dynamics \cite{Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,PollicottSharp2007}; we take them as standing hypotheses and do not revisit their proof. Once one assumes the existence of an isolated dominant nontrivial spectral value, however, several consequences can be drawn uniformly from the same datum.

Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-second-main-term-witness} isolates the corresponding second main term in subset counts. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-artin-dominant-channel-pole-order} identifies the exact contribution of that channel to the nontrivial Artin factor. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-statistical-convergence} translates the same hypothesis into exponential convergence of the induced label distributions to the uniform law, and it also supplies witness sets showing that square-root decay fails when the spectral gap does not reach the critical threshold. A single nontrivial channel thus governs all three phenomena.

\subsection{Fold groupoids and central observables}
On the fold-groupoid side, the basic object is the finite-dimensional algebra
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC),
\]
whose blocks are indexed by the stable fold types. Each block carries a pair of canonical involutions coming from the scan and reversal symmetries, and their signs define a fingerprint \(\chi(x)\in \ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\). The first structural statement, proved in Theorem \ref{thm:fold-groupoid-chi-rigidity}, is that any \(\ast\)-automorphism compatible with these symmetries preserves \(\chi\). This already forces the automorphism group to respect the sector decomposition.

The next step is to recover this sector decomposition internally. In Section \ref{sec:central-idempotents} we construct the central projections from canonical involutions, identify their joint spectral measure, and examine what can be read off from the central element \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\). The maximal blocks turn out to be especially rigid: they lie in a single \(\chi\)-sector, and the leading signed moments recover both the maximal multiplicity and its fingerprint. The appendix treats the more explicit window-\(6\) reconstruction problem and quantifies the defect of the three basic central observables.

\subsection{Standing input and organization}
The paper rests on explicit standing inputs in each of the two settings. On the Chebotarev side we use the spectral expansion and Artin factorization stated in Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}; these encapsulate the standard transfer-operator and zeta-factorization consequences available in finite-cover models, compare \cite{ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,Bass1992,StarkTerras1996,StarkTerras2000}. On the fold-groupoid side we assume the maximal-fiber fingerprint and the window-\(6\) fiber histogram recorded in Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}. All later results are conditional on these hypotheses, and no further model-specific input is used.

Once these hypotheses are in place, the rest of the paper is formal. The arguments reduce to entropy identities, Fourier decomposition, spectral projections in finite dimension, and the elementary structure theory of finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebras \cite{Renault1980,Paterson1999,Watatani1990,KadisonRingrose1983,BrownOzawa2008,Jones1983}.

Section \ref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation and states the standing hypotheses. Section \ref{sec:entropy-chain} treats quotient entropy and hidden character energy. Section \ref{sec:artin-pole} develops the second main term, the pole-order statement, and the convergence consequences. Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents} deal with rigidity and central sector projections for the fold-groupoid algebra. Section \ref{sec:concluding-remarks} closes with a brief comparison of the two coarse-graining mechanisms, and Appendix \ref{app:window6} contains the window-\(6\) reconstruction arguments.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:preliminaries}
\subsection{Finite groups, characters, and divergences}
If \(G\) is a finite group, we write \(\widehat G\) for a complete set of irreducible complex representations of \(G\); when \(G\) is abelian, this is identified with its Pontryagin dual of characters. We write \(\mathrm{unif}_G\) for the uniform probability measure on \(G\). For a surjective homomorphism \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) with kernel \(N\), we set
\[
N^\perp:=\{\chi\in\widehat G_2:\chi|_N\equiv 1\}\cong \widehat G_1.
\]
When \(G\) is abelian and \(f:G\to \CC\), its Fourier transform is
\[
\widehat f(\chi):=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G} f(c)\,\overline{\chi(c)}.
\]
With respect to the normalized inner product
\[
\langle f,g\rangle_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}:=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G}f(c)\,\overline{g(c)},
\]
Parseval's identity takes the form
\[
\|f\|_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}^2=\sum_{\chi\in \widehat G}\abs{\widehat f(\chi)}^2.
\]

For probability measures \(\mu,\nu\) on a finite set \(X\) with \(\mu\ll\nu\), we use
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\mu(x)\log\frac{\mu(x)}{\nu(x)},
\]
\[
\chi^2(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\frac{(\mu(x)-\nu(x))^2}{\nu(x)},
\qquad
\|\mu-\nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}:=\frac12\sum_{x\in X}\abs{\mu(x)-\nu(x)}.
\]
When \(X\) is partitioned into fibers of a map \(\pi:X\to Y\), the relative-entropy chain rule reads
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu)=D(\pi_\ast\mu\Vert \pi_\ast\nu)+\sum_{y\in Y}(\pi_\ast\mu)(y)\,D(\mu(\cdot\mid y)\Vert \nu(\cdot\mid y)),
\]
whenever the conditional distributions are defined.

\subsection{Standing spectral input}
Fix a finite group \(G\), a temperature parameter \(u\in(0,1]\), and primitive Chebotarev counts \(B_{n,c}(u)\) indexed by \(c\in G\). We write
\[
B_n(\ind;u):=\sum_{c\in G}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
and, when \(G\) is abelian,
\[
B_n(\chi;u):=\sum_{c\in G}\chi(c)\,B_{n,c}(u)
\quad(\chi\in \widehat G),
\]
and for each irreducible channel \(\rho\) we let \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\) denote the corresponding transfer matrix. The trivial-channel Perron root is
\[
\lambda_1(u):=\spr(M_{K,\ind}(u)),
\]
the nontrivial spectral radius is
\[
\eta(u):=\max_{\rho\neq \ind}\spr(M_{K,\rho}(u)),
\]
and we set
\[
\Lambda(u):=\max\{|\lambda_2(u)|,\eta(u)\},
\]
where \(\lambda_2(u)\) denotes the second-largest eigenvalue modulus in the trivial channel.

The results below use only the following standard spectral consequences of the transfer formalism.

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp}
Assume \(\Lambda(u)<\lambda_1(u)\). Then, uniformly in \(c\in G\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
B_{n,c}(u)
&=
\frac{1}{|G|}\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right),\\
B_n(\ind;u)
&=
\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
Moreover, for every conjugacy-invariant subset \(A\subseteq G\), if we set
\[
B_{n,A}(u):=\sum_{c\in A}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
then there exist linear functionals \(L_{A,\rho}\) on the corresponding channel spaces such that
\[
B_{n,A}(u)
=
\mathrm{unif}_G(A)\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{A,\rho}\!\bigl(M_{K,\rho}(u)^n\bigr).
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}
For a finite group \(G\), define
\[
L_{K,\rho}(z,u):=\det(I-zM_{K,\rho}(u))^{-1}.
\]
Then the associated zeta function satisfies
\[
\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)=\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho},
\]
and hence
\[
\frac{\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)}{L_{K,\ind}(z,u)}
=
\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho}.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}\label{def:kernel-rh}
We write
\[
\textsf{GRH}_{K,G}(u):\qquad \Lambda(u)\le \lambda_1(u)^{1/2}
\]
for the square-root spectral gap condition that plays the role of a generalized Riemann-hypothesis bound in the present transfer-matrix setting.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization} are the only Chebotarev inputs used below. They package the model-dependent analytic content into two statements that can be verified, in concrete transfer settings, by standard spectral and determinant-factorization methods; compare \cite{ParryPollicott1990,Sharp1993,PollicottSharp2001,Bass1992,StarkTerras1996,StarkTerras2000}. Every later argument depends solely on the explicit asymptotic expansion, the channel decomposition of subset counts, and the Artin factorization recorded here.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Fold groupoids and central observables}
We next fix the finite-dimensional groupoid-algebraic framework used in Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:fold-equivalence-groupoid-algebra}
For each resolution \(m\), let \(\Fold_m:\Omega_m\to X_m\) be the fold map from microstates to stable macrostates, where \(\Omega_m\) is a binary microstate set of cardinality \(2^m\). The associated equivalence-relation groupoid is
\[
\mathcal R_m:=\Omega_m\times_{X_m}\Omega_m
=
\{(\omega,\omega')\in \Omega_m\times\Omega_m:\Fold_m(\omega)=\Fold_m(\omega')\},
\]
and we write
\[
A_{m,1}:=\CC[\mathcal R_m]
\]
for its complex groupoid algebra. The fiber multiplicity at \(x\in X_m\) is
\[
d_m(x):=\abs{\Fold_m^{-1}(x)},
\qquad
D_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}d_m(x),
\qquad
\mathcal M_m:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=D_m\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-wedderburn}
Fix orderings of the fibers \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Then there is a \(\ast\)-isomorphism
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC).
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(\widetilde P_x\in Z(A_{m,1})\) denote the unit of the simple ideal \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\subseteq A_{m,1}\); under any such block identification it is the minimal central idempotent projecting onto the \(x\)-block.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Because
\[
\mathcal R_m=\bigsqcup_{x\in X_m}\mathcal R_m(x),
\]
the groupoid algebra decomposes canonically as
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)].
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), the subgroupoid
\[
\mathcal R_m(x):=\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\times \Fold_m^{-1}(x)
\]
is a pair groupoid on \(d_m(x)\) objects. After choosing an ordering
\[
\Fold_m^{-1}(x)=\{\omega_1,\dots,\omega_{d_m(x)}\},
\]
the basis element \(\delta_{(\omega_i,\omega_j)}\in \CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\) is sent to the matrix unit \(E_{ij}\in M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\). Under this identification, convolution on \(\mathcal R_m(x)\) becomes matrix multiplication and the involution becomes conjugate transpose. Since
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)],
\]
this yields the stated block decomposition. The element \(\widetilde P_x\) is simply the identity of the direct summand \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-z2x2-sector-by-orientation}
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(c_x\) and \(\iota_x\) be the scan and reversal permutations of the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Define the fingerprint
\[
\chi(x):=\bigl(\operatorname{sgn}(c_x),\operatorname{sgn}(\iota_x)\bigr)\in\{\pm1\}^2.
\]
For \((\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2\), set
\[
X_m^{\alpha,\beta}:=\{x\in X_m:\chi(x)=(\alpha,\beta)\},
\qquad
\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}:=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m^{\alpha,\beta}}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)].
\]
Then
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{(\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2}\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}
\]
is the sector decomposition determined by the scan-reversal fingerprint.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The sets \(X_m^{\alpha,\beta}\) form a partition of \(X_m\) by definition of \(\chi\). Since
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m}\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)],
\]
regrouping the simple ideals according to the value of \(\chi(x)\) gives the asserted sector decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:fold-groupoid-aut-semidirect-pi0}
Let
\[
\Pi_0:=\{\sigma\in \mathrm{Sym}(X_m):d_m(\sigma(x))=d_m(x)\ \forall x\in X_m\}.
\]
After choosing identifications \(\CC[\mathcal R_m(x)]\cong M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\), the \(\ast\)-automorphism group of \(A_{m,1}\) is isomorphic to
\[
\Aut^\ast(A_{m,1})
\cong
\Bigl(\prod_{x\in X_m}\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\Bigr)\rtimes \Pi_0.
\]
Equivalently, a \(\ast\)-automorphism acts by blockwise projective-unitary conjugation together with a permutation of blocks of equal size.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each \(d\ge 1\), let
\[
X_{m,d}:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=d\}.
\]
By finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure theory, every \(\ast\)-automorphism of
\[
\bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)
\]
permutes the simple summands and preserves their matrix size \cite{KadisonRingrose1983,BrownOzawa2008}. Thus it induces a permutation of \(X_{m,d}\) for each \(d\), equivalently an element of \(\Pi_0\). Once the block permutation is fixed, the restriction to each simple summand \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\) is a \(\ast\)-automorphism of a full matrix algebra, hence is inner and is determined up to scalars by an element of \(\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\). Collecting the blockwise projective-unitary factors yields the displayed semidirect-product decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic}
For every resolution \(m\), there exists a fingerprint
\[
\chi_m=(\alpha_m,\beta_m)\in\{\pm1\}^2
\]
such that
\[
\chi(x)=\chi_m
\qquad\text{for all }x\in \mathcal M_m.
\]
In the odd-\(m\) stable periodic regime, one furthermore has \(\beta_m=+1\).
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-condexp-index-maxfiber}
Let \(E_m\) be the conditional expectation induced by \(\Fold_m\), that is, averaging uniformly on the fibers of \(\Fold_m\). For a probability distribution \(\rho\) on \(\Omega_m\), write \(\rho^\flat:=\rho\circ E_m\); equivalently, \(\rho^\flat\) has the same pushforward to \(X_m\) as \(\rho\) and is uniform on each fiber of \(\Fold_m\). Then
\[
\sup_{\rho}D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m,
\]
where \(\rho\) ranges over probability distributions on \(\Omega_m\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\rho_X:=(\Fold_m)_\ast\rho\), and for each \(x\in X_m\) let \(u_x\) denote the uniform distribution on the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Since \(\rho^\flat\) has the same macroscopic marginal as \(\rho\) and fiberwise conditionals \(u_x\), the relative-entropy chain rule gives
\[
D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)
=
\sum_{x\in X_m}\rho_X(x)\,
D\!\bigl(\rho(\cdot\mid x)\Vert u_x\bigr).
\]
For each \(x\), the divergence to the uniform measure on a fiber of size \(d_m(x)\) is at most \(\log d_m(x)\le \log D_m\). Hence \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)\le \log D_m\) for every \(\rho\). Conversely, if \(x_\star\in X_m\) satisfies \(d_m(x_\star)=D_m\), choose \(\rho\) to be a Dirac mass on a point of \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x_\star)\). Then \(\rho^\flat\) is the uniform distribution on that same fiber, and therefore \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m\).
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}
For \(m=6\), direct enumeration yields
\[
\begin{aligned}
d_6(x)&\in\{2,3,4\},\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=2\}&=8,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=3\}&=4,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=4\}&=9.
\end{aligned}
\]
and therefore
\[
\abs{X_6}=21.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist} are the only fold-model inputs used below. The later arguments require only the constancy of the maximal-fiber fingerprint and the stated window-\(6\) multiplicity data.
\end{remark}

\section{Concluding remarks}\label{sec:concluding-remarks}
The two halves of the paper furnish parallel coarse-graining ledgers. In the Chebotarev setting, quotienting by a finite abelian kernel removes exactly the Fourier contribution carried by characters that do not descend, and an isolated dominant nontrivial channel controls subset biases, the first nontrivial Artin singularity, and the threshold at which square-root decay can fail. In the fold-groupoid setting, the central involutions recover the fingerprint sectors intrinsically, while the window-\(6\) calculation shows quantitatively that the three basic central observables do not resolve the full block decomposition.

The main value of the paper is therefore structural. All analytic and combinatorial input is concentrated in the standing hypotheses of Section \ref{sec:preliminaries}; once those are fixed, the subsequent arguments identify exactly what the chosen coarse observables retain, what they lose, and how that loss is encoded by canonical projections or moments.
