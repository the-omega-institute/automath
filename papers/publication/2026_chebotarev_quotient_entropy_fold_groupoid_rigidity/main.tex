\begin{abstract}
We study two coarse-graining procedures that arise in rather different settings. For primitive Chebotarev counts on a finite abelian label group, passage to a quotient preserves exactly the Fourier mass carried by characters that are trivial on the kernel. This yields an exact chain rule for the relative entropy and an orthogonal decomposition of the \(L^2\)-defect into visible and hidden character energies. Under an isolated dominant nontrivial channel, we obtain a second main term for subset counts, determine the corresponding pole order in the nontrivial Artin factor, and deduce exponential convergence of the primitive label distributions to Haar measure together with witness sets that rule out square-root decay when the spectral gap is too small. On the fold-groupoid side, we prove that scan-reversal compatible automorphisms preserve the \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\)-fingerprint, realize the associated sector projections by central involutions, and describe what can and cannot be recovered from the central observables \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\), \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{scan}}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rev}}\). The more computational window-\(6\) reconstruction is recorded in an appendix.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
The Chebotarev density theorem describes the asymptotic distribution of Frobenius classes in finite Galois extensions and remains one of the basic templates for equidistribution in arithmetic \cite{Tschebotareff1926,NeukirchSchmidtWingberg2008,IwaniecKowalski2004}. In many counting problems, however, one does not observe the full Frobenius label. One passes to a quotient of the label group, or one records only a derived observable. The first purpose of this paper is to isolate, in a form that is both exact and usable, what survives such a passage to a coarser label space.

There is a parallel question on the operator-algebraic side. Fold groupoids give rise to finite semisimple groupoid algebras with a canonical scan-reversal fingerprint in \(\ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\). Here too one is led naturally to coarse observables: central traces, central involutions, and moment data. The second purpose of the paper is to show that these observables capture a substantial part of the block structure, while stopping short of a complete reconstruction in the explicit window-\(6\) model.

The common theme is that both problems ask for a precise bookkeeping of information under coarse graining. In the Chebotarev setting this bookkeeping is Fourier-theoretic; in the fold-groupoid setting it is encoded in the center of a finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra. The arguments themselves are elementary once the standing spectral and combinatorial inputs have been fixed, and we have tried to keep those inputs sharply separated from the formal consequences derived from them.

\subsection{Quotients of primitive Chebotarev data}
Let \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) be a surjective homomorphism of finite abelian groups. Starting from primitive Chebotarev counts on \(G_2\), one obtains a coarser distribution on \(G_1\) by pushforward. At the level of information theory, the relevant identities are classical: one has the chain rule for relative entropy and Parseval's identity on finite abelian groups \cite{CoverThomas2006,CsiszarKorner2011,DemboZeitouni1998,Diaconis1988,Terras1999}. What seems not to have been recorded in this form is that, in the primitive Chebotarev setting, these identities admit a clean arithmetic reading.

Our first pair of results shows that the quotient distribution retains exactly the Fourier modes corresponding to characters that are trivial on the kernel \(K=\ker \pi\), and nothing else. In particular, the loss under quotienting is measured by a canonical nonnegative hidden-energy term. The relative-entropy statement is given in Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-quotient-relative-entropy-chain}, and the corresponding \(L^2\)-decomposition appears in Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-visible-hidden-character-energy}. Together they identify the visible and invisible parts of the primitive label distribution without any appeal to asymptotics.

\subsection{Dominant channels}
The analytic input on the Chebotarev side is the standard channel decomposition coming from the transfer matrices \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\). Once there is an isolated dominant nontrivial spectral value, the asymptotic picture becomes rigid. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-second-main-term-witness} extracts the corresponding second main term in subset counts. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-artin-dominant-channel-pole-order} identifies the precise pole order contributed to the nontrivial Artin factor. Theorem \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-statistical-convergence} then converts the same spectral input into exponential convergence of the primitive label distributions to uniformity and, in the opposite direction, into explicit witness sets showing that square-root decay fails whenever the spectral gap is too weak.

What matters here is that the same dominant channel controls three different outputs: an oscillating correction term in counting, the first nontrivial singularity of the Artin factor, and the speed of convergence of the induced probability measures. The three statements are proved in a common framework, but we have separated them so that each may be used independently.

\subsection{Fold groupoids}
On the fold-groupoid side, the basic object is the finite-dimensional algebra
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC),
\]
whose blocks are indexed by the stable fold types. Each block carries a pair of canonical involutions coming from the scan and reversal symmetries, and their signs define a fingerprint \(\chi(x)\in \ZZ_2\times\ZZ_2\). The first structural statement, proved in Theorem \ref{thm:fold-groupoid-chi-rigidity}, is that any \(\ast\)-automorphism compatible with these symmetries preserves \(\chi\). This already forces the automorphism group to respect the sector decomposition.

The next step is to realize the sector decomposition internally. In Section \ref{sec:central-idempotents} we construct the associated central projections from two canonical central involutions, identify the joint spectral measure of these involutions, and analyze the information carried by the central element \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\). The resulting picture is quite rigid at the top of the spectrum: maximal fibers lie in a single \(\chi\)-sector, and the leading signed moments recover both the maximal fiber size and the associated fingerprint. The more explicit reconstruction results for the window-\(6\) model, together with the resulting defect statement for the three central observables, are collected in Appendix \ref{app:window6}.

\subsection{Standing input and organization}
The body of the paper uses only two external inputs on each side. For Chebotarev counts we assume the spectral expansion and Artin factorization recorded in Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}. For fold groupoids we assume the maximal-fiber fingerprint and the window-\(6\) fiber histogram from Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}. Once these are granted, all remaining arguments reduce to entropy identities, Fourier decomposition, and elementary finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra theory \cite{Renault1980,Paterson1999,Watatani1990,BrownOzawa2008,Jones1983}.

Section \ref{sec:preliminaries} fixes notation and states the standing hypotheses. Section \ref{sec:entropy-chain} treats quotient entropy and hidden character energy. Section \ref{sec:artin-pole} develops the second main term, the pole-order statement, and the convergence consequences. Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents} deal with rigidity and central sector projections for the fold-groupoid algebra. Section \ref{sec:concluding-remarks} closes with a brief comparison of the two coarse-graining mechanisms, and Appendix \ref{app:window6} contains the window-\(6\) reconstruction arguments.

\section{Preliminaries}\label{sec:preliminaries}
\subsection{Finite groups, characters, and divergences}
If \(G\) is a finite group, we write \(\widehat G\) for a complete set of irreducible complex representations of \(G\); when \(G\) is abelian, this is identified with its Pontryagin dual of characters. We write \(\mathrm{unif}_G\) for the uniform probability measure on \(G\). For a surjective homomorphism \(\pi:G_2\twoheadrightarrow G_1\) with kernel \(K\), we set
\[
K^\perp:=\{\chi\in\widehat G_2:\chi|_K\equiv 1\}\cong \widehat G_1.
\]
When \(G\) is abelian and \(f:G\to \CC\), its Fourier transform is
\[
\widehat f(\chi):=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G} f(c)\,\overline{\chi(c)}.
\]
With respect to the normalized inner product
\[
\langle f,g\rangle_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}:=\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{c\in G}f(c)\,\overline{g(c)},
\]
Parseval's identity takes the form
\[
\|f\|_{L^2(\mathrm{unif}_G)}^2=\sum_{\chi\in \widehat G}\abs{\widehat f(\chi)}^2.
\]

For probability measures \(\mu,\nu\) on a finite set \(X\) with \(\mu\ll\nu\), we use
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\mu(x)\log\frac{\mu(x)}{\nu(x)},
\]
\[
\chi^2(\mu\Vert \nu):=\sum_{x\in X}\frac{(\mu(x)-\nu(x))^2}{\nu(x)},
\qquad
\|\mu-\nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}:=\frac12\sum_{x\in X}\abs{\mu(x)-\nu(x)}.
\]
When \(X\) is partitioned into fibers of a map \(\pi:X\to Y\), the relative-entropy chain rule reads
\[
D(\mu\Vert \nu)=D(\pi_\ast\mu\Vert \pi_\ast\nu)+\sum_{y\in Y}(\pi_\ast\mu)(y)\,D(\mu(\cdot\mid y)\Vert \nu(\cdot\mid y)),
\]
whenever the conditional distributions are defined.

\subsection{Standing spectral input}
Fix a finite group \(G\), a temperature parameter \(u\in(0,1]\), and primitive Chebotarev counts \(B_{n,c}(u)\) indexed by \(c\in G\). We write
\[
B_n(\ind;u):=\sum_{c\in G}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
and, when \(G\) is abelian,
\[
B_n(\chi;u):=\sum_{c\in G}\chi(c)\,B_{n,c}(u)
\quad(\chi\in \widehat G),
\]
and for each irreducible channel \(\rho\) we let \(M_{K,\rho}(u)\) denote the corresponding transfer matrix. The trivial-channel Perron root is
\[
\lambda_1(u):=\spr(M_{K,\ind}(u)),
\]
the nontrivial spectral radius is
\[
\eta(u):=\max_{\rho\neq \ind}\spr(M_{K,\rho}(u)),
\]
and we set
\[
\Lambda(u):=\max\{|\lambda_2(u)|,\eta(u)\},
\]
where \(\lambda_2(u)\) denotes the second-largest eigenvalue modulus in the trivial channel.

The results below use only the following standard spectral consequences of the transfer formalism.

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp}
Assume \(\Lambda(u)<\lambda_1(u)\). Then, uniformly in \(c\in G\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
B_{n,c}(u)
&=
\frac{1}{|G|}\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right),\\
B_n(\ind;u)
&=
\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+O\!\left(\frac{\Lambda(u)^n}{n}\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
Moreover, for every conjugacy-invariant subset \(A\subseteq G\), if we set
\[
B_{n,A}(u):=\sum_{c\in A}B_{n,c}(u),
\]
then there exist linear functionals \(L_{A,\rho}\) on the corresponding channel spaces such that
\[
B_{n,A}(u)
=
\mathrm{unif}_G(A)\frac{\lambda_1(u)^n}{n}
+\frac{1}{n}\sum_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{A,\rho}\!\bigl(M_{K,\rho}(u)^n\bigr).
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}\label{prop:kernel-artin-factorization}
For a finite group \(G\), define
\[
L_{K,\rho}(z,u):=\det(I-zM_{K,\rho}(u))^{-1}.
\]
Then the associated zeta function satisfies
\[
\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)=\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho},
\]
and hence
\[
\frac{\zeta_{K,G}(z,u)}{L_{K,\ind}(z,u)}
=
\prod_{\rho\in\widehat G\setminus\{\ind\}}L_{K,\rho}(z,u)^{\dim\rho}.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{definition}\label{def:kernel-rh}
We write
\[
\textsf{GRH}_{K,G}(u):\qquad \Lambda(u)\le \lambda_1(u)^{1/2}
\]
for the square-root spectral gap condition that plays the role of a generalized Riemann-hypothesis bound in the present transfer-matrix setting.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:kernel-chebotarev-exp} and \ref{prop:kernel-artin-factorization} are the only Chebotarev inputs used below. Every later argument depends solely on the explicit asymptotic expansion, the channel decomposition of subset counts, and the Artin factorization recorded here.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Fold groupoids and central observables}
We next fix the finite-dimensional groupoid-algebraic framework used in Sections \ref{sec:groupoid-rigidity} and \ref{sec:central-idempotents}.

\begin{definition}\label{def:fold-equivalence-groupoid-algebra}
For each resolution \(m\), let \(\Fold_m:\Omega_m\to X_m\) be the fold map from microstates to stable macrostates, where \(\Omega_m\) is a binary microstate set of cardinality \(2^m\). The associated equivalence-relation groupoid is
\[
\mathcal R_m:=\Omega_m\times_{X_m}\Omega_m
=
\{(\omega,\omega')\in \Omega_m\times\Omega_m:\Fold_m(\omega)=\Fold_m(\omega')\},
\]
and we write
\[
A_{m,1}:=\CC[\mathcal R_m]
\]
for its complex groupoid algebra. The fiber multiplicity at \(x\in X_m\) is
\[
d_m(x):=\abs{\Fold_m^{-1}(x)},
\qquad
D_m:=\max_{x\in X_m}d_m(x),
\qquad
\mathcal M_m:=\{x\in X_m:d_m(x)=D_m\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-wedderburn}
There is a canonical \(\ast\)-isomorphism
\[
A_{m,1}\cong \bigoplus_{x\in X_m}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC).
\]
For each \(x\in X_m\), we denote by \(\widetilde P_x\in Z(A_{m,1})\) the minimal central idempotent projecting onto the block \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\) determines a full equivalence-relation subgroupoid whose algebra is canonically \(M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)\); summing over all fibers gives the direct-sum decomposition.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-groupoid-z2x2-sector-by-orientation}
For each \(x\in X_m\), let \(c_x\) and \(\iota_x\) be the scan and reversal permutations of the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Define the fingerprint
\[
\chi(x):=\bigl(\operatorname{sgn}(c_x),\operatorname{sgn}(\iota_x)\bigr)\in\{\pm1\}^2.
\]
For \((\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2\), set
\[
X_m^{\alpha,\beta}:=\{x\in X_m:\chi(x)=(\alpha,\beta)\},
\qquad
\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}:=\bigoplus_{x\in X_m^{\alpha,\beta}}M_{d_m(x)}(\CC).
\]
Then
\[
A_{m,1}=\bigoplus_{(\alpha,\beta)\in\{\pm1\}^2}\CC[\mathcal R_m]^{\alpha,\beta}
\]
is the sector decomposition determined by the scan-reversal fingerprint.
\end{proposition}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:fold-groupoid-aut-semidirect-pi0}
Let
\[
\Pi_0:=\{\sigma\in \mathrm{Sym}(X_m):d_m(\sigma(x))=d_m(x)\ \forall x\in X_m\}.
\]
Then the \(\ast\)-automorphism group of \(A_{m,1}\) is naturally isomorphic to
\[
\Aut^\ast(A_{m,1})
\cong
\Bigl(\prod_{x\in X_m}\mathrm{PU}(d_m(x))\Bigr)\rtimes \Pi_0.
\]
Equivalently, a \(\ast\)-automorphism acts by blockwise projective-unitary conjugation together with a permutation of blocks of equal size.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure theory, every \(\ast\)-automorphism of
\[
\bigoplus_{x\in X_m} M_{d_m(x)}(\CC)
\]
permutes the simple summands and is inner on each of them. Compatibility with matrix size forces the permutation to lie in \(\Pi_0\).
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic}
For every resolution \(m\), there exists a fingerprint
\[
\chi_m=(\alpha_m,\beta_m)\in\{\pm1\}^2
\]
such that
\[
\chi(x)=\chi_m
\qquad\text{for all }x\in \mathcal M_m.
\]
In the odd-\(m\) stable periodic regime, one furthermore has \(\beta_m=+1\).
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:fold-condexp-index-maxfiber}
Let \(E_m\) be the conditional expectation induced by \(\Fold_m\), that is, averaging uniformly on the fibers of \(\Fold_m\). For a probability distribution \(\rho\) on \(\Omega_m\), write \(\rho^\flat:=\rho\circ E_m\); equivalently, \(\rho^\flat\) has the same pushforward to \(X_m\) as \(\rho\) and is uniform on each fiber of \(\Fold_m\). Then
\[
\sup_{\rho}D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m,
\]
where \(\rho\) ranges over probability distributions on \(\Omega_m\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\rho_X:=(\Fold_m)_\ast\rho\), and for each \(x\in X_m\) let \(u_x\) denote the uniform distribution on the fiber \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x)\). Since \(\rho^\flat\) has the same macroscopic marginal as \(\rho\) and fiberwise conditionals \(u_x\), the relative-entropy chain rule gives
\[
D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)
=
\sum_{x\in X_m}\rho_X(x)\,
D\!\bigl(\rho(\cdot\mid x)\Vert u_x\bigr).
\]
For each \(x\), the divergence to the uniform measure on a fiber of size \(d_m(x)\) is at most \(\log d_m(x)\le \log D_m\). Hence \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)\le \log D_m\) for every \(\rho\). Conversely, if \(x_\star\in X_m\) satisfies \(d_m(x_\star)=D_m\), choose \(\rho\) to be a Dirac mass on a point of \(\Fold_m^{-1}(x_\star)\). Then \(\rho^\flat\) is the uniform distribution on that same fiber, and therefore \(D(\rho\Vert \rho^\flat)=\log D_m\).
\end{proof}

\begin{assumption}\label{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist}
For \(m=6\), direct enumeration yields
\[
\begin{aligned}
d_6(x)&\in\{2,3,4\},\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=2\}&=8,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=3\}&=4,\\
\#\{x\in X_6:d_6(x)=4\}&=9.
\end{aligned}
\]
and therefore
\[
\abs{X_6}=21.
\]
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}
Assumptions \ref{thm:pom-maxfiber-orientation-charge-periodic} and \ref{cor:fold-bin-m6-fiber-hist} are the only fold-model inputs used below. The later arguments require only the constancy of the maximal-fiber fingerprint and the stated window-\(6\) multiplicity data.
\end{remark}

\section{Concluding remarks}\label{sec:concluding-remarks}
The two parts of the paper are formally quite close. For primitive Chebotarev counts, quotienting by a finite abelian kernel discards exactly the Fourier contribution of the characters that do not descend. For fold groupoids, passing to the central observables generated by \(\mathrm{IndW}(E_{m,1})\), \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{scan}}\), and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{rev}}\) likewise discards part of the block data, and the appendix makes this loss explicit in window \(6\). In both settings one can therefore describe the visible information by a concrete projection mechanism and identify the residual term without ambiguity.

It is also worth stressing that the analytic and algebraic inputs enter only through the standing assumptions of Section \ref{sec:preliminaries}. Once those are fixed, the rest of the argument is formal: entropy chain rules, Parseval decompositions, and finite-dimensional \(C^\ast\)-algebra structure theory suffice. The point of view taken here is that this separation is useful in its own right, since it isolates precisely which parts of the analysis depend on the underlying model and which do not.
