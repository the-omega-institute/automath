\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection*{Problem}

Let \((X,\mathcal{B},\mu,T)\) be a measure-preserving system and let
\(\mathcal W=\{W_i:i\in\Sigma\}\) be a finite measurable observation
partition.  The coding map
\(\omega_\infty(x)=(s_t(x))_{t\geq 0}\), where
\(T^t x\in W_{s_t(x)}\), defines a measurable symbolic factor over the
finite alphabet \(\Sigma\).  For a visible event \(\mathsf P\), the
depth-\(m\) Bayes error
\[
  \varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)
\]
is the smallest classification error achievable from the first \(m\)
visible symbols.  This paper studies the sequence
\(m\mapsto \varepsilon_m(\mathsf P;\mu)\) when \(\mathsf P\) is defined
by first entry into a symbolic hole.  The main question is how much of
the open system can be recovered from the finite-prefix Bayes error
rather than from direct access to survivor probabilities or transfer
operators.

\subsection*{Setting}

Fix a topologically mixing one-step subshift of finite type \(Y\), a
one-step potential \(\varphi\), and the equilibrium Markov measure
\(\nu_\varphi\).  Let \(H\) be a Markov hole with mixing survivor
subshift \(Y_H\), and write
\[
  \gamma_H(\varphi):=P_Y(\varphi)-P_{Y_H}(\varphi)
\]
for the escape rate.  For a non-empty proper subset
\(R\subsetneq \ZZ/q\ZZ\), let \(\mathsf P_R\) be the event that the
first entry time into \(H\) is finite and belongs to \(R\) modulo
\(q\).
There is one unavoidable degeneracy: from some safe state and residue,
the future may force the first-hit residue either always into \(R\) or
always outside \(R\).  Proposition~\ref{prop:degenerate-residue-dichotomy}
classifies this case.  Along each residue subsequence the Bayes error is
either eventually zero or has the survivor-mass asymptotic governed by a
quasistationary ambiguity amplitude.  The main amplitude theorem below
treats the two-terminal case, where every safe state and residue can
still hit both sides of the residue partition.

\subsection*{Main finite-state theorem}

The paper is organized around one finite-state result.  The genuinely
new input is the exact conversion of the Bayes error for first-entry
residue events into a killed path sum with residue-dependent terminal
ambiguity weights.  Once that identity is in hand, the escape exponent
and the leading amplitude are finite Perron--Frobenius consequences of
the killed matrix.  The degenerate case is handled separately by
Proposition~\ref{prop:degenerate-residue-dichotomy}, which shows that on
each residue subsequence the Bayes error is either eventually zero or
has the same survivor-mass asymptotic.

\begin{theorem}[Main finite-state theorem]
\label{thm:main-amplitude}
Let \(Y\subseteq A^{\NNzero}\) be a topologically mixing one-step
subshift of finite type, let \(\nu_\varphi\) be the fully supported
equilibrium Markov measure of a one-step potential, and write \(K\) for
its transition matrix.  Let
\[
  H=\bigcup_{h\in A_{\mathrm{hole}}}[h],
  \qquad
  \emptyset\neq A_{\mathrm{hole}}\subsetneq A,
  \qquad
  \mathcal S=A\setminus A_{\mathrm{hole}},
\]
and assume that the survivor subshift \(Y_H\) is topologically mixing.
Fix \(q\geq2\) and
\(\emptyset\neq R\subsetneq \ZZ/q\ZZ\).  Let
\[
  \mathsf P_R
  :=\{y:\tau_H(y)<\infty,\ \tau_H(y)\bmod q\in R\}.
\]
Assume the two-terminal residue reachability hypothesis on the
\(q\)-cyclic safe graph: from every \((i,r)\in\mathcal S\times\ZZ/q\ZZ\)
there are admissible futures whose first entry into
\(A_{\mathrm{hole}}\) occurs at residues in both \(R-r\) and
\((\ZZ/q\ZZ\setminus R)-r\).

For \(i\in\mathcal S\), let \(\PP_i\) be the Markov law started at \(i\),
set
\[
  \tau_H^+:=\inf\{n\geq1:y_n\in A_{\mathrm{hole}}\},
  \qquad
  p_{i,r}:=\PP_i(r+\tau_H^+\in R\bmod q),
\]
and define \(b_r(i):=\min\{p_{i,r},1-p_{i,r}\}\).  Let \(B_H\) be the
killed transition matrix on \(\mathcal S\),
\[
  B_H(i,j):=K_{ij}\mathbf 1_{\{j\in\mathcal S\}},
\]
let \(\mathcal S_\infty\) be the states appearing in \(Y_H\), put
\(\mathcal T=\mathcal S\setminus\mathcal S_\infty\), and order
\(\mathcal S=\mathcal S_\infty\sqcup\mathcal T\) so that
\[
  B_H=
  \begin{pmatrix}
    B_\infty & C\\
    0 & Q
  \end{pmatrix},
\]
where \(B_\infty=B_H|_{\mathcal S_\infty}\).  Let
\(\rho_H:=\rho(B_\infty)=\rho(B_H)\), let \(\ell_\infty>0\) be a left
Perron--Frobenius eigenvector of \(B_\infty\), and define
\[
  \ell_H^{\mathsf T}
  :=
  \bigl(
    \ell_\infty^{\mathsf T},
    \ell_\infty^{\mathsf T}C(\rho_H I-Q)^{-1}
  \bigr),
  \qquad
  \xi_H:=\frac{\ell_H}{\ell_H^{\mathsf T}\mathbf 1}.
\]
Then, writing \(u_i=\pi_i\) for the stationary weights on
\(\mathcal S\), the Bayes error has the exact formula
\[
  \varepsilon_m(\mathsf P_R;\nu_\varphi)
  = u^{\mathsf T}B_H^{m-1}b_{m-1\bmod q}
  \qquad (m\geq1).
\]
Moreover
\[
  \theta\,\nu_\varphi(\tau_H\ge m)
  \leq \varepsilon_m(\mathsf P_R;\nu_\varphi)
  \leq \frac12\,\nu_\varphi(\tau_H\ge m)
\]
for some \(\theta>0\), and
\[
  \lim_{m\to\infty}
  -\frac1m\log\varepsilon_m(\mathsf P_R;\nu_\varphi)
  =
  P_Y(\varphi)-P_{Y_H}(\varphi).
\]
Finally, for every residue class \(r\),
\[
  \lim_{n\to\infty}
  \frac{\varepsilon_{nq+r+1}(\mathsf P_R;\nu_\varphi)}
       {\nu_\varphi(\tau_H\ge nq+r+1)}
  = \xi_H^{\mathsf T}b_r.
\]
Thus finite observation recovers the escape-rate pressure gap together
with a residue-dependent quasistationary ambiguity amplitude.
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{Scope of the remaining results.}
Section~\ref{sec:open-system} is organized so that
Theorem~\ref{thm:first-entry-escape-rate} is the single central theorem
of the finite-state part of the paper: it states the killed-matrix
formula, the escape-rate conclusion, and the residuewise amplitude
limit in one place.  The later
Theorem~\ref{thm:quasistationary-ambiguity} is included only to isolate
the finite-matrix Perron--Frobenius calculation that proves the final
amplitude clause of Theorem~\ref{thm:first-entry-escape-rate}.  The
degenerate-residue companion
Proposition~\ref{prop:degenerate-residue-dichotomy} records the same
killed-matrix formula without the two-terminal reachability hypothesis
the residue subsequences on which the error eventually vanishes.  The
rest of the paper is explicitly secondary to this finite-state theorem.

Corollary~\ref{cor:main-renyi} records the survivor analogue of the
standard \(L^s\)-spectrum or R\'enyi pressure identity for Gibbs weights;
compare \citet{Renyi1961,HentschelProcaccia1983,Pesin1997}.  Its role
here is not to introduce a new pressure formalism, but to keep the
conditioned collision estimates tied to the same killed matrix.  In
particular, at \(s=2\) the conditioned survivor law has R\'enyi-\(2\)
entropy rate
\[
  h_{2,H}:=2P_{Y_H}(\varphi)-P_{Y_H}(2\varphi),
\]
and together with Chen--Stein this yields iid Poisson birthday
thresholds on the scale
\[
  \binom{N_m}{2}e^{-m h_{2,H}}\asymp 1,
\]
provided
\[
  N_m^3S_3^H(m)\to0,
  \qquad
  S_3^H(m)=e^{-mh_{3,H}+o(m)},
  \qquad
  h_{3,H}:=3P_{Y_H}(\varphi)-P_{Y_H}(3\varphi).
\]
At the pure exponential threshold
\(N_m=e^{(h_{2,H}/2+o(1))m}\), the required condition is
\[
  h_{3,H}>\frac32h_{2,H}.
\]
These are iid statements about independent survivor prefixes.
No orbit-sampling Poisson theorem is asserted.

Proposition~\ref{thm:main-resonance} is presented only as an algebraic
corollary of the same killed matrix.  It is a finite-dimensional
resolvent reformulation of the same killed path sum.  Its possible-pole
set is the ordinary spectrum of the lifted matrix \(\widehat B_H\), and
the scalar content is the non-cancellation criterion deciding which
lifted eigenvalues are actually visible in the one-variable generating
function.  It is therefore a finite-matrix bookkeeping statement, not a
Banach-space Ruelle-resonance theorem.

Appendix~\ref{app:holder-finite-cylinder} has a different role.  It
contains a standalone \(C^\alpha\) open-transfer theorem for one-step
Markov holes and, after block recoding, a finite-prefix Gibbs comparison
for general finite-cylinder holes.  Neither appendix theorem is used as
a hypothesis in the finite-state resolvent proposition, and neither
upgrades the main theorem beyond the finite-state setting stated above.

\paragraph{Conditional hypothesis map.}
The standing data are a mixing one-step SFT \(Y\), a one-step potential
\(\varphi\) with equilibrium Markov measure \(\nu_\varphi\), and a
non-empty proper one-step Markov hole
\(H=\bigcup_{h\in A_{\mathrm{hole}}}[h]\) with
\(\emptyset\neq A_{\mathrm{hole}}\subsetneq A\).  The additional
assumptions are disclosed result by result:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item The finite-state theorem and its degenerate-residue companion
  use the mixing survivor subshift \(Y_H\), the finite residue data
  \(q\ge2\) and \(\emptyset\neq R\subsetneq\ZZ/q\ZZ\), and in the
  non-degenerate case the two-terminal residue reachability condition in
  the finite safe graph.
  \item Corollary~\ref{cor:main-renyi} uses only the one-step Markov
  model and the mixing survivor subshift; it does not use a residue
  modulus, a residue set, or the two-terminal reachability condition.
  \item Proposition~\ref{thm:main-resonance} uses the same finite Markov
  data as the main theorem, and its spectral non-cancellation criterion
  is a conclusion deciding whether a possible lifted pole is visible.
  \item Appendix~\ref{app:holder-finite-cylinder} first chooses a block
  length dominating the ambient Markov memory and the cylinder length of
  the hole, then works on the explicit Banach space \(C^\alpha\) with
  Gibbs distortion and finite reachability.  These are appendix
  hypotheses only.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Context and comparison}

Thermodynamic formalism for symbolic systems and equilibrium states is
classical; we use standard material from
\citet{Bowen2008,Ruelle1978,Walters1975GMeasures,WaltersErgodicTheory}.
The direct open-system literature studies survivor probabilities,
escape rates, local escape laws, or hitting statistics themselves.  The
open-hole viewpoint goes back to \citet{PianigianiYorke1979} and, for
symbolic or thermodynamic settings, includes
\citet{ColletMartinezSchmitt1997}, \citet{DemersYoung2006},
\citet{KellerLiverani2009}, \citet{DemersWrightYoung2012},
\citet{FergusonPollicott2012}, \citet{DemersTodd2017}, and
\citet{BruinDemers2022}.  For symbolic holes, Markov holes, symbolic
codings of hyperbolic open systems, and dependence on the placement of
the hole, see \citet{BundfussKrugerTroubetzkoy2011},
\citet{BunimovichYurchenko2011}, \citet{CheriyathAgarwal2023},
\citet{BonannoCristadoroLenci2022}, and
\citet{AgarwalCheriyathTikekar2024}.  For the relation between hitting
and escaping statistics and for local escape laws in mixing systems, see
\citet{BruinDemersTodd2018,HaydnYang2020}.  As recent ETDS benchmarks
with direct escape observables, \citet{DemersTodd2021} derive
asymptotic escape rates and limiting conditionally invariant
distributions for multimodal maps with small holes, while
\citet{AtnipFroylandGonzalezTokmanVaienti2023} develop equilibrium
states for random open and closed interval systems and relate Lyapunov
exponents to escape through random holes.

The present paper asks a different question.  Its observable is not
survivor mass, a local escape rate, or a hitting-time law.  Instead, one
observes only the first \(m\) visible symbols and asks for the Bayes
error in deciding whether the first entrance time into the hole falls in
a prescribed residue class.  A safe prefix leaves a residue-dependent
future ambiguity, and the Bayes error therefore sums ambiguity weights
over survivor prefixes rather than summing survivor mass with terminal
weight \(1\).  This produces the terminal vectors \(b_r\), the
degenerate-residue dichotomy, and the quasistationary amplitudes
\(\xi_H^{\mathsf T}b_r\), which are not part of the direct
escape/hitting statements just cited.

At the operator-theoretic level, killed transfer operators and spectral
descriptions of open systems appear in
\citet{KellerLiverani2009,FroylandStancevic2010,DemersWrightYoung2012,FergusonPollicott2012,AtnipFroylandGonzalezTokmanVaienti2023}.
This is the natural comparison point for the resolvent language used
here.  However, Proposition~\ref{thm:main-resonance} below is only a
finite-dimensional resolvent packaging of one killed matrix.  It is not
a Banach-space resonance theorem.  The Poisson approximation input is
the Chen--Stein method of \citet{ArratiaGoldsteinGordon1989}; what is
specific here is the finite-observation inversion problem: from the
finite-prefix Bayes error one recovers the escape-rate pressure gap and
the residue-dependent quasistationary amplitudes, while the cyclotomic
resolvent and survivor R\'enyi spectrum are secondary corollaries.

\subsection*{Theorem-level comparison}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item Theorem~\ref{thm:main-amplitude}, proved in
  Theorems~\ref{thm:first-entry-escape-rate} and
  \ref{thm:quasistationary-ambiguity}, should be compared with direct
  escape and hitting results such as
  \citet{BruinDemersTodd2018,HaydnYang2020,DemersTodd2021}.  Those works
  estimate survivor mass, local escape rates, or hitting-time
  distributions directly.  Here the distinct step is the exact
  finite-prefix identity
  \[
    \varepsilon_m(\mathsf P_R;\nu_\varphi)
    =u^{\mathsf T}B_H^{m-1}b_{m-1\bmod q},
  \]
  with residue-dependent ambiguity weights \(b_r\).  The usual escape
  exponent is then recovered from this weighted killed path sum, and the
  new residue-dependent quantity is the quasistationary amplitude
  \(\xi_H^{\mathsf T}b_r\).
  \item Corollary~\ref{cor:main-renyi} is a survivor-pressure corollary
  of the same killed-matrix formalism.  Relative to the escape/hitting
  literature, it is bookkeeping rather than a new escape theorem: once
  survivor weights are isolated, the R\'enyi pressure identity and the
  iid collision threshold follow from standard thermodynamic formalism
  together with Chen--Stein.
  \item Proposition~\ref{thm:main-resonance} should be read against
  operator papers such as
  \citet{KellerLiverani2009,FroylandStancevic2010,AtnipFroylandGonzalezTokmanVaienti2023}.
  Its content is finite-state and scalar: it identifies the possible
  poles of the Bayes-error generating function and the exact
  non-cancellation test deciding which possible poles are visible.  It
  does not construct a Banach-space open transfer operator or a general
  Ruelle-resonance theory.
  \item Appendix~\ref{app:holder-finite-cylinder} is the only part of
  the paper that gives an explicit Banach-space open-operator theorem.
  It is included to treat finite-cylinder holes after block recoding and
  to justify the supporting escape exponent in that setting.  It does
  not replace the finite-state proof of
  Theorem~\ref{thm:main-amplitude}, and it is not an input to
  Proposition~\ref{thm:main-resonance}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Theorem hierarchy}

The manuscript is organized around one dynamical theme: finite
observation detects the open-system pressure gap and its
quasistationary refinement.
Section~\ref{sec:open-system} is therefore the center of gravity.  Its
first subsection culminates in a single theorem that already contains
the exact killed-matrix formula, the escape-rate conclusion, and the
residuewise quasistationary amplitude.  The later quasistationary
theorem in the same section is auxiliary and supplies the finite-matrix
proof of that last clause.  Section~\ref{sec:double-budget} records a
secondary pressure corollary and its iid collision consequences.
Section~\ref{sec:resolvent} is an algebraic afterthought: it packages
the same killed matrix in cyclotomic-resolvent language and does not
carry new dynamical content beyond the central theorem.  The H\"older
finite-cylinder appendix is retained only as supporting block-recoding
material for finite-cylinder holes and is not part of the finite-state
resolvent argument.

\subsection*{Organization}

Section~\ref{sec:preliminaries} introduces the visible symbolic factor
and finite observation \(\sigma\)-algebras.
Section~\ref{sec:clarity} develops the exact Bayes error formulas and
the weighted boundary transfer theorem.
Section~\ref{sec:open-system} proves
Theorem~\ref{thm:main-amplitude} through a single central finite-state
theorem together with an auxiliary amplitude computation.
Section~\ref{sec:double-budget} records the secondary pressure
corollary and derives the collision consequences.
Section~\ref{sec:resolvent} records the algebraic cyclotomic-resolvent
corollary.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} collects brief final remarks.
Appendix~\ref{app:holder-finite-cylinder} gives the supporting H\"older
finite-cylinder open-operator and escape-exponent theorems after
block recoding with block length at least the ambient Markov memory.
