\begin{abstract}
We consider the stationary binary record obtained by sampling a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector along a static worldline in a static KMS spacetime. For ideal one-step dead time the sampled law is the stationary Markov measure on the golden-mean shift, and it coincides with the Parry measure precisely when $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. For exponential recovery the record is a renewal process with strictly increasing hazard; in particular it is never Bernoulli, but there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ at which it becomes exact $1$-dependent. Substituting the local KMS response turns these two thresholds into detector-defined shells. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the shells are lapse level sets, and comparing them yields an exact ratio law in which the common response amplitude cancels. We derive corresponding near-horizon asymptotics and inverse formulas for static spherical families. The full sampled record therefore carries geometric information not visible at the level of the mean transition rate alone.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates and Tolman redshift are the standard observables in discussions of Unruh and Hawking thermality \cite{Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. A sampled detector, however, produces more than a rate. Once readouts are made at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$, one obtains a stationary binary process, and its symbolic support, waiting-time law, and covariance profile become observable features of the experiment.

We study this record for a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector moving on a static worldline in a static KMS state. After each readout the detector is reset, and we compare two refractory prescriptions: ideal one-step dead time and exponential recovery at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. The point is that each model singles out an exact stochastic threshold, and after one inserts the local KMS response these thresholds define codimension-one loci in the static region.

In the ideal model the sampled record is the Markov measure on the golden-mean shift. Exact symbolic thermality, in the sense of coincidence with the Parry measure on that shift, occurs exactly at
\[
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\]
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$. For exponential recovery the visible process has full support and strictly increasing hazard, so it is neither Bernoulli nor finite-order Markov. Nevertheless there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ at which the record becomes exact $1$-dependent.

The geometric content appears once these thresholds are combined with the local KMS response. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime both become lapse level sets, hence detector-defined shells. Comparing the two shell equations removes the common response amplitude exactly and yields a shell-pair ratio law, near-horizon asymptotics in the Hartle--Hawking case, and inverse formulas in static spherical families. Without the homogeneity assumption one still obtains an exact spectral-transport identity relating the two shell points.

Work on Unruh--DeWitt detectors has concentrated mainly on response rates, thermalization, and detector-based measurement schemes \cite{Fewster2020,FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click,PranziniEtAl2024,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT}. On the stochastic side, dead-time and renewal effects are classical \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014}. The novelty here is to treat the full stationary record as the primary object and to extract from it exact thresholds that survive the passage to geometry. Additional short-time and counting-statistics consequences of the recovery model are collected in the supplementary material.

The paper is organized as follows. In \Cref{sec:records} we formulate the sampled record model and derive the two detector-side thresholds. \Cref{sec:kms-shells} lifts them to shell equations in a static KMS background. \Cref{sec:inverse} compares the resulting shell pair and derives the ratio law, the near-horizon hierarchy, and the inverse results.

\section{Sampled detector records}\label{sec:records}

We sample a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. At time $n\Delta\tau$ we read out the internal two-level system, record a click if the detector is excited, and reset it to the ground state. The observed output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ means that the detector clicks during the $n$th sampling interval. The empirical click density
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n
\]
captures only the one-point statistic. The objects relevant here are the symbolic support of the stationary law, the zero-run hazard
\[
h_k=\PP(G=k\mid G\ge k), \qquad k\ge0,
\]
where $G$ denotes the number of zeros between successive clicks, and the covariance profile
\[
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n), \qquad n\ge1.
\]
In the ideal model, exact symbolic thermality means coincidence with the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift. In the recovery model the support is the full two-shift, whose Parry measure is the fair Bernoulli law \cite{LindMarcus1995}; the exact threshold of interest is therefore exact $1$-dependence, meaning that $\sigma(A_i:i\le0)$ and $\sigma(A_j:j\ge2)$ are independent.

\subsection{Ideal one-step dead time}

In the ideal dead-time model a click suppresses the next sample. Writing
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-beta-main}
p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}, \qquad q_\beta=1-p_\beta,
\end{equation}
the sampled output is the stationary Markov chain on the golden-mean shift with transition matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-mean-main}
P_\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p_\beta & 1-p_\beta \\
 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The natural entropy benchmark on this shift is the Parry measure, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy compatible with the constraint $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ideal-main}
For the ideal dead-time detector, the sampled output realizes exact symbolic thermality if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-threshold-main}
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\end{equation}
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ is the golden ratio.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
After a click the detector is refractory for the whole next interval, so the block $11$ is forbidden. After a zero the detector is ready, and the next entry is $1$ with probability $q_\beta$. This gives \eqref{eq:golden-mean-main}. The Parry measure on the golden-mean shift has transition probabilities $P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1}$, $P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2}$, and $P(1,0)=1$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. Matching the free parameter yields $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$, hence \eqref{eq:parry-threshold-main}.
\end{proof}

The term ``exact symbolic thermality'' refers only to the stationary word law of the sampled record; it is not a statement about Gibbs equilibration of the reduced detector state.

\subsection{Exponential recovery}

We now replace the hard refractory step by exponential recovery at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$. Throughout this subsection and below we write $\Gamma\equiv\Gamma_+$ when no confusion can arise. Set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-c-main}
p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}, \qquad c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-b-main}
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds,
\qquad
b=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\end{equation}
When $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, these become
\[
a=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\qquad
b=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\]

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:recovery-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the zero-run length $G$ between successive clicks has law
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-main}
\PP(G=k)=g_k=e_D^{\top}T_0^kT_1\mathbf 1,
\qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t0-t1-main}
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 0 \\
 b & c
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
 0 & 1-p \\
 0 & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
If $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-closed-main}
g_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\end{equation}
and the click hazard after $k$ zeros is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard-main}
h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}
     {\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k},
\qquad k\ge0.
\end{equation}
The hazard is strictly increasing; in particular the visible process is not an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$.

Let $\rho=\PP(A_0=1)$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda-main}
\lambda=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}-\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}}
{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cov-main}
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},\qquad n\ge1.
\end{equation}
Consequently there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>0$ determined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-c-main}
\Delta\tau=
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma_c},
\end{equation}
such that the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$. Below $\Gamma_c$ all covariances are negative; above $\Gamma_c$ they alternate in sign.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Each click resets the hidden detector state to the refractory state $D$, so the click times are regeneration times and the visible process is renewal. This gives \eqref{eq:gap-main}. Since $T_0$ is lower triangular,
\[
T_0^k=
\begin{pmatrix}
p^k & 0\\[0.4ex]
b\,\dfrac{p^k-c^k}{p-c} & c^k
\end{pmatrix},
\]
and substitution into \eqref{eq:gap-main} gives \eqref{eq:gap-closed-main}. Summing the tail yields
\[
\sum_{j\ge k}g_j=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\]
and therefore \eqref{eq:hazard-main}. A direct subtraction gives
\[
h_{k+1}-h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(p-c)^2p^kc^k}
{(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^{k+1}-\Gamma c^{k+1})}>0.
\]
If the visible process were an $r$-step Markov chain for some finite $r$, then for every $k\ge r$ one would have
\[
h_k
=
\PP(A_{k+1}=1\mid A_0=1,\ A_1=\cdots=A_k=0)
=
\PP(A_{r+1}=1\mid A_1=\cdots=A_r=0),
\]
so $(h_k)_{k\ge r}$ would be constant, contrary to the strict increase.

For the covariance, write
\[
P=T_0+T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
p & 1-p\\
b & 1-b
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
u=T_1\mathbf 1=\binom{1-p}{a}.
\]
The nontrivial eigenvalue of $P$ is $\lambda=p-b$, which is \eqref{eq:lambda-main}. If $\pi$ denotes the stationary distribution of $P$, then $\rho=\pi u$. Since a click places the next hidden state deterministically at $D$,
\[
\PP(A_0=1,A_n=1)=\rho\,e_D^\top P^{n-1}u,\qquad n\ge1.
\]
The spectral decomposition of a two-state matrix gives
\[
P^{n-1}u=\rho\mathbf 1+\lambda^{n-1}(u-\rho\mathbf 1),
\]
and therefore
\[
e_D^\top P^{n-1}u=\rho+\lambda^{n-1}(a-\rho),
\]
which yields \eqref{eq:cov-main}. Since $\rho$ is a strict convex combination of $a$ and the ready-state click probability $1-p$, one has $a<\rho<1-p$, so $a-\rho<0$ and the sign pattern follows from the sign of $\lambda$. Finally, $\lambda=0$ is equivalent to
\[
\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}
\;=\;
\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau},
\]
or, after taking logarithms, to \eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}. The uniqueness of $\Gamma_c$ follows because the function
\[
\Gamma\longmapsto \frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
\]
is strictly decreasing on $(0,\infty)\setminus\{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\}$ and extends continuously across $\Gamma=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ with value $1/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, because
\[
\frac{d}{d\Gamma}
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
=
\;-\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}/\Gamma-1-\log(\kappa_{\mathrm r}/\Gamma)}
        {(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)^2}<0.
\]
When $\lambda=0$, the two-state matrix $P$ has rank one and therefore equals $\mathbf 1\pi$. Inserting this factor at the separating time step factorizes cylinder probabilities across time $1$, which is exactly the claimed $1$-dependence.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:no-exact-main}
No finite-recovery detector with positive parameters $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ coincides with a Bernoulli process. In particular, it does not realize the Parry measure of the full two-shift.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
A Bernoulli process is characterized by a constant hazard sequence. By \Cref{prop:recovery-main} the finite-recovery hazard is strictly increasing. The two laws therefore cannot coincide.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:edge-cases}
The formulas extend continuously to the equal-rate case $\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma$. In particular, l'H\^opital's rule gives
\[
g_k\big|_{\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma}
=
p^k\bigl[(1-p)+\Gamma\Delta\tau\bigl(k(1-p)-p\bigr)\bigr].
\]
The formulas for $a$, $b$, and $\lambda$ extend in the same way. It is also convenient to write the critical rate explicitly:
\[
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
-\frac{W_0(-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\,e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau})}
{\Delta\tau\bigl(1+W_0(-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\,e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau})\bigr)},
\]
where $W_0$ is the principal branch of the Lambert $W$-function. In particular, $\Gamma_c=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ when $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau=1$.
\end{remark}

Two auxiliary consequences of the recovery model are proved in the supplementary material. First, as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$ the probability of observing the inadmissible word $11$ in any fixed window is $O((\Delta\tau)^2)$, so the law approaches the golden-mean grammar. Second, the spectral density admits a closed form and the counting Fano factor is strictly less than $1$, so the sampled record is uniformly sub-Poissonian.

\section{Shell equations in static KMS backgrounds}\label{sec:kms-shells}

We now place the detector in a static KMS background. The thresholds from \Cref{sec:records} are detector-theoretic; after the local excitation rate is inserted they become geometric loci in the static region.

Let $N(x)$ be the lapse function of a static spacetime and assume that the field is in a global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state for Killing time. Along a static worldline at $x$, Tolman scaling gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-main}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
At weak coupling, the excitation rate of a detector with energy gap $\Omega$ has the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-rate-main}
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ denotes the local commutator spectral weight \cite{KayWald1991,BuchholzSchlemmer2007,Solveen2012,GranseePinamontiVerch2017}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:shells-general-main}
Fix detector parameters $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ in a static KMS background, and let $U$ be a region on which $x\mapsto N(x)$ and $x\mapsto\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ are $C^1$ with $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The ideal dead-time detector realizes exact symbolic thermality exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-phi-general-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\log\varphi
\right\}.
\end{equation}
\item The finite-recovery detector reaches the memory transition exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-mem-general-main}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
On $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
Whenever the corresponding defining function has nonvanishing gradient at a point of the locus, that locus is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface near the point.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The ideal threshold is $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ by \Cref{prop:ideal-main}. Substituting \eqref{eq:kms-rate-main} yields \eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main}. Likewise, \Cref{prop:recovery-main} shows that the finite-recovery record is exact $1$-dependent precisely when $\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, and substituting the KMS rate gives \eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main}. The regularity statement is then an immediate consequence of the level-set theorem.
\end{proof}

The shell equations make sense without any homogeneity assumption. Their simplest form occurs when the spectral weight is spatially constant.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:homogeneous-main}
Assume that $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ on the region under study. Then both shells are pure lapse level sets. Their associated local inverse temperatures are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}
\beta_{\varphi}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-level-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\varphi}\},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Under the homogeneity assumption, \eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main} and \eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main} reduce to scalar equations for $e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1$. Solving them gives \eqref{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}. Since $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x)$ by \eqref{eq:tolman-main}, \eqref{eq:lapse-level-main} follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:order-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and set
\[
\Gamma_{\varphi}=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}.
\]
Let $N_{\varphi}$ and $N_{\mathrm{mem}}$ denote the lapse values of $\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:order-main}
N_{\varphi}<N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_{\varphi}>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if $\Gamma_{\varphi}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{cor:homogeneous-main}, the shell ordering is equivalent to $\beta_{\varphi}<\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}$. The logarithm is increasing, hence
\[
\beta_{\varphi}<\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\frac{\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
<
\frac{1}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}.
\]
This is exactly \eqref{eq:order-main}.
\end{proof}

\section{Shell pairs and inverse problems}\label{sec:inverse}

We now compare the two shells attached to a single detector design.

\subsection{Amplitude cancellation}

Both shell equations carry the same multiplicative response amplitude. Dividing them removes that amplitude before any inversion is attempted.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:ratio-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and let a fixed detector design produce shell radii $r_{\varphi}$ and $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ for $\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in a static spherically symmetric background with lapse $N(r)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
The common multiplicative response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels exactly.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Under the homogeneity assumption,
\[
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}
{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\varphi})}-1}
=\log\varphi,
\qquad
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\]
Dividing the first identity by the second yields \eqref{eq:ratio-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:spectral-transport-main}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:shells-general-main}, let $x_{\varphi}\in\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}\in\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ be shell points for the same detector design. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-transport-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_{\varphi}}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}
Thus deviations from \eqref{eq:ratio-main} record the transport of spectral weight between the two shell points.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply \eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main} and \eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main} at $x_{\varphi}$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$, respectively, and divide the two identities.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Near-horizon asymptotics}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:horizon-main}
Assume the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:ratio-main}. Suppose in addition that the static region ends at a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-main}
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)
\end{equation}
as the proper distance $\rho\downarrow0$. Then the shell distances satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\varphi}}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
In Schwarzschild, if $A_H=16\pi M^2$ is the horizon area and $A_{\varphi}$ and $A_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are the areas of the shell spheres, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:area-ratio-main}
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left[
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\right]^2
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and $N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)$,
\[
\beta_\infty\Omega N(\rho)=2\pi\Omega\rho+O(\rho^3).
\]
Using $e^x-1=x+O(x^2)$ as $x\downarrow0$, one gets
\[
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(\rho)}-1=2\pi\Omega\rho\bigl(1+O(\rho)\bigr).
\]
Substituting the two shell points into \eqref{eq:ratio-main} and cancelling $2\pi\Omega$ gives \eqref{eq:rho-ratio-main}.

For Schwarzschild, let $r_H=2M$. If $r=r_H+\delta$, then
\[
\rho=2\sqrt{r_H\delta}+O(\delta^{3/2}),
\qquad
\delta=\frac{\rho^2}{4r_H}+O(\rho^4).
\]
Hence
\[
A-A_H
=4\pi\bigl[(r_H+\delta)^2-r_H^2\bigr]
=8\pi r_H\delta+O(\delta^2)
=2\pi\rho^2+O(\rho^4).
\]
Therefore
\[
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left(\frac{\rho_{\varphi}}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}\right)^2
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}^2+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^2)\Bigr),
\]
and \eqref{eq:area-ratio-main} follows from \eqref{eq:rho-ratio-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Fast-sampling asymptotics]\label{rem:fast-sampling}
Set $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $L=\log(1/s)$. Writing $x=\Gamma_c/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, the defining relation \eqref{eq:gamma-c-main} becomes
\[
s=\frac{\log x}{x-1}.
\]
As $s\downarrow0$, write $x=(L+u)/s$. Then the defining relation becomes
\[
L+u=\log(L+u)-\log s,
\]
hence $u=\log(L+u)=\log L+o(1)$. Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-main}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\rho_{\varphi}}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus the memory shell collapses toward the horizon parametrically faster than the ideal shell.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Static spherical inversion}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:schw-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define the detector constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-constants-main}
\beta_{\varphi}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right).
\end{equation}
If the two shells generated by the same detector design occur at distinct radii $r_{\varphi}\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in Schwarzschild, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-infty-main}
\beta_\infty^2=
\frac{\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_{\varphi}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mass-main}
M=
\frac{r_{\varphi}r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)}
{2\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{cor:homogeneous-main},
\[
\beta_\infty N(r_{\varphi})=\beta_{\varphi}(D),
\qquad
\beta_\infty N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D).
\]
For Schwarzschild, $N(r)=\sqrt{1-2M/r}$. Squaring both identities gives
\[
\beta_\infty^2\left(1-\frac{2M}{r_{\varphi}}\right)=\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2,
\qquad
\beta_\infty^2\left(1-\frac{2M}{r_{\mathrm{mem}}}\right)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2.
\]
This is a linear system for the unknowns $\beta_\infty^2$ and $2M\beta_\infty^2$, and solving it yields \eqref{eq:beta-infty-main} and \eqref{eq:mass-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:two-mode-main}
Let $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ parametrize a $C^1$ family of static spherically symmetric backgrounds, with parameter $\theta\in\Theta$ determining both the lapse $N(r;\theta)$ and the global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty(\theta)$. Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime at fixed gap $\Omega$ and fixed hardware prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.

Fix one detector hardware design $(\Omega,\lambda_c)$ and operate it in two modes
\[
D_m=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m}),
\qquad m=1,2,
\]
with observed shell radii $r_{\varphi,m}$ and $r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}$. Define the known constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-constants-main}
C_m=\frac{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}{\log\varphi},
\qquad m=1,2,
\end{equation}
and the shell-ratio functions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-functions-main}
F_m(\theta)=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1}
-C_m.
\end{equation}
If $\theta_*\in\Theta$ satisfies $F_1(\theta_*)=F_2(\theta_*)=0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main}
\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0,
\end{equation}
then the two shell pairs determine $\theta$ locally uniquely near $\theta_*$. In particular, the reconstruction is self-calibrating: no prior knowledge of $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is required.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $F=(F_1,F_2):\Theta\to\RR^2$. By construction each $F_m$ is $C^1$ in $\theta$, and \Cref{thm:ratio-main} shows that the observed shell data satisfy $F(\theta_*)=0$ at the true parameter value. The common prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ does not appear in \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-functions-main}, so the equations are already independent of that amplitude. If \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main} holds, the inverse function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002} yields an open neighbourhood $U$ of $\theta_*$ on which $F$ is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism onto its image. In particular,
\[
F^{-1}(0)\cap U=\{\theta_*\},
\]
which is the claimed local uniqueness.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:jacobian-main}
The equality $C_1=C_2$ is an obvious degeneracy: in that case the two ratio equations coincide at the level of their right-hand sides. Distinct values of $C_m$ are therefore a necessary first check, but they do not by themselves imply \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main}; the Jacobian may fail for other geometric reasons as well.
\end{remark}
