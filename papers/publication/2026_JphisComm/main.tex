\begin{abstract}
We study the stationary binary record produced by a sampled two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector along a static worldline in a static KMS spacetime. For ideal one-step dead time the sampled law is the stationary Markov measure on the golden-mean shift, and coincidence with its Parry measure occurs exactly at $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$. For exponential finite recovery the visible record is a renewal process with strictly increasing hazard; in particular it is not finite-order Markov and cannot coincide with any Bernoulli law on the full shift. Nevertheless exact $1$-dependence occurs at a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$. Substituting the local KMS response turns these thresholds into detector-defined shells. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the shells are lapse level sets and satisfy an exact shell-pair ratio law in which the common response amplitude cancels. We also derive a near-horizon hierarchy and inverse formulas in static spherical geometries. Thus the full stationary click record contains geometric information beyond the mean transition rate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Transition rates, detailed balance, and Tolman redshift are the standard observables in discussions of Unruh and Hawking thermality \cite{Unruh1976,DeWitt1979,BirDav1982,Takagi1986,CrispinoHiguchiMatsas2008,LoukoSatz2006,LoukoSatz2008}. A sampled detector, however, does not produce a rate alone. Once the detector is read at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$, one obtains a binary click record with its own symbolic support, waiting-time law, and covariance profile. These are shaped by dead time and recovery, and there is no reason a priori for them to collapse to the mean excitation rate.

The purpose of this paper is to show that, in a static KMS background, two record-level thresholds define codimension-one loci selected by the detector design. We work with a sampled two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector along a stationary worldline, under weak coupling and discrete readout, and we compare two refractory prescriptions: ideal one-step dead time and exponential finite recovery.

There is by now extensive work on repeated measurements of Unruh--DeWitt detectors, detector-based measurement schemes in quantum field theory, detector thermalization, and detector probes of background geometry \cite{Fewster2020,PranziniEtAl2024,PranziniKeskiVakkuri2025AQFT,PoloGomezEtAl2022,TjoaEtAl2023click,PercheLocalized2023,PercheMartinMartinez2022,FewsterJuarezLouko2016,GarayMartinMartinezRamon2016,JuarezAubryMoustos2022,PercheTherm2023,NgMannMartinMartinez2018,NgMannMartinMartinezGeon,TjoaMann2020}. On the stochastic side, dead-time effects, renewal laws, spectral densities, and inverse counting statistics are classical in radiation detection and transport theory \cite{Muller1973,CantorTeich1975,Brandes2008,BrudererEtAl2014}. What appears to be absent from both strands is a treatment of sampled stationary detector records as symbolic-dynamical objects whose exact stochastic thresholds define geometric hypersurfaces. The novelty of the present paper lies precisely in this passage from rate-based detector thermometry to exact selectors extracted from the full stationary word law.

For ideal dead time the record is the Markov law on the golden-mean shift. Exact symbolic thermality, in the sense that the record coincides with the Parry measure on that shift, occurs if and only if
\[
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\]
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$. This gives the first threshold. For finite recovery the visible record has full support but strictly increasing hazard, and therefore cannot coincide with any Bernoulli process, in particular not with the Parry measure of the full two-shift \cite{LindMarcus1995}. On the other hand, there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ at which the record becomes exact $1$-dependent; equivalently, the nontrivial eigenvalue of the hidden two-state kernel vanishes. This gives a second threshold of a different nature.

The geometric content appears once the local KMS response is substituted into these thresholds. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime both conditions reduce to lapse level sets, hence to detector-defined shells in the static region. Comparing the two shell equations eliminates the unknown common response amplitude exactly, and from this one obtains a shell-pair ratio law, a near-horizon hierarchy in the Hartle--Hawking case, and inverse formulas in static spherical families. Outside the homogeneous regime the same comparison yields an exact spectral-transport identity.

What emerges is not a detector-independent invariant of the background. The shell equations depend on the detector gap, the sampling step, the recovery law, and the response model. The point is rather that, within this class of models, the full stationary click record singles out two exact thresholds invisible to a rate-only description. In that sense the detector acts as a stochastic-record probe and not merely as a rate meter.

The paper is organized as follows. In \Cref{sec:operational,sec:records} we define the record-level observables and derive the ideal and finite-recovery laws together with their thresholds and basic signatures. \Cref{sec:kms-shells} lifts these thresholds to shell equations in static KMS backgrounds. \Cref{sec:inverse} then studies shell pairs, their ratio law, and the corresponding inverse statements.

\section{Record-level observables}\label{sec:operational}

We consider a two-level Unruh--DeWitt detector sampled at proper-time intervals of length $\Delta\tau$ along a stationary worldline. The observed output is a stationary binary process
\[
A_0,A_1,A_2,\dots \in \{0,1\},
\]
where $A_n=1$ means that the detector clicks during the $n$th sampling interval. The empirical click density
\[
\widehat\rho_N=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n
\]
captures only the one-point statistic. The full record carries more structure.

Three record-level quantities will be used throughout. First, the symbolic support of the stationary law specifies which finite words may occur. Second, if a click is followed by $G$ zeros before the next click, then $G$ is the \emph{zero-run length}; its hazard is the sequence
\[
h_k=\PP(G=k\mid G\ge k), \qquad k\ge0.
\]
Third, we consider the covariance profile
\[
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n), \qquad n\ge1.
\]

The two threshold notions are as follows. In the ideal model, \emph{exact symbolic thermality} means that the stationary record agrees with the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift. In the finite-recovery model the support is the full two-shift, whose Parry measure is the fair Bernoulli law \cite{LindMarcus1995}; \Cref{cor:no-exact-main} shows that this maximal-entropy benchmark is never attained. The exact threshold of interest there is therefore \emph{exact $1$-dependence}, meaning that $\sigma(A_i:i\le0)$ and $\sigma(A_j:j\ge2)$ are independent, or equivalently that blocks separated by at least one intermediate time step are independent. These are the thresholds that will later be lifted to shell equations.

\section{Detector laws and record thresholds}\label{sec:records}

\subsection{Measurement protocol and click model}

At each time step $n\Delta\tau$ one reads out the detector's internal two-level system, records a click if the detector is excited, and resets it to the ground state. Between two successive readouts the field drives excitation at the Unruh--DeWitt rate $\Gamma_+$, while the refractory state, when present, recovers at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. This gives a hidden two-state dynamics whose output is the visible binary record. The point of the paper is that the stationary law of that record contains more information than the mean excitation rate.

\subsection{Ideal one-step dead time}

In the ideal dead-time model a click suppresses the next sample. Writing
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-beta-main}
p_\beta=e^{-\Gamma_+\Delta\tau}, \qquad q_\beta=1-p_\beta,
\end{equation}
the sampled output is the stationary Markov chain on the golden-mean shift with transition matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:golden-mean-main}
P_\beta=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p_\beta & 1-p_\beta \\
 1 & 0
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The natural entropy benchmark on this shift is the Parry measure, namely the unique measure of maximal entropy compatible with the constraint $11\notin\operatorname{supp}\mu$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:ideal-main}
For the ideal dead-time detector, the sampled output realizes exact symbolic thermality if and only if
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parry-threshold-main}
p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi,
\end{equation}
where $\varphi=(1+\sqrt5)/2$ is the golden ratio.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
After a click the detector is refractory for the whole next interval, so the block $11$ is forbidden. After a zero the detector is ready, and the next entry is $1$ with probability $q_\beta=1-p_\beta$. This yields \eqref{eq:golden-mean-main}. The Parry measure on the golden-mean shift is characterized by the transition probabilities $P(0,0)=\varphi^{-1}$, $P(0,1)=\varphi^{-2}$, and $P(1,0)=1$ \cite{Parry1964,LindMarcus1995}. Matching the free parameter gives $p_\beta=\varphi^{-1}$, which is equivalent to \eqref{eq:parry-threshold-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[On the thermality benchmark]\label{rem:thermality-notions}
The threshold in \Cref{prop:ideal-main} is not a statement about detailed balance or about Gibbs thermalization of the reduced detector state. It is a statement about the full stationary word distribution of the sampled record. We retain the term ``exact symbolic thermality'' because the Parry measure is the entropy-maximizing law within the dead-time grammar class, but the content is entirely record-theoretic.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Finite recovery, renewal structure, and exact \texorpdfstring{$1$}{1}-dependence}

We now replace the hard refractory step by exponential recovery at rate $\kappa_{\mathrm r}>0$. Throughout this subsection and below we write $\Gamma\equiv\Gamma_+$ when no confusion is possible. Set
\begin{equation}\label{eq:p-c-main}
p=e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}, \qquad c=e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-b-main}
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds,
\qquad
b=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\,ds.
\end{equation}
When $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, these become
\[
a=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)-\Gamma(1-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\qquad
b=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(p-c)}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\]

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:recovery-main}
For the finite-recovery detector, the zero-run length $G$ between successive clicks has law
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-main}
\PP(G=k)=g_k=e_D^{\top}T_0^kT_1\mathbf 1,
\qquad k\ge0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:t0-t1-main}
T_0=
\begin{pmatrix}
 p & 0 \\
 b & c
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
 0 & 1-p \\
 0 & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
If $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-closed-main}
g_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\end{equation}
and the click hazard after $k$ zeros is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hazard-main}
h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}(1-p)p^k-\Gamma(1-c)c^k}
     {\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k},
\qquad k\ge0.
\end{equation}
The hazard is strictly increasing, so the visible process is not an $r$-step Markov chain for any finite $r$.

Let $\rho=\PP(A_0=1)$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lambda-main}
\lambda=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}-\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau}}
{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cov-main}
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},\qquad n\ge1.
\end{equation}
Consequently there is a unique critical rate $\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)>0$ determined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-c-main}
\Delta\tau=
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma_c}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma_c},
\end{equation}
such that the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$. Below $\Gamma_c$ all covariances are negative; above $\Gamma_c$ they alternate in sign.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Every observed click resets the hidden detector state to the refractory state $D$, independently of the earlier past. The click times are therefore regeneration times, so the visible process is renewal and \eqref{eq:gap-main} follows immediately. Since $T_0$ is lower triangular,
\[
T_0^k=
\begin{pmatrix}
p^k & 0\\[0.4ex]
b\,\dfrac{p^k-c^k}{p-c} & c^k
\end{pmatrix},
\]
and substitution into \eqref{eq:gap-main} gives \eqref{eq:gap-closed-main}. Summing the geometric tail yields
\[
\sum_{j\ge k}g_j=\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma},
\]
which implies \eqref{eq:hazard-main}. A direct subtraction then gives
\[
h_{k+1}-h_k=
\frac{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(p-c)^2p^kc^k}
{(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^k-\Gamma c^k)(\kappa_{\mathrm r}p^{k+1}-\Gamma c^{k+1})}>0.
\]
If the visible process were an $r$-step Markov chain for some finite $r$, then for every $k\ge r$ one would have
\[
h_k
=
\PP(A_{k+1}=1\mid A_0=1,\ A_1=\cdots=A_k=0)
=
\PP(A_{r+1}=1\mid A_1=\cdots=A_r=0),
\]
where the second equality uses the $r$-step Markov property and stationarity. Thus $(h_k)_{k\ge r}$ would be constant, contradicting the strict increase proved above. Hence no finite-order Markov reduction is possible.

For the covariance, write
\[
P=T_0+T_1=
\begin{pmatrix}
p & 1-p\\
b & 1-b
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
u=T_1\mathbf 1=\binom{1-p}{a}.
\]
The nontrivial eigenvalue of $P$ is $\lambda=p-b$, which is exactly \eqref{eq:lambda-main}. If $\pi$ is the stationary distribution of $P$, then $\rho=\pi u$. Since a click places the next hidden state deterministically at $D$,
\[
\PP(A_0=1,A_n=1)=\rho\,e_D^\top P^{n-1}u,\qquad n\ge1.
\]
The spectral decomposition of a two-state matrix gives
\[
P^{n-1}u=\rho\mathbf 1+\lambda^{n-1}(u-\rho\mathbf 1),
\]
hence
\[
e_D^\top P^{n-1}u=\rho+\lambda^{n-1}(a-\rho),
\]
which yields \eqref{eq:cov-main}. Moreover, $\rho$ is a strict convex combination of $a$ and the ready-state click probability $1-p$, so $a<\rho<1-p$ and therefore $a-\rho<0$. This, together with the sign of $\lambda$, gives the covariance sign pattern stated above. Finally, $\lambda=0$ is equivalent to
\[
\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau}
\;=\;
\Gamma e^{-\Gamma\Delta\tau},
\]
or, after taking logarithms, to \eqref{eq:gamma-c-main}. The uniqueness of $\Gamma_c$ follows because the function
\[
\Gamma\longmapsto \frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
\]
is strictly decreasing on $(0,\infty)\setminus\{\kappa_{\mathrm r}\}$ and extends continuously across $\Gamma=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ with value $1/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. Indeed, with $y=\kappa_{\mathrm r}/\Gamma$,
\[
\frac{d}{d\Gamma}
\frac{\log\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\log\Gamma}{\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma}
=
-\frac{y-1-\log y}{(\kappa_{\mathrm r}-\Gamma)^2}<0
\]
for $\Gamma\neq\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, because $y-1-\log y>0$ whenever $y\neq1$. When $\lambda=0$, the two-state transition matrix $P$ has rank one and therefore equals $\mathbf 1\pi$, where $\pi$ is its stationary distribution. In the hidden-state matrix representation of the output process, inserting this factor at the separating time step factorizes any cylinder probability across time $1$, so cylinder events depending on $\{A_i:i\le0\}$ and on $\{A_j:j\ge2\}$ are independent. Thus the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent at $\Gamma=\Gamma_c$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:no-exact-main}
No finite-recovery detector with positive parameters $(\Gamma,\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$ coincides with a Bernoulli process. In particular, it does not realize the Parry measure of the full two-shift.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
A Bernoulli process is characterized by a constant hazard sequence. By \Cref{prop:recovery-main} the finite-recovery hazard is strictly increasing. The two laws therefore cannot coincide.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Equal-rate limit and Lambert \(W\) expression for \(\Gamma_c\)]\label{rem:edge-cases}
\Cref{prop:recovery-main} was stated for $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\neq\Gamma$. In the equal-rate limit $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\to\Gamma$, \eqref{eq:gap-closed-main} is a $0/0$ form, and l'H\^opital's rule gives
\[
g_k\big|_{\kappa_{\mathrm r}=\Gamma}
=
p^k\bigl[(1-p)+\Gamma\Delta\tau\bigl(k(1-p)-p\bigr)\bigr].
\]
The formulas for $a$, $b$, and $\lambda$ extend similarly, and the strict monotonicity of $h_k$ follows by continuity.

The critical rate \eqref{eq:gamma-c-main} may be written explicitly. Set $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $x=\Gamma_c/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$. Then $s=(\log x)/(x-1)$, or equivalently $e^{sx}(1-sx)=e^s$. Hence
\[
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
-\frac{W_0(-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\,e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau})}
{\Delta\tau\bigl(1+W_0(-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau\,e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau})\bigr)},
\]
with the principal branch $W_0$ on $(-e^{-1},0)$. In particular, $\Gamma_c=\kappa_{\mathrm r}$ when $\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau=1$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Soft approach to the ideal grammar}

The obstruction in \Cref{cor:no-exact-main} is exact, but the finite-recovery law still approaches the ideal grammar in the small-$\Delta\tau$ regime.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:soft-main}
In the finite-recovery model,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:soft-main}
\PP(A_{n+1}=1\mid A_n=1)=a
=\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2+O((\Delta\tau)^3)
\qquad (\Delta\tau\downarrow0).
\end{equation}
For a fixed window length $m\ge2$, let
\[
V_m=\{(a_0,\dots,a_{m-1})\in\{0,1\}^m:\exists\,k\le m-2,\ a_ka_{k+1}=11\}.
\]
Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:window-main}
\PP\!\bigl((A_0,\dots,A_{m-1})\in V_m\bigr)
\le
\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2\,(m-1)
+O\!\bigl(m(\Delta\tau)^3\bigr).
\end{equation}
Thus, for every fixed $m$, the finite-recovery record concentrates on the golden-mean admissible set as $\Delta\tau\downarrow0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
After a click the next interval begins in the refractory state, so a second consecutive click requires recovery at some time $s\in[0,\Delta\tau]$ followed by excitation during the remaining time. Hence
\[
a=\int_0^{\Delta\tau}\kappa_{\mathrm r}e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}\bigl(1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}\bigr)\,ds.
\]
Using
\[
e^{-\kappa_{\mathrm r}s}=1+O(\Delta\tau),
\qquad
1-e^{-\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)}=\Gamma(\Delta\tau-s)+O((\Delta\tau)^2),
\]
uniformly in $s$, one obtains
\[
a=
\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma\int_0^{\Delta\tau}(\Delta\tau-s)\,ds
+O((\Delta\tau)^3)
=
\frac12\,\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Gamma(\Delta\tau)^2+O((\Delta\tau)^3),
\]
which is \eqref{eq:soft-main}.

For the window estimate, use the union bound:
\[
\PP(V_m)\le \sum_{k=0}^{m-2}\PP(A_k=1,A_{k+1}=1).
\]
By stationarity,
\[
\PP(A_k=1,A_{k+1}=1)=\PP(A_0=1)\PP(A_1=1\mid A_0=1)\le a.
\]
Substituting the expansion of $a$ yields \eqref{eq:window-main}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Spectral density and universal antibunching}

The finite-recovery renewal law has a second structural consequence. Define
\[
f(\theta)=\rho(1-\rho)+2\sum_{n\ge1}\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)\cos(n\theta),
\qquad \theta\in[-\pi,\pi].
\]
The covariance formula in \Cref{prop:recovery-main} allows one to sum the series explicitly.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:spectrum-main}
For the stationary finite-recovery record,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectrum-main}
f(\theta)=
\rho(1-\rho)
+
2\rho(a-\rho)\,
\frac{\cos\theta-\lambda}{1-2\lambda\cos\theta+\lambda^2},
\end{equation}
and $f$ is strictly increasing on $[0,\pi]$. If
\[
N_N=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}A_n,
\]
then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fano-main}
\lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{\Var(N_N)}{\EE N_N}
=
1-\rho+\frac{2(a-\rho)}{1-\lambda}
<1.
\end{equation}
Hence the finite-recovery record is strictly sub-Poissonian throughout the parameter range.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \eqref{eq:cov-main},
\[
\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n)=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1},\qquad n\ge1.
\]
Therefore
\[
f(\theta)=
\rho(1-\rho)+2\rho(a-\rho)\sum_{n\ge1}\lambda^{n-1}\cos(n\theta).
\]
Since $|\lambda|<1$,
\[
\sum_{n\ge1}\lambda^{n-1}e^{in\theta}
=
\frac{e^{i\theta}}{1-\lambda e^{i\theta}},
\]
and taking real parts gives \eqref{eq:spectrum-main}. Writing $x=\cos\theta$,
\[
R_\lambda(x)=\frac{x-\lambda}{1-2\lambda x+\lambda^2}
\]
satisfies
\[
R_\lambda'(x)=\frac{1-\lambda^2}{(1-2\lambda x+\lambda^2)^2}>0.
\]
Since $a-\rho<0$ and $\cos\theta$ decreases on $[0,\pi]$, the function $f$ is strictly increasing on that interval.

For the counting variance, stationarity gives
\[
\Var(N_N)=N\gamma_0+2\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}(N-n)\gamma_n,
\qquad
\gamma_n=\operatorname{Cov}(A_0,A_n).
\]
Using $\gamma_0=\rho(1-\rho)$ and $\gamma_n=\rho(a-\rho)\lambda^{n-1}$,
\[
\Var(N_N)=
N\rho(1-\rho)+2\rho(a-\rho)\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}(N-n)\lambda^{n-1}.
\]
The geometric sum evaluates to
\[
\sum_{n=1}^{N-1}(N-n)\lambda^{n-1}
=
\frac{N(1-\lambda)-1+\lambda^N}{(1-\lambda)^2}.
\]
Since $\EE N_N=N\rho$, division by $N\rho$ and passage to the limit yield \eqref{eq:fano-main}. The strict inequality follows from $a-\rho<0$ and $1-\lambda>0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Finite samples]\label{rem:finite-sample}
The shell conditions are defined in terms of stationary observables, but they are still accessible from finite data. Given a record $(A_0,\dots,A_{N-1})$, one may estimate the hazard sequence, low-lag covariances, and block-counting Fano factor empirically. Since the finite-recovery output is stationary, ergodic, and regenerative at click times, the corresponding estimators converge almost surely for fixed lags and window lengths. In particular, the memory threshold may be detected through the sign change of the lag-$2$ covariance, whereas the ideal threshold is tested by comparing the empirical transition probability $P(0,0)$ with the Parry value $\varphi^{-1}$.
\end{remark}

\section{Shell equations in static KMS backgrounds}\label{sec:kms-shells}

We now embed the detector in a static KMS background. The record thresholds just obtained are purely detector-theoretic. Once the local excitation rate is inserted, they become geometric loci in the static region.

Let $N(x)$ be the lapse function of a static spacetime and assume that the field is in a global $\beta_\infty$-KMS state for Killing time. Along a static worldline at $x$, Tolman scaling gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tolman-main}
\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x).
\end{equation}
At weak coupling, the excitation rate of a detector with energy gap $\Omega$ has the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kms-rate-main}
\Gamma_+(x,\Omega)=
\lambda_c^2\,
\frac{\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$ denotes the local commutator spectral weight \cite{KayWald1991,BuchholzSchlemmer2007,Solveen2012,GranseePinamontiVerch2017}.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:shells-general-main}
Fix a detector design $(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau,\kappa_{\mathrm r})$ in a static KMS background on a region $U$ where $x\mapsto N(x)$ and $x\mapsto\mathcal S_x(\Omega)$ are $C^1$ and $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)>0$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)},leftmargin=2.2em,itemsep=0.25em]
\item The ideal dead-time detector realizes exact symbolic thermality exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-phi-general-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}=\log\varphi
\right\}.
\end{equation}
\item The finite-recovery detector reaches the memory transition exactly on the locus
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shell-mem-general-main}
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\left\{x:
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S_x(\Omega)}{e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
On $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$, the sampled record is exact $1$-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
Whenever the corresponding defining function has nonvanishing gradient at a point of the locus, that locus is a smooth codimension-one hypersurface near the point.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The ideal threshold is $\Gamma_+\Delta\tau=\log\varphi$ by \Cref{prop:ideal-main}. Substituting \eqref{eq:kms-rate-main} yields \eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main}. Likewise, \Cref{prop:recovery-main} shows that the finite-recovery record is exact $1$-dependent precisely when $\Gamma_+=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$, and substituting the KMS rate gives \eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main}. The regularity statement is then an immediate consequence of the level-set theorem.
\end{proof}

The shell equations exist without any homogeneity assumption. A particularly transparent form appears when the spectral weight is spatially constant.

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:homogeneous-main}
Assume that $\mathcal S_x(\Omega)\equiv\mathcal S(\Omega)$ on the region under study. Then both shells are pure lapse level sets. Their associated local inverse temperatures are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}
\beta_{\varphi}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right),
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lapse-level-main}
\Sigma_{\varphi}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\varphi}\},
\qquad
\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}=\{x:\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}\}.
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Under the homogeneity assumption, \eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main} and \eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main} reduce to scalar equations for $e^{\beta_\infty N(x)\Omega}-1$. Solving them gives \eqref{eq:betaphi-betamem-main}. Since $\beta_{\mathrm{loc}}(x)=\beta_\infty N(x)$ by \eqref{eq:tolman-main}, \eqref{eq:lapse-level-main} follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:order-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and set
\[
\Gamma_{\varphi}=\frac{\log\varphi}{\Delta\tau}.
\]
Let $N_{\varphi}$ and $N_{\mathrm{mem}}$ denote the lapse values of $\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:order-main}
N_{\varphi}<N_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\Gamma_{\varphi}>\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau),
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if $\Gamma_{\varphi}=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{cor:homogeneous-main}, the shell ordering is equivalent to $\beta_{\varphi}<\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}$. The logarithm is increasing, hence
\[
\beta_{\varphi}<\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}
\iff
\frac{\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}
<
\frac{1}{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}.
\]
This is exactly \eqref{eq:order-main}.
\end{proof}

\section{Shell pairs and inverse statements}\label{sec:inverse}

The preceding section associates two shells to one detector design. We now compare them.

\subsection{Common-amplitude cancellation and spectral transport}

The point of the shell pair is that both shell equations carry the same multiplicative response amplitude. Dividing them removes that amplitude before any inversion is attempted.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:ratio-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and let a fixed detector design generate an exact-clock shell at $r_{\varphi}$ and a memory shell at $r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in a static spherically symmetric background with lapse $N(r)$. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}.
\end{equation}
The common multiplicative response amplitude $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ cancels exactly.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Under the homogeneity assumption,
\[
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}
{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\varphi})}-1}
=\log\varphi,
\qquad
\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau).
\]
Dividing the first identity by the second yields \eqref{eq:ratio-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:spectral-transport-main}
Under the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:shells-general-main}, let $x_{\varphi}\in\Sigma_{\varphi}$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}\in\Sigma_{\mathrm{mem}}$ be shell points for the same detector design. Then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spectral-transport-main}
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\varphi})}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(x_{\mathrm{mem}})}-1}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\frac{\mathcal S_{x_{\varphi}}(\Omega)}{\mathcal S_{x_{\mathrm{mem}}}(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}
Thus deviations from \eqref{eq:ratio-main} measure the transport of spectral weight between the two shell points.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply \eqref{eq:shell-phi-general-main} and \eqref{eq:shell-mem-general-main} at $x_{\varphi}$ and $x_{\mathrm{mem}}$, respectively, and divide the two identities.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Near-horizon universal hierarchy}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:horizon-main}
Assume the hypotheses of \Cref{thm:ratio-main}. Suppose in addition that the static region ends at a nonextremal Hartle--Hawking horizon, so that $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horizon-main}
N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)
\end{equation}
as the proper distance $\rho\downarrow0$. Then the shell distances satisfy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\varphi}}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}
=
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
In Schwarzschild, if $A_H=16\pi M^2$ is the horizon area and $A_{\varphi}$ and $A_{\mathrm{mem}}$ are the areas of the shell spheres, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:area-ratio-main}
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left[
\frac{\Delta\tau\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}{\log\varphi}
\right]^2
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}})\Bigr).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $\beta_\infty=2\pi/\kappa_{\mathrm H}$ and $N(\rho)=\kappa_{\mathrm H}\rho+O(\rho^3)$,
\[
\beta_\infty\Omega N(\rho)=2\pi\Omega\rho+O(\rho^3).
\]
Using $e^x-1=x+O(x^2)$ as $x\downarrow0$, one gets
\[
e^{\beta_\infty\Omega N(\rho)}-1=2\pi\Omega\rho\bigl(1+O(\rho)\bigr).
\]
Substituting the two shell points into \eqref{eq:ratio-main} and cancelling $2\pi\Omega$ gives \eqref{eq:rho-ratio-main}.

For Schwarzschild, let $r_H=2M$. If $r=r_H+\delta$, then
\[
\rho=2\sqrt{r_H\delta}+O(\delta^{3/2}),
\qquad
\delta=\frac{\rho^2}{4r_H}+O(\rho^4).
\]
Hence
\[
A-A_H
=4\pi\bigl[(r_H+\delta)^2-r_H^2\bigr]
=8\pi r_H\delta+O(\delta^2)
=2\pi\rho^2+O(\rho^4).
\]
Therefore
\[
\frac{A_{\varphi}-A_H}{A_{\mathrm{mem}}-A_H}
=
\left(\frac{\rho_{\varphi}}{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}\right)^2
\Bigl(1+O(\rho_{\varphi}^2+\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}^2)\Bigr),
\]
and \eqref{eq:area-ratio-main} follows from \eqref{eq:rho-ratio-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Fast sampling]\label{rem:fast-sampling}
Set $s=\kappa_{\mathrm r}\Delta\tau$ and $L=\log(1/s)$. Writing $x=\Gamma_c/\kappa_{\mathrm r}$, the defining relation \eqref{eq:gamma-c-main} becomes
\[
s=\frac{\log x}{x-1}.
\]
As $s\downarrow0$, write $x=(L+u)/s$. Then the defining relation becomes
\[
L+u=\log(L+u)-\log s,
\]
hence $u=\log(L+u)=\log L+o(1)$. Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-sampling-main}
\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)
=
\frac{L+\log L+o(1)}{\Delta\tau},
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fast-ratio-main}
\frac{\rho_{\mathrm{mem}}}{\rho_{\varphi}}
\sim
\frac{\log\varphi}{L+\log L}
\longrightarrow0.
\end{equation}
Thus the memory shell collapses toward the horizon parametrically faster than the exact-clock shell.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Static spherical inversion}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:schw-main}
Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime and define the detector constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-constants-main}
\beta_{\varphi}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)\Delta\tau}{\log\varphi}\right),
\qquad
\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)=
\frac{1}{\Omega}
\log\!\left(1+\frac{\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)}
{\Gamma_c(\kappa_{\mathrm r},\Delta\tau)}\right).
\end{equation}
If the two shells generated by the same detector design occur at distinct radii $r_{\varphi}\neq r_{\mathrm{mem}}$ in Schwarzschild, then
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta-infty-main}
\beta_\infty^2=
\frac{\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}}
{r_{\varphi}-r_{\mathrm{mem}}},
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mass-main}
M=
\frac{r_{\varphi}r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2\bigr)}
{2\bigl(\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2r_{\varphi}-\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2r_{\mathrm{mem}}\bigr)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{cor:homogeneous-main},
\[
\beta_\infty N(r_{\varphi})=\beta_{\varphi}(D),
\qquad
\beta_\infty N(r_{\mathrm{mem}})=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D).
\]
For Schwarzschild, $N(r)=\sqrt{1-2M/r}$. Squaring both identities gives
\[
\beta_\infty^2\left(1-\frac{2M}{r_{\varphi}}\right)=\beta_{\varphi}(D)^2,
\qquad
\beta_\infty^2\left(1-\frac{2M}{r_{\mathrm{mem}}}\right)=\beta_{\mathrm{mem}}(D)^2.
\]
This is a linear system for the unknowns $\beta_\infty^2$ and $2M\beta_\infty^2$, and solving it yields \eqref{eq:beta-infty-main} and \eqref{eq:mass-main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:two-mode-main}
Let $\Theta\subset\RR^2$ parametrize a $C^1$ family of static spherically symmetric backgrounds, with parameter $\theta\in\Theta$ determining both the lapse $N(r;\theta)$ and the global inverse temperature $\beta_\infty(\theta)$. Assume the homogeneous spectral-weight regime at fixed gap $\Omega$ and fixed hardware prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$.

Fix one detector hardware design $(\Omega,\lambda_c)$ and operate it in two modes
\[
D_m=(\Omega,\lambda_c,\Delta\tau_m,\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m}),
\qquad m=1,2,
\]
with observed shell radii $r_{\varphi,m}$ and $r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m}$. Define the known constants
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-constants-main}
C_m=\frac{\Delta\tau_m\,\Gamma_c(\kappa_{{\mathrm r},m},\Delta\tau_m)}{\log\varphi},
\qquad m=1,2,
\end{equation}
and the shell-ratio functions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-functions-main}
F_m(\theta)=
\frac{e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{\varphi,m};\theta)}-1}
     {e^{\beta_\infty(\theta)\Omega N(r_{{\mathrm{mem}},m};\theta)}-1}
-C_m.
\end{equation}
If $\theta_*\in\Theta$ satisfies $F_1(\theta_*)=F_2(\theta_*)=0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main}
\det D(F_1,F_2)(\theta_*)\neq0,
\end{equation}
then the two shell pairs determine $\theta$ locally uniquely near $\theta_*$. In particular, the reconstruction is self-calibrating: no prior knowledge of $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ is required.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $F=(F_1,F_2):\Theta\to\RR^2$. By construction each $F_m$ is $C^1$ in $\theta$, and \Cref{thm:ratio-main} shows that the observed shell data satisfy $F(\theta_*)=0$ at the true parameter value. The common prefactor $\lambda_c^2\mathcal S(\Omega)$ does not appear in \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-functions-main}, so the equations are already independent of that amplitude. If \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main} holds, the inverse function theorem \cite{KrantzParks2002} yields an open neighbourhood $U$ of $\theta_*$ on which $F$ is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism onto its image. In particular,
\[
F^{-1}(0)\cap U=\{\theta_*\},
\]
which is the claimed local uniqueness.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:jacobian-main}
The equality $C_1=C_2$ is an obvious degeneracy: in that case the two ratio equations coincide at the level of their right-hand sides. Distinct values of $C_m$ are therefore a necessary first check, but they do not by themselves imply \eqref{eq:self-calibrating-jacobian-main}; the Jacobian may fail for other geometric reasons as well.
\end{remark}

\section{Concluding remarks}\label{sec:discussion}

The ideal dead-time and finite-recovery models lead to two distinct record-level thresholds. In the first case the threshold is entropic: it is the point at which the sampled law agrees with the Parry measure on the golden-mean shift. In the second case the threshold is probabilistic: it is the unique rate at which the renewal law becomes exact $1$-dependent. The soft-dead-time limit and the antibunching statement show that these thresholds are accompanied by quantitative signatures in the record itself.

Once the detector is placed in a static KMS background, the two thresholds lift to shell equations. In the homogeneous spectral-weight regime the shell pair obeys an exact ratio law in which the common response amplitude cancels, and this in turn leads to the near-horizon hierarchy and the inverse formulas above. Outside that regime the correction is still explicit, namely the ratio of spectral weights at the two shell points. The results should therefore be read as exact statements within the stated detector-response model, not as detector-independent invariants of the background.
