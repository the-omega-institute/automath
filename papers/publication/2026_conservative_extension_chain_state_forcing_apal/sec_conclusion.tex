\section{Concluding Remarks}\label{sec:conclusion}

The paper separates three levels of the local-to-global problem. At the logical level, \Cref{thm:pointwise-irreducibility} isolates a concrete but deliberately limited gap between pointwise semantics and compatible local sectionability inside the one-variable parameter-free automorphism-invariant lower fragment fixed in the paper. At the geometric level, visible value classes are organized by the sheaf of connected components, and gluing failure is carried by the component gerbes attached to visible branches once a gluing-sensitive realization lift has been fixed. At the homological level, a finite abelian obstruction class yields the subgroup $K_\omega=\operatorname{Im}(\mathrm{ev}_\omega)$ and the quotient $A_{\mathrm{vis}}^\omega=A/K_\omega$, thereby separating the $H_2$-visible part of the obstruction from the pure $\operatorname{Ext}$ residual within the homology-equivalent comparison class of presentations used in the paper.

Within those explicit hypotheses, the paper refines the usual no-global-section picture in three directions. In the unique-branch case, the comparison with the support-presheaf approach to contextuality shows that a nontrivial obstruction is invisible to every admissible character precisely when the surviving class lies in the $\operatorname{Ext}$-summand of the universal coefficient sequence. In the multibranch case, the branch-sensitive and branch-uniform quotients are related by an explicit short exact sequence measuring the loss of information when the branch label is forgotten. Under the additional hypothesis $H_1(N(\mathcal U),\mathbb Z)=0$, the mixed realization theorem shows that, within the fixed connected finite-nerve framework of the paper, these hidden subgroups and aggregate quotients can be produced by explicit local-object and prestack data, with the neutral branches characterized exactly by the zero homomorphisms. When $H_2(N(\mathcal U),\mathbb Z)\cong \mathbb Z^\beta$, the realizable hidden subgroups are precisely the subgroups of the band generated by at most $\beta$ elements, so maximal collapse is possible exactly at the ranks permitted by the second homology.

The bouquet good-cover theorem isolates one natural class in which the cofinal acyclic-cover hypothesis is automatic rather than imposed abstractly. For the good-cover family of a bouquet $\bigvee_{j=1}^{\beta}S^2$, the pure two-branch resolution kernels are exactly the finite abelian groups $G$ with $d(G)\le 2\beta$ that are not cyclic $p$-groups. This should be read as an internal classification result for that fixed topological family, not as a substitute for a general existence theorem for gluing-sensitive lifts.

The appendices record two supplementary directions that are not used in the core proofs. One is a refinement-dynamic extension of the semantics together with the associated finite-state complexity bounds. The other is a GHZ/Mermin amplitude lift showing concretely where additional realization data enter the gerbe layer. No general existence theorem for those data is claimed here.
