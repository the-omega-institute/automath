\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

Formal semantics is usually presented as truth at a fully specified point of evaluation. That picture is exact when the relevant assignment or world has already been fixed. It is too coarse, however, when information is only partial. One may have ruled out some realizations without having selected a unique one; one may have an admissible reference before a global object has formed; or one may have compatible local data that fail to glue. In such situations the natural semantic notion is not pointwise truth but forcing at an information state.

Several familiar traditions isolate parts of this picture. Kripke and Beth semantics replace absolute truth by forcing at stages of information and impose persistence under refinement \cite{Beth1956,Kripke1965,TroelstraVanDalen1988}. Sheaf-theoretic semantics separates local from global existence and treats gluing failure as a structural defect rather than as a mere lack of value \cite{MacLaneMoerdijk1994}. Related local-to-global obstruction viewpoints also appear in the sheaf-theoretic analysis of contextuality and its cohomological refinements \cite{AbramskyBrandenburger2011,Caru2017}. Dynamic semantics explains later decision by elimination of possibilities rather than by revision of truth conditions. The present paper combines these themes in order to address a more specific question: once an admitted reference carries a finite abelian gluing obstruction, what part of that obstruction remains semantically visible without enlarging the state space, and how much additional information is required to recover the hidden part?

The scope of the paper is deliberately narrow. We study a self-contained four-layer fragment of state-forcing semantics, isolating the components needed for the cohomological visibility problem: pointwise semantics, information-state forcing, local-object semantics with structural absence and gluing obstructions, and the refinement dynamics required to state the monotonicity results.

The logical point of the paper is twofold. First, the passage from pointwise semantics to information states and local objects is not cosmetic: the gluing predicates that drive the obstruction theory are provably invisible at the lower forcing language. Second, once those predicates are available, one can ask a semantic question that does not arise at the lower layers at all, namely which part of a gluing obstruction is visible without enlarging the state space and which part remains hidden.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Three Main Results.}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Theorem A: forcing necessity.}
After separating admissible reference, local sectionability, compatible local sectionability, and global sectionability, we show that compatible local existence is characterized by sheafification (\Cref{thm:sheafification-characterization}) and that typed readouts obey an address-before-value constraint (\Cref{prop:address-before-value}). The key logical separation result is that compatible local sectionability and gluing-level absence cannot be defined by any formula in the information-state forcing language without the local-object layer (\Cref{thm:pointwise-irreducibility}). This is the theorem that justifies the enrichment on logical grounds rather than on expository convenience.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Theorem B: branched gerbe semantics.}
Once an abelian band $\Band$ has been fixed on the slice site, every local object functor admits a canonical split realization prestack (\Cref{prop:split-realization-prestack}), so the existence of a groupoid-valued lift is not itself the issue. The substantive hypotheses are semantic: we work with gluing-sensitive lifts whose stackifications recover the sheafification of isomorphism classes (\Cref{prop:stackification-components}), identify visible value classes with the global connected components of the realization stack (\Cref{thm:visible-value-components}), and are globally conservative at the terminal fibre when global existence is compared back to the original local object functor. In that setting, each visible branch $v$ determines a banded component gerbe $\mathfrak G_v$ (\Cref{thm:component-gerbe-decomposition}), and gluing-level absence occurs exactly when visible branches exist but all of their component gerbes are non-neutral (\Cref{thm:gerbe-null-semantics}). Under the additional branch-constancy hypothesis, the realization stack decomposes as
\[
\mathfrak L_r\simeq \coprod_{v\in \Vis_{p,s}(r)} \mathfrak G_v
\]
(\Cref{cor:branch-decomposition}). Only when $|\Vis_{p,s}(r)|=1$ does one recover a single obstruction class attached to the reference.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Theorem C: visible quotients and the standard contextuality anchor.}
Fixing one visible branch $v$, a finite abelian obstruction presentation yields the strict visible quotient $A/H_\alpha$, initial among coefficient reductions trivializing that chosen branch presentation (\Cref{thm:initial-visible-quotient}) and detected exactly by the presentation-admissible character channels (\Cref{thm:character-definability-barrier,cor:visible-quotient-character-separation}). At the class level, a finite Cech nerve for $\omega_v$ yields a homological evaluation map
\[
\mathrm{ev}_{\omega_v}:H_2(N(\mathcal U),\mathbb Z)\to A,
\]
whose cokernel is the intrinsic visible quotient (\Cref{thm:intrinsic-visible-quotient}). This quotient captures precisely the $H_2$-visible part of the obstruction, while the blind remainder is exactly the pure $\operatorname{Ext}$-residual (\Cref{thm:character-blind-obstructions}). In the unique-branch support-presheaf setting, the theory recovers the Abramsky--Brandenburger no-global-section picture and refines it by identifying the precise blind residual (\Cref{thm:unique-branch-contextuality-comparison}). This is the paper's main external anchor to an established line of work.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Secondary consequences.}
The multi-branch theory shows that visibility depends essentially on whether branch information is available. If the branch label is known, visibility is controlled by $\bigcap_v K_v$; if the same channel must remain admissible on every branch, it is controlled by $\sum_v K_v$; and the exact branch-preserving strictification budget is $\max_v |K_v|$ (\Cref{thm:branch-sensitive-visible-quotient,thm:branch-uniform-visible-quotient,thm:multi-branch-strictification-budget}). More sharply, the canonical comparison map
\[
A_{\mathrm{vis}}^{\mathrm{br}}(r)\twoheadrightarrow A_{\mathrm{vis}}^{\mathrm{unif}}(r)
\]
has kernel $(\sum_v K_v)/(\bigcap_v K_v)$, so the hidden budget behind uniform visibility factors exactly into a branch-resolution term and a residual within-branch term (\Cref{thm:branch-comparison-exact-sequence,thm:branch-resolution-budget,cor:uniform-hidden-budget-factorization}). Together with \Cref{ex:one-bit-hidden-obstruction,ex:rp2-character-blind,ex:two-branch-sphere-separation}, this gives concrete visible, blind, and multi-branch test cases.

The hypotheses used in these results are stratified rather than cumulative. Branch constancy is used only for the decomposition corollary, not for the branch-gerbe theorem itself. Global conservativity enters only when stack-global objects are compared back to the original local object functor. Finite nerve presentations appear only in the finite abelian visibility theorems. The logical separation result and the basic gluing semantics therefore do not depend on the full finite obstruction package.

These results are organized as follows. \Cref{sec:preliminaries} isolates the conservative-extension framework, and \Cref{sec:information-states} introduces information states, persistence, singleton conservativity, and lifted pointwise soundness. \Cref{sec:null-decomposition} develops the local-object layer, proves the forcing-necessity theorem, and sets up the realization-prestack semantics. The subsequent obstruction-theoretic and homological subsections establish the branch-gerbe semantics, the visible quotients, the exact multi-branch comparison sequence, and the Abramsky--Brandenburger comparison. \Cref{sec:multiaxis-refinement} then records the monotonicity results under refinement, while the appendix contains only the finite-state complexity upper bounds.
